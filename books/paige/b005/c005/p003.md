# Part 3: The Main Event

## Tuesday, November 11, 2008 — 19:30 PM The PERT Lounge, Barnett Hall

The PERT Lounge was designed to be a recreational space—a place with couches, a TV, and board games where the high schoolers could relax after a day of assessments.

To Harper, it was a pressure cooker waiting to explode.

She rolled her purple chair into the corner, parking behind a bookshelf filled with battered paperbacks. She pulled out her sketchbook, trying to look busy, trying to be invisible. She was drawing a schematic for a soundboard, focusing on the clean, straight lines of the faders.

The room was packed. It was "Free Time," which meant "Chaos Time."

The door banged open.

A group of boys walked in. They were wearing black t-shirts with skulls on them. The leader was a big kid named Travis. He was a Senior, broad-shouldered and loud, with a sparse mustache that he seemed very proud of.

He scanned the room. He ignored the group of girls watching TV. His eyes landed on the corner.

On Harper.

He broke away from his pack and sauntered over. He didn't respect the perimeter. He walked right past the bookshelf, stepping into the small triangle of space Harper had claimed for herself.

"Hey, Wheels," Travis grinned, leaning his arm against the bookshelf, effectively boxing her in.

Harper gripped her pencil. "I'm working," she whispered, not looking up.

"Working?" Travis laughed. He reached out and tapped the page of her sketchbook with a thick finger. "That looks like homework. We're supposed to be chilling."

"I like homework," Harper said, her voice tight. "Please move. You're blocking the light."

Travis didn't move. He leaned in closer. He smelled of stale cafeteria chili and too much body spray.

"You're feisty," Travis said, his voice dropping to a low, suggestive register that made Harper’s skin crawl. "I like that. You know, you're pretty cute for a cripple."

Harper flinched. The slur hit her like a slap.

She looked up at him. She saw the arrogance in his eyes. He was seventeen, maybe eighteen. He was a man-sized boy who thought the world was his playground.

"Don't call me that," Harper said, her hand shaking.

"Just teasing," Travis smirked. He reached out and grabbed the handle of her wheelchair. He gave it a little jiggle, rocking her back and forth. "So, what's your deal? You got a boyfriend back home? Or are you waiting for a real man?"

Harper felt a cold spike of terror in her chest. He was touching her chair. To a wheelchair user, the chair was part of their body. He was touching her.

She ran the math in her head.

Variable: Age. He looked old. He had to be a Senior. He was probably turning eighteen any day now.

If he turns eighteen, Harper thought, panic rising, and I am sixteen... that changes the legal code. If he touches me then... is it a felony? Or just creepy?

She didn't know the specific statutes of the Virginia Code, but she knew the feeling in her gut. He was a predator. And he was getting too close.

"Let go of my chair," Harper said, her voice rising in pitch.

"Calm down," Travis laughed, leaning down so his face was inches from hers. "I'm just being nice. Why don't we go for a roll? There's a vending machine down the hall. My treat."

"No," Harper said.

"Come on," Travis insisted, his grip tightening on her handle. "Don't be a stuck-up—"

"SHE SAID NO!"

The voice boomed across the lounge.

Mr. Henderson, the vocational evaluator who was on chaperone duty, crossed the room in three long strides. He stepped between Travis and Harper, breaking the line of sight.

"Step away, Travis," Henderson ordered, his voice hard. "Now."

Travis let go of the chair. He held up his hands, feigning innocence. "Whoa, chill. We were just talking."

"She didn't look like she was talking," Henderson said. "She looked like she was cornered. Back off. Go find your friends."

Travis glared at Henderson, then shot a look of pure venom at Harper over the teacher's shoulder. It was a look that said: You got me in trouble.

Harper shrank back into her chair, pulling her knees to her chest. She was shaking violently.

He's huge, she thought. And he's angry. And he's going to be an adult soon. He could hurt me.

This was why she couldn't stay. It wasn't just the noise. It was the vulnerability. At home, Freddie and Jessica and Paige were the wall. Here, she was just a girl in a chair in a corner.

Travis turned away, his ego bruised, his face red. He needed to reassert dominance. He needed to prove he still owned the room.

He looked at the center of the lounge.

The group of girls—the "Pop Squad"—were huddled around the TV, watching Star Search Academy. They were laughing and singing along.

Travis’s eyes narrowed.

"It's Tuesday," Travis announced loudly, marching toward them.

"So?" one of the girls asked, not looking away from the screen.

"Tuesday is Ignition," Travis grunted.

He reached out and snatched the remote control from the table.

Click.

The screen flickered. The singing competition vanished. Instead, heavy metal music blasted from the speakers. Pyrotechnics exploded on the screen. The logo flashed in chrome letters:

## GWF: GLOBAL WRESTLING FEDERATION

"Hey!" the girl screamed, jumping up. "We were watching that!"

"Wrestling is on," Travis said, sitting on the sofa and putting his feet up. "Deal with it."

"Give it back!" The girl lunged for the remote.

Travis held it over his head. "Get away from me."

"You can't just take the TV!" another boy from the Pop Squad shouted, stepping up. "Change it back!"

"Make me," Travis sneered.

The shouting escalated. It wasn't just about the TV anymore. It was about dominance. It was about territory.

Harper shrank into her corner. Her heart was hammering against her ribs.

Violence imminent, her brain screamed. Evacuate.

But she was blocked in. The argument was happening between her and the door.

"Change. It. Back!"

Travis stood up. He grabbed a metal folding chair from the card table.

"I said," Travis roared, "IT'S IGNITION!"

He threw the chair.

It didn't hit anyone, but it smashed against the drywall with a deafening CRASH, leaving a dent in the plaster.

The room erupted. Screams. Shouting. People scattering.

Harper pulled her knees to her chest. She pulled her hood up tight, covering her face. She jammed her fingers into her ears, trying to block out the sound of the shouting and the GWF commentators screaming on the TV.

Static. It's all static.

Staff members rushed in, blowing whistles. "Everyone to your rooms! Now! Move!"

Harper waited until the herd dispersed. She rolled out of her corner, shaking, and fled down the hallway to Suite 200.
