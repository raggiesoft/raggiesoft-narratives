# Part 5: The Permit Phase

## Wednesday, June 27, 2007 — 14:00 PM

The kitchen table was covered in blueprints.

They weren't professional architectural drawings; they were CAD drawings Freddie had generated on his **Iron-Logic** laptop using the dimensions Jenna had gathered with a laser measure. The schematic clearly showed the "aperture"—the precise rectangular cutout required for the lift car to pass through the living room floor.

Jessica walked through the front door, slamming a thick folder onto the counter with a triumphant *thud*.

"Victory," Jessica announced. "The Town of Christiansburg has officially authorized the modifications. We have the building permit for the structural alteration (the aperture in the floor), the electrical permit for the 220-volt screw-drive motor, and the plumbing permit for the basement wet room."

"Did they ask about the joists?" Sarah asked, looking up from her label maker. "Cutting a structural member requires heading it off. Did they question the load calculations?"

"The clerk asked why we were cutting a hole in a perfectly good hardwood floor," Jessica grinned, pouring herself a glass of water. "I told her we were installing a 'Through-Floor Accessibility Conveyance' for a wheelchair user."

She leaned against the counter, looking pleased with herself.

"Technically, the ADA doesn't apply to private residences," Jessica explained, tapping the permit. "Legally, they could have bogged us down in code reviews for weeks. But no town official wants to be the person who said 'no' to a disabled kid getting to his bedroom. I said 'accessibility,' and she stamped it so hard the desk shook."

"Social engineering," Freddie noted from **The Rover**, inspecting the red stamp on the permit. "You leveraged the fear of litigation."

"I leveraged the culture of compliance," Jessica corrected. "The ADA is a powerful shield, even when it’s just a suggestion."

"Excellent," Freddie decided. "Bureaucratic hack successful."

"Now," Jessica said, pointing to the drawing of the basement corner. "Phase Two: The Wet Room. We need to finalize the materials list. The plumber is coming tomorrow to rough in the shower drain."

They looked at the schematic.

**The Pittsburgh Toilet Upgrade:**

- **Existing:** One exposed toilet on a concrete slab.

- **New:** A framed enclosure (6' x 8'). A roll-in tiled shower with a trench drain. A wall-mounted sink (wheelchair height).

"The walls are easy," Jenna said. "2x4s and green-board. But the door?"

She looked at Freddie.

"Standard doors are opaque," Jenna noted. "Wood. Hollow core. If you're in there, and the door is closed... you're in a box."

Freddie stiffened slightly. He thought about the closet in Pasco. He thought about the feeling of walls closing in, cutting him off from the Unit.

"I do not like boxes," Freddie whispered. "Visual confirmation is... preferred."

"We could leave the door off?" Sarah suggested. "Like the upstairs bathroom when we shower?"

"Moisture control," Jessica countered. "If we take hot showers down there, the steam will rot the floor joists above if we don't contain it. We need a door to keep the humidity in the wet room."

"Glass," Paige said.

Everyone looked at her.

"A glass door," Paige clarified. "Like a patio door. Or a full-lite interior door. Clear glass. Tempered."

"See-through?" Sarah asked, blinking. "For a bathroom?"

"We are already naked," Paige shrugged. "We already use a clear shower curtain upstairs. What difference does a clear door make? It keeps the steam in, but Freddie can look out and see us sitting on the Mega-Bed. He never feels trapped."

Freddie visualized it.

He is in the shower. The warm water is hitting his back. He looks up. Instead of a white wooden door blocking his view, he sees a pane of glass. Through it, he sees the basement—Jenna working on her servers, Sarah organizing shelves.

He is not cut off. He is contained, but connected.

"Glass," Freddie decided. "Transparency allows for safety monitoring. If I slip... you see it instantly."

"Plus," Jenna grinned, "it looks cool. Very industrial. Like a lab."

"Glass door it is," Jessica noted, writing it down. "And the shower curtain?"

"Clear vinyl," Freddie and Paige said in unison.

"Standard protocol," Freddie added. "No hiding."

## Thursday, June 28, 2007 — 08:00 AM: The Cut

The contractor Jessica had hired—a gruff man named Miller who usually built decks—arrived with a reciprocating saw and a solemn expression.

"You sure about this?" Miller asked, looking at the pristine hardwood floor in the corner of the living room. "Once I cut this hole, there ain't no putting it back."

"Cut it," Jessica commanded.

Freddie watched from the kitchen, wearing his earplugs.

RRRRRR-CHHHK.

The saw bit into the wood. Dust flew. The noise was a deafening vibration that rattled the plates in the cabinets.

Freddie gripped Paige’s hand. He felt the floor shake.

It wasn't destruction. It was surgery. They were carving a new artery into the heart of the house.

An hour later, it was done.

A perfect, rectangular hole, 4.5 feet by 5 feet, gaped in the living room floor.

Jenna poked her head up through it from the basement, grinning like a goblin.

"Hello from the underworld!" she shouted. "The portal is open!"

## Friday, June 29, 2007 — 13:00 PM The Installation

The **Access-Pro TFL-3000** arrived on a flatbed truck.

It didn't look like an elevator. It looked like the spine of a robot.

Two massive, skeletal steel tracks were bolted vertically to the basement floor and the living room wall, passing straight through the newly cut aperture. The lift car itself wasn't a box; it was a sleek, three-sided capsule that rode between the rails.

"Through-Floor technology," Freddie noted, rolling **The Rover** closer to inspect the drive mechanism. "No hoistway. No pit. It utilizes the structure of the house itself."

"Look at the cap," Jessica pointed out.

Sitting on top of the lift car was a rectangular panel. Miller, the contractor, was currently attaching the piece of hardwood flooring he had cut out of the living room to this panel.

"The Sealing Plug," Sarah identified. "When the lift is down, that panel fills the hole in the living room floor. When the lift is up, the bottom of the car fills the hole. The breach is always sealed."

"Binary state," Freddie nodded. "Open or Closed. No fall hazard."

The installers wired it to the new 220v circuit. They tested it.

*Whirrrrrrrr.*

The lift rose from the basement. It moved silently on the rails. As it crested the living room floor, the bottom of the car locked flush with the hardwood.

Then, they sent it down. As the car descended, the "Plug" on the roof of the car settled perfectly into the opening, turning the living room floor back into a solid surface.

"Ready for a test drive?" the installer asked.

Freddie looked at the open cab. It was metal. It was industrial. It looked like a turbo-lift from a starship.

It was perfect.

He rolled The Rover onto the platform. He locked his brakes.

"Going down," Freddie announced.

He pressed the button.

*Whirrrrrrrr.*

The floor dropped away.

He sank smoothly through the aperture. For a moment, he was in the "between"—seeing the cross-section of the floor joists, the wiring, the guts of the house.

Then, he was through.

Above him, the "Plug" clicked into place, sealing the ceiling of the basement and restoring the floor of the living room.

He descended into the cool, grey light of the Underworld.

He landed gently on the concrete floor.

Jenna was waiting for him, standing next to the newly framed (but not yet tiled) bathroom walls.

"Welcome to the Bridge, Captain," Jenna said, saluting.

Freddie looked up. The ceiling was sealed. He was in the bunker. And he hadn't used the stairs.

"Access confirmed," Freddie whispered, tears pricking his eyes. "The Base is... fully operational."
