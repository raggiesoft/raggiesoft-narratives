# Part 4: The Restoration

## Monday, May 24, 2004 — 12:30 PM 105 Pepper Street SE

The front door clicked shut. Jessica threw the deadbolt. She hung her keys on the hook and turned to face her siblings.

"Done," Jessica announced. "The letter is delivered. The threat is neutralized. She knows."

Freddie was sitting on the beige sofa in the living room. Paige was already sitting next to him, her arm draped over his shoulder, her hand resting on his chest.

"She accepted the data?" Freddie asked quietly.

"She folded," Jessica said, kicking off her boots. "She admitted she isn't pregnant. She admitted it was a ploy for housing. And she knows that if she comes within fifty feet of this house, I will have her arrested."

Jessica walked over to the sofa. She looked at Freddie. She saw the lingering tension in his jaw, the shadow of the fear Vanessa had planted.

"It's over, Captain," Jessica said softly. "The virus is deleted. The system is clean."

Freddie let out a long breath. He leaned his head back against Paige’s shoulder.

"She wanted to isolate the variable," Freddie whispered. "She wanted to be the only input."

"She was jealous," Paige said, tightening her grip on him. "She hated that you loved us. She wanted to own you."

"But she failed," Jessica said, sitting down on Freddie’s other side. She wrapped her arm around his waist, locking him into the center of the formation. "Because she didn't understand the architecture. She thought she was fighting two sisters. She didn't know she was fighting an army."

Freddie looked at them. He felt the familiar, grounding pressure of their bodies pressing against him. This was the sensation Vanessa had tried to shame out of him. This was the "sickness" she had tried to cure.

But it didn't feel sick. It felt like gravity.

"I missed this," Freddie admitted, his voice thick with emotion. "In Seattle... on the bus... I missed the weight."

"We're here," Paige promised, kissing his temple. "We're heavy. And we're staying."

"And speaking of armies," Jessica grinned, pulling her phone out of her pocket. "I think it's time we called in the reinforcements. Aunt Linda called while I was at the library. She's bringing the girls up for the weekend. All of them."

Freddie’s eyes widened. "The Satellites?"

"Sarah, Jenna, Harper, Amanda, and Elara," Jessica listed. "They're coming on Friday. They want to see the new house. And Harper wants to race you."

Freddie smiled. A real, genuine smile that reached his eyes.

"Vanessa would not survive that encounter," Freddie noted. "Seven sisters. Simultaneous input."

"Her head would explode," Paige laughed. "Can you imagine? She freaked out over two of us holding your hand. If she saw Amanda sitting in your lap and Harper hanging off your wheelchair and Jenna using you as a pillow... she would stroke out."

"It is a high-density environment," Freddie agreed. "But I like the density."

"So do we," Jessica said, squeezing him.

She looked at the clock.

"Okay. Protocol shift. We spent the morning dealing with lawyers and liars. The rest of the day is ours. What do you want to do, Freddie? No judgments. No 'Wolf'. Just you."

Freddie thought about it. He looked at the window. He looked across the street.

"I want... lunch," Freddie decided. "The Thick-Stack."

"Brawny Burger?" Jessica asked.

"It is proximal," Freddie said, pointing out the window. "We can walk. The weather is optimal."

"Done," Jessica said. "Let's go. I'm starving."

They stood up. They didn't need to drive. They walked out the front door, locking the fortress behind them.

They crossed **Pepper Street SE**.

They walked through the small asphalt parking lot that separated their house from the commercial strip.

Ahead of them, at **103 Roanoke Street**, the backlit sign of **Brawny Burger** glowed in the afternoon sun. The smell of charbroiled beef wafted across the pavement.

"Target acquired," Freddie said.

They walked inside. The air conditioning was cool. The booths were red vinyl.

They ordered three **Thick-Stack** combos. They sat in a corner booth.

Freddie took a bite of his burger. It was greasy, salty, and perfect.

"Status?" Paige asked, dipping a fry in her shake.

"Optimal," Freddie said.

He looked out the window. He could see his house across the street. It was safe. It was his.

"After this," Freddie said, wiping ketchup from his lip. "We return to Base. We activate the TV. And we watch *The Local*."

"Pajama protocol?" Jessica asked.

"Immediate execution," Freddie ordered.

They finished their meal. They walked back across the street, a three-person unit moving in lockstep.

They entered the house. They stripped off the "Business Armor" and the "Public Defense" clothes. They put on soft flannel pants and worn-out t-shirts.

The "Heap" formed naturally on the sofa.

Freddie lay in the middle. Paige was on his left, Jessica on his right. They tangled their legs together. They rested their heads on his shoulders.

There was no shame. There was no fear.

Vanessa was gone, alone in a library bathroom with her lies.

But here, in the fortress of 105 Pepper Street, Freddie Avery was surrounded by the only thing that mattered.

He closed his eyes, feeling the steady heartbeat of his sisters on either side.

"Status," Freddie whispered.

"Optimal," they answered together.

The Unit was online. And it was unbreakable.
