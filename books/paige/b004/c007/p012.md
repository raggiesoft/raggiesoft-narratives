# Part 12: The Refueling Protocol

## Thursday, May 13, 2004 — 15:10 PM (CST) Concourse C, Chicago O'Hare

The elevator doors opened with a polite chime.

They stepped out into Concourse C. The contrast was jarring. The neon madness and throbbing bass of the tunnel were gone, replaced by the mundane, grey-carpeted reality of the terminal. It smelled of floor wax and grease.

"Reality restored," Freddie breathed, pulling his hood down just enough to see his periphery.

"That was intense," Paige admitted, wiping a bead of sweat from her forehead. "I felt like we were inside a pinball machine."

"We need to re-calibrate," Jessica said, looking at the departure board. "The flight to Roanoke is at Gate C4. We have an hour. We need real food. The peanuts were just a patch."

They scanned the food court options. There were the usual fast-food suspects, but the lines were long and chaotic. Jessica pointed to a sit-down restaurant with dark wood paneling and a view of the tarmac called **The Lakeshore Grill**.

"Booths," Jessica noted. "High backs. Sound dampening. Let's go."

They rolled the wheelchair to the hostess stand. The restaurant was busy, but Jessica’s "General" energy got them a corner booth in the back, away from the clattering kitchen doors.

Freddie slid into the booth next to Paige. The vinyl was cool and cracked with age. He took off his sunglasses for the first time in hours, blinking in the dim light.

A waitress in a striped apron appeared, tossing coasters onto the table. "What can I get you folks started with?"

"Three ice waters," Jessica said immediately. "And we're ready to order."

She didn't need to look at the menu. She knew the "Safe Foods" protocol by heart.

"For him and her," Jessica said, gesturing to the twins. "The Club Sandwich. Turkey, ham, bacon. But listen carefully: **No onions. No tomatoes.**"

"No garden?" the waitress clarified, scribbling on her pad.

"Lettuce is acceptable if it is crisp," Freddie added quietly. "But tomatoes compromise the structural integrity of the toasted bread. They create moisture pockets."

"No tomatoes, got it," the waitress nodded, not batting an eye. "And for you?"

"The Cheeseburger," Jessica said. "Cheddar. Medium-well. And a basket of fries for the table. Extra crispy if you can."

"You got it. Be right out."

When the food arrived ten minutes later, it was a masterpiece of beige predictability.

The club sandwiches were cut into perfect triangles, held together by frilled toothpicks. The toast was golden brown. There wasn't a red, soggy tomato slice in sight.

Freddie pulled the toothpicks out one by one, lining them up on the table.

"Inspection complete," Freddie announced. "Safe to consume."

He took a bite. It was crunchy, salty, and dry. It was exactly what his nervous system needed after the neon assault of the tunnel.

Paige ate her half of the sandwich with equal enthusiasm. "Oh my god," she mumbled around a mouthful of bacon. "Real food. I thought I was going to survive on peanuts forever."

Jessica attacked her burger. It was greasy and heavy, grounding her.

They ate in silence for a few minutes, the only sound the clinking of ice in their water glasses and the distant roar of jets taking off outside the window.

"We did it," Paige said, dipping a fry into a puddle of ketchup. "We crossed the country. We navigated the biggest airport in the world. We survived the tunnel."

"One leg left," Jessica reminded them, wiping her hands on a napkin. "The hop to Roanoke. It's short. Less than ninety minutes."

"And then?" Freddie asked, looking up from his sandwich.

"And then Mom's house," Jessica said. "The minivan. The New River Valley. Quiet."

Freddie nodded. He took a long sip of water.

"I am ready for 'Quiet'," he decided.

Jessica signaled for the check. She paid quickly—Aunt Marge’s credit card taking one last hit for the team.

"Let's move," Jessica said, standing up. "Gate C4. It's time to bring you home."

They gathered their bags. Freddie settled back into the wheelchair, feeling full and heavy. The panic of the morning felt distant now. He had fuel. He had his sisters. And the departure board showed the one word he had been waiting to see for weeks: **ROANOKE - ON TIME.**
