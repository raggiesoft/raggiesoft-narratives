# Part 13: The Propeller Protocol

## Thursday, May 13, 2004 — 16:30 PM (CST) Gate C4A (Ground Level), Chicago O'Hare

The gate for the Roanoke flight wasn't a gate. It was a door that led to the concrete.

"Gateway Express Flight 104 service to Roanoke," the agent announced. "This is a prop service. Any carry-on bags that do not fit under the seat must be pink-tagged and left on the cart."

Freddie looked out the window. The plane was small. It had high wings and two massive, six-bladed propellers hanging off them.

"Turboprop," Freddie identified. "ATR-72 or similar variant. 2-by-2 configuration. It’s loud."

"It's a puddle jumper," Jessica corrected, grabbing the pink tags for their duffels. "But it gets us there."

Because there was no jet bridge, the boarding process for a wheelchair was different. A large white truck with a scissor-lift box on the back—known as an **Ambulift**—pulled up to the side of the plane.

"Your chariot awaits," the ramp agent said, guiding Freddie’s wheelchair away from the stairs the other passengers were using and toward the truck.

They rolled onto the lift platform. The doors hissed shut.

*Whirrrrrrrr.*

The hydraulics engaged. The entire box lifted straight up, rising ten feet into the air until it aligned perfectly with the aircraft’s rear door.

"Vertical integration," Freddie noted, enjoying the mechanical precision of it.

The door opened, and they rolled directly into the cabin. It was tight. The ceiling was low—Freddie would have had to duck if he were standing.

"Row 8," Jessica directed. "Right over the wing."

The configuration was 2x2. Freddie slid into the window seat (8A), looking directly at the engine nacelle. Paige took the aisle seat (8B). Jessica sat across the narrow aisle in 8C.

"Propellers mean vibration," Freddie warned as he buckled the seatbelt. "It will be a different frequency than the jet."

"We can handle it," Paige said, yawning. She looked exhausted. The adrenaline of the tunnel run was fading, leaving her drained.

The engines kicked over. *Whine-CHUG-CHUG-CHUG-ROAR.*

The blades spun into a blur. The cabin filled with a steady, rhythmic thrumming that rattled the plastic window shade.

"Harmonic resonance," Freddie murmured, feeling it in his teeth. It wasn't unpleasant; it was just... present.

The plane taxied quickly and sprinted down the runway. Unlike the heavy 757, the turboprop leaped into the air. They banked hard, aiming southeast toward the Appalachian Mountains.

As soon as they leveled off, the vibration settled into a white-noise drone.

Paige shifted in her seat. She tried to lean against the aisle armrest, but it was hard and uncomfortable. She looked at Freddie.

He was staring out the window, watching the prop spin. He looked stable. He looked... okay.

"Freddie?" Paige asked softly.

"Status?" Freddie asked, turning to her.

"Can I...?" Paige gestured to his shoulder. "I'm crashing. I need to dock."

Freddie blinked. Usually, he was the one asking for the anchor. He was the one who needed the pressure. But looking at his twin sister, he saw the dark circles under her eyes. She had fought the TSA, the crowds, and the Tunnel for him.

He lifted the armrest between them.

"Permission granted," Freddie said gently. "Initiate docking."

Paige let out a sigh that was half-laugh, half-sob. She curled up, leaning heavily into him. She tucked her head into the crook of his neck, her arm looping through his.

Freddie stiffened for a microsecond, then relaxed. He leaned his head against hers, resting his cheek on her hair.

He felt strong. For the first time in days, he wasn't the burden. He was the support. He was the wall she could lean on.

"I've got you," Freddie whispered, repeating the words she had said to him on the ascent.

"Thanks, brother," Paige mumbled, her eyes already closing. "Wake me when we see the Star."

Across the aisle, Jessica watched them. She saw Freddie sitting up straighter, taking his job as "The Pillow" seriously. She saw Paige finally letting her guard down.

Jessica smiled, leaning her head back against her own seat. The propellers droned on, carrying the three of them over the Ohio Valley, closer and closer to the green hills of home.
