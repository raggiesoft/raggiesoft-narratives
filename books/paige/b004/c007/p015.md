# Part 15: The Final Mile

## Thursday, May 13, 2004 — 07:45 PM (EST)Long Term Parking Lot A, Roanoke Regional Airport

The air in the parking lot was heavy, humid, and smelled like damp earth. To Freddie, it smelled like victory.

They had retrieved their duffel bags from the carousel—which had taken all of five minutes, given the small size of the airport—and returned the wheelchair to the attendant near the sliding doors. Freddie was walking on his own now. He was tired, his legs felt like jelly, but the ground beneath his feet was Virginian asphalt.

He stopped in front of the car.

It was a dark green **Takara Sanctuary**. A sensible, reliable family sedan. But to Paige, it was a chariot of precision.

"Hello, beautiful," Paige whispered, patting the hood.

She unlocked the doors.

"Shotgun," Freddie called out instantly, grabbing the handle of the front passenger door.

"Accepted," Jessica said, tossing her bag into the trunk. "I'll take the Executive Suite in the back. I'm off duty."

Paige slid into the driver’s seat. She didn't just sit; she docked. She adjusted the mirror. She depressed the clutch pedal with her left foot—a firm, mechanical resistance that felt a thousand times better than the mushy rental car pedal in Seattle.

She reached for the gearshift. It was a 5-speed manual stick rising from the center console. She wiggled it in neutral. *Left-right. Left-right.*

"System check," Paige smiled, the tension of the flight melting away. "Transmission is manual. Driver is in control."

"Engage," Freddie commanded, buckling his seatbelt.

Paige turned the key. The engine purred to life. She shifted into first gear, eased off the clutch, and felt the car bite the pavement. They rolled out of the lot, paid the ticket (Aunt Marge again), and merged onto the access road.

## 08:10 PM — Interstate 81 South

They merged from the city spur onto the main artery. **Interstate 81.**

It was a river of steel. The sun was setting behind the mountains, casting long purple shadows, but the highway was alive.

"Traffic report," Freddie announced, staring out the windshield. "Volume is high. Flow is continuous. Zero accidents reported."

He watched a massive tractor-trailer roar past them in the left lane, its diesel stack puffing a small cloud of black smoke.

"Look at the torque," Freddie marveled. "Hauling 80,000 pounds uphill."

"It's a beautiful sight," Jessica agreed from the back seat, leaning her head against the window.

Paige downshifted to fourth to pass a slow-moving minivan, revving the engine slightly. She felt connected to the road. No autopilot. No turbulence. Just gears and tires.

They cruised south, passing the Salem exit, then the climb up Christiansburg Mountain. The truck lane was packed with heavy haulers grinding their way up the grade, hazard lights flashing.

Freddie counted them. "One. Two. Three. Four... Big rigs. All of them."

They crested the hill. The New River Valley opened up before them.

"Exit 114," Paige said, flipping her turn signal. "Descending."

They took the off-ramp, swinging onto Route 8. The highway noise faded, replaced by the slower rhythm of town driving. They passed the fast-food joints, the gas stations, and the familiar brick buildings of Christiansburg.

They turned left. Then right.

The street sign flashed in the headlights: **Pepper Street SE**.

"Target acquired," Freddie whispered.

Paige slowed down. She pulled up to the curb in front of a modest house. It was dark. The porch light was off because they hadn't been there to flip the switch in days. It looked quiet. It looked empty. It looked safe.

## 105 Pepper Street SE

"We're here," Paige said, shifting into neutral and pulling the handbrake. *Click-click-click.*

The engine idled for a second.

"Home," Freddie said.

He didn't wait. He unbuckled his seatbelt and opened the door. He stepped out onto the sidewalk. He listened.

He didn't hear sirens. He didn't hear Vanessa calling him a project. He didn't hear his mother criticizing him for wanting a wheelchair or judging them for needing to sleep in a pile to feel safe.

He heard crickets. He heard the distant hum of I-81. He heard the wind in the trees.

"The audio profile," Freddie said, closing his eyes and taking a deep breath of the cool evening air. "It is quiet. It is ours."

"It's perfect," Jessica said, stepping out of the back seat and stretching. "No judges. No juries. Just us."

They stood there for a moment, the three of them, looking at the house. This was the one place where the "rules" didn't apply. If Freddie wanted to use a wheelchair here, he could. If they wanted to sleep in the living room together because the trauma of the last week was too much to handle alone, they could.

"Let's go inside," Paige said, grabbing the keys from the ignition. "I'm unlocking the door. Freddie, you turn on the lights. Jessica, bring the snacks."

"Copy that," Jessica said, grabbing the half-eaten bag of peanuts.

They walked up the path together, crossing the threshold into the only world that truly understood them.
