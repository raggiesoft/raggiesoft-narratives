# Part 14: The Valley Protocol

## Thursday, May 13, 2004 — 19:10 PM (EST) Gateway Express Flight 104, Descending into Roanoke

The pitch of the propellers changed. The steady drone shifted to a higher, biting whine as the blades bit into the thicker air of the Blue Ridge Mountains.

"Wake up," Freddie whispered, nudging Paige’s head gently with his own. "We are crossing the ridge."

Paige blinked awake, pulling her arm back from Freddie’s grasp. She rubbed her eyes, looking out the scratched plexiglass window.

Below them, the world had changed. The flat, grey grid of Chicago was gone. In its place was the rolling, deep green ocean of the Appalachian Mountains.

"Home," Paige breathed, pressing her hand against the plastic.

Freddie leaned forward, his nose almost touching the window. He wasn't looking for the mountains. He was looking for the infrastructure.

He scanned the valley floor. And then, he saw it.

A long, grey ribbon of asphalt cutting through the green, packed bumper-to-bumper with 18-wheelers. **Interstate 81.**

"The Artery," Freddie announced, a massive smile breaking across his face. "Look at the volume. That is a heavy freight day. Look at the spacing. It's beautiful."

He watched the trucks—tiny white rectangles from this height—moving in their relentless, logical supply chain. He had thought he would never see them again. He thought he was trapped in Washington forever. But there they were, hauling cargo north to New York and south to Tennessee, right through his backyard.

"I missed the trucks," Freddie admitted, his voice thick with emotion.

"I know you did," Jessica smiled from across the aisle.

The plane banked hard to the left, circling the "bowl" of the valley to line up with Runway 33.

As the wing dipped, the view opened up to **Mill Mountain**.

Standing proud on the summit, white steel against the dark green trees, was the **Roanoke Star**.

It wasn't illuminated yet—the sun was still low in the western sky, casting long shadows over the valley—but the massive white star structure was unmistakable. It stood watch over the city like a guardian.

"Visual confirmation," Freddie reported, pointing a trembling finger. "The Star. It is present. We are in the correct sector."

"We're home, Freddie," Paige said, reaching over to squeeze his hand. "We actually made it."

The turboprop dropped lower. They flew over the familiar landmarks—the mall, the car dealerships on Williamson Road, the train tracks.

*Thump-squeak.*

The wheels hit the Virginia tarmac. It wasn't the smooth, endless concrete of O'Hare. It was rougher, shorter, and felt like a handshake.

The propellers roared into reverse, slowing them down before they ran out of runway. The plane turned off the active strip and taxied toward the small, brick terminal.

It was quiet here. There were no lines of jets waiting to take off. There was just the mountains, the evening light, and the smell of honeysuckle and diesel fuel that seeped into the cabin as the engines wound down.

"Welcome to Roanoke," the flight attendant announced, her voice sounding tired but friendly. "Local time is 7:15 PM. Please stay seated until the prop has come to a complete stop."

Freddie unbuckled his seatbelt the second the light turned off.

"Mission Status," Freddie declared, looking at his sisters.

"Mission Accomplished," Jessica said, unbuckling her own belt.

Paige let out a long, satisfied sigh, stretching her legs. She was thinking about the parking lot.

"Long Term A," Paige said, smiling at the thought. "The Sanctuary is waiting. And thank God for that."

She flexed her left foot, imagining the clutch pedal. After days of riding in shuttles, airplanes, and that horrible automatic rental car in Seattle (the "slushbox," as she called it, where the gears changed with the enthusiasm of a wet sponge), she was desperate for control.

"I'm driving," Paige announced firmly. "I need to feel a real transmission. I need to shift gears myself."

"Agreed," Jessica said, grabbing her bag. "Get us to the car. Get us home."
