# Part 1: The Final Bill

## Thursday, May 13, 2004 — 07:30 AM (PST) The Emerald Grille (Lobby Level), The Airport Grand Hotel

You could tell the quality of a hotel by its breakfast.

If it was a "motel" (like the ones they had avoided), breakfast was a sad table in the lobby with stale donuts and a dispenser of watery orange juice. If it was a "mid-range" hotel, it was a buffet with rubbery scrambled eggs and a waffle maker you had to operate yourself.

But this was **The Airport Grand**.

Here, breakfast wasn't free. It was a destination.

They sat in a booth upholstered in dark green velvet. The table was set with heavy linen napkins, silver forks that felt cold and substantial in the hand, and crystal water glasses. The air smelled of fresh-ground coffee and expensive bacon.

"This is correct," Freddie noted, unfolding his napkin and placing it across his lap. "The acoustics are dampened. The lighting is low. It is calm."

"It's expensive," Jessica corrected, looking at the menu. "Twelve dollars for eggs? That's highway robbery."

"It's 'Airport Pricing'," Paige said, scanning her own menu. "But we have the budget. Let's eat like kings before we get shoved into a metal tube for five hours."

A waiter in a white shirt and black vest approached the table with a silver coffee pot.

"Good morning," he said softly. "Coffee to start?"

"Please," Jessica said, turning her cup over.

"And for you, sir?" the waiter asked Freddie.

Freddie didn't hesitate. He had been denied his preferred liquid fuel for the entire trip because of Vanessa.

"Chocolate milk," Freddie ordered. "Large. No ice."

"Excellent choice," the waiter nodded.

When the glass arrived, it was tall, cold, and dark brown. Freddie took a long sip. It was thick, sweet, and coated his throat.

He remembered Vanessa watching him drink milk once at the university cafeteria. She had wrinkled her nose in disgust. *"Ugh, Freddie. It’s mucus. It’s for baby cows. It makes you soft."*

Freddie wiped a chocolate mustache from his lip.

"Vanessa was wrong," Freddie whispered. "Calcium is structural. Sugar is energy."

"She hated everything that made people happy," Paige noted, ordering her own stack of buttermilk pancakes.

"I am happy," Freddie decided.

He looked at the waiter. "I will have the Belgian Waffle. Two eggs, over easy. Bacon, crisp. And wheat toast. Dry."

"Wheat toast," the waiter wrote. "Not sourdough?"

"Never sourdough," Freddie and Paige said in unison.

The meal arrived on large, hot ceramic plates. It was efficient and delicious. Freddie cut his waffle into a precise grid, dipping each square into the syrup in a systematic pattern.

When they were finished, the waiter didn't bring a cash register receipt. He brought a leather folio.

Jessica opened it. She didn't reach for cash. She didn't reach for her wallet.

"Room 512," she said, uncapping a pen. She wrote the room number on the 'Charge to Room' line and signed her name with a flourish. *Jessica Avery.*

"Thank you, Ms. Avery," the waiter said, taking the folio. "It will be added to your final folio at checkout."

Freddie watched the transaction.

"That is... efficient," Freddie noted. "We eat now. We pay later. It consolidates the data into one final transaction."

"It's how business travelers do it," Jessica said, standing up. "And today, we are strictly business."

"Agreed," Freddie said, finishing the last of his chocolate milk.

He stood up, feeling full, caffeinated (via the sugar), and ready.

"Let's go checkout," Jessica said. "The shuttle leaves in thirty minutes."

They walked out of the quiet, velvet-lined restaurant and toward the front desk, ready to settle the account and close the file on Washington State.
