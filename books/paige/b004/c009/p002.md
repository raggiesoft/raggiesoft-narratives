# Part 2: The Hardware Upgrade

## Tuesday, August 3, 2004 — 19:30 PM 105 Pepper Street SE

The living room was transformed into a command center. The coffee table was covered in glossy brochures Freddie had requested by mail: *TiLite*, *Quickie*, *Colours*.

Freddie sat on the floor, surrounded by the paperwork. He wasn't looking at the "Lifestyle" photos of people playing basketball or smiling on beaches. He was looking at the technical specifications table on the back page of the *TiLite TR* brochure.

"Titanium," Freddie murmured, tracing the line item. "Grade 9. Seamless aerospace tubing."

"Strength-to-weight ratio is optimal," Jessica agreed, reading over his shoulder. "And it absorbs vibration. That means less static on the sidewalk."

"Matte black," Paige added, pointing to the color swatch. "Stealth mode. No shiny chrome to attract attention."

"The Rover," Freddie christened it. "It is not a chair. It is a vehicle."

*Rring. Rring.*

The phone on the wall chirped. The caller ID flashed: **ELLEN AVERY (NORFOLK).**

The atmosphere in the room shifted instantly. The warm, creative energy of the "Rover Project" evaporated, replaced by the familiar tension of the "Parental Firewall."

"I'll handle it," Jessica said, standing up. She picked up the receiver. "Hello, Mother."

Freddie and Paige went silent. They could hear Ellen’s voice tinny and sharp on the other end.

*"Jessica? I just got a notification from the insurance company. Something about a 'Durable Medical Equipment' pre-authorization? Please tell me Freddie isn't... he isn't getting a wheelchair."*

Jessica leaned against the counter. "He is. We're ordering a custom rigid-frame. Titanium."

*"Absolutely not,"* Ellen snapped. *"He can walk, Jessica. The doctors said if he keeps up with his PT, he can walk. Getting a chair is giving up. It’s... it’s making him an invalid. What will people think?"*

Jessica rolled her eyes. It was always about the *optics*. To Ellen and Robert, disability was a failure of discipline. If Freddie just "tried harder," he would be normal. If he just "focused," he wouldn't be autistic.

"People will think he's moving efficiently," Jessica said coolly. "Walking causes him pain. It drains his battery. The chair extends his range."

*"He's nineteen years old!"* Ellen argued. *"He shouldn't be rolling around like an old man. And Great Aunt Marge... God rest her soul... she would be heartbroken to see him stop trying."*

Freddie flinched. He looked at the photo of Great Aunt Marge on the mantle. He remembered the last time he saw her in June, before the end. She had been frail, but she had been certain.

*Memory Log: Marge.* *"Sit down, Freddie ," Marge had wheezed. "Stop trying to be a soldier. Just be comfortable."*

Freddie reached out. He took the phone from Jessica.

"Mother," Freddie said. His voice was flat.

*"Freddie,"* Ellen’s voice softened into that pitying tone he hated. *"Honey, listen to me. Don't let your sisters talk you into this. You're strong. You don't need a wheelchair. You just need to—"*

"I do not need to struggle," Freddie interrupted.

*"But—"*

"The walking variable is inefficient," Freddie stated, staring at the TiLite brochure. "It consumes 60% of my processing power just to maintain balance. That leaves only 40% for observation, calculation, and interaction. With the Rover, mobility cost drops to 10%. Processing power increases to 90%."

*"Freddie, life isn't a computer program,"* Ellen sighed. *"It's about appearance. It's about fitting in."*

"I do not fit in," Freddie said. "I am a divergent system. And regarding Aunt Marge..."

He looked at the checkbook sitting on the counter. The one linked to the trust fund Marge had left them. The fund that paid the mortgage, the bills, and the grocery tab. The fund Ellen had no access to.

"Marge provided the resources for optimization," Freddie said. "She did not attach conditions of 'normalcy.' She accepted the output as is."

*"She was an enabler,"* Ellen muttered. *"And she let you three... well, I don't want to get into the sleeping arrangements again. It's unnatural, Freddie. Adults don't share a bed. It's sick."*

"It is secure," Freddie corrected. "It is a zero-friction environment. And since Marge pays the mortgage, her protocol overrides yours."

He handed the phone back to Jessica.

"Authorize the purchase," Freddie commanded.

Jessica grinned. She put the phone back to her ear.

"You heard the Captain, Mother. We're buying the chair. And we're buying the 'Sport Package' wheels because they look cool. We'll send you a picture."

She hung up without saying goodbye.

## The Purchase

"Status?" Paige asked, rubbing Freddie's back.

Freddie looked at the brochure. He felt a weight lift off his chest. For years, he had walked because *they* wanted him to. He had endured the cramps, the spasms, the fatigue, just to avoid the "shame" of the chair.

But Marge hadn't cared about shame. Marge had cared about *him*.

"Status: Upgrading," Freddie said. He pointed to the order form. "Camber the wheels 2 degrees. Better turning radius."

"Done," Jessica said, grabbing a pen. "Let's build this tank."

That night, they didn't just sleep; they hibernated.

Freddie lay in the middle of the King bed.

- **Left Flank:** Paige, her leg thrown over his hip to anchor him.

- **Right Flank:** Jessica, back-to-back with him, a solid wall against the door.

Ellen called it "sick." Robert called it "weird." But as Freddie drifted off, surrounded by the heat and scent of his sisters, he knew the truth.

It wasn't sick. It was **survival**. And for the first time in his life, he was going to stop apologizing for surviving.

"Rover arrives in two weeks," Freddie whispered into the dark.

"Two weeks," Paige promised, kissing his shoulder. "Then we see how fast you can really go."
