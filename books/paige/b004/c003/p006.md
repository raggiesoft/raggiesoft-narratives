# Part 6: The Detour

## Wednesday, May 5, 2004 — 09:15 AM Interstate 81 North

The silver **Avalon** sedan tore down the left lane of Interstate 81, the engine whining as the RPMs climbed.

Jessica Avery was driving. Her hand rested on the stick shift, gripping the knob so hard her knuckles were white.

*Clutch. Shift. Fourth to Fifth. Gas.*

She drove with a terrifying, mechanical precision. She wasn't driving to class. She wasn't driving to the grocery store. She was driving to war.

In the passenger seat, Paige was curled into a ball. She clutched Freddie’s orange pill bottle in one hand and the printout of the flight itinerary in the other. She stared out the window at the blurred trees, her eyes red-rimmed and unblinking.

"We're making good time," Jessica announced, her voice tight. "ETA to Roanoke Long Term Parking is 09:40. Flight departs at 11:00."

"He's been gone twelve hours," Paige whispered, the math playing on a loop in her head. "Twelve hours without meds. Twelve hours of vibration. Jess, what if he gets off the bus before Seattle? What if he panics and gets off in... I don't know, Missouri?"

"He won't," Jessica said, downshifting smoothly as a truck cut them off. "He follows the plan. The ticket says Seattle. He will go to Seattle. And we will be there."

They pulled into the **Roanoke Regional Airport**. Jessica bypassed the expensive garage and headed for the Long Term lot. She parked the Avalon under a light pole—Position G-14.

"Move," Jessica commanded.

They grabbed their single duffel bags. They ran to the shuttle stop.

Inside the terminal, **Gateway Airlines** was a chaotic sea of people. It was a budget carrier, famous for its "cattle call" boarding process. No assigned seats. First come, first serve.

"We need Boarding Group A," Jessica said, eyeing the monitors. "If we get stuck in middle seats, we can't plan."

They checked in at the kiosk. **Group A, Position 14 & 15.**

"Perfect," Jessica breathed.

They moved through security. Paige carried Freddie’s medication in a clear plastic bag, ready to fight any TSA agent who questioned it. No one did.

At **Gate 4**, the plane was waiting. A 737 painted in gaudy orange and blue.

"Flight 492 to Nashville," the agent announced. "Group A, line up."

Jessica and Paige stood at the front of the line.

"We're coming, Captain," Paige whispered, clutching the boarding pass. "We're flying over the void."

## Wednesday, May 5, 2004 — 18:30 PM Fort Collins, Colorado

The landscape had changed. The endless flat cornfields of Kansas had given way to the rolling, scrub-brush foothills of the Rockies.

Freddie sat by the window. He was exhausted. The vibration of the bus had settled into his bones, a constant, buzzing frequency that made his teeth ache.

He looked for the mountains. He wanted to see the snow-capped peaks Vanessa had promised.

But as they passed **Fort Collins**, the bus didn't stay on the massive, comforting concrete ribbon of **Interstate 25**.

The blinker clicked. The bus took an exit.

"Query?" Freddie asked, his voice raspy. "Why are we exiting the primary artery?"

"Detour," Vanessa said, checking the map she had bought at the last gas station. "The main highway goes through the Eisenhower Tunnel. It's high altitude. Sometimes they route the buses north to avoid the grade and the traffic."

The bus turned onto **US-287**.

It wasn't an interstate. It was a two-lane blacktop cutting straight through the high desert.

The civilization of the highway—the gas stations, the billboards, the other cars—vanished.

Suddenly, they were alone.

To the left, jagged rock formations rose up like teeth. To the right, endless sagebrush. The sun was dipping below the horizon, casting long, menacing shadows across the road.

Freddie gripped the armrest.

"This is incorrect," Freddie whispered. "This is a secondary route. It is dark. There are no lights."

"It's a shortcut to Laramie," Vanessa soothed, though she looked a little nervous herself. "It's fine, Freddie. Look at the rocks. It's scenic."

"It is desolate," Freddie corrected.

The bus picked up speed, roaring down the empty road. Without the streetlights of the interstate, the cabin was plunged into darkness.

Freddie felt the isolation wrap around him. On I-81 or I-70, help was always an exit away. Here, on this lonely stretch of 287, if the bus broke down, they would be swallowed by the night.

He squeezed his eyes shut and started to rock.

*Paige would not take this road,* he thought. *Paige takes the well-lit path.*

## Thursday, May 6, 2004 — 02:00 AM Salt Lake City, Utah

The bus hissed into the **Salt Lake City** intermodal hub. It had been a grueling run through Wyoming, bouncing over expansion joints in the dark.

"Bus change," the driver announced. "Layover is two hours. Next bus departs at 04:00."

"Okay," Vanessa yawned, standing up. "Let's get off. We need food."

"Stand by," Freddie murmured.

He tried to stand up.

A bolt of white-hot lightning shot up his spine.

"Ah!" Freddie gasped, collapsing back into the seat.

"Freddie?" Vanessa asked, looking back. "Leg cramp?"

"Back," Freddie gritted out, tears springing to his eyes. "Lower quadrant. Burning."

He tried again. He used his arms to push himself up, keeping his weight off his tailbone. He shuffled into the aisle, his legs stiff and uncooperative.

They made it off the bus and into the terminal. It was bright, sterile, and cold.

"Restroom," Freddie said urgently.

Vanessa pointed to the men's room. "I'll wait here."

Freddie shuffled into the restroom. It was empty. He moved into the large accessible stall and locked the door.

He leaned against the sink, breathing hard. The burning sensation on his lower back wasn't fading. It was throbbing.

He turned around and lifted the back of his shirt in the mirror.

He twisted his neck to see.

There, right at the base of his spine, the skin wasn't just red. It was angry. A dark, purple bruise the size of a half-dollar had formed, and in the center, the skin was broken and weeping. **Pressure Sore. Stage 2.**

Freddie stared at it in horror.

At home, Jessica checked his skin every night. Paige made sure he shifted his weight every hour. They had special cushions. They had protocols.

Vanessa had told him to "walk it off" in Colby. She hadn't reminded him to shift. She didn't know the schematic.

He let his shirt fall. The fabric scraped against the open wound, sending a fresh wave of nausea through him.

He unlocked the stall. He walked out to the waiting area.

Vanessa was buying a bag of pretzels.

"You okay?" she asked cheerfully. "Ready for the last leg?"

Freddie looked at her. He wanted to scream. He wanted to tell her he was bleeding. He wanted to demand a doctor.

But he heard her voice from the lobby in his head: *He's a burden. He can't function.*

If he told her he had a sore, she would be right. She would see how broken he was.

"Affirmative," Freddie lied, his voice trembling. "Systems nominal."

"Great," Vanessa smiled, handing him a pretzel. "Next stop, Idaho. Then... Seattle."

Freddie took the pretzel. He sat down gingerly on the hard plastic bench, forcing himself not to wince as the sore pressed against the seat.

He was 2,000 miles from home. He was injured. And he was alone.
