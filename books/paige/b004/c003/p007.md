# Part 7: The Burning Mile

## Thursday, May 6, 2004 — 10:30 AM (CST) Gateway Airlines Flight 492 (Approaching Nashville)

The Boeing 737 banked sharply, the engines whining as they began their descent into Nashville.

"Ladies and gentlemen, we're starting our initial approach into BNA," the captain’s voice crackled over the intercom. "Weather in Nashville is sunny and seventy-five."

In Seat 14A, Paige Avery didn't look out the window. She was gripping the armrests so hard her knuckles were white.

"We land in twenty minutes," Jessica said from Seat 14B. She had her **MagnaByte** laptop open on the tray table, running a spreadsheet of arrival times. "Layover is forty-five minutes. Tight, but manageable. We stay in the A-terminal."

"He's still moving," Paige whispered. She wasn't looking at the flight tracker. She was staring at the back of the seat in front of her, visualizing a silver bus moving across a map in her head. "I can feel it. He's bouncing."

"He's in Utah," Jessica calculated, checking her watch. "Or crossing into Idaho. We are travelling at 500 miles per hour. He is travelling at 65. We will pass him over the Rockies."

"Did you pack the cushion?" Paige asked suddenly, panic flaring in her eyes. "The gel cushion for his chair? If he's sitting on a bus seat for this long..."

"I couldn't bring the chair cushion, Paige," Jessica said gently. "It wouldn't fit in the overhead. But I grabbed the memory foam travel pillow. It's better than nothing."

Paige squeezed her eyes shut. She knew Freddie’s skin. She knew how fragile it was.

"He's hurting," Paige murmured. "I know he is. And he's not telling her."

"We'll fix it," Jessica promised, closing her laptop with a snap. "Tonight. In Seattle. We fix everything."

## Thursday, May 6, 2004 — 13:00 PM (MST) Interstate 84 West, Idaho

The landscape had flattened out into a relentless, monotonous expanse of brown scrub brush and gray asphalt. This was **The Idaho Line**.

Inside the bus, the air was stale and hot. The air conditioning was struggling against the high desert sun beating down on the metal roof.

Freddie sat in the window seat. He wasn't looking at the scenery anymore. He was focused entirely on the screaming nerve endings in his lower back.

The pressure sore—the "secret fire" —was no longer a dull ache. It was a sharp, biting agony that throbbed with every vibration of the engine.

*Shift right,* his brain commanded. He leaned to the right, pressing his shoulder against the hot window.

*Pain spike.* The movement pulled the skin.

*Shift left.* He leaned toward Vanessa.

Vanessa looked up from her magazine. She lowered her sunglasses.

"You're fidgeting, Captain," she said. Her voice wasn't mean, but it was tired. She was exhausted too. "Try to sit still. We'll be in Boise soon."

"It hurts," Freddie whispered. He didn't say *skin*. He didn't say *sore*. He just said the generic word.

"I know," Vanessa sighed. "It's a long ride. My back hurts too. Just try to sleep."

She didn't offer to help him move. She didn't check his positioning. She just told him to endure it.

Freddie looked at her.

In the beginning, back in Roanoke, she had been the Pilot. The partner in the adventure. Now, as he looked at her profile against the endless gray plains, the lyrics of his own future song began to form in the static of his mind.

*Are you my lover, or are you my guard?* *Is this a journey, or a prison yard?*

She held his phone in her bag. She held the tickets. She held the water bottle. She held the key to every single part of him .

"Boise," Freddie croaked, his throat dry.

"Just a quick stop," Vanessa said. "Driver swap. Then we push to Washington."

The bus rumbled on. Mile after mile. The vibration traveled up the seat, through his spine, and into his teeth.

He closed his eyes and tried to summon the image of the King Bed on Pepper Street. He tried to feel the weight of the blanket. But the image was fuzzy, distorted by the pain and the distance.

*I am drifting,* Freddie thought. *The anchor is gone.*
