# Part 4: The Supply Run

## Monday, May 10, 2004 — 16:00 PM (PST) Ashford, Pierce County

"Logistics run," Jessica announced, grabbing the car keys. "The fridge is empty, and the Vanguard is thirsty. We need fuel and food."

They piled back into the car. The drive from the lodge to the "town" of Ashford took less than three minutes.

Ashford wasn't a city. It was a strip of civilization carved out of the forest: a few inns, a pottery shop, and the main hub of local life—**The Ashford Outpost**.

It was a gas station, but it looked like a log cabin that had grown gas pumps.

"Pulling in," Paige said, steering the white sedan up to the pump.

"I'll handle the fuel," Jessica said. "You two handle the rations. We need dinner. Something hot. Something simple."

"Copy that," Freddie said.

He stepped out of the car. The air smelled of gasoline, yes, but also of damp earth and the massive cedar trees that loomed right behind the store.

He walked into the store with Paige.

It was perfect.

It wasn't a sprawling supermarket with fluorescent glare and forty aisles of choices. It was a village store. Tight, cluttered, and cozy. The aisles were narrow wooden shelves packed with camping gear, fishing bait, and cans of soup.

"Scale: Manageable," Freddie whispered, adjusting his glasses.

He walked down the aisle. He didn't have to dodge carts. He didn't have to filter out a thousand faces. There was just a guy in a fishing hat buying worms and a woman buying milk.

"What's the menu, Captain?" Paige asked, grabbing a red plastic hand basket.

Freddie scanned the shelves. He didn't want fancy. He wanted *grounding*.

"Macaroni and Cheese," Freddie decided, pointing to the shelf. "The deluxe kind. The one with the liquid cheese pouch. Not the powder."

"Shells and Cheddar," Paige identified, tossing two blue boxes into the basket. "Solid choice."

"And meat tubes," Freddie added.

"Hot dogs?"

"Affirmative. Beef franks. We can roast them on the woodstove if the power fails. Or just boil them."

They moved through the small store, gathering the components of a comfort meal.

- A bag of **Ridged Potato Chips** (essential for the crunch texture).

- A gallon of 2% milk.

- A pack of **Milk Chocolate Bars**, **Honey Grahams**, and **Marshmallows**.

"S'mores protocol?" Paige grinned, holding up the bag of marshmallows.

"Essential thermal experiment," Freddie agreed.

They brought their haul to the counter. The cashier was a teenage girl reading a paperback book. She didn't look at Freddie like he was weird. She just scanned the items.

"Heading up the mountain?" she asked.

"Just to the lodge," Paige said.

"Nice night for a fire," the girl said. "Total was \$24.50."

Freddie watched the transaction. Simple. Exchange of goods. Friendly data. No threats.

They walked back out to the pumps. Jessica was just clicking the nozzle off.

"Tank full," Jessica reported. "Rations?"

"Secured," Freddie said, holding up the grocery bag. "We have the components for a feast."

## 17:30 PM — The Bear's Den

Back at the cabin, the sun was beginning to dip behind the tree line, casting long, indigo shadows through the forest.

Inside, the cabin was warm. Paige had made good on her promise; a small fire was crackling in the cast-iron woodstove, orange light dancing behind the glass door.

Jessica took charge of the kitchenette. The smell of boiling pasta and rich, melting cheese soon filled the small space, masking the scent of the rental car's upholstery that still clung to their clothes.

They didn't eat at the small dining table. They ate on the floor, sitting on the thick rug in front of the fire.

Freddie held his bowl of shells and cheese. It was hot, salty, and creamy—pure comfort data. He took a bite of a hot dog.

"Status?" Jessica asked, sitting cross-legged next to him.

Freddie looked at the fire. He listened to the river outside. He looked at his sisters, eating macaroni in their socks on a cabin floor in the shadow of a volcano.

He thought about the "fancy" dinner Vanessa had promised him at the Space Needle—a rotating view, expensive wine, strangers watching him eat.

This was better. This was real.

"Optimal," Freddie whispered, scraping his spoon against the bowl. "Battery is charging."

"Good," Paige smiled, reaching over to steal a ridged chip from his plate. "Because after this, we are testing the S'mores Protocol. And I don't roast marshmallows lightly. I burn them."

"Carbonized sugar is an acceptable variant," Freddie allowed.

He took another bite. He was full. He was warm. He was home—even if home was temporarily a log cabin in Pierce County.
