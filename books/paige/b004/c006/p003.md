# Part 3: The Bear's Den

## Monday, May 10, 2004 — 15:30 PM (PST) Cabin 4, The Iron Creek Lodge

The silence was the first thing Freddie noticed.

Usually, when he woke up, there was a background track: the hum of a refrigerator, the distant whine of traffic, or the frantic pounding of his own heart.

But here, waking up in the center of the Alaska King bed, there was only the sound of breathing.

He lay still. To his left, Jessica was breathing in a slow, deep rhythm. To his right, Paige was snoring softly—a tiny, vibrating snuffle that she always denied doing.

The weight of the heavy down duvet was perfect. It was a "Class 4" weighted blanket, pressing him into the mattress.

He wiggled his toes. He was wearing his thick grey wool socks. The flannel sheets felt soft against the denim of his jeans.

"Status: Restored," Freddie whispered to the ceiling.

Beside him, Paige shifted. She groaned, stretching her arms over her head, her knuckles cracking. She blinked her eyes open, staring at the massive log beams crossing the ceiling.

"Ugh," Paige muttered, rubbing her face. "I had a dream I was driving a go-kart in a swamp."

"Interpretation?" Jessica mumbled, not opening her eyes yet.

"That stupid rental car," Paige complained, sitting up and shaking her hair out. "The transmission feels like pudding. You step on the gas and it just... thinks about it for three seconds before doing anything. I need a clutch. I need to tell the car what to do, not ask it for permission."

"It got us here," Freddie pointed out, pushing himself up on his elbows. "The slushbox was adequate."

"Don't encourage the machines to be lazy, Freddie," Paige teased, nudging him with her shoulder. She looked at him, really looked at him.

His eyes were clear behind his glasses. His hands weren't shaking. The "vibration" that usually hummed under his skin—the anxiety of existing in a loud world—was gone.

"You look good," Paige said softly. "You look... settled."

"I am regulated," Freddie agreed. He looked around the massive bed. "The perimeter held."

"Okay," Jessica said, throwing the duvet back and swinging her legs over the side of the high mattress. Her feet, clad in white cotton socks, hit the wooden floor with a soft *thud*. "Nap protocol complete. Let's inspect the Base."

They slid off the bed. The floorboards were wide-plank pine, smooth and polished. They were cool through their socks, but not cold.

They walked out of the bedroom into the main living area.

"Architecture check," Freddie announced, looking up.

The ceiling vaulted twenty feet high, supported by peeled cedar logs. Two skylights let in the afternoon sun, filtering it through the canopy of the fir trees outside so the light on the rug was dappled and green.

"Leather," Freddie noted, walking to the sofa. He ran his hand over the back. "Cool to the touch. No microfiber static."

"And look at this," Paige said, kneeling in front of the hearth.

It wasn't just a fireplace; it was a black cast-iron woodstove with a glass door. A stack of dry, split birch logs was piled neatly in a copper bin next to it.

"Thermal unit," Freddie said, joining her. "Controlled combustion. We can seal the door and watch the fire without the smoke smell getting in the fabric."

"I'll build one tonight," Paige promised. "Get it roaring."

They moved to the kitchenette. It was small but efficient. Slate countertops (smooth, cold). A small fridge (quiet). A basket on the counter contained packets of local coffee and hot cocoa mix.

"Cocoa detected," Jessica noted, holding up a packet.

"Inventory approved," Freddie smiled.

He walked to the sliding glass door that led to the back porch. He didn't open it yet; he just pressed his hand against the glass.

Outside, the forest was a wall. Ferns grew waist-high right up to the porch railing. Moss hung from the maple trees like green beards. It was dense. It was wild.

"Visual isolation," Freddie observed. "I cannot see the road. I cannot see the office. I cannot see the other cabins."

"Just the trees," Jessica said, coming up to stand on his left.

"And the river," Paige added, standing on his right.

They could hear it clearly now—the constant, rushing white noise of the Nisqually River churning over glacial rocks. It sounded like a massive ventilation fan, scrubbing the air clean.

Freddie looked down at his socks on the wood floor. He wiggled his toes again.

He felt the floor beneath him. Solid. Real.

"This is better than the hotel," Freddie decided. "The hotel was a transition point. This... this is a bunker."

"A Bear's Den," Paige corrected, wrapping an arm around his waist. "And we're hibernating."

"Hungry?" Jessica asked, opening the mini-fridge to check the supplies they had bought at a gas station. "I think we have cheese, crackers, and very expensive beef jerky."

"Rations," Freddie nodded. "Acceptable."

He turned away from the window. He didn't need to check the perimeter anymore. The woods were doing it for him. The static of the city—the buses, the neon, the disappointment of Vanessa—felt a thousand miles away.

Here, there were only logs, fire, river, and the Unit.

"Let's eat," Freddie said. "Then... fire protocol."
