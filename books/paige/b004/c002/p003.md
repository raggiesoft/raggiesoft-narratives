# Part 3: The Split Screen

## Sector A: The Sanctuary

**Location:** 105 Pepper Street **Status:** Decontamination

The master bathroom was a sealed ecosystem of warmth and steam. The exhaust fan had been left off intentionally, allowing the humidity to build until the mirrors were fogged and the world outside the door felt a million miles away.

Freddie wasn't sitting on a bench. He was deep in the water, fully submerged up to his chest. He sat with his legs extended, the water lapping against his collarbones, enveloping him in a heavy, liquid blanket that dulled the sensory sharp edges of the day.

Outside the tub, kneeling on the plush bath mats like acolytes at a shrine, were his sisters.

They were naked, having discarded the armor of the day, but they remained on the dry land of the tile floor. They leaned over the porcelain rim, their arms submerged as they tended to him.

Jessica was at his head. She had a cup, pouring warm water over his hair, rinsing away the sweat of the library.

Paige was at his side. She held a soft, soapy sponge, running it down his arm in long, rhythmic strokes.

"Query," Freddie murmured, his voice echoing slightly against the tile. "Why did she classify the proximity as 'tragic'?"

He looked at Paige. His glasses were off, so she was a soft, beautiful blur.

"She used the word 'sacrifice'," Freddie added, the word tasting bitter. "She implied a loss of function on your part."

Paige paused the sponge. She rested her wet hand on his shoulder, her thumb stroking his skin.

"Because she's running a different operating system, Freddie," Paige said softly. "In her world—the 'normal' world—independence is the highest value. If you aren't standing alone, you're failing."

"But standing alone is inefficient," Freddie countered, splashing the water slightly. "A single column is vulnerable to shear force. A tripod is stable."

"She doesn't know about tripods," Jessica said from behind him, massaging conditioner into his scalp. "She’s a solo act. Remember what she said about her brother? They fight. They compete. To her, love is a zero-sum game. If I give love to you, I have less for myself."

"Is that how you calculate it?" Freddie asked, turning his head slightly to look at Paige. "Do you have less?"

"I have more," Paige said fiercely.

She leaned further over the tub, ignoring the water soaking the front of her torso. She looked him right in the eyes.

"Think about Sarah and Emily," Paige said, referencing the girls from the coffee shop. "They spent three hours yesterday talking about how lonely they are. They go to these parties hoping to find *one person* who understands them. One person who makes them feel safe."

She squeezed his shoulder.

"I already have that," Paige whispered. "I have you. I have Jess. Why would I wander around a loud, smelly frat house looking for something I already have at home?"

"She thinks you are... constrained," Freddie said. "Trapped in my orbit."

"I'm not trapped," Paige smiled, a genuine, warm expression that melted the last of the library's ice. "I'm anchored. There's a difference. A trap holds you down. An anchor keeps you from drifting away."

"She was projecting," Jessica diagnosed, her voice clinical and calm as she rinsed his hair. "She looked at us, and she didn't see a burden. She saw a team. And she realized she’s playing the game alone."

Jessica tapped his shoulder. "Sit up. Let me get your back."

Freddie sat up, water cascading off his chest. He leaned forward.

Jessica began to wash his back, her movements firm and sure. Paige moved to his other arm, continuing the symmetry.

"She’s weird, Freddie," Jessica decided. "Not bad. Just... lonely. She’s trying to decode us because she wants what we have. She just doesn't know how to ask for it without trying to break it first."

"So we do not delete her?" Freddie asked.

"Not yet," Paige said, rinsing the sponge. "But we keep the shields up. She can help with the math. She can help with the code. But she doesn't get a key to the house."

"Agreed," Freddie whispered.

He sank back down into the water, letting it cover him up to his chin.

On either side of him, his sisters watched over him. They weren't missing out on anything. They weren't sacrificing anything.

In the steam-filled silence of 105 Pepper Street, the Avery Unit was exactly where it was supposed to be: together.

## Sector B: The Transaction

**Location:** Westgate Hall (CPI Campus Housing), Room 304 **Status:** Discharge

The air in the cramped dorm room smelled of cheap body spray and the lingering humidity of exertion.

The door to the hallway clicked shut. The heavy deadbolt slid home with a final *clack*.

**Braden** (a sophomore from Sigma Chi, or maybe Pike—it didn't matter) was gone. He hadn't asked for their numbers. They hadn't offered. He had retrieved his backwards baseball cap, mumbled a "Catch ya later," and exited the premises.

It was a successful transaction. The biological pressure had been vented.

**Lauren**, Vanessa’s roommate, flopped back onto her twin XL mattress. She pulled the duvet up to her chin, looking tousled and smug.

"Well," Lauren yawned, checking the time on her Nokia brick phone. "That was efficient. Solid six out of ten. But he had good stamina."

Vanessa stood by the mini-fridge. She grabbed a bottle of water and downed half of it in one long pull. She was wearing a silk robe that looked out of place in the cinderblock room.

"He served the function," Vanessa said coolly.

She walked to her desk, which was meticulously organized—textbooks stacked by height, pens in a cup, laptop closed.

"So," Lauren asked, rolling onto her side to watch Vanessa. "Does this mean you're finally done with Project Boy? The one with the twin shadows?"

Vanessa wiped a smudge of eyeliner from her cheek. She stared at her reflection in the small mirror mounted on the back of the door.

She thought about the library. She thought about Paige’s fierce, trembling arms wrapped around Freddie. She thought about Jessica’s eyes—cold, calculating, and dangerous.

She had tried to use logic to break an emotional bond. It was like trying to cut a diamond with a plastic knife.

"No," Vanessa said softly. "I'm not done."

"Van, come on," Lauren groaned. "The guy is a walking red flag. He brings his *sisters* to class. They bathe him. I heard you say they bathe him! That is... it's too much work. Just find a nice engineering major who can walk without a chaperone."

Vanessa turned around. Her eyes were sharp.

"You don't get it, Lauren," Vanessa said. "Freddie isn't like Braden. Braden is... noise. He's static. He comes in, he takes what he wants, he leaves. There's no signal."

She walked over to the window, looking out at the dark campus.

"Freddie has a signal," Vanessa whispered. "He builds worlds in his head. He has this... intense, focused capacity for devotion. Did you see how he looked at Paige? He looked at her like she was the only thing keeping him on the planet."

"Yeah," Lauren countered. "Because she's his sister. It's weird."

"It's potent," Vanessa corrected. "I want that directed at *me*."

She leaned her forehead against the cold glass.

"I made a mistake today," Vanessa admitted. "I attacked the Unit. I tried to shame Paige into leaving. It backfired. They just tightened the formation."

"So you're giving up?" Lauren asked hopefully.

"No," Vanessa smiled, but it didn't reach her eyes. "I'm changing tactics."

"Firewalls cannot be brute-forced," she murmured to herself, using Freddie's own terminology. "They have to be bypassed."

She turned back to the room.

"I'm going to apologize," Vanessa announced. "I'm going to be the perfect friend. I'm going to bring Jessica coffee. I'm going to ask Paige for advice on... I don't know, hair care. I'm going to make them think I'm harmless."

"You? Harmless?" Lauren snorted. "Good luck selling that."

"I'm a Business major," Vanessa said, smoothing her robe. "I know how to sell anything."

She climbed into her own bed. The sheets were cold. The room was quiet.

Unlike the steam-filled bathroom on Pepper Street, where three people were currently safe and warm and together, Vanessa lay in the dark alone.

"I'll make it work," she whispered to the ceiling.

She would wait. She would watch. And the next time a crack appeared in the Avery Unit, she wouldn't try to pry it open with a crowbar. She would slip in like water.

And then, she would freeze.
