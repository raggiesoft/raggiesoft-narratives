# Part 2: The Mode Switch

## Sunday, May 9, 2004 — 09:45 AM (PST) Convention Place Station

The walk from the Cascade Suites to the transit station was short—just a few blocks through the crisp, sunny Seattle morning. The air smelled of rain-washed pavement and coffee, a sharp contrast to the diesel fumes of the Pathfinder terminal across the street.

They arrived at Convention Place Station, the northern terminus of the Downtown Seattle Transit Tunnel. It wasn't a dark, underground hole; it was a sunken, open-air plaza where the city streets dipped down to meet the subway level.

"There," Freddie said, pointing to the dedicated bus lane. "The insertion point."

They watched as a massive, articulated bus—painted in the teal, yellow, and white livery of the Transit Authority of the Puget Sound (TAPS)—rolled into the station from the street. The diesel engine was rumbling, a low, familiar growl.

"Wait," Freddie whispered, holding up a hand. "Watch the transformation."

The bus came to a halt at the designated "changeover" line. The driver engaged the parking brake.

Then, the magic happened.

The roar of the diesel engine cut out abruptly. Silence descended on the bay.

With a mechanical whir, two long poles rose from the roof of the bus. They extended upward until they made contact with the thick copper wires strung overhead.

Snap-hiss.

The connection was made. The bus hummed to life—not with a roar, but with a high-pitched, electric whine.

"Mode switch complete," Freddie beamed, his eyes wide behind his glasses. "Diesel propulsion offline. Electric traction online. It is a hybrid organism."

"That is pretty cool," Paige admitted, squeezing his arm. "It's like it plugged itself in."

"It is cleaner," Freddie noted approvingly. "No exhaust in the tunnel. Smart engineering."

The bus pulled forward to the boarding platform, gliding silently now. The doors folded open.

"Ride Free Area," Jessica reminded them. "We just walk on."

They stepped aboard. Freddie took a window seat near the articulation joint. Paige sat next to him; Jessica took the seat across the aisle.

The interior didn't smell like the Pathfinder. It smelled of ozone and electricity.

"Next stop," the automated voice announced, crisp and clear. "Westlake Station."

The bus accelerated. It plunged into the dark mouth of the tunnel.

Freddie tensed for a microsecond, expecting the shaking, the noise, the claustrophobia.

But the ride was smooth. The electric motor provided a constant, linear acceleration with zero vibration. The tunnel walls whipped by, illuminated by the bus's headlights, but it felt controlled. It felt like a spaceship launching from a bay.

"Status?" Paige whispered, leaning close.

Freddie looked out at the tunnel lights. He felt the smooth glide of the tires on the dedicated roadway.

"Silent running," Freddie whispered back, relaxing into the seat. "This is... acceptable."

## 09:55 AM — Westlake Station

The bus glided to a halt at Westlake Station. They stepped off onto the mezzanine platform.

"Upstairs," Jessica said, pointing to the signs. "To the Monorail."

They took the escalator up, rising from the subterranean tunnel into the light-filled atrium of the Westlake Center mall.

"Level 3," Freddie recited from the research. "The station is integrated into the architecture."

They reached the Monorail platform. And there it was.

The Metro-Rail.

It wasn't a bus. It wasn't a train. It was a retro-futuristic red streak sitting on a concrete beam high above the street. It looked like something out of a 1960s sci-fi cover—The World of Tomorrow.

"Target acquired," Freddie grinned. "The Monorail. Built for the 1962 World's Fair."

"It looks fast," Paige said.

"It travels the one-mile track in exactly two minutes," Freddie said. "It is a dedicated point-to-point shuttle."

They bought their tickets (\$1.50 each). They boarded the red train. The windows were massive, curving up into the roof to offer a panoramic view of the sky.

Freddie sat right at the front. He looked down through the windshield at the concrete beam stretching out ahead of them, threading between the skyscrapers.

"Ready for launch?" Jessica asked from the seat behind him.

"Ready," Freddie said.

The doors slid shut. The Metro-Rail surged forward. They were flying above the traffic, above the noise, gliding on a beam of concrete toward the white needle piercing the blue sky in the distance.

"Destination," Freddie announced, watching the tower grow larger in the window. "The Sky-Spire."
