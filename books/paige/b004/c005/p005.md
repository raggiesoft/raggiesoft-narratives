# Part 5: The Wheelchair Epiphany

## Sunday, May 9, 2004 — 15:30 PM (PST) The Bayfront Aquarium

The hour in the **Underwater Dome** had been a total reset. The cool blue light, the silent circling of the sharks, and the weightless feeling of the water pressing against the glass had scrubbed the last of the bus vibration from Freddie's bones.

But more importantly, the chair had worked.

Freddie sat in the blue vinyl wheelchair, his legs relaxed for the first time in four days. He wasn't fighting gravity. He wasn't spending 60% of his processing power just trying to stay upright. He was just... looking.

"Time to surface," Jessica announced gently, checking her watch. "The tank is closing."

"Acknowledged," Freddie said.

Jessica unlocked the brakes. She spun him around with an effortless pivot.

As they rolled toward the exit, Paige didn't let go. She walked beside the chair, her hand still resting on the armrest, her fingers laced tightly through his.

"You okay?" Paige asked, looking down at him.

"I am... efficient," Freddie realized. "The energy cost of this excursion was minimal. If I had walked, I would be in system failure by now. But I am still operational."

He looked at the wheels. He thought about **Harper**, his younger sister back in Virginia Beach. Harper was fourteen. She had cerebral palsy too—mild, like his, but painful. She walked with a limp. She fell a lot.

And she was stuck with Ellen.

*Mom says we just need to try harder,* Freddie thought bitterly. *She says the chair is giving up.*

But looking at his sisters—Jessica pushing him without complaint, Paige holding his hand with pride—he realized Ellen was wrong. The chair wasn't giving up. It was a tool. It was a vehicle.

"I have a hypothesis," Freddie said quietly as they emerged from the dark exhibit into the bright, overcast afternoon of the pier.

"What's that?" Jessica asked, navigating the ramp down to the sidewalk.

"The chair," Freddie said, tapping the armrest. "It is not a defeat. It is a strategy. Harper... she struggles. She hurts. Mom makes her walk everywhere."

"I know," Paige said, her face darkening. "Mom thinks it builds character."

"It builds pain," Freddie corrected. "I think... when we get back... we should tell her. We should tell Harper about the chair."

"We'll do better than that," Jessica said firmly. "We'll buy her one."

Freddie looked up, surprised. "We can?"

"We have the Avery Grant," Jessica reminded him. "Aunt Marge left that money for 'Educational and Medical Necessities.' I think a titanium chariot for Harper counts as a necessity."

Freddie smiled. He imagined Harper spinning in a chair like this, fast and purple (her favorite color), instead of limping behind them.

"Purple," Freddie decided. "She would want purple."

"Done," Paige agreed, squeezing his hand. "Project: Harper's Wheels is a go."

They reached the curb. Jessica parked the chair.

"Okay, Captain," she said. "We have to leave the rental here. But the lesson stands. If you need wheels, we get wheels."

They helped him stand. His legs were stiff, but because he hadn't walked the aquarium, they weren't trembling. He felt strong enough to make the transit back.

"One more thing," Paige said, stopping him before they started walking. She looked him dead in the eye, still holding his hand.

"About what Vanessa said. About Italy. And New York."

Freddie flinched. "We deleted that file."

"I want to overwrite it," Paige said fiercely. "Listen to me. If Jessica gets a job in New York... we all go to New York. If I get a fellowship in Italy... we all go to Italy. If you get a job at NASA in Houston... we all go to Houston."

She squeezed his fingers until his knuckles cracked.

"We are a unit, Freddie. We move together. If one of us relocates, the headquarters relocates. No one gets left behind. Ever."

Jessica stepped up on his other side. "I second that motion. The Avery Corporation is a package deal. You don't get one without the other two."

Freddie looked at them. The fear that had been gnawing at him since the lobby—the fear that he was holding them back—evaporated. They weren't staying because they were stuck. They were staying because they were a team.

"Agreed," Freddie whispered. "The Unit moves as one."

"Good," Paige said. "Now let's get out of here. I need a nap."
