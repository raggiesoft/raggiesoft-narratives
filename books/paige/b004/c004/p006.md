# Part 6: The Signal Cut

## Saturday, May 8, 2004 — 14:22 PM (PST) The Cascade Suites, Room 412

The reverb of "Sanctuary" had barely faded when the DJ’s voice returned, slick and energetic, jarring against the heavy emotional air in the room.

"That was Mirage, holding you close. But sometimes, you gotta fight your way out of the dark before you can find the light. Here’s a track that starts in the gutter and ends in the sky. This is 'Haven City' by Steel Horizon."

The speaker thumped. A heavy, distorted drum machine kicked in—thud-clank, thud-clank—accompanied by a pulsing, predatory synth-bass.

Freddie flinched. The sound was too close to the memory of the Pathfinder bus engine idling in the terminal. It sounded like the headache that had been splitting his skull for three days.

"Off," Freddie gasped. "Turn it off."

Jessica moved instantly. Her hand slapped down on the snooze/off button. Click.

The driving drums and wailing guitars were severed instantly. The silence that rushed back into the room was heavy, ringing in their ears.

Freddie lay panting, his chest heaving against Paige’s side. He squeezed his eyes shut, trying to banish the afterimage of the music.

"Input overload," Freddie whispered. "System... saturation."

"We know," Jessica soothed, smoothing the hair back from his forehead. "We pushed it too fast. No more radio."

They lay there for a long time, letting Freddie’s heart rate settle. The room was just a room again. The smell of verbena soap was grounding. The warmth of his sisters was real.

Freddie took a deep breath. He thought about the lyrics he had almost heard. Take me home.

He thought about the house on Pepper Street. He thought about safety. But then, he thought about the map in his head.

Currently, the sector labeled "Seattle" was a corrupted file. It contained only pain: the bus terminal, the rain, the feeling of being dragged across a bathroom floor. If he left now, that data would be permanent. Seattle would always be a monster.

He opened his eyes. He looked at Jessica. Then he looked at Paige.

"I have... a request," Freddie whispered.

"Anything," Jessica said instantly. "We can call the airline right now. We can be on a plane tonight."

"Negative," Freddie said. He shifted, trying to sit up. "I do not want to evacuate."

Paige frowned, helping him prop himself up against the headboard. "You don't?"

"If we leave now," Freddie said, his voice trembling but gaining strength, "the data remains corrupted. Vanessa wins. She defined this location as a prison."

He looked at his hands.

"I want to redefine the location," Freddie stated. "I want to overwrite the bad data with good data. I want... I want to be a tourist."

Jessica and Paige exchanged a look. It was a look of pure relief and pride.

"Okay," Jessica smiled. "Tourist Protocol. Let's plan it."

"Living room," Freddie requested. "I require... the sofa configuration."

They helped him out of bed. They moved to the small sitting area of the suite. Freddie sat in the middle of the beige loveseat. Paige sat on his right; Jessica sat on his left.

Freddie wrapped his arm around Paige’s waist. He wrapped his other arm around Jessica’s waist. He pulled them both in tight, anchoring himself in the center of the unit.

"Secure," Freddie breathed. "Now. Show me the options."

Paige grinned. She reached for her bag on the floor and pulled out a stack of glossy brochures she had grabbed from the lobby rack days ago.

"I was hoping you'd say that," Paige said. She spread them out on the coffee table.

She picked up the first one. It showed a sleek, white tower rising above the city, crowned with a flying saucer-shaped observation deck.

"The Seattle Sky-Spire," Paige announced. "It’s 605 feet tall. Elevators take forty-one seconds to reach the top. It has a rotating floor."

Freddie leaned forward, adjusting his glasses. He traced the curve of the tower on the paper.

"It looks like a docking pylon," Freddie whispered. "From Stellar Voyage."

"It does," Paige agreed. "And from up there? The bus station looks like a toy. You can look down on it. You can see the grid."

"Elevation," Freddie nodded. "Perspective."

Jessica picked up another brochure. THE BAYFRONT AQUARIUM.

"And this," Jessica said. "It's built on a pier. They have a dome room under the water. Blue lights. Quiet tunnels. Fish moving in perfect, silent orbits. It’s sensory soothing, Freddie. It’s a tank."

Freddie looked at the blue picture. He imagined the silence. He imagined the water.

"Biological systems," Freddie noted. "Predictable movement."

He looked at his sisters. They weren't looking at him with pity. They were looking at him like he was the Captain planning a shore leave mission.

"I want to do this," Freddie said firmly. "I want to go to the Spire. I want to go to the Aquarium. I want to remember Seattle as the place where we went. Not the place where she took me."

"Then we stay," Jessica declared. "We have the rental car. We have the suite. We extend the stay for three days."

"Operation: Decompression," Paige named it. "We reclaim the map."

"Reclaim the map," Freddie echoed.

He squeezed them both tighter. The fear of the city outside the window began to fade, replaced by the anticipation of a new schematic.

"Tomorrow," Freddie said. "We execute the itinerary."

"Tomorrow," Jessica agreed.

Safe in the sandwich configuration, holding the promise of a new memory in his mind, Freddie Avery closed his eyes. He wasn't running away. He was rewriting the code.

## Saturday, May 8, 2004 — 14:26 PM (PST) The Cascade Suites, Room 412

The decision to stay had settled over the room like a fresh blanket, but the emotional exertion of the "overwrite" discussion had drained Freddie’s remaining battery. He sat in the middle of the beige loveseat, his arms still wrapped around both sisters, his breathing slowly syncing with theirs.

"Wait," Paige said suddenly, her eyes widening. "We forgot the fuel."

"Fuel?" Freddie murmured.

"The Comfort Fuel," Paige said. "Jessica bought the canister. White Hot Chocolate. With the little marshmallows."

Jessica stood up instantly. "You're right. Critical oversight. I'll deploy the beverages."

Paige started to shift, intending to help, but Jessica put a hand on her shoulder.

"Negative," Jessica commanded softly. "You stay. Maintain the anchor."

Jessica knew the schematic. If she left, Freddie would have an open flank. If Paige left too, he would be adrift. He needed at least one point of contact to maintain structural integrity.

"Understood," Paige said, settling back.

Jessica walked to the kitchenette.

As soon as her weight left the sofa cushion, Freddie reacted. The loss of the left-side pressure triggered a defensive reflex. He didn't sit up straight; he collapsed.

"Brake," Freddie whispered. "Engaging."

He slid sideways. He swung his legs up onto the sofa where Jessica had just been sitting. He laid his head down in Paige’s lap, curling his body into a tight C-shape against the back cushions. He buried his face in the soft fabric of her pajama shorts, hiding from the light, hiding from the room.

"Brake engaged," Paige confirmed gently.

She draped her left arm over his side, her hand coming to rest flat against the center of his chest, directly over his heart. She felt the rhythm—still a little fast, but slowing down.

"I am... stationary," Freddie mumbled into her leg.

"I know," Paige smoothed his hair. "You just stay there. We'll bring the fuel to the tank."

In the kitchenette, Jessica worked with quiet efficiency. She heated the milk in the microwave—two minutes, stir, two minutes. She spooned the white powder into three mugs. She added the hot milk, stirring until it was a creamy, frothy white. She added a generous dollop of whipped cream from the can they had bought.

She carried two mugs over to the living area.

"Order up," Jessica said softly.

She set the mugs on the coffee table, within easy reach of Freddie’s hand if he chose to extract it from the cocoon.

"One for the Captain," she whispered. "One for the Anchor."

She looked at the sofa. Freddie was fully extended, his legs taking up the cushion she had vacated. He was locked in. If she tried to sit back down, she would have to ask him to move his legs, and right now, asking him to move was like asking a stone to dance.

"I'll be right back," Jessica said.

She went back to the kitchen, retrieved her own mug, and returned.

She looked at the available seating. The armchair was too far away. The other side of the sofa was occupied by Freddie’s feet.

Jessica didn't hesitate. She didn't ask him to scoot. She respected the protocol.

She sat down on the sturdy rectangular coffee table, directly facing them. She sat cross-legged, her knees brushing against the sofa cushion, creating a close, intimate circle.

"Table seating," Jessica smiled, blowing on her cocoa. "Better view of the team anyway."

Freddie peeled one eye open. He saw Jessica sitting right there, inches away, holding her mug. He saw the steam rising from his own mug on the table next to her hip.

He reached out a hand—shaky but determined.

"Refuel," Freddie whispered.

Jessica picked up his mug and handed it to him. He took it, holding it close to his chest, letting the warmth seep into his hands.

"Refueling," Freddie agreed.

He took a sip. It was sweet, hot, and tasted like safety. He didn't sit up. He drank it right there in Paige’s lap, while Jessica watched over them from her perch on the table, the three of them locked in a quiet, perfect triangle of recovery.

## Saturday, May 8, 2004 — 14:45 PM (PST) The Cascade Suites, Room 412

The white hot chocolate was warm in Freddie’s stomach, a soothing ballast against the cold rain streaking the windowpanes. He lay still on the beige loveseat, his legs still draped over the cushion where Jessica had been, his head heavy and secure in Paige’s lap.

The "Emergency Brake" was fully engaged. He wasn't moving. He didn't want to move.

Jessica sat on the coffee table, her knees brushing against the sofa, her own mug cradled in her hands. She wasn't rushing him. She was just... there.

Freddie took a breath. The air smelled of cocoa and the faint, clean scent of Paige’s skin. It was time to clear the cache.

"I need... to run a diagnostic," Freddie whispered, staring at the buttons on Paige’s pajama top.

"On what?" Paige asked softly, her hand resuming its rhythmic stroke through his hair.

"On the corruption," Freddie said. "The Vanessa Virus. I need to understand how the logic bomb was planted. I need to patch the security flaw so it... so it never happens again."

He felt Paige tense slightly, then force herself to relax.

"Okay," Jessica said, her voice calm and clinical. "Let's debug it. What did she say to you? What was the input?"

Freddie closed his eyes. He replayed the scene in the lobby of Copernicus Hall. The way Vanessa had leaned in close. The way she had made her voice sound like she was the only one telling him the truth.

"She said... she said you were leaving," Freddie murmured. "She said she heard you in the bathroom. That Jessica had an internship in New York. And Paige had a study abroad program in Italy."

Paige stopped stroking his hair. "Italy?"

"She said you turned them down," Freddie continued, his voice trembling. "She said you sacrificed your dreams because... because you couldn't leave me. She said I was the gravity well. The black hole. That if I didn't leave... you would eventually hate me for trapping you."

He opened his eyes, looking up at Paige with a raw, terrifying vulnerability.

"Is there... was there an Italy file?"

Paige looked down at him. Her expression wasn't guilty. It was baffled.

"Freddie," Paige said, shaking her head. "I looked at a brochure for Italy once. In the student center trash can. I picked it up because it had a pretty picture of a statue. I looked at it for ten seconds, thought 'Wow, that's a lot of walking,' and threw it back away."

She leaned down, pressing her forehead against his.

"There was no application," she whispered. "There was no acceptance letter. I never wanted to go to Italy. I don't speak Italian. I hate flying. And most importantly... you aren't there."

"And New York?" Freddie asked, shifting his gaze to Jessica.

Jessica snorted. It was a derisive, un-ladylike sound.

"New York?" Jessica said. "The city that smells like garbage and has a cost of living that would bankrupt us in a week? No. Never. I applied for exactly zero internships in New York. My plan has always been to find a firm in Roanoke or Blacksburg so I can drive or take the bus home every night."

She reached out and squeezed his ankle, which was resting near her on the sofa.

"Vanessa made it up," Jessica stated firmly. "She took a tiny fragment of data—maybe she saw me reading a magazine about New York—and she weaponized it. She built a fiction designed to target your specific insecurity: that you are a burden."

"It was... effective," Freddie admitted. "The logic held. I believed I was a constraint."

"You aren't a constraint," Paige said fiercely. "You're the Point of Origin. You're where the map starts."

She adjusted her position, pulling him tighter against her stomach.

"Freddie, listen to me. I don't want to go to Europe. I don't want to go backpacking to 'find myself.' I know exactly where I am."

She looked around the small hotel room—the stocked kitchenette, the blackout curtains, the massive bed where they had all slept in a pile.

"My priority... my dream... isn't a place," Paige said. "It's a state. It's waking up in the morning, rolling over, and touching your arm. It's feeling you breathe. It's knowing—knowing—that you are safe."

She looked him in the eye.

"When I wake up and you're there, sandwiched between us... that is the best moment of my day. Every single time. I don't want to wake up in Rome alone. I want to wake up in Christiansburg with you."

"Forever?" Freddie whispered.

"Forever," Paige promised.

"Even... even when we are old?" Freddie asked. "The social contract suggests that siblings separate. That you find... partners."

"We found our partners," Jessica said simply. "We found each other. The Avery Unit is a closed system, Freddie. We don't need external variables."

She leaned forward on the coffee table.

"I want the King bed," Jessica declared. "I want the sandwich configuration. I want to sleep next to you for the rest of my life because it stops my nightmares too. It’s not just for you, Freddie. We need it. We need the weight."

Freddie processed this. The data was overwriting the Vanessa Virus.

Vanessa's Logic: He is a burden holding them back. New Logic: He is the anchor keeping them grounded. They want to be held.

The "trap" wasn't real. The "sacrifice" wasn't real.

"So," Freddie said, his voice gaining a little strength. "New York is deleted. Italy is deleted."

"Purged," Paige confirmed.

"And the bed... is permanent architecture?"

"Permanent," Jessica agreed. "We buy a new mattress every ten years if we have to. But the formation stays."

Freddie let out a long, shuddering breath. He felt the last of the poison leave his system. He wasn't a jailer. He was a brother.

He snuggled deeper into Paige’s lap, pressing his face against her stomach. He felt the tension leave his legs.

"Patch installed," Freddie whispered. "System secure."

"Good," Paige said, resting her hand over his heart again. "Now, stay right there. The Emergency Brake is still active. We aren't moving until you say so."

"I say... never," Freddie mumbled, closing his eyes.

"Never works for us," Jessica smiled, picking up her mug.

Safe in the lap of his sister, guarded by the other, Freddie Avery finally stopped running. The virus was gone. The code was clean. And the Unit was unbreakable.

## Saturday, May 8, 2004 — 18:30 PM (PST) The Cascade Suites, Room 412

The afternoon light behind the curtains had faded to evening grey, and the quiet conversation about Italy and New York had settled the dust in Freddie's mind. The "Vanessa Virus" had been quarantined, if not fully deleted.

But another system was now sending urgent alerts.

Gurgle.

Freddie’s stomach growled—a low, persistent rumble that vibrated against Paige’s thigh.

"Status check," Paige whispered, a smile touching her lips.

"Fuel levels... critical," Freddie admitted. His voice was stronger now, less like a ghost and more like a tired man waking up.

He shifted. For the first time in hours, he didn't just burrow deeper; he tried to sit up.

The "Emergency Brake" was disengaging.

"Assist?" Paige asked, feeling him struggle against the gravity of exhaustion.

"Affirmative," Freddie said. "I wish to... participate. I want to sit at the table."

Paige placed a hand on his back and another on his shoulder, leveraging him up while he pushed weakly against the cushions. He swayed for a second once he was vertical, blinking behind his glasses.

"Steady," Jessica said from her perch on the coffee table. She stood up, stretching her legs. "Okay, Captain. Table it is."

They flanked him for the short migration. It was only ten feet from the sofa to the small, round dining table near the kitchenette, but they moved like a security detail. Jessica took his left arm, Paige took his right. They didn't drag him; they just offered a solid framework he could lean on.

Freddie sat in one of the dining chairs. It was firm, upright. It felt like a return to civilization.

"Menu options?" Freddie asked, looking at the kitchenette.

"I'm cooking," Jessica announced, tying her hair back. "You stay right there with Paige. I’ve got the supplies."

She opened the freezer. She pulled out the bag they had bought at Market Street Grocery.

Crispy Chicken Strips.

She opened the cupboard. Creamy Macaroni & Cheese (the deluxe kind with the foil pouch of liquid gold, not the powder).

"Comfort Protocol," Jessica winked. "Chicken and Mac. Zero spice. Maximum cheese."

"Acceptable," Freddie said, watching her work. "High caloric density."

Paige sat in the chair next to him, turning it slightly so her knees brushed his. She kept her hand on his forearm, a constant tactile reminder that he wasn't alone.

While the water boiled and the oven preheated, Jessica laid out the new mission parameters.

"Okay," Jessica said, stirring the macaroni. "If we're doing the tourist thing tomorrow, we need a transport plan. I don't want to drive the rental car downtown. Parking is a nightmare, and the traffic is chaos."

"Agreed," Freddie said. "Urban driving introduces stress variables."

Paige pulled a transit map from the stack of brochures. TRANSIT AUTHORITY OF THE PUGET SOUND (TAPS).

"Look at this," Paige said, spreading the map on the table. "They have a unified zone downtown. It’s called the Ride Free Area."

Freddie leaned in, adjusting his glasses. He traced the shaded box on the map.

"Ride Free?"

"Between 6 AM and 7 PM," Paige read. "Any bus trip that starts and ends inside this zone is zero fare. You just hop on."

"Frictionless entry," Freddie noted approvingly. "No payment interaction required."

"And look," Jessica pointed with a wooden spoon. "Our hotel is here, on Stewart. We're inside the zone. We can catch any bus right outside the door and ride it all the way to the waterfront or the retail core without spending a dime."

"Efficient," Freddie said.

Paige traced a line north. "The zone ends here at Battery Street. If we get off at the last stop, we're only a few blocks from the Sky-Spire."

She looked at the scale of the map. Then she looked at Freddie.

"It's about a six-block walk," Paige estimated. "From the bus stop to the Spire."

Freddie frowned. He ran a diagnostic on his legs. They were stiff, sore, and currently running on reserve power. Six blocks on city pavement was a marathon.

"Range anxiety," Freddie whispered. "Six blocks is... significant."

"We don't have to walk it," Jessica called out from the stove, dumping the cheese sauce into the pot. "There's a relay."

She pointed to a blue line on the map. THE METRO-RAIL.

"It's an elevated train," Jessica explained. "It runs from the downtown mall directly to the Seattle Exposition Park. It drops you right at the base of the Spire. It’s a two-minute ride."

"A monorail?" Freddie asked, his eyes lighting up. "Elevated transit?"

"Exactly," Paige said. "We take the free bus to the mall. We take the elevator up to the platform. We ride the train to the Spire. Minimal walking."

"And if we do walk," Jessica added, bringing the plates to the table, "we break it up. We stop at every bench. We stop at every coffee shop—well, for us to get coffee, you can get water. We aren't in a rush, Freddie. If you need to sit, we sit. If you need to stop, we stop."

She set a plate in front of him. Steaming mac and cheese. Three golden chicken strips.

"We move at your pace," Paige promised, squeezing his arm. "The Captain sets the speed."

Freddie looked at the food. He looked at the map. He looked at his sisters.

"The Metro-Rail," Freddie decided, picking up his fork. "That is the optimal vector."

He took a bite of the chicken. It was hot, mild, and safe.

"Tomorrow," Freddie said, chewing slowly. "We take the train."

"Tomorrow," Jessica agreed, clinking her fork against his.

They ate in the quiet hotel room, the map of the city spread out between them, plotting a course for a day that would replace the grey memory of the bus station with the bright, elevated view of a family that had found its way back to each other.

To be held by the water and his sisters simultaneously.

He looked at Jessica, who was stacking the plates. He looked at Paige, who was folding a napkin.

They were all nude. In the soft light of the hotel room, stripped of the jeans and hoodies and armor of the outside world, it didn't feel exposed. It felt honest. It meant there were no hidden weapons, no hidden agendas. Just skin. Just the Unit.

Freddie cleared his throat. The "Admin" voice he usually used for requests—I require hygiene protocol—felt wrong. It felt too cold for the warmth in his chest.

"Jessica?" Freddie said softly. "Paige?"

They both stopped and looked at him instantly.

"System report?" Jessica asked.

"Systems are... grateful," Freddie whispered. He looked down at his hands. "I have a request. A... favor."

"Name it," Paige said.

## 19:45 PM — The Bath

The hotel bathroom was dim, lit only by the light spilling in from the kitchenette. Jessica started the water. She didn't make it scalding hot this time; she made it warm, a gentle, enveloping temperature. She poured in a little of the hotel’s complimentary bubble bath—something she usually dismissed as frivolous, but tonight, it seemed necessary.

"Ready, Captain?" Paige asked.

"Ready," Freddie said. "Thank you."

They helped him into the tub. He slid into the bubbles, the water rising to his chest. He sighed, a long, trembling sound of pure contentment.

Jessica knelt on the bathmat to his left. Paige knelt to his right. They didn't grab scrub brushes or medical saline this time. They grabbed soft washcloths.

"Arms up," Paige whispered playfully.

Freddie lifted his arms.

Paige began to wash his left arm. She moved slowly, the cloth gliding over his skin in long, soothing strokes.

"Thank you, Paige," Freddie whispered, watching her hand. "Thank you for finding me."

"I'll always find you," Paige said, her voice thick. She kissed his wet shoulder.

Jessica took his right hand. She washed his fingers, one by one, massaging the stiffness out of his knuckles where he had gripped the wheelchair armrests for so long.

"Thank you, Jessica," Freddie said, turning his head to look at her. "Thank you for the plane. For the food. For... everything."

"It's just logistics, Freddie," Jessica smiled, squeezing his soapy hand. "That's what I do."

"It is not just logistics," Freddie corrected gently. "It is love. Thank you."

They washed his chest. They washed his neck. They were careful of his lower back, washing around the dressing with infinite tenderness.

It wasn't a medical procedure. It was a ritual. With every stroke of the cloth, they were telling him: You are here. You are ours. You are safe.

Freddie closed his eyes. He listened to the water lapping against the porcelain. He felt the steam on his face. He felt four hands touching him, caring for him, asking for nothing in return.

He thought about Vanessa. She had wanted to take. She had wanted to own.

His sisters just wanted him to be clean.

"I am... so happy," Freddie whispered, the words bubbling up from a place deep inside him. "I am overwhelmed with data... but it is good data."

"Good data," Jessica agreed, rinsing the soap from his back with a cup.

"Okay," Paige said softly. "One last rinse."

She took the handheld shower wand. She turned it to a gentle spray. She rinsed the bubbles away, leaving his skin warm and clean.

"All done," Paige announced.

Freddie looked at them. They were wet from the splashing, their hair frizzy in the humidity, kneeling on the hard tile floor just to be close to him.

"Thank you," Freddie said again. He didn't know how else to say it. The vocabulary of the "Iron City" didn't have a word for this feeling.

"You're welcome, Freddie," Jessica said. "Now. Let's get you dry. The King bed is waiting."

They helped him stand. They wrapped him in warm towels. And as they walked him back to the "Silo," Freddie realized that the "Robert Virus"—the voice that said he was broken—was silent.

He wasn't broken. He was just loved.
