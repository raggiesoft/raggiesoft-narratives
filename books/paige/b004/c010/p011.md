# Part 11: The Zero-Privacy Protocol

## Sunday, June 20, 2004 — 12:30 PM The Underworld (Basement)

Two hours had passed. The pile of dirty clothes had shrunk, replaced by neat, geometric stacks of folded laundry on the folding table Sarah had set up.

The basement air was thick with the scent of Fresh-Breeze detergent and warm lint. The Titan-Wash machine was grinding through Load 3 (Heavy Denims), while the dryer tumbled Load 2 (Delicates).

Sarah wiped sweat from her forehead. "Status check," she announced, folding a pair of jeans with a sharp snap. "We are at 60% completion. The denim cycle is taking longer than anticipated."

"It’s because Freddie wears those heavy carpenter jeans," Jenna noted, tossing a pair of socks into the 'orphan' pile. "They weigh a ton when they're wet."

Jenna reached for another can of Citrus-Pop. She cracked the tab. Hiss-snap.

"You know," Jenna said, taking a sip. "All this liquid input equals output."

She stood up. She didn't announce it dramatically this time. She just walked over to the corner.

Harper didn't flinch. She didn't turn around. She just kept matching socks.

"Turn up the radio," Jenna called out casually over her shoulder. "I don't need a soundtrack."

Sarah reached over and turned up the volume on the small boombox they had brought down. A pop song filled the basement, masking the sound.

Jenna used the Pittsburgh Toilet. She washed her hands. She came back.

"Next load," Jenna said, sitting back down as if she had just fetched a glass of water.

Ten minutes later, Sarah stood up. She dusted off her knees.

"My turn," Sarah stated pragmatically. "I’m not walking up two flights of stairs just to wait for Aunt Linda."

Sarah—the Lieutenant, the one who enforced the rules—walked to the corner. She didn't ask anyone to turn around. She trusted her squad.

Harper watched her out of the corner of her eye. She saw Sarah unbutton her jeans, sit down, and stare blankly at the furnace while she did her business. Sarah looked bored. She looked completely unbothered.

It really isn't a big deal, Harper realized. It’s just us.

A few minutes later, the urge hit Harper again. The basement was cold, but the dryer made it warm, and the combination always made her have to go.

She stood up.

"I have to use the asset," Harper said, testing the words.

"Go for it," Jenna said, not looking up from a t-shirt she was folding. "Don't forget to flush."

Harper walked to the corner.

She lowered her pants. She sat on the cold porcelain.

She looked at her sisters. Jenna was tossing a shirt to Sarah. Sarah was stacking it. They were ten feet away. They could see her.

And they didn't care.

Harper relaxed. She let it go.

For the first time in her life, she felt a total absence of social anxiety. In the outside world—at school, at the mall—privacy was a fortress you had to defend. You locked doors. You checked for gaps in the stall. You died of shame if someone heard a noise.

But here, in the Avery Underworld, the walls were down.

We don't hide anything, Harper thought. We don't hide our bodies. We don't hide our problems. We don't hide Freddie.

She stood up, pulled up her pants, and flushed.

KA-WHOOSH.

The roar of the water was the sound of victory.

Harper washed her hands and walked back to the group. She sat down on the concrete.

"You know," Harper said, picking up a pair of Freddie’s socks. "I think I like this toilet. It’s honest."

"It’s efficient," Sarah corrected, handing her another sock. "Now hurry up. The dryer is buzzing. Load 3 is ready for transfer."

The Laundry Brigade worked on. They rotated through the loads, and they rotated through the corner, bound together by soap, warm flannel, and the absolute freedom of having nothing left to hide.
