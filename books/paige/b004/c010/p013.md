# Part 13: The Rotation of the Guard

## Sunday, June 20, 2004 — 14:00 PM The Living Room (Surface Level)

The front door opened with a rattle of keys. Jessica and Aunt Linda backed into the entryway, arms laden with plastic bags from the party store and the grocery market.

"Mission successful," Jessica announced, kicking the door shut with her heel. "We have enough sugar to kill a horse and... oh."

She stopped. Aunt Linda stopped beside her.

They looked at the living room.

It was a sculpture of human entanglement. Freddie was barely visible. He was the buried foundation of a structure built entirely of sisters.

- Paige was the immovable wall on his left, her arm locked through his, her eyes darting up to check the door before relaxing when she saw it was just them.

- Amanda was draped over his lap, asleep, her face pressed into his neck.

- Elara was perched on the back of the sofa, her arms wrapped around his forehead, her chin resting on his hair.

Aunt Linda set her bags down quietly. She watched them for a moment.

She knew what her sister, Ellen, would say. Ellen would purse her lips and talk about "propriety." She would say it was "unseemly" for a nineteen-year-old man to be cuddling with his sisters like a litter of puppies. She would worry about what the neighbors thought.

She knew what Robert would say. He would clear his throat, uncomfortable with the emotion, and tell Freddie to "man up" and stop being a pillow.

And she knew what that girl Vanessa would say. She would call it "codependent." She would say it was "sick." She would try to cut the wires one by one to isolate the power source.

Linda looked at Freddie’s face. His eyes were closed. He wasn't flinching. He wasn't grey and shaking like he had been after the divorce. He looked peaceful. He looked anchored.

Screw them, Linda thought fiercely. This isn't sick. This is survival.

Just then, the basement door creaked open.

The Laundry Brigade emerged. Sarah, Jenna, and Harper walked into the hallway, carrying baskets of warm, folded clothes. They smelled like Fresh-Breeze and victory.

They stopped. They looked at the sofa. They looked at the monopoly the twins held on Freddie.

Jenna put her hands on her hips. "Hey! We’ve been slaving away in the dungeon for three hours! We want in."

Elara tightened her grip on Freddie’s head. "Squatter's rights!"

"Negative," Aunt Linda stepped in, her voice firm but kind. "We operate on a fair-trade policy in this house."

She walked over to the sofa. She reached out and gently poked Elara in the ribs.

"Okay, monkeys. Detach. You've had him all morning. The Laundry Brigade needs to recharge."

Amanda stirred, blinking sleepily. She looked at Linda, then at Harper standing by the laundry basket with a hopeful look on her face.

Amanda sighed—a long, dramatic exhale through her nose—and untangled herself from Freddie’s neck. She climbed off his lap, patted his knee twice (a thank-you), and slid to the floor.

Elara groaned but slid down from the back of the sofa. "Fine. But I'm sitting next to Jessica."

"Deal," Jessica said, dropping onto the love seat.

The path was clear. Paige, of course, did not move. She shifted slightly to make room, but her grip on Freddie’s left arm didn't loosen by a millimeter. She was the permanent fixture.

"Rotation initiated," Freddie announced softly, adjusting his glasses which Elara had knocked crooked.

Harper moved first. She needed this. After the shame of the Pittsburgh Toilet and the cold basement, she needed to know she was still welcome in the warm spot.

She walked over and sat exactly where Amanda had been—right on Freddie’s lap. She was heavy, leggy, and awkward, but Freddie wrapped his right arm around her instantly, pulling her against his chest.

"Welcome back, Specialist," Freddie whispered into her hair.

Sarah sat on the coffee table in front of him, leaning forward to rest her forehead against his knee. "Laundry is at 100%. Socks are matched."

"Efficiency noted," Freddie said, reaching out to tap her shoulder.

Jenna flopped onto the sofa on his right side, wedging herself into the gap Elara had vacated. She grabbed Freddie’s hand and threw her leg over his shins.

"We conquered the toilet, Freddie," Jenna bragged, closing her eyes. "We are warriors."

"You are a formidable unit," Freddie agreed.

Linda watched the pile reform. The faces changed, but the structure remained the same. Freddie was the core, and his sisters were the shielding.

"Okay," Linda whispered to Jessica, picking up the grocery bags again. "Let's get this ice cream in the freezer before it melts. I think the army is occupied for a while."

"Yeah," Jessica smiled, looking at her siblings. "They're good."

Linda looked one last time at Freddie, buried once again under a fresh set of sisters, looking happier than she had ever seen him.

Eat your heart out, Vanessa, Linda thought with a grim sense of satisfaction. You can't break this.
