# Part 15: Clearance Denied

## Sunday, June 20, 2004 — 14:20 PM The Underworld (Basement)

The door at the top of the stairs clicked shut, sealing the younger sisters in the concrete bunker.

Sarah sighed and sat down on her air mattress, shuffling a deck of worn playing cards. Jenna cracked open another soda, leaning against the dryer. Amanda and Elara were on the floor, building a tower out of plastic cups they had found in the pantry.

But Harper didn't sit down.

She stood at the bottom of the wooden staircase, staring up at the door. Her hand gripped the banister.

"Come on, Harper," Sarah called out, dealing the cards onto the sleeping bag. "Crazy Eights. You're in."

"I don't want to play," Harper whispered. She took a step up. The wood creaked.

"Harper, stop," Jenna warned. "Aunt Linda said to stay down here."

"I just want to listen," Harper said, taking another step. "I want to know what they're saying. Why did they make us leave? We're part of the Unit too."

"It's adult business," Sarah said, flipping a card. "That means boring stuff. Taxes. Insurance. The divorce."

"It's not taxes," Harper insisted. She climbed two more steps, putting her ear close to the gap under the door.

For a moment, there was only the low rumble of voices—Aunt Linda’s calm tone, Freddie’s soft murmur.

Then, the sound changed.

It wasn't a word. It was a sound Harper had never heard before. It was a ragged, high-pitched gasp. Then a sob. A deep, wrenching sound that vibrated through the floorboards.

Harper froze. Her eyes went wide.

"He's crying," Harper whispered, her voice trembling. "Freddie is crying."

On the floor, Amanda stopped stacking cups. She looked up at the ceiling, her expression darkening. Elara frowned, dropping a red cup.

"He never cries," Harper said, panic rising in her chest. "He didn't cry when he fell. He didn't cry when Mom yelled. Why is he crying?"

Another sob filtered down—a sound of pure, raw heartbreak.

"I have to go," Harper decided.

She scrambled up the stairs, her feet thumping on the wood. "I have to fix it! He needs a cuddle!"

"Harper, no!"

Sarah moved fast. She vaulted off the air mattress and sprinted to the stairs. Jenna was right behind her.

Harper reached for the doorknob.

"Let me out!" Harper yelled. "He's sad! I can help!"

Sarah grabbed Harper’s ankle through the railing. Jenna grabbed the back of her shirt. They hauled her backward.

"No!" Harper kicked, tears springing to her own eyes. "Let go! He needs me! I fit in his lap! I can make it better!"

"You can't go up there!" Sarah hissed, wrapping her arms around Harper’s waist and pulling her down to the concrete floor. "Aunt Linda said stay out!"

"But he's hurt!" Harper sobbed, struggling against Sarah’s grip. "Listen to him! He sounds broken!"

"He is broken!" Jenna snapped, her voice cracking slightly as she helped pin Harper down. "That's why they're talking! They're fixing him!"

"I can fix him!" Harper screamed.

"No, you can't!" Sarah shouted, finally wrestling Harper into a sitting position on the bottom step. "You're eleven, Harper! You can't fix this!"

The basement went silent, except for Harper’s heavy breathing and the muffled sounds of Freddie weeping upstairs.

Sarah took a deep breath, adjusting her glasses. She looked at Harper, whose face was red and wet with tears.

"Listen to me," Sarah said, her voice shaking but firm. "Freddie has... bad data. From the bad girl. From Vanessa."

Harper sniffled. "The one he ran away from?"

"Yes," Sarah said. "She put bad things in his head. And now... he has to get them out. And getting them out hurts. It's like... it's like popping a blister. It hurts before it heals."

"But why can't I hold him?" Harper whined, wiping her nose on her sleeve. "He likes it when I hold him."

"Because right now," Jenna said softly, sitting down next to them, "he needs the Triad. He needs Paige and Jessica and Aunt Linda. They have... higher clearance."

"Clearance?" Harper asked small voice.

"Yeah," Sarah nodded, using Freddie’s word. "Level 1 Clearance. We are Level 2. We handle the laundry. We handle the cuddles after. But the Triad handles the heavy stuff."

Harper looked at the top of the stairs. She could still hear the faint sounds of comfort—Jessica’s voice low and soothing, Paige’s fierce whispering.

"Is he going to be okay?" Harper whispered.

Amanda walked over. She stood in front of Harper. She reached out and patted Harper’s head, then pointed to the ceiling and gave a thumbs up.

"Amanda says yes," Elara translated from the floor. "She says the bad noise is stopping."

Harper listened. The sobbing had quieted. It was replaced by a steady, low murmur. The storm had passed.

"Okay," Harper whispered. She leaned her head on Sarah’s shoulder. "I hate Level 2."

"Me too," Sarah admitted, wrapping an arm around her little sister. "Being a kid sucks. You don't know anything."

"But we know he's safe," Jenna reminded them. "He's upstairs. He's not on a bus."

"Yeah," Harper sighed. She looked at the cards scattered on the sleeping bag. "Can I be the dealer?"

"Sure," Sarah said, standing up and pulling Harper with her. "But if you cheat, I'm telling Freddie."

"I never cheat," Harper lied, wiping her eyes.

They went back to the circle. They played cards in the cool, grey light of the Underworld, waiting for the signal that the adults were done and the Captain was ready for his Satellites again.

## Sunday, June 20, 2004 — 14:35 PM The Underworld (Basement)

The card table was a blue sleeping bag pulled taut over the concrete floor. The five sisters sat cross-legged around it in a circle.

Sarah shuffled the deck. She did it the way Freddie did—a perfect bridge shuffle that made a satisfying thwip-thwip-thwip sound.

"Game is Crazy Eights," Sarah announced, dealing five cards to each player. "Eights are wild. Twos are pickup-two. Queens are skip. Acetaminophen rules."

"It's 'Aces High,' not 'Acetaminophen,'" Jenna corrected, picking up her hand and fanning it out. "You spend too much time reading Freddie's medical dictionaries."

"Standard rules apply," Sarah ignored her. "And since we have five players, we are using two decks mixed together. The draw pile is heavy."

Harper picked up her cards. She held them close to her chest, shielding them from Jenna’s prying eyes.

She looked at her hand: Two Hearts, a King of Spades, a Five of Diamonds, a Seven of Clubs, and... the Eight of Hearts. The magic card.

Usually, this was the part where Harper would slide the Seven of Clubs under her leg. Or maybe she would cough and accidentally-on-purpose drop a bad card into the discard pile when no one was looking. Cheating was her way of leveling the playing field against the older, smarter twins.

She glanced at Jenna. Jenna was distracted, trying to crack her knuckles.

Harper’s thumb hovered over the Seven. She could drop it. It would be so easy.

Then she looked up at the ceiling.

It was quiet upstairs now. The crying had stopped. Freddie was recovering.

She remembered Sarah’s threat. If you cheat, I'm telling Freddie.

Harper imagined Freddie’s face. Not angry—he never really got angry. But disappointed. She imagined him looking at her with those sad eyes behind his glasses and saying, “Your data is corrupt, Harper.”

She didn't want to be corrupt. She wanted to be Level 1. She wanted to be worthy of the hug she was going to get when the door opened.

Harper tightened her grip on the cards. She pushed the Seven back into the fan.

I’m playing clean, she told herself. I’m going to win for real.

"Play commences," Sarah stated. She flipped the top card: Four of Diamonds.

"Boom," Jenna said, slapping down a Four of Spades. "Match."

Amanda and Elara (playing as a single team) whispered to each other. Elara nodded. Amanda placed a Four of Hearts on the pile.

"Stop changing the suit!" Jenna groaned. "I was hoarding Spades!"

It was Harper’s turn. The suit was Hearts. She had a Two of Hearts.

"Pickup two, Sarah," Harper said, slapping the card down.

Sarah glared at her over her glasses. "Aggressive."

Sarah drew two cards. "Fine. My turn."

The game moved fast. Cards slapped against the sleeping bag. The tension in the basement shifted from "scared listening" to "competitive focus."

Five minutes later, it was down to the wire.

Jenna had one card left. She was grinning, bouncing her leg. "UNO! Wait, wrong game. Last card!"

"Don't get cocky," Sarah muttered, checking her own hand of three cards.

The twins played a Queen.

"Skip Jenna!" Elara shouted.

"Hey!" Jenna yelled. "That's sabotage!"

"Strategy," Elara smirked. Amanda high-fived her.

The play passed Jenna. It came to Harper.

Harper looked at her hand. She had the Eight of Hearts. The wild card.

The current pile was a Queen of Diamonds.

Harper looked at Jenna, who was clutching her single, winning card (which Harper was 90% sure was a Spade).

If Harper played the Eight, she could change the suit to anything. She could change it to Hearts. Jenna didn't have Hearts. Jenna would have to draw.

Harper played the card.

"Eight," Harper announced clearly. "Suit is Hearts."

Jenna’s jaw dropped. She looked at her last card (the King of Spades). She looked at Harper.

"You little..." Jenna narrowed her eyes. "You peeked! You saw my hand!"

"I didn't!" Harper shot back, sitting up straight. "I didn't look! I just guessed because you were complaining about Hearts earlier!"

Jenna looked at Sarah. "She cheated. Lieutenant, execute her."

Sarah paused. She looked at Harper. She studied Harper’s face—the defiant chin, the wide, honest eyes, the hands placed flat on the sleeping bag where everyone could see them.

Sarah had been watching. Harper hadn't dropped a card. She hadn't leaned over. She had played a perfect, clean game.

"Negative," Sarah ruled. "Her play was valid. Logic dictates you were hoarding Spades. Harper intercepted the pattern."

Sarah smiled a tiny, proud smile.

"Good kill, Harper."

Harper beamed. She felt lighter than air. She beat Jenna, and she didn't have to hide anything.

"Draw, Jenna," Harper commanded, pointing to the deck.

Jenna groaned and snatched a card from the pile. "I hate this game. I hate Level 2."

Upstairs, the floorboards creaked. Heavy footsteps moved toward the basement door.

The lock clicked. The door opened.

"Coast is clear!" Aunt Linda’s voice called down, sounding cheerful and relieved. "Who wants ice cream?"

Harper threw her cards into the air—ruining the game instantly.

"ME!" she screamed.

She scrambled up, abandoning the victory for the prize she really wanted. She bolted up the stairs, past Aunt Linda, and ran straight into the living room.

Freddie was still sitting on the sofa, sandwiched between Paige and Jessica. His eyes were red, and he looked tired, but when he saw Harper, he opened his arms.

"Come to port," Freddie whispered.

Harper launched herself at him. She climbed into his lap and buried her face in his neck, smelling the soap and the salt of his tears.

"I didn't cheat," Harper whispered fiercely into his ear. "I played honest. I promise."

Freddie held her tight, resting his chin on her head.

"I believe you," Freddie said. "Good girl."

## Sunday, June 20, 2004 — 14:40 PM The Living Room

The force of Harper’s hug knocked the wind out of him, but Freddie didn't pull away. He adjusted his grip, wrapping his arms around her waist to secure her. She was trembling against him, her face pressed so hard into his shoulder that he could feel the dampness of her tears soaking through his flannel shirt.

Paige reached out and rubbed Harper’s back. Jessica leaned in, resting her chin on Freddie’s shoulder, watching the youngest sister with a soft, sad expression.

"I heard you," Harper whispered into his neck, her voice thick with mucus and emotion. "I heard you through the floor. It sounded like you were hurting."

Freddie took a steadying breath. He didn't try to lie. He didn't try to say 'I wasn't crying' or 'It was just allergies.' Harper was smart. She dealt in facts, just like him.

"I was hurting," Freddie admitted, his voice low and rumbling against her ear. "But it was a necessary hurt. Like resetting a bone."

Harper pulled back just an inch. She looked at him. Her eyes were red, but they were sharp. She scanned his face, looking for the cracks.

"It was her, wasn't it?" Harper said. It wasn't a question. "The girl from the porch. Vanessa."

Freddie blinked. He looked at Aunt Linda, who was standing by the kitchen door. Linda gave a small, barely perceptible nod. She knows.

"Yes," Freddie said. "She introduced... significant errors into my system."

"She tricked you," Harper stated firmly. She wiped her nose with the back of her hand. "She made you think we didn't want you. That's why you went on the bus."

Freddie felt a pang in his chest. He hadn't realized how much she had pieced together from the whispers and the tension.

"She did," Freddie whispered. "I thought I was... taking up too much space. I thought you would be better off without the extra weight."

Harper frowned. Her eyebrows scrunched together in a way that looked exactly like Jessica.

"That's stupid," Harper declared.

Freddie let out a tiny, surprised huff of laughter. "Is it?"

"Yeah," Harper said. "You're the Captain. The satellites fall out of orbit without the planet. You can't just leave."

She reached up and touched his cheek, her fingers sticky from the card game but warm.

"Besides," Harper added, her voice dropping to a conspiratorial whisper. "Jessica and Paige went and got you. They flew on a plane. That means you're worth a lot of money."

"They rescued the asset," Freddie agreed, glancing at his twin. Paige smiled, squeezing his arm.

"So you're fixed now?" Harper asked, looking deep into his eyes behind his glasses. "The bad thoughts are gone?"

"The data has been overwritten," Freddie promised. "I told Aunt Linda everything. We purged the system. I am empty of the bad stuff."

He squeezed her tighter.

"And now I am refilling the space with good stuff. Like you."

Harper relaxed. Her shoulders dropped. The panic that had driven her up the stairs evaporated, replaced by the heavy, sleepy comfort of being exactly where she wanted to be.

"Good," Harper murmured, resting her head back on his shoulder. "Don't let her back in. She's banned."

"Banned for life," Jessica confirmed from his right side.

The basement door opened again. Sarah, Jenna, Amanda, and Elara filed out. They saw Harper safe in Freddie’s lap. They saw Freddie’s dry eyes. They saw the calm returning to the room.

"Status?" Sarah asked, adjusting her glasses.

"Stable," Freddie announced.

"Excellent," Aunt Linda clapped her hands together, breaking the heavy mood. "Because I believe I promised ice cream. And I have a gallon of Mountain-Peak Vanilla and a gallon of Fudge-Swirl melting on the counter."

"Ice cream!" Elara cheered.

Harper shifted on Freddie’s lap. She didn't get down.

"Can I eat mine here?" Harper asked. "I don't want to move."

Freddie looked at Jessica. Jessica shrugged, smiling. "It’s a flannel shirt, Mike. It washes."

"Affirmative," Freddie said to Harper. "You remain at port. I will request a delivery."

"I'll get it," Paige said, standing up and pressing a kiss to the top of Freddie’s head. "One bowl of fudge swirl, coming up."

Freddie sat back against the beige cushions. He held Harper. He listened to his sisters arguing over bowls in the kitchen. He felt the phantom weight of the suicide note dissolving in his memory, replaced by the very real weight of an eleven-year-old girl who refused to let him go.

He was home.
