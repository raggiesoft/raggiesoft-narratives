# Part 14: The Black Box Review

## Sunday, June 20, 2004 — 14:15 PM The Living Room

The laughter had died down. The ice cream was put away. Aunt Linda stood in the center of the living room, her hands clasped in front of her. She looked at the sprawling pile of happiness on the sofa, but her expression was serious.

"Alright," Aunt Linda announced. "I need the room."

The girls looked up.

"Laundry Brigade + Twins," Linda commanded. "Head to the Underworld. I need to speak to the Triad. Adults only."

Harper groaned, clinging to Freddie’s shirt. "But I just got back on! I’m comfortable!"

"Harper," Linda said, her voice dropping that specific octave that meant business. "Basement. Now."

Sarah and Jenna stood up immediately. They recognized the tone. This wasn't a punishment; it was a debriefing. They grabbed Amanda and Elara.

"Come on," Sarah said, pulling Harper up by her arm. "Let's go play cards."

Harper pouted, her lower lip trembling slightly as she looked at Freddie, but she let go. She trudged after her sisters. The basement door clicked shut.

Silence descended on the living room.

Linda pulled the ottoman over to the sofa and sat down, so she was knee-to-knee with Freddie.

Freddie was still in the center of the sofa. Paige was clamped to his left side. Jessica had moved from the love seat to his right side, taking the space Jenna had vacated. They sandwiched him.

"So," Linda said softly, looking Freddie in the eye. "We’ve done the food. We’ve done the laundry. We’ve done the distraction."

She leaned forward.

"Talk to me, Freddie. Really talk to me. What did she do to you?"

Freddie felt the air pressure in the room drop. His heart rate spiked. Warning: Accessing Corrupted Files.

He felt Paige’s hand tighten on his bicep. He felt Jessica’s hand cover his on his knee.

"She... reconfigured the narrative," Freddie whispered, staring at his hands.

"How?" Linda asked.

"She told me I was a parasite," Freddie said. The word hung in the air, ugly and clinical. "She said that my reliance on the Unit was preventing Jessica and Paige from executing their primary functions."

He took a shaky breath.

"She said Jessica wanted to move to New York," Freddie continued, his voice trembling. "To work in fashion. To be successful. She said I was the reason Jessica was stuck in 'muddy Virginia.'"

Jessica squeezed his hand so hard her knuckles turned white. "She lied, Freddie. I hate New York. It smells like garbage and anxiety."

"She said Paige wanted Italy," Freddie went on, tears pricking his eyes. "To study Art History. To live in a villa. She said Paige was only staying here... out of pity. Because I couldn't function alone."

"I have never wanted to go to Italy without you," Paige hissed, her voice furious. "Never."

"I know," Freddie choked out. "I know that now. But then... the data felt real. She isolated me. She repeated the input until it became truth."

He looked up at Linda. His glasses were fogging up.

"She made me write a note," Freddie confessed. A tear escaped, tracking down his cheek. "A suicide note. She dictated it. She told me to write that I was 'setting them free.' That I was deleting myself so they could live."

Linda’s face went pale. She brought a hand to her mouth.

"I was going to do it," Freddie whispered, his body starting to shake. "I was on the bus. I was going to wait until we got to Seattle, sign the papers she wanted... and then terminate the connection. I thought... I thought I was saving them."

The shaking became violent. The "Pasco Memory" tried to surface—the cold, the fear, the emptiness.

"Ground him," Jessica ordered sharply.

It happened instantly.

Paige threw her leg over his, pinning him to the sofa. She buried her face in his neck. "You are here. You are here. You are here."

Jessica wrapped both arms around his chest from the other side, crushing him between them. "Overwrite, Freddie. Overwrite the data. We came for you."

"You came," Freddie sobbed, collapsing into them. "You flew to Seattle. You found me."

"We will always find you," Paige promised fiercely. "There is no distance we won't travel."

"We turned it into a vacation," Freddie gasped, struggling to regulate his breathing. "We went to the Sky-Spire. We ate fish. We overwrote the bad data with good data."

"Yes," Jessica whispered into his ear. "We fixed it."

"I am never leaving," Freddie vowed, his voice breaking. "I am never leaving the Unit again. I cannot survive the silence."

They held him for a long time. The only sound in the room was Freddie’s ragged breathing and the steady hum of the air conditioner.

Finally, the shaking stopped. The pressure of his sisters—the sheer physical weight of their love—forced the panic back into its box.

Aunt Linda reached out. She placed her hand gently on Freddie’s knee, right on top of Jessica’s hand.

"She was a monster, Freddie," Linda said, her voice shaking with suppressed rage. "What she did... that wasn't love. That was torture."

She looked at the three of them. She saw how they were tangled together. She saw how Paige was practically sitting in his lap, how Jessica was shielding him.

She knew what the world would say. She knew what psychologists might say about "enmeshment."

"I know you three sleep together," Linda said suddenly.

Freddie froze. Paige tensed up, ready to fight.

"I saw the bed in the Master room," Linda continued calmly. "I saw the pillows. And I know you've been doing it since you got back."

She looked Freddie dead in the eye.

"Does it stop the nightmares?" Linda asked.

"Yes," Freddie whispered. "When I feel them... the terror cannot manifest."

"Does it make you feel safe?" Linda asked.

"Yes," Freddie said. "It is the only time the system fully powers down."

Linda nodded. She sat back on the ottoman.

"Then to hell with anyone who has a problem with it," Linda declared.

Paige blinked. "Really?"

"Really," Linda said firmly. "After what you've been through? After almost losing him? If sleeping in a puppy pile keeps him here, on this earth, safe and sound... then you buy a bigger bed if you have to."

She smiled, a sad, weary smile.

"Ellen would hate it," Linda noted. "Which makes me like it even more."

Freddie let out a wet, shaky laugh. He wiped his eyes on his shoulder.

"Thank you, Aunt Linda," Freddie said.

"Don't thank me," Linda said, standing up and smoothing her skirt. "Just promise me one thing."

"Anything," Freddie said.

"Don't you ever believe you're a burden," Linda said, pointing a finger at him. "You aren't the anchor holding them down, Freddie. You're the gravity holding them together. Look at them."

Freddie looked. He saw Paige, fierce and protective. He saw Jessica, strong and steady.

"Without you," Linda said, "they drift away. You keep them home."

She walked to the basement door.

"Now," Linda said, her voice lightening. "I'm going to release the prisoners before Harper claws through the drywall. Are you good?"

Freddie took a deep breath. He felt the weight of his sisters. He felt the truth of Linda’s words.

"Status," Freddie whispered.

"Green," Paige answered.

"Green," Jessica answered.

"Optimal," Freddie confirmed.
