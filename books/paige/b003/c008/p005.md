# Part 5: The Recovery Cycle

## Tuesday, July 15, 2003 09:30 AM

The morning sun pressed against the blackout curtains of the **Silo**, but inside, the darkness held.

Freddie woke up slowly. He didn't bolt upright this time. He floated to the surface of consciousness, anchored by the heavy, reassuring weight of the **Sandwich Configuration**.

To his left, Jessica was a warm, solid wall. Her arm was still draped over his chest, a protective bar across the gate. To his right, Paige was curled into his side, her leg hooked over his ankles, pinning him to the mattress.

Freddie blinked in the dark. The memory of the closet nightmare was still there—a jagged edge in his mind—but it felt distant now. It was a file that had been quarantined.

"Status?" Paige whispered. She wasn't asleep. She had felt his breathing change.

"Battery... low," Freddie admitted, his voice raspy. "Recharge incomplete. Sleep cycle was interrupted."

"I know," Paige murmured, squeezing him. "It was a rough night."

"We're taking a zero day," Jessica announced from the darkness. She lifted her arm, stretching. "No bus. No campus. No Krystal."

"Zero day," Freddie repeated. "Acceptable."

They lay there for another ten minutes, just existing in the safe, warm pile of the Unit. Finally, the biological imperative of morning (coffee and bathrooms) forced a movement.

"Armor up," Jessica said, sitting up and rubbing her eyes. "We need to secure the perimeter if we're going to the living room."

They climbed out of the King Bed. The air was cool on their skin.

Freddie walked to the hamper. He ignored the jeans he had worn yesterday. He ignored the orange hoodie (too heavy for today).

He pulled out his "Safe Mode" uniform: soft, worn-in grey jersey pajama pants and a white tag-less t-shirt.

Paige pulled on a pair of loose cotton shorts and one of Freddie’s old grey t-shirts that she had claimed years ago. It smelled like him—laundry detergent and safety.

Jessica threw on her flannel pants and a tank top.

"Ready?" Jessica asked.

"Ready," Freddie nodded. He felt heavy, his limbs moving through syrup, but he wasn't shaking.

## 10:00 AM

The living room of **105 Pepper Street** was filled with the soft morning light filtering through the blinds.

Jessica went to the kitchen to start the coffee maker. The gurgle and hiss of the machine was a familiar, grounding sound.

"Breakfast?" Jessica called out.

"Cereal," Freddie said, moving toward the beige sofa. "Dry. No milk. Texture maintenance."

He reached the sofa. He didn't sit. He looked at Paige.

Paige didn't need to be asked. She sat down in the corner of the sofa, leaning back against the armrest. She extended her legs along the cushions, creating a nest.

She patted her lap. "Docking bay open."

Freddie climbed onto the sofa. He lay down, positioning his head carefully in her lap. He curled his legs up, fitting his body into the curve of hers.

Paige rested her arm along his side, her hand settling on his chest, right over his heart.

Freddie closed his eyes.

This was the variable Krystal didn't understand. She thought this was about "touch." She thought it was about skin.

It wasn't. It was about **Architecture**.

Paige’s lap wasn't just a pillow; it was a structural support beam. She knew exactly how to hold his head so his neck muscles could release. She knew exactly how much weight to put on his chest to stop the sensory static.

If Krystal touched him, it was a demand. *Look at me. Want me.* When Paige touched him, it was a promise. *I've got you. Rest.*

"Pressure applied," Paige whispered, looking down at his face. She saw the dark circles under his eyes, the tension still lingering in his jaw.

"System... stabilizing," Freddie breathed.

Jessica walked in with a bowl of dry Honey Oat Rings and a mug of coffee. She set the bowl on the floor within Freddie’s reach.

She sat in the recliner, picking up a book.

"We aren't doing anything today," Jessica stated firmly. "We are watching TV. We are eating carbs. And we are staying inside the firewall."

"Firewall active," Freddie murmured.

He reached blindly for a handful of cereal, crunching quietly. He felt Paige’s fingers start to card through his hair, a slow, rhythmic motion that sent waves of calm down his spine.

The nightmare of the closet faded. The image of the flashlight dissolved.

Here, in the light of the living room, with the taste of oats and the weight of his sister’s hand on his heart, Freddie Avery let the system go offline. He drifted into a doze, safe in the only place in the world where he didn't have to be on guard.
