# Part 6: The Clear Curtain

## Monday, June 23, 2003 21:00 PM

The sun had finally dipped below the ridge, leaving the sky a bruised purple over the Iron City. Inside **105 Pepper Street**, the lights were low, casting long shadows across the hallway.

Jessica walked into the bathroom holding a plastic package she had picked up during their supply run to **Valu-Mart**. "Upgrade time," she announced.

Freddie stood in the doorway, watching. He was wearing his orange hoodie over his boxers—his transition armor. He watched Jessica grip the old, opaque white shower curtain. *Zip. Zip. Zip.* The metal rings slid off the rod with a rhythmic clatter. The white curtain pooled on the floor like a deflated parachute. To Freddie, it looked like a wall coming down.

Jessica tore open the new package. The smell of fresh vinyl filled the small room—a sharp, chemical scent that Freddie associated with "New" and "Clean." She unfolded it. It was heavy-duty, commercial-grade plastic. And it was completely transparent.

She hung it up, hook by hook. "Visual confirmation," Jessica said, sliding it closed. The curtain rippled, but it didn't block anything. She stood inside the tub. Freddie could see her perfectly through the plastic—her silhouette, her face, the maroon CPI t-shirt she was wearing. "Water stays in," she said, tapping the liner with her fingernail. *Tick-tick.* "Data flows out."

Freddie nodded slowly. His brain processed the variable. *Old Curtain:* Isolation. Unknown variables. Fear of abandonment. *New Curtain:* Containment. Visibility. Constant stream of data.

"Logical compromise," Freddie whispered. "It maintains the moisture barrier but prevents visual disconnect."

"Exactly," Paige said, appearing behind him holding her toothbrush. She looked through the clear plastic at Jessica. "No more open curtains. No more wet floors. But you never have to wonder if we left. We are right here."

## 21:15 PM The Routine

"Testing phase," Jessica said, stepping out of the tub. "You're up, Freddie. Let's verify the protocol."

Freddie stepped into the bathroom. The "Robert Virus" attempted to run its startup script—*You are eighteen. You cannot need help. This is wrong.* But the script failed. He had read the manual. He had seen the diagrams. The body was just a machine. Washing it was just maintenance.

He stripped off the orange hoodie and his boxers. He didn't rush. He placed them in the hamper. He stepped into the tub. He sat on the porcelain edge, just like yesterday. But today, he wasn't crashing. He was calibrating.

He turned the water on. Warm spray hit his back. He looked through the clear liner. He could see the sink. He could see the mirror. He could see Paige standing right there, leaning against the counter, squeezing toothpaste onto her brush. She wasn't leaving. She was maintaining the perimeter.

"Do you need assistance?" Paige asked, catching his eye through the plastic. Freddie paused. He could wash himself. His motor control was functional today. But the "Science Class" protocol wasn't just about utility; it was about desensitization. It was about proving, physically, that the shame was gone.

"Affirmative," Freddie whispered. "Scrub assist."

Paige set her toothbrush down. She didn't hesitate. She pulled the clear curtain back just enough to reach in. She picked up the washcloth and the bar of soap. She lathered it up until it was thick with white foam.

"Arms up," Paige said gently. Freddie lifted his arms. Paige washed him. She started at his shoulders, working the warm, soapy cloth down his back in firm, circular motions. *Scrub. Scrub. Scrub.* It was rhythmic. It was tactile. It was the same deep pressure she applied when she hugged him, but transferred through the medium of the sponge.

"Pressure okay?" Paige asked. "Optimal," Freddie said, closing his eyes. He felt the tension draining out of his trapezius muscles. He wasn't being "bathed like a baby." He was being cared for. She moved to his chest, scrubbing away the sweat of the day, the invisible residue of the bookstore anxiety, and the last clinging atoms of the "Robert Virus."

She rinsed him off with the handheld sprayer. The warm water cascaded down his skin. "All clear," Paige announced, turning off the tap. She handed him the towel. "Dry off. Meet us in the Silo."

Freddie stood up. He dried himself behind the clear curtain, watching Paige finish brushing her teeth. He wrapped the towel around his waist. He looked in the mirror. He looked clean. He looked stable. "Test successful," Freddie whispered to his reflection.
