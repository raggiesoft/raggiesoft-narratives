# Part 8: The Warning

## Monday, July 14, 2003 15:12 PM

The silence in the student lounge following Jessica's explosion was brief. It was replaced not by conversation, but by a sound that triggered a primal instinct in anyone who grew up in the mountains.

It started as a low growl outside, penetrating the brick walls, rising steadily in pitch until it became a deafening, mechanical wail.

*WHOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOO-OOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOOO.*

The Blacksburg Civil Defense siren.

It cut through the sound of the rain like a knife. Inside **Archer Hall**, there were no flashing lights, no strobes—just the eerie, vibrating hum of that siren coming from everywhere at once.

The music in the lounge cut out abruptly. The PA system crackled to life with a burst of static, overriding the ambient hum of the building.

*"May I have your attention please,"* a calm, male voice announced, echoing through the ceiling speakers. *"The National Weather Service has issued a Tornado Warning for Blacksburg and the immediate campus area. Seek shelter immediately in an interior hallway on the lowest floor. Stay away from windows. This is not a drill."*

Freddie didn't move. He couldn't.

The siren, combined with the earlier confrontation and the thunder, had severed the connection between his brain and his legs. He was in **Total System Lockout**. His hands were clamped over his ears, his eyes wide and unseeing. He was shaking, a high-frequency tremor that rattled his teeth.

"Freddie," Jessica said, her voice sharp and urgent. "We have to move. Interior hallway. Now."

"Can't," Freddie choked out. "Legs offline."

Jessica didn't hesitate. She looked at Paige.

"Transport protocol," Jessica commanded. "Grab his right side. I’ve got the left."

They moved with practiced efficiency. Jessica hooked her arm under Freddie’s left armpit; Paige did the same on the right.

"On three," Jessica counted. "One. Two. Up."

They hoisted him. Freddie was dead weight, his legs dragging uselessly on the terrazzo floor. He didn't try to help. He simply tucked his head into his chest, surrendering control to the Unit.

They half-carried, half-dragged him out of the lounge, away from the terrifying plate-glass windows that were now rattling with the force of the wind. They turned the corner into the main interior corridor—a long, windowless tunnel of cinderblock and linoleum.

"There," Paige shouted, pointing to a heavy oak study table bolted to the floor against the wall.

"Under," Jessica ordered.

They lowered Freddie to the floor. He scrambled under the table, curling into the tightest ball his spastic muscles would allow. Paige crawled in immediately after him, sitting cross-legged and pulling his head into her lap. Jessica slid in last, taking the position at the opening, using her body to block the view of the hallway.

They were a knot of limbs in the dark space beneath the table.

"Siren," Freddie whimpered, pressing his face into Paige’s stomach. "Too loud. Frequency is... hostile."

"I know," Paige whispered, wrapping her arms around his head, covering his hands with hers to create a double seal against the noise. "I’ve got you. I’m blocking it."

"Radar indicated rotation," Jessica reported, checking the small screen of her phone. "It means they saw a hook echo. It doesn't mean it’s on the ground. It’s just a precaution."

*CRACK-BOOM.*

Thunder shook the floor beneath them, louder than before.

Suddenly, the overhead fluorescent lights buzzed and died, plunging the hallway into darkness. A second later, the emergency backup lights kicked on—dim, yellow beams that cast long, eerie shadows down the corridor.

Freddie let out a sound that was half-sob, half-scream, muffled entirely by Paige’s shirt.

"Squeeze him," Jessica said. "Maximum pressure."

Jessica wrapped her arms around both of them from behind, crushing them into a single, solid mass. Paige squeezed Freddie from the front.

They held him in the **Press**, the ultimate Avery defense. They became a human weighted blanket.

"We are the bunker," Paige whispered into Freddie’s ear, over and over. "We are concrete. We are steel. Nothing gets through the table. Nothing gets through us."

Time distorted.

**15:25 PM.** The siren wailed on outside, a relentless cycle of doom. Freddie’s shaking was violent, sweating through his shirt. He was completely non-verbal, his mind retreated to a safe room deep inside his head where there was no sound.

**15:35 PM.** The siren finally winded down, dropping in pitch until it faded into silence. The quiet that followed was heavy, filled only by the sound of rain hammering the roof—but the rain sounded different now. Heavier. Metallic.

"Hail," Jessica noted. "That's the core passing over."

They didn't move. They held the formation.

**15:45 PM.** The PA system crackled again, loud in the quiet hallway.

*"Attention. The Tornado Warning has expired. The severe weather threat has passed. Please remain cautious of slick floors and debris. All clear."*

Jessica let out a breath that shuddered in her chest. She loosened her grip on Paige’s back.

"All clear," Jessica announced softly. "Freddie? Did you hear that? The warning is over."

Freddie didn't move for a long time. Slowly, incrementally, he uncurled. He pulled his face away from Paige’s shirt. His glasses were crooked. His face was pale and wet with sweat and tears.

"Status?" Paige asked gently, wiping his cheek with her thumb.

"Battery... zero percent," Freddie whispered. His voice was a rasp. "System depleted."

"I bet," Jessica said, crawling out from under the table and standing up. She stretched her cramped legs. "Come on. Let's get you out from under there."

They helped him crawl out. He sat on the floor under the dim emergency lights, leaning back against the wall, eyes closed.

"Home," Freddie whispered. "Requesting... immediate transit to Base."

"We're going," Jessica promised. She looked down the hall toward the doors. "It’s still raining pretty hard, but the hail stopped. No tornado. Just a radar scare."

"Scare was sufficient," Freddie mumbled.

"Yeah," Paige agreed, helping him stand. His legs were wobbly, trembling with the aftershocks of adrenaline, but they held.

They walked slowly toward the exit. Freddie didn't look for Krystal. He didn't check the architectural lines of the hallway. He didn't think about his diagnosis letter.

He just walked, flanked by his sisters, moving toward the bus stop. He was empty, drained of every ounce of energy. But he was walking. The storm had thrown everything it had at him—sex, rage, thunder, wind—and the Unit was still standing.
