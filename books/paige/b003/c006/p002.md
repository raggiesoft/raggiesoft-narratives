# Part 2: The Signal

## Wednesday, July 9, 2003 16:00 PM

The decontamination process was a staged operation. Freddie was clean, cool, and dressed in a fresh pair of soft grey lounge pants and a white undershirt.

They had established a temporary base camp in the living room while the bathroom was occupied. The **MagnaView** TV was the focal point.

"Rotation protocol," Jessica announced, grabbing her towel. "I’m taking the first shift in the shower to wash my hair. Paige, you hold the perimeter."

"Copy," Paige said. She was wearing a loose t-shirt and panties—her "at-home" uniform—sitting next to Freddie on the beige sofa. "We aren't going anywhere."

Jessica disappeared into the hallway. A moment later, the sound of the shower started again—a distant, white-noise hiss behind the closed door.

Freddie held the remote control. He aimed it at the TV. He wasn't looking for news (too much friction) or cartoons (too loud). He needed something stable.

He cycled through the channels. *Static. News. Soap Opera. Static.*

He landed on **Channel 11**, a local independent station known as **WNRV**. In the afternoons, WNRV didn't play trashy talk shows. They played "The Classics."

The screen flickered. A logo appeared: a radio tower transmitting lightning bolts over a city skyline.

*Title Card:* **"W-JAM in Philly"**

Freddie had never seen this show before. The date on the screen said **1978**.

Then, the music started.

It wasn't a jarring orchestral blast like the graduation song. It was a soft, rhythmic piano melody, joined quickly by a smooth, melodic bass line.

A singer with a warm, raspy voice began to croon. It sounded like a friend talking to you across a table in a diner.

*"Baby... if you're listenin' to the wind..."*

Freddie stopped breathing for a second. The sound wasn't just audio; it was a texture. It was like velvet. It was warm, analog, and perfectly balanced.

*"...and you're lookin' for a place to fit on in..."*

He watched the opening montage. He saw a man with a mustache running a soundboard. He saw a blonde woman smiling at a receptionist's desk. He saw a guy in sunglasses pointing coolly at a microphone.

*"...just turn that dial to the right, and let the music light the night..."*

Freddie lowered the remote. He didn't change the channel. He stared at the screen, mesmerized.

"Status?" Paige asked, noticing his stillness.

"The audio profile," Freddie whispered, not looking away. "It is... welcoming. It lacks aggression."

*"W-JAM... we're livin' on the air in Philly..."*

The song ended on a gentle fade-out that felt like a hug.

"What is this?" Freddie asked.

"I think it's **W-JAM**," Paige said, reading the screen. "It's an old sitcom about a radio station."

"A radio station," Freddie repeated. "An audio transmission hub."

He liked the concept immediately. A radio station was a system. It had transmitters. It had frequencies. It had a control board with buttons and sliders.

The show started. The scene opened in the broadcast booth. The DJ—the guy in sunglasses—was queuing up a vinyl record. He moved with a practiced, rhythmic efficiency that Freddie recognized.

*He is operating the machine,* Freddie thought. *He is the Admin of the airwaves.*

The door to the hallway opened. Jessica walked in, drying her hair with a towel. She was wearing a fresh t-shirt and shorts, looking scrubbed and pink from the heat of the shower.

"All clear," Jessica said. "Water pressure is nominal. Paige, you're up."

"Roger," Paige said, standing up. "Freddie found a new show. It's about radio."

Jessica walked over and sat in Paige’s spot, instantly re-establishing the contact pressure against Freddie’s side. She looked at the TV.

"Oh, **W-JAM**," Jessica smiled. "Dad used to hate this show. He said the DJs had too much hair."

"Dad was incorrect," Freddie stated, watching the DJ slide a fader up. "The hair density is irrelevant. The signal quality is the primary variable."

"Fair point," Jessica agreed, wrapping the towel around her wet hair. "Is it funny?"

"I do not know yet," Freddie admitted. "But the theme song... it felt like the King Bed."

Jessica looked at him. That was the highest praise in the Avery dictionary.

"Warm?" she asked.

"Warm," Freddie confirmed. "And safe."

Paige grabbed her towel. "Okay, I'm going in. Don't let him change the channel. We need to see if the blonde receptionist runs the place."

"Hypothesis: She is the true Matriarch," Freddie predicted.

"Likely," Jessica nodded.

As Paige went to shower, Freddie settled back into the cushion, pressing his shoulder against Jessica. On the screen, the chaotic, funny, and warm world of the fictional radio station unfolded.

For the first time all day, Freddie wasn't analyzing threats or dodging smoke. He was just watching a system that worked.

"I will add this to the scheduled recordings," Freddie decided. "Daily at 16:00."

"Adding to the queue," Jessica said, reaching for the popcorn bowl. "Welcome to the playlist, W-JAM."

## Wednesday, July 9, 2003 16:30 PM

The credits for *W-JAM in Philly* rolled, the funky bass line fading into a commercial for a local car dealership (Holt Automotive).

Freddie remained rooted to the spot. The warm feeling from the first show lingered, a sense of having witnessed a functional system.

"Channel hold," Freddie instructed. "Do not switch inputs."

"Holding," Jessica confirmed, crunching a piece of popcorn. She was still in her post-shower towel turban, relaxed and pink-cheeked.

The screen flickered. The station ID for **WNRV** played. Then, a new show began.

It didn't start with a high-energy montage. It started with a slow, sepia-toned slide show of old city streets—cobblestones, gas lamps, and people in bowler hats.

Then, a piano began to play.

It wasn't funky. It was... melancholic. But hopeful.

A gentle, male voice began to sing.

*"Making your way in the world today takes everything you've got..."*

Freddie felt a tightness in his chest loosen. The lyrics resonated instantly. He knew what it took to make his way in the world—it took structure, it took admins, it took energy he didn't always have.

*"Taking a break from all your worries, sure would help a lot..."*

"True," Freddie whispered.

*"Wouldn't you like to get away?"*

The chorus hit, and the energy lifted. It wasn't loud; it was an invitation.

*"Sometimes you want to go..."*

*"Where everybody knows your name..."*

Freddie’s eyes widened. That was the core desire. Not to be famous, but to be *known*. To walk into a room and not be a glitch. To be a recognized user with full permissions.

*"And they're always glad you came..."*

The title card appeared over a shot of a cozy, basement-level pub with stained glass windows:

**THE LOCAL**

"What is this?" Freddie asked, entranced.

"It's **The Local**," Jessica said, smiling fondly. "It's about a bar in Boston. The owner is an ex-baseball player for the **Beacons**."

The show started. A tall man with great hair stood behind the bar, polishing a glass. The door opened. A heavy-set man walked in.

"Norm!" the entire bar shouted in unison.

Freddie sat up straight. "They... acknowledged him."

"It's the ritual," Jessica explained. "Every time he enters, the system recognizes him. Immediate validation."

"Norm," Freddie whispered, testing the sound. "It is a login script."

The door to the hallway opened. Paige walked in. She was clean, dressed in soft shorts and a tank top, her hair damp and smelling of apple shampoo.

"Decontamination complete," Paige announced. She saw Freddie staring at the screen. "Another one?"

"It is called **The Local**," Freddie said, not looking away. "The primary variable is community. They know the names."

Paige walked over and sat on Freddie’s other side, completing the formation on the beige sofa. She leaned her head on his shoulder.

"Do you like it?" she asked.

"The theme song," Freddie said, his voice quiet. "It describes a sanctuary. A place where the social mask is not required."

*"You want to go where people know, people are all the same..."* the song finished on the TV. *"You want to go where everybody knows your name."*

"That's what this is," Paige whispered, squeezing his arm. "Here. 105 Pepper Street. We know your name, Freddie."

"And we're always glad you came," Jessica added, poking his ribs gently.

Freddie looked at the TV, where the characters were bickering affectionately. He looked at his sisters, clean and safe in their fortress.

He realized that **The Local** wasn't just a bar on TV. It was a blueprint for what they were building right here.

"I will add this to the recording schedule," Freddie decided firmly. "Daily at 16:30. It is... essential viewing."

"Double feature," Jessica agreed. "W-JAM and The Local. The Avery Afternoon Block."

As the show continued, Freddie didn't just stay seated. He shifted, sliding down until he was horizontal again, swinging his legs up onto the sofa. He laid his head back down in Paige's lap, exactly as he had done at Newman Hall.

But this time, there was no smoke. There was no Krystal. There was no sweat or gritty humidity.

There was only the scent of apple shampoo, the cool air of the silent house, and the rhythmic, grounding motion of Paige’s hand resuming its place in his hair.

The heat outside, the confusion of the day, the friction of the world—it all felt very far away. Here, in the cool light of the TV, listening to the laughter of the bar where everyone belonged, Freddie closed his eyes for a moment.

He was in the Safe Zone. He was with the people who knew his name. And that was all he needed.
