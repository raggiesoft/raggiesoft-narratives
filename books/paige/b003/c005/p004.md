# Part 4: The Predator

The journey from the classroom door to the building lobby was only fifty yards, but to Freddie, it felt like a forced march across a gravity-heavy planet.

His arms were draped heavily over his sisters’ shoulders. To his left, Jessica bore his weight, her shoulder pressing into his armpit. She was wearing a maroon CPI t-shirt emblazoned with the school mascot—**The Forger**—a muscular, bearded blacksmith raising a hammer. The graphic seemed to mock the current situation; while the painted man on her chest was made of iron, Freddie felt like he was made of wet sand.

To his right was Paige. She was wearing a plain navy blue t-shirt that was, objectively, a size too small. The fabric pulled tight across her chest and shoulders, outlining her bust in a way that usually made Freddie avert his gaze out of respect. But right now, he wasn't looking at her geometry; he was relying on her structure. She gripped his waist firmly, her hip locking against his thigh with every step to prevent his knee from buckling.

Freddie himself wore a simple tan t-shirt that fit perfectly, though it was currently damp with the sweat of exertion.

"Almost there," Paige whispered, adjusting her grip on his belt loop. "The bench is at twelve o'clock. Visual confirmation."

They rounded the corner into the lobby. The air was cooler here, the sunlight streaming in through the glass walls.

"Target acquired," Jessica murmured. "Preparing for docking procedure."

But the path to the bench was blocked.

Leaning against a pillar, right in the center of the lobby, was the girl.

She hadn't left the building. She was waiting. Her arms were crossed over her chest, pushing up her cleavage in a way that seemed calculated. She was popping gum with a rhythmic *snap-crack* that cut through the lobby noise.

When she saw them emerge—Freddie sagging between his sisters, his face pale—her eyes lit up. She didn't see three college students moving as a unit. She didn't see the Forger on Jessica’s shirt or the family resemblance in their faces.

She saw a target. She saw a guy who, in her estimation, probably spent his Saturday nights alone and would be grateful for a scrap of attention from someone like her.

She pushed off the pillar and stepped directly into their path.

"Hey," she purred. Her voice had dropped an octave from the shriek she’d used in class. It was now smoky, suggestive.

The Avery unit came to a halt. Freddie blinked, trying to clear the sweat from his eyes. He looked at her, then past her to the bench.

"Obstruction," Freddie whispered to Paige. "Path blocked."

The girl ignored the women holding him up. To her, they were background noise—staff, aides, maybe volunteers from the disability office. She scanned over Paige’s tight navy shirt with a dismissive sneer, writing her off completely.

"Rough class, huh?" the girl said, stepping closer, invading Freddie's personal space. She smelled of cheap vanilla perfume and ambition. "That teacher is such a prude. I bet you were bored out of your mind."

Freddie frowned. "The material was... standard," he rasped. "She enforced the syllabus."

The girl laughed, tossing her hair. "You're funny. I like that." She looked him up and down, her gaze lingering on his denim-clad legs, then moving up to his eyes with a predatory smirk.

"You look like you’ve had a hard day," she said, lowering her voice to a conspiratorial whisper. "You look tense. Maybe you need someone to help you... relax?"

She reached out, her hand aiming for the tan fabric of his chest. "I was thinking, since I got kicked out, maybe I need a private tutor? Someone to show me the... practical application?"

Jessica stiffened. Her grip on Freddie’s waist tightened. The Forger on her shirt seemed to glare.

Freddie stared at the girl. His brain attempted to parse the data. *Input:* Girl from class. *Status:* Expelled. *Action:* Blocking the bench. *Dialogue:* Illogical. Suggestive.

"I am not a tutor," Freddie said flatly. "I am a student. And I require a seat. My legs are failing."

The girl pouted, a theatrical expression. "Aww, poor baby. Your legs hurt?" She took another step, virtually chest-to-chest with him now. "I bet I can make you forget all about your legs. I bet I can make you feel *real* good."

She shot a dismissive glance at Paige and Jessica. "Why don't you ditch the help? We can go to my dorm. It's close."

The silence that followed was heavy.

"The help?" Jessica repeated. Her voice was ice cold.

The girl rolled her eyes. "Yeah. The nurses. The aides. Whatever. You guys can go. I’ve got him from here."

She reached for Freddie’s arm, trying to pull him away from Paige’s navy-clad shoulder.

That was a tactical error.

Paige didn't let go. She clamped down on Freddie’s bicep like a vice. She didn't look at the girl with anger; she looked at her with the pity one reserves for a mosquito about to hit a bug zapper.

"He isn't going anywhere," Paige said pleasantly.

"Excuse me?" the girl snapped, her mask slipping. "I'm talking to him. Not you."

"And he is talking to you," Jessica interjected, stepping forward slightly, effectively using her shoulder to box the girl out. "He said he needs to sit down. You are in the way."

"He wants to go with me," the girl insisted, looking at Freddie with that same sultry, desperate look. "Don't you, tiger? You want to have some fun? You want to know what a real woman feels like?"

Freddie looked at her. He looked at the heavy makeup and the popped collar. Then he looked at the two women holding him up.

He looked at Jessica in her maroon school colors, the architect of his safety. He looked at Paige in her tight navy tee, the anchor of his sanity. These were the women who bathed him when he couldn't move, who slept next to him to keep the nightmares away, who learned an entire medical textbook just to make him feel safe.

He knew exactly what a real woman felt like. He was holding two of them.

"No," Freddie said. His voice wasn't loud, but it was clear. "I do not want 'fun.' I want stability. And you represent chaos."

The girl’s mouth fell open.

"Chaos?" she sputtered.

"Variable rejected," Freddie confirmed. "Please move. I need the bench."

He didn't wait for her to move. He gave the signal—a slight lean forward.

"Forward," Jessica commanded.

The phalanx moved. They didn't walk around her; they walked *through* the space she was occupying. The girl had to scramble backward, her sneakers squeaking on the tile, to avoid being run over by the sheer momentum of the Avery unit.

She stood there in the middle of the lobby, mouth agape, as the three siblings bypassed her completely.

They reached the bench. Paige and Jessica coordinated the descent, lowering Freddie gently until he was seated. He leaned back against the wall, closing his eyes, letting out a long, ragged exhale.

"Safe," Freddie whispered.

"Safe," Paige confirmed, sitting next to him and wrapping her arm around his shoulders.

The girl watched them for a moment, her face twisting into a sneer of rejection. She huffed, spun on her heel, and stormed out the front door, muttering something about "freaks."

Jessica watched her go, brushing a piece of lint off her Forger t-shirt. Then she sat on Freddie’s other side.

"She thought we were staff," Jessica noted dryly. "Nurses."

"She thought I was desperate," Freddie corrected, opening one eye. "She calculated that disability equals a zero-sum game for romantic selection. She assumed I would take any input provided."

"She sucks at math," Paige said, resting her head on his shoulder.

"Agreed," Freddie said, closing his eye again. He felt the warmth of his sisters on both sides—maroon on the left, navy on the right. The bench was hard, the lobby was bright, but the perimeter was secure.

"Five minutes," Freddie requested. "Then the bus."

"Take ten," Jessica said, crossing her legs in her blue jeans. "We aren't going anywhere."
