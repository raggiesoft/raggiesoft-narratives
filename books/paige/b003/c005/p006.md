# Part 6: The Safe Zone

## 13:15 PM

The walk to the **CPI Transit Center** was a silent march. The adrenaline from the confrontation in the lobby was fading, replaced by the heavy, humid weight of a Virginia afternoon.

A white-and-maroon bus was already idling in the loop, its diesel engine rumbling with a deep, patient rhythm. The destination sign glowed amber: **1 FALLING BRANCH**

"That’s us," Jessica said, her voice tired. "Let’s get out of here."

They boarded. Freddie pulled his **PolyPass** from his pocket and held it up for the driver to see. The driver gave a quick nod of acknowledgment—a visual check, no beep, no machine. Just a flash of plastic to prove he belonged.

Jessica and Paige flashed their IDs behind him, and they moved to the back of the bus, finding a row of empty seats near the rear exit door.

Freddie slid in next to the window. Paige sat in the middle. Jessica took the aisle seat, effectively boxing them in.

As the bus hissed and pulled away from the curb, merging into the campus traffic, Paige let out a long, frustrated sigh. She slumped in her seat, crossing her arms over her chest—not to hold Freddie this time, but to hide herself.

She looked down at the navy blue t-shirt. It was tight. She knew it was tight. It was a laundry error, a remnant from high school that had accidentally made it into the college rotation. But looking at the way the fabric pulled across her chest, she felt a sudden, burning wave of resentment.

"I hate it," Paige muttered, tugging at the hem of the shirt.

"The fabric?" Freddie asked, looking at her. "Is the texture abrasive?"

"No," Paige said, shaking her head. She looked out the window at the passing stone buildings of the university. "I hate that I can't just... exist. I hate that I walk into a room to learn about anatomy, and some jerk thinks I'm there to *be* the anatomy."

She turned to Freddie. Her eyes were wet, frustrated tears gathering in the corners.

"He didn't even look at my face, Freddie. He looked at my chest. He talked to my t-shirt. It’s like... it’s like the rest of me didn't even matter. I was just a shape to him."

Freddie processed the data. He recalled the boy's gaze in the lobby—the vector, the smirk, the comments about "heavy lifting."

"He reduced you to geometry," Freddie stated. "He ignored the architecture."

"Exactly," Paige whispered. She wiped her eyes. "It’s exhausting. Being looked at like... like meat."

Freddie wasn't looking at her chest. He never did.

And he never will. He had seen her naked—in the shower, in the King Bed—and even then, stripped of all fabric, he would not stare at her chest. He would always lock his eyes to her eyes, overriding his natural autistic urge to look away, specifically out of respect.

His eyes were focused entirely on her face, scanning her expression for data on her emotional state. His hand was resting on his own knee, tapping a slow, rhythmic pattern. He wasn't assessing her curves; he was assessing her stability.

To Freddie, she wasn't a collection of "parts." She wasn't "breasts" or "hips."

She was **Paige**. She was the Anchor. She was the person who knew how to wash his hair without getting soap in his eyes. She was the weight that stopped the world from spinning.

She was his sister.

The relief of that realization hit Paige harder than the anger. In a world full of boys who only saw what was under her shirt, Freddie was the one man who only cared about what was in her heart.

"Come here," Paige whispered.

She didn't wait for him to initiate. She leaned forward and wrapped her arms around him. It wasn't a protective hug this time; it was a seeking one. She buried her face in the hollow of his neck, breathing in the scent of his laundry detergent and the faint, metallic smell of the bus.

She pressed herself against him. Her chest was pressed against his arm, but there was no weirdness. There was no tension.

Freddie reacted instantly. He didn't pull away. He didn't get awkward. He lifted his arm and wrapped it around her shoulders, pulling her in tighter. He rested his chin on the top of her head.

"Pressure applied," Freddie murmured.

"Yeah," Paige breathed, closing her eyes. "Pressure applied."

She felt safe. She felt human. For the first time all day, she didn't feel like an object. She felt like a person who was loved.

"You are not geometry," Freddie said softly, his voice vibrating against her hair. "You are the structural integrity of the system. You are the Safe Person."

Paige squeezed him, a fierce, bone-crushing hug that poured all her gratitude into him.

"And you're mine," she whispered. "My safe person."

Jessica watched them from the aisle seat. She saw the way the other passengers on the bus glanced at them—a guy and a girl hugging intensely in the back row.

Let them look, Jessica thought. They didn't know the code. They didn't know that this wasn't romance; it was survival.

"Next stop," the automated voice announced. *"Pepper Plaza."*

Paige pulled back, but she didn't let go of his hand. She looked down at her shirt one last time, then shrugged.

"I'm burning this shirt when we get home," she decided.

"Illogical," Freddie countered, adjusting his glasses. "Cotton is a useful material. Use it for rags. Cleaning the Avalon."

Paige laughed, a real, genuine sound that chased the shadows away. "Okay. Rags it is."

The bus hissed to a halt. The doors opened. They stepped out into the sunshine of Christiansburg, leaving the predators and the geometry behind, walking toward the safety of the Base.
