# Part 1: Ground Rules

The fluorescent lights of the classroom hummed, a stark contrast to the humid warmth of a Blacksburg summer afternoon outside. This was HPE 204: Dimensions of Human Sexuality.

For Freddie, this class was a strategic maneuver. The university’s Health and Physical Education requirement was mandatory for graduation, usually filled by sweating through tennis, jogging, or swimming. With his cerebral palsy, those options ranged from "difficult" to "logistical nightmare." But HPE 204 counted for the same credit, and the only muscle he had to exercise here was his brain.

He sat in the second row, the sweet spot for serious students who didn’t want to appear eager. Flanking him were Paige and Jessica, the three of them claiming a block of desks like a defensive line.

"I give it ten minutes before the frat boys in the back row crack," Jessica whispered, leaning in from Freddie's left. She had her notebook open but her pen was capped, her eyes scanning the room with amused cynicism.

"Five," Paige countered from his right. She had already written the date and the course title at the top of her page in neat, architectural block letters. "Look at them. They’re already vibrating."

She wasn’t wrong. The room was set for thirty, and while half the class looked like Freddie—students checking a box for a degree requirement—the other half were the types who had seen the word "Sexuality" in the course catalog and assumed this would be an easy A or a comedy hour.

The door shut with a heavy click, and Dr. Aris Thorne walked to the podium. She was a woman who radiated a clinical, no-nonsense authority. She didn’t smile. She didn’t try to be cool. She set a stack of syllabi on the desk and looked over her glasses at the room.

"Welcome to Dimensions of Human Sexuality," Dr. Thorne said. Her voice was calm but projected to the back corners of the room without a microphone. "Let us establish the ground rules immediately."

The shuffling of backpacks ceased.

"In this room, there are no secrets," she began, pacing slowly in front of the whiteboard. "We are studying human biology, psychology, and sociology. We will treat these subjects with the scientific dignity they deserve. If you are here to giggle, drop the class now. There is a waiting list of students who actually need this credit."

Freddie adjusted his posture, grateful for the sturdy desk chair. He saw Paige nod in approval.

"I will use proper terminology," Dr. Thorne continued. "You will hear words like 'penis,' 'vulva,' 'intercourse,' and 'masturbation' frequently. However..." She paused, picking up a dry-erase marker. "You are entering a world where you may be educators, healthcare providers, or counselors. The people you interact with will not always use textbooks terms."

She turned and wrote a single word on the board: *Terminology*.

"We will learn the clinical definitions," she said, facing them again. "But we will also introduce and discuss the slang. The street terms. The words you hear in movies and locker rooms. Not because I want you to use them in your papers, but because when a patient or a student comes to you confused and uses those words, you need to know exactly what they are talking about without flinching."

From the back row, a stifled snort broke the silence. It was the distinct sound of someone trying to hold back a laugh and failing.

Dr. Thorne didn't even look up from her notes. "I assume that was a involuntary reflex," she said dryly. "Get it out of your system now. By Chapter 3, the novelty will have worn off."

Jessica leaned toward Freddie, her voice barely a breath. "She's good."

"She has to be," Freddie whispered back.

"This is a safe space," Dr. Thorne said, her eyes locking on the back row. "Questions are encouraged. Ignorance is expected—that is why you are here. But disrespect will result in an immediate ejection. We are discussing the mechanics of human life. You will respect the subject, and you will respect each other."

She tapped the stack of papers on her desk.

"Syllabuses are coming around. We start with anatomy on Wednesday. I suggest you do the reading. If you are uncomfortable discussing the human body, the registrar closes at 5:00 PM."

Freddie took a syllabus as the pile passed him, handing the rest to Paige. He glanced at the course schedule. It was dense, clinical, and thorough. It was exactly what he needed to get his degree without worrying about his legs giving out on a track. He uncapped his pen. He was ready to work.
