# Part 7: Dinner

## Wednesday, July 23, 2003 17:15 PM — 105 Pepper Street

They pulled into the driveway. The house stood waiting, white and solid.

"Okay," Jessica said, killing the engine. "This is the hard part. The dismount."

Freddie unbuckled his seatbelt. He took a deep breath. He knew his legs were going to scream.

"I got the food," Paige said, hopping out of the back and grabbing the warm, grease-stained bags from his lap. "You just focus on the landing gear."

Jessica came around to the passenger side and opened the door. She offered her arm.

"On three," she said. "One. Two. Up."

Freddie grabbed her arm and hauled himself out. His knees buckled slightly, trembling under the sudden return of gravity, but he locked them.

"Stable?" Jessica asked.

"Stable," Freddie gritted out. "Forward motion."

He walked stiffly up the concrete path, focusing on the front door. Every step was a victory.

They entered the house. The air conditioning hit them instantly—cool, dry, and smelling of lemon polish.

"Dining room," Paige called out, heading for the small wooden table adjacent to the kitchen.

"Audio protocol," Jessica said as she locked the door. She picked up the remote for the small counter-top TV in the kitchen—a secondary unit to the massive MagnaView in the living room.

She pressed power.

*Click.*

The screen flared to life, displaying the blue-and-gold graphics of **WNRV-TV Channel 11**, the local GBS affiliate broadcasting out of Blacksburg.

*"...temperatures remaining in the upper 80s through the evening,"* the weatherman announced, waving at a map of the New River Valley. *"So if you're headed out to the County Fair this weekend, bring plenty of water."*

"Background noise established," Jessica said, turning the volume to a low, companionable hum.

They sat down at the dining table. Freddie sank into his chair with a groan of pure relief. The familiar cadence of the local news anchors—talking about road work on I-81 and a bake sale in Riner—was a grounding signal. It meant they were back in civilization.

"You made it," Jessica smiled, distributing the food.

She placed the massive, foil-wrapped **Thick-Stack** in front of him. She set the large cup of bubbling dark soda next to it.

Freddie unwrapped the burger. It was hot, heavy, and simple.

He took a bite. The grease, the cheese, the beef—it hit his system like a recharge pack.

"Status?" Paige asked, dipping a fry into her strawberry shake.

Freddie chewed slowly. He looked at his sisters. They were sweaty, tired, and smelling of the woods. They were eating fast food at a cheap table in a rental house, bathed in the soft glow of the local news.

But they had climbed a mountain. They had beaten the test. They had secured the perimeter.

"Status," Freddie said, taking a long pull of the cold **Mega-Cola**. "Optimal."

On the TV, the news anchor shuffled his papers. *"Coming up at 5:30, we'll have a report on the new construction over at the university. But first, a word from **Holt Automotive**."*

He took another bite.

"The camping trip," Freddie said between chews. "I have determined the menu."

"Oh?" Jessica raised an eyebrow over her **Crispy Chicken Sandwich**. "What are we eating in the wild?"

"Hot dogs," Freddie stated firmly. "Roasted on a stick. It is the only logical campfire protein."

"Hot dogs it is," Paige agreed. "And s'mores?"

"S'mores are mandatory," Freddie confirmed.

They ate their Brawny Burgers in the quiet safety of the Iron City, planning their next mission while the WNRV news ticker scrolled by, knowing that whatever mountain came next, they would climb it the same way: together.
