# Part 4: The Transit & The Trail

## 11:15 AM — Alumni Mall

The celebration at the bench was brief; the heat of the day was rising, and the logistics of the next phase required execution.

"We have a vehicle problem," Freddie noted, checking his watch as they stood up. "The Avalon is docked at Base. We are at CPI. Displacement is six miles."

"Standard retrieval protocol," Jessica said, swinging her backpack onto one shoulder. "We catch the **Route 1** southbound. It drops us right at our front door."

They walked from Henderson Hall across the Drillfield to **Alumni Mall**.

There was no terminal here, no sleek concrete loop. The "hub" was simply the side of the road. Buses lined up bumper-to-bumper along the curb across from **Newman Hall**, fighting for space with cars and pedestrians jaywalking to the student center. It was a chaotic, diesel-choked strip of asphalt that served as the nerve center of the transit network.

They stood at the signpost marking the Route 1 stop, waiting as a bus pulled away, revealing the next one in the queue.

"Inbound," Freddie announced.

The white-and-maroon **Blue Ridge Transit** bus hissed as it kneeled to the curb, its tires grinding against the concrete gutter. The amber LED sign read:

**1 FALLING BRANCH**

"Boarding," Jessica commanded.

They climbed the steps, flashing their **PolyPasses**. The chirp of the card reader was a familiar, comforting sound—the sound of authorized access in the middle of the street-side chaos.

They moved to the back. Freddie took the window seat. The bus pulled out, merging aggressively into the traffic on the Mall before turning onto Main Street.

For thirty minutes, Freddie decompressed. The exam was over. The diagrams were labeled. The "Robert Virus" had tried to delete his confidence, but the system had held. He watched the familiar landmarks of the commute roll by: the stone arches of the university giving way to the strip malls of the 460 corridor, the transition from "Gown" to "Town."

"Approaching **Pepper Plaza**," the automated voice announced.

The bus turned off the highway and navigated into the small, concrete transit hub in downtown Christiansburg. It wasn't pretty—just a sawtooth curb cut into a lot with a few heavy, weathered shelters—but it was efficient. The bus docked in its bay with a heavy *thud* of air brakes.

## 11:45 AM — 105 Pepper Street

They stepped off the bus onto the stained concrete of the plaza. The heat in the valley was heavy, but the sight of their white house directly diagonally across the street was grounding.

"Quick turnaround," Paige said as they jogged across the street to the driveway. "Bathroom, water, car."

They entered the cool sanctuary of the house just long enough to grab water bottles and use the facilities. Freddie swapped his testing pencils for his hiking gear—zipping his safety-orange hoodie over his t-shirt despite the July heat. It was his armor against insects, branches, and the unknown variables of the woods.

"I'm driving," Jessica announced, grabbing the keys to the silver **Horizon Avalon** from the hook. "It’s a mountain road. I want the control of the stick."

They piled into the car. Jessica depressed the clutch, shifted into Reverse, and backed out of the driveway. She shifted to First, and they rolled past the bus idling in the plaza, heading for the mountains.

## 12:45 PM — The Trailhead

The drive took them out of the valley and up a winding, two-lane road that climbed steadily into the canopy of the Appalachian chain. The air changed as they ascended—thinning out, losing the heavy humidity of the town and gaining the sharp, pine-resin scent of the high altitude.

Jessica parked the Avalon in the gravel lot of the **Wind Rock** trailhead. She set the handbrake with a firm *ratchet-click*.

"Engine off," she said. "We are on foot."

" hydration check," Paige said, handing Freddie a water bottle.

"The trail is this way," Jessica pointed to a blaze painted on a tree.

They started the climb.

It wasn't easy. The path was a mix of packed dirt and jagged limestone rocks—an ankle-twisting nightmare for anyone, but a complex physics calculation for Freddie.

Every step required conscious input. *Angle of foot placement. Friction coefficient of the rock. Balance distribution.*

His spastic muscles fought him. Ten minutes in, his right toe dragged, catching on an exposed root. He stumbled forward, gravity taking hold.

"Gotcha," Paige said instantly.

She didn't gasp. She didn't panic. She had linked arms with him the moment they left the car, and now she simply tightened her grip, using her own momentum to pull him upright.

"Course correction," Freddie murmured, regaining his footing.

"Keep moving," Paige said comfortably. "We're a tank. Tanks go over everything."

They fell into a rhythm. Jessica walked ahead, scouting the path like a point man.

"Big step coming up," she’d call out. "Loose gravel on the left. Stay center."

Paige walked beside him, their hips bumping occasionally, her body acting as a constant gyroscopic stabilizer. She adjusted her speed to match his, absorbing his stumbles before they became falls.

They climbed for forty-five minutes. Freddie was sweating under his hoodie. His legs burned with lactic acid. The sensory input of the forest—the crunch of dry leaves, the cry of a hawk, the wind in the trees—was intense, but it wasn't static. It was organic. It was a natural frequency he could process.

"Almost there," Jessica called from a ridge above them. "One last push."

They scrambled up a final embankment of grey stone.

And then, the world opened up.

## 13:30 PM — The Summit

They stepped out onto a massive, flat outcropping of rock that jutted out over the valley floor like the prow of a ship.

There were no trees here. Just stone, wind, and sky.

Freddie stopped. He forgot about his burning legs. He forgot about the sweat trickling down his back.

Below him, the **New River Valley** spread out like a living map. He could see the tiny ribbon of the river reflecting the sun. He could see the geometric grid of the farmlands.

In the distance, he could see the toy-like structures of the university—the "Iron City" reduced to a circuit board.

"The scale," Freddie whispered, adjusting his glasses. "It is... infinite."

He walked toward the edge (not too close; Paige held his belt loop just in case).

He looked at the horizon. It was a flat, blue line where the mountains met the sky.

"We are above the noise," Paige said, standing next to him. The wind whipped her hair across her face, but she looked peaceful.

"High altitude," Freddie agreed. "Signal clarity is 100%."

Jessica sat down on the warm rock, opening her backpack. "Who wants a victory snack? I have **Choco-Bricks** and **Fizzy-Pop**."

Freddie sat down carefully, pulling his knees to his chest. He took the chocolate wafer bar.

He ate it slowly, looking out at the world.

For the first time in his life, he didn't feel small. He felt like he was sitting on the bridge of his own starship, looking out at the galaxy.

He had passed the test. He had navigated the medical system. He had climbed the mountain.

He looked at his sisters.

"We are functional," Freddie stated.

"We are fully operational," Jessica corrected, clinking her soda can against his water bottle.

"To the Unit," Paige said, clinking hers.

"To the Unit," Freddie whispered.

They sat there for an hour, watching the cloud shadows drift across the valley—three specks on a rock who had built their own world out of spare parts, bus schedules, and stubborn love.

And for Freddie Avery, the view was perfect.
