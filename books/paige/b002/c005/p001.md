# Part 1: The Diagnosis

## Sunday, October 27, 2002 07:30 AM

The morning sun filtered through the blinds of the Christiansburg apartment, casting slats of dust-mote light across the living room. It was quiet. The roaring static of the stadium, the pounding rhythm of the stomp, and the screaming meltdown of the afternoon were all distant memories.

Jessica stood in the doorway of her bedroom, holding a steaming mug of coffee. She looked at the beige sofa bed.

It was a tangle of limbs and jersey sheets. Freddie and Paige were asleep, but they weren't just sleeping near each other; they were structurally integrated. Freddie was curled on his side, his face pressed against Paige’s shoulder, his arm draped heavily over her waist. Paige had one arm wrapped around his head, her hand resting protectively on his hair, her legs tangled with his.

It looked less like two people sleeping and more like a single organism that had evolved to survive a hostile environment.

Jessica took a sip of coffee. She thought about the Hall Director’s face. *Single-gender policy.* She thought about Freddie’s scream in the lobby. *System failure.*

She walked softly across the carpet, careful not to creak the floorboards, and sat down at her desk in the corner. She pressed the power button on her bulky beige tower.

The computer hummed to life. The fans whirred. The CRT monitor flickered, then glowed with the **Quantum OS 2000 Professional** startup screen. A clean, blue progress bar filled across the bottom.

Jessica waited for the desktop to load. She double-clicked the **WebCompass** icon. The dial-up modem screeched its electronic handshake—*reeee-urrrr-ding-ding*—connecting her to the outside world.

She opened the search bar. She typed the word the Hall Director had whispered.

*A-U-T-I-S-M.*

She hit Enter.

The progress bar at the bottom of the browser crawled forward. *Connecting... Waiting for reply...*

Finally, a list of blue hyperlinks appeared on the white background. It wasn't a sleek, modern interface; it was a wall of text. She clicked the top result: "Autism Society of America."

The page loaded one element at a time. The banner graphic slowly rasterized from top to bottom.

She started reading.

*...a developmental disability that significantly affects verbal and nonverbal communication and social interaction...*

*...evident before age three...*

*...adversely affects a child's educational performance...*

Jessica frowned. That sounded severe. That sounded like the non-verbal kids she had seen in special education classes who needed full-time aides. That didn't sound like Freddie, who could explain the physics of a football game or code a directory in Nix-OS.

She scrolled down, the text jumping slightly as the browser refreshed. She found a section labeled **"The Spectrum."**

*...symptoms can vary widely...*

*...hypersensitivity to sensory input (sight, sound, touch)...*

Jessica paused. *The cafeteria. The earplugs. The hoodie.*

*...insistence on sameness; resistance to change...*

*The Blueprint. The schedule. The meltdown when the plan broke.*

*...intense absorption in specific interests or topics...*

*The Terminal. The Architecture. The obsession with systems.*

*...difficulties in social reciprocity; lack of eye contact...*

*He looks at my nose. He never looks at my eyes.*

Jessica felt a chill go down her spine. It wasn't a chill of fear; it was the chill of recognition. It was the feeling of finding a key that fit a lock she had been trying to pick for seventeen years.

"Jessica?"

She spun around in her chair.

Freddie was sitting up on the sofa bed. His hair was a mess, sticking up in tufts. He was rubbing his eyes behind his glasses. Paige was stirring beside him, blinking in the morning light.

"Hey," Jessica said softly. "Did I wake you?"

"The modem," Freddie noted, his voice raspy with sleep. "The frequency is piercing."

"Sorry," Jessica grimaced. "Technological limitations."

She hesitated, then turned her chair fully toward them.

"Freddie," she said. "Paige. Grab a blanket. Come over here. I want to show you something."

Paige sat up, wrapping the top sheet around her shoulders like a cape. She nudged Freddie. They got up and walked over to the desk, pulling up two folding chairs to sit on either side of Jessica.

"What is the research topic?" Freddie asked, squinting at the glowing screen.

"Yesterday," Jessica started, choosing her words carefully. "When the Hall Director asked if you had been evaluated... she used a word."

"Autism," Paige said. She remembered.

"Right," Jessica nodded. "I'm looking it up. And I want you to read this list of... specifications."

She pointed to the section on **Sensory Processing**.

*Individuals may experience sensory overload. Ordinary sounds may be painful. Fluorescent lights may be blinding. Fabrics may feel like sandpaper.*

Freddie leaned forward. He read the text. He read it again.

"That is... correct," Freddie whispered. "The cafeteria is not just loud. It is physical pain. It is static."

Jessica scrolled down. **Social Interaction.**

*Difficulty understanding unwritten social rules. Interpretation of language is often literal. confusion with metaphors or sarcasm.*

"Literal," Freddie said. "I do not understand the subtext. I only hear the text."

Jessica scrolled again. **Routine and Structure.**

*A strong need for predictability. Changes in routine can cause severe distress or 'meltdowns,' which are an involuntary reaction to being overwhelmed, not a tantrum.*

Paige gasped softly. She put her hand on Freddie’s arm.

"A meltdown," Paige whispered. "Not a tantrum. It's not you being difficult, Freddie. It's... it's a system crash."

Freddie stared at the screen. For years, his father had told him he was broken. That he was weird. That he just needed to "toughen up" and "act normal."

But this screen wasn't saying he was broken. It was saying he was *different*. It was describing an operating system that processed data differently than the standard **Quantum OS** build most people ran.

"It is a manual," Freddie said, his voice filled with wonder. "There is a manual."

"Yeah," Jessica smiled, tears prickling her eyes. "There's a manual."

She looked at her brother.

"You aren't a 'bad kid,' Freddie. You aren't 'defective.' You're autistic. Your brain is wired for high-resolution input in a low-resolution world. That's why you need the earplugs. That's why you need the schedule. And that's why..." she looked at Paige, "...that's why you need the anchor."

Freddie processed the new variable. *Autism.*

It fit. It explained the Terminal—why he loved the black void and the blinking cursor. It explained the "Engine"—why he connected with the raw, unfiltered mechanics of the world. It explained why he couldn't sleep alone—because the sensory input of the dark was too chaotic without a ground wire.

"I am... autistic," Freddie tested the word. It didn't feel heavy. It felt precise.

"We all have to learn the new code," Jessica said firmly. "Me, you, Paige. And eventually... Dad."

Paige stiffened. "Dad won't read the manual."

"Then we'll read it for him," Jessica said. "But right now? We know. And that means we can build a better Blueprint."

Freddie looked at the screen one last time. He memorized the symptoms. He memorized the data.

"Hypothesis accepted," Freddie said, turning to his sisters. "Now. What is the breakfast protocol?"

Jessica laughed, wiping her eyes. "Pancakes. The protocol is pancakes."

"With high-viscous syrup?" Freddie checked.

"Maximum viscosity," Jessica promised.

"Excellent," Freddie said, standing up. "Let us proceed."
