# Part 2: The Firewall

## 10:00 AM

The peaceful Sunday morning atmosphere—pancakes, syrup, and the quiet humming of the computer tower—was shattered by three heavy, authoritative knocks on the front door.

*THUD. THUD. THUD.*

Freddie froze. He was sitting at the small kitchen table, a forkful of pancake halfway to his mouth. He didn't need to look at the door to know who it was. The rhythm was distinctive. It wasn't the polite knock of a neighbor or the casual rap of a delivery driver. It was the knock of an owner inspecting his property.

"Robert," Paige whispered, her face going pale.

Jessica stood up instantly, putting her coffee mug down on the counter with a sharp *clack*. She held up a hand, signaling them to stay put.

"Hold position," Jessica ordered softly.

She walked to the door, moving on the balls of her feet. She leaned in, pressing her eye to the peephole.

Distorted by the fish-eye lens, but unmistakable in his stiff posture and angry expression, stood their father. He was wearing his "court clothes"—a charcoal suit that looked out of place in the grim hallway of a college apartment complex. He looked impatient. He looked ready to issue commands.

"Open the door," his voice came through the wood, muffled but distinct. "I know they are in there. I am here to collect my children."

*My children.* Not "Jessica." Not "Paige." Not "Freddie." Just possessive nouns.

Jessica stepped back from the door, careful not to make the floorboards creak. She turned to Freddie and Paige.

"It's Dad," Jessica whispered. "He wants to take you. He’s demanding to speak to you."

She looked at them, her expression fierce and protective.

"This is your call," Jessica said. "You are seventeen. You aren't toddlers. If you want to talk to him, I will open the door. If you don't, it stays locked. I am the admin of this network. He has no root access here."

Freddie looked at the door. He imagined opening it. He imagined the wall of noise, the criticism, the demands to "stop acting weird," the dismissal of the "Twin Dorm" plan as nonsense. He imagined the static rising until it drowned out the clarity he had just found on the computer screen.

He looked at Paige. She was gripping the edge of the table, her knuckles white.

Freddie shook his head. A sharp, definitive *No*.

Paige shook her head too. "No," she whispered. "Please, Jess. No."

Jessica nodded. "Understood. Access denied."

She didn't yell through the door. She didn't try to argue with him. She simply turned the deadbolt a second time, engaging the heavy security lock she had installed herself.

*Click-THUNK.*

Outside, Robert knocked again, louder this time.

"I know you're in there!" he shouted. "This is ridiculous. I have a schedule to keep! Send them out!"

Jessica walked back to the table and sat down. She picked up her coffee.

"Eat your pancakes," she said calmly. "He'll time out eventually."

They sat in silence for ten agonizing minutes. Robert knocked a few more times, paced the hallway (they could hear his heavy dress shoes clicking on the concrete), and muttered something about "ungrateful."

Finally, the sound of retreating footsteps echoed down the hall. The heavy fire door to the stairwell slammed shut.

"Target has left the perimeter," Jessica announced.

Freddie let out a breath he didn't know he was holding. He looked at his sister. She was the firewall. She had held the line.

## 11:30 AM

"We have to move," Jessica said, checking her watch. "The handover is at 13:00. Mom is meeting us in Staunton."

They packed quickly. Freddie folded his new orange hoodie carefully, placing it in his backpack like a sacred relic. He packed the Candidate Kit folder with the campus map. He packed the memory of the Terminal.

They walked down to Jessica's car, a battered Kyoto Citizen that she kept running with duct tape and prayer.

"I wish I could keep you," Jessica said as they pulled onto Interstate 81, heading north. "I really do. But the legal code is strict. You're still minors. If I kidnap you, I lose my scholarship, and then I can't help anyone."

"It is logical," Freddie said from the back seat. "We must return to base to complete the academic cycle."

"Just until June," Paige added. "Then we graduate. Then we come back."

"Exactly," Jessica said, merging onto the highway. "Eye on the horizon."

## 13:00 PM

They pulled off the highway in Staunton, Virginia, spotting the massive, glowing red-and-yellow sign of **Zane's Market**.

It wasn't just a gas station; it was a cathedral of convenience. Twelve gas pumps sat under a brightly lit canopy, but the main attraction was the sprawling glass-fronted store. In 2002, Zane's was revolutionary—it was one of the first chains to introduce touch-screen food ordering, a beacon of high-tech caloric intake.

"Mom's car is here," Paige pointed to the station wagon parked near the air pumps.

Jessica pulled in next to it. Their mother was waiting, looking anxious, but she smiled when she saw them.

"Lunch protocol," Jessica declared. "I'm not sending you back on an empty stomach. Come on, my treat."

They all walked inside. The interior was a sensory blast of bright lights and red tile, but it was a happy kind of noise. They walked past the racks of chips and sodas to the **"Z-Kitchen"** ordering terminals.

Freddie stood in front of the touch screen. He loved this interface. No talking to a cashier. No misheard orders. Just buttons.

*Item: Zane Burger.* *Option: No Onions. No Tomatoes.* *Option: Extra Cheese.* *Side: Fryz.* *Confirm.*

"It is efficient," Freddie noted, tapping the screen with satisfaction.

They grabbed a booth in the corner with their tray of food. For twenty minutes, it felt like a normal family. Mom asked about the tour (Jessica censored the meltdown and the housing rejection, focusing on the engineering lab and the football game). They ate their fries and drank massive sodas.

"Your father is... heading to the courthouse," Mom said quietly, dipping a fry in ketchup. "He's very focused on the proceedings tomorrow. He was furious that you didn't open the door, Jessica."

"He didn't have a warrant," Jessica joked dryly. "And the tenants declined entry."

Mom sighed, but she reached across the table and squeezed Jessica's hand. "Thank you for taking them. They look... lighter."

"We saw the machine," Freddie said suddenly.

"The machine?" Mom asked.

"The stadium," Paige explained. "The stomp. It was amazing, Mom. Freddie loved it."

They finished their meal. The reality of the handoff began to settle in.

## 13:45 PM

They walked back out to the cars. The bright neon of Zane's Market hummed above them.

"Okay," Jessica said, her voice catching slightly. "This is it. You guys go back, finish senior year, and keep your grades up. I've got school and work tomorrow, so I have to head back to Blacksburg."

She hugged Paige hard. Then she stood in front of Freddie.

"Check your email," Jessica whispered. "I'll send you the links to the research we found this morning. Read the manual, Freddie. Learn the code."

"Affirmative," Freddie said. He looked at her nose. "Thank you for the... Stomp."

"Anytime, little brother."

She watched them climb into their mother’s station wagon. As they pulled away, heading east toward Norfolk and the silence of their father's house, Jessica didn't wave. She stood under the glowing red sign of Zane's Market, watching them go, already planning the architecture of their escape in June.
