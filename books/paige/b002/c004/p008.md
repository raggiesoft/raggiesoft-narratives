# Part 8: The Iron Sea

## 17:30 PM

The sun had dipped below the mountains, turning the sky into a bruised purple, but the campus was brighter than noon.

They left the quiet refuge of the bench and descended toward the south end of campus. As they walked, the atmosphere changed. It wasn't just the air getting colder; it was the ground getting hotter.

A low rumble—like a distant thunderstorm that refused to break—began to vibrate through the soles of Freddie’s shoes.

"The Transit Center is empty," Freddie observed, looking at the bus loops they had used earlier. They were deserted, the bays dark.

"Diversion protocol," Jessica explained, pointing down the road. "On Game Day, the hub moves. The center of gravity shifts."

As they crested the hill overlooking Washington Street, Freddie saw it.

A convoy of **Blue Ridge Transit** buses was roaring toward the stadium in a continuous, unbroken line. They were massive, 60-foot articulated leviathans, groaning under the weight of passengers.

Every single window was steamed up. Every single bus was packed to "crush load"—bodies pressed against the glass, maroon jerseys everywhere.

Freddie focused on the LED destination signs. They were synchronized.

*CRUCIBLE SHUTTLE*.*..click...* *GO FORGERS!*

"The logic," Freddie whispered, watching the buses discharge thousands of people onto the sidewalk in a coordinated flood. "It is a mass injection system."

"It's an invasion," Jessica grinned, applying a stripe of maroon greasepaint to her left cheek and an orange stripe to her right. "And we're the army."

## 18:00 PM

The plaza outside **The Crucible** was a sensory riot. The smell of grilled sausages and funnel cake hung heavy in the air.

"Equipment check," Jessica said, steering them toward a white tent marked **FORGER FAN SHOP**. "You are currently out of uniform. That is a tactical error."

They ducked inside the tent. It was crowded, but Paige kept a tight grip on Freddie’s sleeve.

"Pick a color," Jessica commanded. "Maroon or Orange. We don't do neutral here."

Paige grabbed a thick, maroon hooded sweatshirt. "Maroon matches the brick," she decided.

Freddie touched a bright, safety-orange hoodie. It was loud. It was visible. It was the color of a warning sign.

"Orange," Freddie said. "High visibility."

"Good choice," Jessica said, grabbing it and tossing it on the counter. She also grabbed a small blister pack from a display near the register.

She paid with cash, handing the bag to Paige.

"Put them on," Jessica said. "And Freddie? Install these."

She handed him the blister pack. **High-Fidelity Earplugs**.

Freddie opened the pack. He rolled the foam cylinders and inserted them into his ears. The roar of the crowd instantly dulled. The sharp, piercing spikes of laughter and shouting were smoothed out into a manageable, muted hum.

"Better?" Paige asked, pulling her maroon hood up.

Freddie pulled his orange hood up, creating a tunnel for his vision. He nodded. "The gain has been reduced. It is acceptable."

## 18:30 PM

They approached the South End Zone entrance. The stadium loomed over them, a massive brutalist structure of concrete and steel that looked less like a sports arena and more like a reactor core.

"Hold hands," Jessica ordered, stopping in the crush of the crowd. "Do not let go. If you get separated, you go to the anvil statue and wait. But do not get separated."

Paige grabbed Freddie’s left hand. Her grip was a vice, a physical reminder of her promise on the bench. Jessica grabbed his right.

They moved as a chain. Jessica pulled her **CPI ID** (PolyPass) out of her pocket. At the turnstile, she scanned it three times—*Beep. Beep. Beep.*—holding the gate for her siblings.

They burst into the concourse.

The noise was physical. Even with the earplugs, Freddie could feel it in his chest cavity. It was a deep, resonant thrumming, the sound of sixty thousand people breathing and shouting in unison.

"Food!" Jessica shouted over the noise, pointing to a concession stand. "We need calories!"

They waited in line, moving efficiently. Three hot dogs. Three large sodas. A tub of popcorn.

"Section 22, Row 14," Jessica read from her ticket stub. "That’s the North End Zone. The Anvil. Student Section. We're in the blast radius."

They climbed the concrete stairs. As they emerged from the tunnel into the open bowl of the stadium, the sheer scale of the machine hit Freddie.

The field was a brilliant, impossible green under the stadium lights. The stands were a vertical wall of color—a sea of maroon and orange that stretched up to the stars.

"This is The Crucible," Jessica announced, leading them down the bleachers.

They found their row. Jessica took the aisle seat. Paige took the inside. They put Freddie in the middle, sandwiching him between them.

"Safe?" Paige asked, leaning close so he could hear her through the plugs.

Freddie looked around. To his left, a student was painted entirely silver. To his right, a group was chanting a complex mathematical cheer.

*Cosine, Secant, Tangent, Sine!* *Three-point-one-four-one-five-nine!* *Go! Forgers!*

Freddie blinked. He looked at the massive scoreboard.

**CPI FORGERS vs. TALLAHASSEE STATE RENEGADES**

"Tallahassee State," Jessica noted, following his gaze. "Ranked number four in the nation. They are a powerhouse. They have speed. They have money. They think they’re going to walk in here and crush a bunch of engineering nerds."

She looked at Freddie, her face painted for war.

"But they forgot one thing, Freddie."

"What?" Freddie asked, clutching his soda.

"They aren't playing on grass," Jessica smiled, stomping her boot on the aluminum bleacher. "They're playing in a factory."

She checked the countdown clock on the scoreboard. **00:05:00**.

Jessica leaned in, putting a hand on Freddie’s shoulder. Her expression was serious, almost religious.

"Okay, listen to me," she said, her voice cutting through the ambient roar. "In exactly five minutes, the lights are going to go out. It’s going to get dark. And then, it’s going to get loud. Louder than anything you’ve ever heard."

Freddie tightened his grip on his soda cup. "The Stomp?"

"The Stomp," Jessica confirmed. "Just remember the rhythm. It’s a grid. It’s structure. Don't fight it. Ride it."

Freddie nodded, checking his earplugs one last time. He looked at the clock ticking down. **00:04:58**.

"I am ready," Freddie whispered. "Initialize the sequence."
