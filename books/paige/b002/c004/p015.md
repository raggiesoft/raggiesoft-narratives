# Part 15: The Shuttle

## 22:30 PM

The game was over, but the logistics were just beginning.

They exited The Crucible not into a void, but into a massive, organized mobilization of heavy transport. The road in front of the stadium had been converted into a bus terminal. A fleet of **Blue Ridge Transit** buses was platooned bumper-to-bumper, their engines idling in a deep, collective growl that vibrated the pavement.

"Target identification," Jessica said, scanning the LED signs above the windshields.

The signs were flipping in unison, a synchronized pulse of orange text.

*FALLING BRANCH SHUTTLE*.*..click...* *GO FORGERS!*

*PEPPER PLAZA SHUTTLE*.*..click...* *GO FORGERS!*

"We need Pepper Plaza," Jessica said, pointing to a line that snaked down the sidewalk. "It’s long, but look at Falling Branch."

Freddie looked. The line for the **Falling Branch Shuttle**—the commuter lot out by Interstate 81—stretched for hundreds of yards, a sea of shivering fans waiting to get back to their cars.

"That is the high-volume vector," Freddie noted. "Commuter traffic."

Further down the line, there was confusion. A group of fans was arguing with a transit supervisor, pointing at their watches.

"They missed the **Roanoke Valley Transit**," Jessica noted sympathetically. "The Roanoke Express leaves at 19:45 PM, game or no game. Those guys are stranded."

Near the end of the platoon, a different kind of bus was loading. It was a coach bus, distinct from the city transit fleet. Its sign didn't flash the school cheer. It simply read: **BLUEWATER via FALLING BRANCH**.

"That’s the **Bluewater Transit Maroon Express**," Jessica explained. "That’s the long haul. Ninety minutes down I-81 and I-77. Those guys won't be home until midnight."

"We are fortunate," Freddie calculated. "Our transit time is minimal."

"Exactly," Jessica said. "Come on. Let's get you home."

## 22:45 PM

They reached the front of the line for the **Pepper Plaza Shuttle**. It was a massive, 60-foot articulated bus. The doors folded open.

"IDs?" Freddie asked, reaching for his pocket.

"Negative," Jessica said, guiding him forward. "Game Day Protocol. No fares. Just load and go. They prioritize flow rate over revenue."

They stepped onto the bus. It was "crush load"—standing room only. Jessica grabbed a strap near the articulation joint, bracing herself.

"No seats," Jessica called out.

"Improvise," Paige said.

She pushed Freddie toward a single vacant seat near the wheel well. He sat down, and without a word, Paige sat down on his lap, facing forward.

Around them, other couples and friends were doing the same. The bus was packed so tight that personal space was a mathematical impossibility.

The doors hissed shut. The air brakes released. The bus lurched forward, accelerating onto the express lane.

Freddie wrapped his arms tightly around Paige’s stomach, interlocking his fingers. He buried his face in the back of her maroon hoodie.

Normally, the crowding, the smell of strangers, and the lurching motion would have triggered a sensory overload. But the weight of Paige on his lap acted like a heavy blanket. She was a physical shield between him and the rest of the world. He tightened his grip, pulling her closer.

*Pressure. Gravity. Safety.*

He realized, with a sudden clarity, that he liked this configuration. It felt like she was physically pressing his frayed nerves back into his body, holding his chaotic system together with her own mass.

"Twenty minutes," Paige whispered, leaning back against him. "Non-stop."

"Acknowledged," Freddie mumbled into her hood.

## 23:05 PM

The bus hissed to a halt at **Pepper Plaza**. The doors opened, spilling the exhausted, happy crowd onto the concrete.

The air in Christiansburg was silent compared to the roar of The Crucible. They walked the one block to the apartment building, their footsteps echoing on the pavement. Freddie moved on autopilot, his legs heavy, his battery drained to 0%.

They climbed the concrete stairs to Apartment 2B. Jessica unlocked the door.

The familiar, quiet smell of the apartment hit Freddie like a sedative. He didn't say a word. He walked straight to the beige sofa, dropped his backpack on the floor, and collapsed face-first onto the cushions.

He didn't take off his orange hoodie. He didn't take off his shoes. He just shut down.

"System offline," Jessica whispered, locking the deadbolt.

Paige sat down on the edge of the sofa bed. She looked at him—a lump of orange fabric and exhaustion. She reached out and gently tapped his leg.

"Hey," she whispered. "Let's make you comfortable."

She worked the laces of his sneakers, pulling them off one by one and setting them neatly by the door. She tugged at the cuffs of his jeans. Freddie groaned but lifted his hips enough for her to slide the denim down and off, leaving him in his boxers and the orange hoodie.

"Better," Paige breathed.

She stood up and kicked off her own shoes. She peeled off her jeans, tossing them onto the chair.

"Be right back," she whispered.

She padded into the bathroom and closed the door. In the privacy of the small room, she reached under her maroon shirt and unhooked her bra, pulling it out through her sleeve. She sighed as the constriction released. She splashed cold water on her face, washing away the greasepaint stripes.

She walked back out, wearing just her oversized maroon t-shirt and underwear. She looked soft, tired, and safe.

Jessica was already standing by her bedroom door.

"Goodnight, guys," Jessica said softly. "We conquered the world today."

"Goodnight, Jess," Paige said. "Thanks for the ride."

Jessica smiled, closed her door, and the click of the latch signaled the final end of the day.

Paige climbed onto the sofa bed. She pulled the jersey sheet up over them.

Freddie shifted, sensing her heat. He turned toward her, curling into his usual position, his forehead pressing against her shoulder. He was still wearing the orange hoodie, hood up, like a protective shell.

"Paige?" Freddie whispered. His voice was thick with sleep.

"Yeah?"

"The Housing Director... she said no."

"I know," Paige whispered, running her hand up and down his arm, over the rough fabric of the hoodie.

"But... the variable is critical," Freddie said, his voice trembling slightly. "I cannot... sleep... without the anchor."

Paige shifted, wrapping her arms around him, pulling him so close that there was no space left between them.

"Listen to me, Freddie," she said, her voice fierce in the quiet room. "I promise you. I am willing to share a bed with you for the rest of your life if it helps with even one night terror. I don't care what the policy says. I don't care what Dad says."

Freddie let out a long, shuddering breath. He snuggled closer, burying his face in her neck, trying to tell her without words that he would love nothing more than that. That was the only future he wanted.

"I will only consider living somewhere where I am allowed to share a bed with you," Paige vowed, kissing the top of his hooded head. "If the dorm says no, we find an apartment. If the apartment says no, we find a house. But we don't split up. Ever."

The knot of anxiety in Freddie’s chest finally unraveled. The rejection from Bennett Hall didn't matter. The rules didn't matter. The only thing that mattered was the promise.

"Confirmed," Freddie whispered.

The static in his head faded to silence. The rhythm of the day—the bus, the stomp, the game—slowed down to match the steady, rhythmic beating of Paige’s heart.

Freddie Avery closed his eyes, anchored and safe, and drifted into the deep, dreamless sleep of the victorious.
