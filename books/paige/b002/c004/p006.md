# Part 6: The Old Lady on the Hill

## 14:00 PM

They arrived at **Bennett Hall**.

It sat perched on the steep incline of the campus geography, a brick-and-limestone structure that looked less like a dormitory and more like a fortress that had seen better days.

"They call her 'The Old Lady on the Hill'," Jessica explained as they approached the lower entrance at the bottom of the slope. "She’s one of the oldest dorms. No air conditioning in the rooms, radiator heat that clanks all night, and the elevators are slower than a dial-up modem. But the location is prime."

They entered the first-floor lobby. It was a functional space with tile floors, a front desk, and a few worn sofas. A "Welcome to CPI" banner hung over a folding table where a woman with a kind face and a name tag reading **HALL DIRECTOR** was organizing pamphlets.

"Check-in is here," Jessica said, signing them in on a clipboard.

"Welcome," the Hall Director smiled. "The tour group is gathering by the elevator. Your guide is Mandy."

Waiting by the silver elevator doors was a girl in a manual wheelchair. She wore a maroon CPI polo and had a bright, efficient energy about her.

"Hi everyone," Mandy said, spinning her chair to face the small group of parents and students. "I'm Mandy. I'm a sophomore, and I live in Bennett. We're going to head up to the second floor to see my suite. Fair warning: it's tight. We're in a housing crisis, so we're getting really cozy this year."

She pressed the call button.

"Since the building is built into the hill," Mandy explained, "the first floor is down here, but the second floor actually exits out onto the top of the hill on the other side. I use the elevator to avoid the climb, and if you have a documented disability, you can get access to use it as a pass-through too."

The elevator dinged. They piled in and rode up to the second floor.

## 14:15 PM

Mandy led them down a hallway that smelled of popcorn and floor wax, stopping at a door marked **214**. She tapped a sensor next to the handle with her ID card, and the heavy door clicked and swung open automatically.

"Welcome to the sardine can," Mandy joked, rolling inside.

Freddie stepped in and immediately felt the walls close in.

The room was designed for two people. It currently held three.

To his left was a single Twin XL bed. "This is my spot," Mandy explained. "I have the standalone bed so I can transfer easily."

To the right was a bunk bed.

"My roommate Aubrie is on the bottom," Mandy pointed to the lower bunk. "And Madison takes the altitude training on the top."

The room was packed. There were two desks and two dressers, which meant sharing. There was a sink on the wall with no cabinet underneath, allowing Mandy to roll right up to it.

"It is... dense," Freddie whispered, calculating the square footage per person.

"It gets better," Mandy said. "Or worse, depending on how you look at it."

She opened a door at the back of the room. It revealed a tiny, tiled room containing a single toilet and grab bars on the walls.

"This connects us to Room 216," Mandy explained. "On the other side are Hannah, Zoe, and Lauren. So, that's six girls sharing one toilet."

"Where do you shower?" a parent asked.

"Communal bathrooms in the hall," Mandy said. "But we have a separate accessible shower room down the hall for privacy or if anyone needs assistance."

Freddie looked at the bunk bed. It was tight. It was chaotic. But he looked at Paige. If they had this—if they had the "bunker"—he could make it work.

## 14:45 PM

The tour concluded, and the group took the elevator back down to the first-floor lobby.

As the other families drifted toward the exit, Freddie stopped. His mind was racing. He needed to confirm the variable. He needed the stamp of approval.

"Jessica," Freddie said. "I have a query for the Administrator."

He walked over to the welcome table where the Hall Director was stacking brochures.

"Excuse me," Freddie said. His voice was steady, hopeful.

The Hall Director looked up, smiling. "Yes? Did you enjoy the tour?"

"The architecture is efficient," Freddie said. "I have a question regarding the assignment protocol. My sister Paige," he gestured to her, "and I are applying. We are twins."

"That's wonderful," the Director said. "Legacies?"

"Yes," Freddie said. "We have a medical requirement. I suffer from night terrors and sensory dysregulation during sleep cycles. Paige provides deep pressure regulation. We share a bed at home to maintain stability."

He took a breath, delivering the solution he had carried in his head for weeks.

"We would like to request a room assignment together. We do not require a suite. A standard double room is sufficient, provided we can configure the beds."

The Hall Director’s smile faltered. She put down the brochure she was holding. She looked at Freddie’s open, earnest face, and then at Paige and Jessica standing just behind him. She realized instantly that this wasn't a joke.

"Honey," the Director said, her voice dropping to a gentle, apologetic tone. "I'm afraid we can't do that."

Freddie blinked. "I can provide documentation. The medical necessity is—"

"It's not about the documentation," she interrupted softly. "It's university policy. All residence halls are strictly single-gender assignment. Men in the male wings, women in the female wings. We cannot place a brother and sister in the same room. It's simply not done."

The words hit Freddie like a physical object.

*Cannot.*

*Single-gender.*

*Not done.*

The logic of the Blueprint—the logic that had sustained him through the noise of the cafeteria, the chaos of the tour, the stress of the day—shattered.

The buzzing in his ears, usually a low hum, screamed into a high-pitched whine. The lobby lights seemed to flare brighter. The floor tilted.

"Freddie?" Paige stepped forward, reaching for his hand.

He pulled away.

"Invalid," Freddie choked out. "The structure is... invalid."

Then, the vibration took over.

He let out a sound that wasn't a word—a guttural, terrified cry of absolute despair. He dropped to his knees on the tile floor, his hands clapping over his ears, rocking violently back and forth.

"Freddie!" Jessica dropped her bag and lunged for him.

"NO! NO! NO!" Freddie screamed, the sound tearing out of his throat. It wasn't a tantrum. It was the sound of a world ending. "SYSTEM FAILURE! SYSTEM FAILURE!"

The lobby went silent. Every head turned.

"It's okay, Freddie, I've got you," Paige was on the floor with him instantly, wrapping her arms around his chest, trying to apply the squeeze, trying to be the anchor.

But he was too far gone. He thrashed against her, his limbs flailing, his eyes squeezed shut, tears streaming down his face. He was non-verbal, lost in a white-hot static of panic.

"Is he okay? Should I call security?" the Hall Director asked, stepping around the table, looking alarmed.

"No security!" Jessica snapped, her voice fierce. "He's having a meltdown. We need a quiet room. Now."

"My office," the Director said, pointing to a door behind the desk. "It's soundproof."

It took both of them to move him. Paige held his torso, Jessica guided his legs, as they shuffled him into the small office. The Director closed the door, cutting off the stares of the lobby.

Freddie curled into a ball in the corner of the office, rocking and keening, a high, thin sound of misery. Paige sat behind him, wrapping her legs around his, pulling him tight against her chest, burying her face in his neck.

"I'm here," she whispered over and over. "I'm here. I'm not leaving."

Jessica knelt in front of him, trying to catch his eyes, but he wouldn't look.

"Freddie," Jessica said, keeping her voice low and steady. "Listen to me. We have other options. We can look at off-campus apartments. We can find a place where the rules are different."

She was careful not to say *share a bed* in front of the Director, but she poured every ounce of reassurance she had into her tone.

"It’s broken," Freddie sobbed, his voice ragged. "The Blueprint is broken."

They sat there for fifteen minutes. Slowly, the screaming stopped. The rocking slowed. The energy drained out of him, leaving him limp and exhausted against Paige’s chest.

The Hall Director watched them from her desk, her expression softening from alarm to sympathy. She looked at the way Paige held him, the way Jessica guarded him.

"Has he..." the Director hesitated, then asked gently. "Has he ever been evaluated for autism? Or being on the spectrum?"

Jessica and Paige looked at each other. They had heard the word *retarded* used by bullies. They had heard *sensitive* used by their mother. They had heard *difficult* used by their father. But *autism*? That was a word for people who couldn't speak at all, or who rocked in corners...

Jessica looked at Freddie, who was currently rocking in a corner, unable to speak.

*Oh.*

"We don't know," Jessica said shortly. "We just know he needs to get out of here."

"Of course," the Director nodded. "Take all the time you need."

"We're leaving," Jessica decided. "He needs to evacuate."

Getting him out was a slow process. Freddie was drained, his face pale and streaked with tears. He stood up on shaky legs.

Paige didn't let go of his hand. She gripped it so tight her knuckles were white.

"Ground me," Freddie whispered, his voice a ghost.

"I've got you," Paige promised.

They walked out of the office, through the silent lobby, and out the front doors into the cool afternoon air of the valley.

Jessica led them to a wooden bench near the treeline, away from the path. Freddie collapsed onto it, staring at the mountains. They didn't look like a fortress anymore. They looked like walls.

Paige sat next to him, pulling his arm through hers.

"We will fix this," Paige said, her voice fierce and absolute. "I don't care what the policy says. I don't care what Dad says. We will find a loophole. You and me. We are not sleeping apart."

Freddie looked at her. The static in his head was still deafening, but her voice cut through it.

"A loophole," Freddie repeated, closing his eyes. "I need a loophole."
