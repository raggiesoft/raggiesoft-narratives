# Part 1: The Transfer Point

## Friday, October 25, 2002

The silence inside the silver sedan was heavier than the Blue Ridge Mountains rising in the distance.

Ellen drove with both hands gripping the wheel at ten and two, her knuckles pale. She wasn't driving like a mother taking her children on a college visit; she was driving like a courier transporting fragile, volatile cargo through a war zone.

In the passenger seat sat Aunt Linda—Ellen’s younger sister. Linda was usually a woman who filled rooms with perfume, loud laughter, and stories about her latest bingo wins. Today, she was muted. She stared out the window at the passing landscape of Augusta County, a look of wistful recognition on her face.

"I miss living out here," Linda said softly, breaking the silence. "Route 254... the way the light hits the fields in the fall. It was always so peaceful."

"You lived here a long time ago, Linda," Ellen murmured, her eyes glued to the road. "Things change."

"The land doesn't," Linda countered gently. She sighed, tracing a finger against the glass. "Sometimes I wish I never left."

In the back seat, Freddie and Paige sat like statues.

It had been exactly one year since Robert moved to the sofa. Eleven days ago, the separation period had officially met the twelve-month legal requirement for a no-fault divorce in the Commonwealth of Virginia. The paperwork had been filed. The "Avery Corporation" was dissolving.

Freddie looked out the window. The scenery was changing—the flat, marshy Tidewater giving way to the rolling hills of the Piedmont, and now, the imposing wall of the mountains.

The "Architecture" of his life was gone. His father lived in a small apartment on the other side of Virginia Beach now. The chaotic family van was gone, replaced by this sensible, used sedan that smelled of the previous owner’s cherry air freshener. The only thing that remained constant was Paige, sitting inches away from him, her thigh pressing against his.

"Are we making good time?" Paige asked, breaking the silence.

"We're fine," Ellen said shortly. "We'll be at the Zane's on Route 250 by 4:30. Just like the schedule says."

They passed the large green highway sign: **EXIT 222 - STAUNTON / FISHERSVILLE / US-250**.

Freddie stared at the word on the sign as it flashed by: **FISHERSVILLE**.

He didn't know why, but the name stuck in his head. *Fishersville.* It sounded quiet. It sounded like a place where things didn't break. He filed the name away in his mental archive, cataloging it under "Locations of Interest," right next to the schematics for warp drives and drum patterns.

"Exit 222," Freddie whispered. "Fishersville."

"We're taking the Staunton side, Freddie," Ellen corrected, signaling right. "We aren't going to Fishersville."

"I know," Freddie said. "Just noting the coordinates."

Ellen took the ramp onto Route 250, merging onto Richmond Avenue. The landscape here was a mix of historic charm and highway commerce. Ellen navigated the traffic, passing fast-food chains and car dealerships until the massive, glowing sign appeared on the left: **ZANE’S MARKET**.
