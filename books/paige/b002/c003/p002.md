# Part 2: The Red Booth

The Zane's at 1250 Richmond Avenue was a beast of a building. It wasn't just a gas station; it was a brightly lit fortress of neon red and yellow, bustling with truckers, weary travelers, and college students heading south to Blacksburg or north to Harrisonburg.

Ellen pulled the sedan into a parking spot away from the pumps.

"There she is," Aunt Linda said, pointing.

Parked near the entrance was a silver Avalon with a **CPI IRONHEADS** sticker on the back glass. Leaning against it was Jessica.

She looked different. She wasn't just the "Matriarch" who managed the household anymore; she was a Sophomore. She was wearing a maroon sweatshirt with **FORGERS** emblazoned across the chest in block letters, and her hair was cut in a sharp, efficient bob. She looked like she belonged to a different tribe.

"Okay," Ellen said, turning off the engine. "Let's eat. Then the handoff."

They walked into the Zane's. To Freddie, it was a paradise of order. The convenience store aisles were bright and symmetrical, but the real marvel was the food counter.

Unlike a fast-food joint where you had to shout over a counter, Zane's used the **"Touch-Tap"** system. A row of glowing screens allowed you to build your order digitally.

"This is efficient," Freddie noted, stepping up to a kiosk. He tapped the screen. *Sub. Turkey. Provolone. Lettuce. No Tomato. Confirm.*

No talking. No confusion. Just data entry.

"I'm getting the fries," Paige said, tapping the screen next to him. "Curly. Large. Side of ranch."

They grabbed their receipt tickets and waited for their numbers to be called. When the food arrived—wrapped in foil and sitting in red plastic baskets—they carried it over to the seating area.

It was a small section partitioned off from the aisles by a low glass wall, filled with hard red vinyl booths and a few high-top tables. They slid into a booth near the window. It was tight—Aunt Linda and Ellen on one side, Jessica, Paige, and Freddie squeezed into the other—but it worked.

"So," Jessica said, unwrapping a massive burger. "How was the drive?"

"Quiet," Ellen said, picking at a salad she had grabbed from the cooler case. "Linda got nostalgic for Route 254."

"It's pretty country," Jessica conceded. "Quieter than the beach."

"Linda, did you bring the itinerary for the return trip?" Ellen asked, shifting gears back to logistics.

"I did," Linda nodded, sipping a fountain soda. "We're going straight back. No stops."

"Good," Ellen said. She looked at the twins. "Jessica has the schedule for tomorrow. The Open House starts at 0800. I want you both to listen to her. No wandering off. No incidents."

"We know the protocol, Mom," Paige said softly, dipping a curly fry into ranch.

"And Freddie," Ellen added, her eyes darting to him. "If it gets... loud. If there's a crowd. You tell Jessica immediately. Do not wait until you crash."

"I will communicate," Freddie whispered, staring at his turkey sub. The bread was perfectly toasted. The consistency was safe.

"He'll be fine, Mom," Jessica said, her voice firm. "We're in Ironhead country now. It's different here. People mind their own business."

They ate quickly. The atmosphere in the red booth was a strange mix of relief and melancholy. It was the last meal before the definitive split—the moment the children crossed the border into their own lives, leaving the wreckage of the marriage behind in the rearview mirror.

"Okay," Ellen said, checking her watch. "It's 5:00. We need to get back."

They cleared the table. The goodbye in the parking lot was brief. Ellen gave the twins a quick, fierce hug, her perfume smelling faintly of stress.

"Be good," she said. "Call me on Sunday when you're on the way back."

"We will," Paige promised.

Ellen and Aunt Linda got back into the beige sedan. As they pulled out onto Route 250, turning back toward the highway, Freddie watched the car disappear. He felt a physical weight lift off his chest.

Jessica turned to them, her hardness melting into a grin.

"Alright, recruits," she said, popping the trunk of her Civic. "Welcome to the DMZ. You made it out."

"The air is thinner here," Freddie observed, looking up at the sky over Staunton. "It feels cleaner."

"It is cleaner," Jessica agreed. "Load up. Next stop: The Iron City."

They threw their bags into the trunk and climbed into the Avalon. The interior of Jessica’s car didn't smell like divorce or air freshener; it smelled of old coffee, textbooks, and freedom.

"Music," Jessica announced, turning the key. "Real music."

She cranked the volume. It wasn't soft rock. It was loud, driving alternative rock. The bass thumped in Freddie’s chest, replacing the anxiety of the drive with a new, energetic rhythm.

As they merged onto I-81 South, leaving Zane's Market behind, Freddie watched the neon sign fade in the mirror. He didn't know it yet, but he had just crossed the border into the most important forty-eight hours of his life.
