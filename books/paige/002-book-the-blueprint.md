# Book: The Blueprint

## Chapter 1: The Cracks

### Part 1: Static

The summer heat in Virginia Beach was usually a physical weight, a wet blanket of humidity that smelled of asphalt and salt. But inside the Avery house, the air felt heavy for a different reason.

Freddie sat at the top of the stairs, pressing his forehead against the banister. He was sixteen now. His legs were longer, his shoulders broader, but the "Architecture"—the internal structure he built to make sense of the world—felt more fragile than it had in years.

Downstairs, the television was off. The younger twins, Amanda and Elara, were napping. It should have been quiet.

But silence, Freddie had learned, had different textures. There was the warm, fuzzy silence of reading a book next to Paige. Then there was this silence—sharp, jagged, and cold.

"I’m just going to the hardware store," his father’s voice drifted up from the hallway. It wasn't a shout, but the pitch was wrong. It was too tight.

"For three hours?" his mother’s voice replied. It lacked its usual maternal warmth. It sounded like glass breaking under water.

"I need to fix the latch, Ellen. It’s broken."

"A lot of things are broken, Robert."

The front door opened and closed. It didn't slam—slamming was against the rules—but the latch clicked into place with a finality that made Freddie flinch.

He pulled his knees to his chest. The house felt like it was vibrating at a low frequency, a subsonic hum of tension that made his teeth ache. He didn't understand the data. The bills were paid (Jessica saw to that). The grades were good. The band was on hiatus for the summer. Yet, the system felt like it was crashing.

"Freddie?"

Freddie looked up. Paige was standing in the doorway of their shared bedroom. She was wearing oversized gym shorts and a tank top, her hair pulled up in a messy bun. She looked tired, the kind of tired that sleep couldn't fix.

"The air is wrong," Freddie whispered, rubbing his arms. "It feels spiky."

"I know," Paige said softly. She walked over and sat on the top step next to him, her shoulder pressing against his. "Dad's just... stressed. Work is busy."

"Mom is cleaning the counters," Freddie noted. "She has cleaned them three times since breakfast. That is a loop. A glitch."

Paige sighed, resting her chin on her knees. She knew what the glitch was, or at least, she suspected it. She saw the way their parents moved around each other like magnets with the same polarity, pushing away whenever they got too close. But she couldn't explain that to Freddie. He dealt in logic, and falling out of love was the most illogical thing in the world.

"Come on," Paige said, standing up and offering him a hand. "Let's go to the room. It's safe there."

Freddie took her hand. His spastic diplegia was always better in the summer warmth, but he still relied on her balance.

They retreated into their bedroom. It was a sanctuary of order. Freddie’s side was meticulous—books stacked by height, drawing pens aligned by millimeter width. Paige’s side was softer, a nest of pillows and blankets.

Freddie sat on the edge of the bed—*their* bed. They were sixteen, and the world said they should have separated years ago. But the world didn't understand the night terrors. The world didn't understand that when the sensory input of the day became too much, Paige was the only anchor that kept Freddie from drifting away.

"I've been thinking," Paige said, closing the door and locking it—a rare move. She turned to lean against the wood. "About the future. About the Blueprint."

Freddie looked up, intrigued. "The schematic?"

"Yeah. The schematic for us." She walked over and sat next to him. The mattress dipped, shifting their weight together. "We have two years left of this. Junior year. Senior year. Then graduation."

"College," Freddie added. "CCHR. Community College of Hampton Roads. It is the logical path."

"Right," Paige nodded. "And after that, maybe a university. But even for CCHR, we have to live somewhere. And this house..." She trailed off, gesturing vaguely toward the door. "It's getting loud, Freddie. And I don't think it's going to get quieter."

Freddie stiffened. Change was terrifying. The house was loud, but it was *his* house.

"What is the variable?" Freddie asked.

"Dorms," Paige stated, her eyes lighting up with a plan that seemed perfectly logical to her. "Student housing. We can apply for a dorm room together."

Freddie processed this. "A shared unit. Controlled environment. Close to classes."

"Exactly," Paige said, leaning back on her hands. "Like in the movies, but better because we won't have random roommates. We'll just put that on the application—that we're twins, and we're a set. They have to let us room together. It makes the most sense."

"Efficiency," Freddie agreed. "Shared resources."

"And the sleeping arrangements," Paige added, her voice dropping to a softer, more serious tone. She looked at the bed they were currently sitting on. "I was thinking... when we get the dorm, we'll just tell them we need one big bed. Or if they give us two small ones, we’ll just push them together and strap the mattresses."

Freddie looked at her. "Is that standard protocol?"

"Why wouldn't it be?" Paige shrugged. "We're family. We're not some random couple trying to sneak around. It's a medical accommodation. They'll understand that you sleep better when I'm there. It’s for regulation."

She reached out and covered his hand with hers. Her palm was warm, the same steady weight that woke him up every morning.

"I don't want to be in a bed across the room from you, Freddie," she admitted. "And I definitely don't want to be in a different room down the hall. I want to keep this. The deep pressure. The white noise. It works."

Freddie thought about the nights—the terrifying gunfire sound of the house settling, the way his muscles locked up in the cold sheets. Then he thought about Paige—the warmth, the safety, the singular unit they formed against the dark.

"It is the only way I sleep," Freddie said simply.

"Then it's the plan," Paige smiled, a look of immense relief washing over her. "We graduate. We get a dorm. We push the beds together. And we get out of the noise."

She moved her hand from his grip to his back, pressing her palm between his shoulder blades—that familiar, grounding spot.

"You and me," she whispered. "Against the static."

Freddie leaned into her touch, the tension in his spine dissolving. The house downstairs was cracking, the parents were drifting, but the Blueprint for the future felt solid.

"Yes," Freddie said. "That is the plan."

### Part 2: The Escape Vehicle

The calendar on the kitchen wall read **June 19, 2001**. It was a Tuesday, and the humidity outside was pushing ninety percent.

Inside the house, the air conditioning was humming, but it wasn't enough to scrub the tension from the atmosphere. Their mother was Upstairs, ostensibly organizing the linen closet for the fourth time that month. Their father was at work, though he had called an hour ago to say he would be "late again" due to a project deadline.

Freddie sat at the kitchen table, staring at a bowl of cereal that had gone soggy. The house felt like a tightly wound spring waiting to snap.

"Grab your shoes," Paige said, walking into the kitchen and twirling a set of keys on her finger.

The keys were new. They belonged to the family’s secondary vehicle, a beat-up sedan that Paige had inherited access to after passing her road test two weeks ago. The plastic rectangle in her wallet—*License Class D*—was the most valuable item in the Avery inventory. It was a ticket out.

"Where are we going?" Freddie asked, pushing the cereal away.

"Emerald Creek," Paige announced. "It’s too quiet in here. And Sarah and Jenna turn twelve on Thursday. Mom and Dad..." She paused, looking up at the ceiling where footsteps creaked overhead. "Mom and Dad are distracted. So we’re doing it. We’re taking them out."

Freddie stood up. The logic was sound. If the primary command structure (the parents) was failing, the secondary command (the older siblings) had to take over.

They walked down the hall to the room shared by the middle set of twins.

The door was open. Inside was a study in contrasts.

On the left side of the room, Jenna was currently doing a handstand against the wall while trying to read a magazine on the floor. Her side of the room was a colorful explosion of clothes, posters, and half-finished craft projects. Her ADHD meant her brain moved at the speed of light, and her environment usually reflected that velocity.

On the right side, Sarah was sitting perfectly still on her bed, arranging her stuffed animals by height and color gradient. Her side was pristine. The comforter was smoothed to military standards. Her OCD demanded order in a chaotic world, and she maintained her perimeter with fierce dedication.

"Happy early birthday, monsters," Paige said, leaning against the doorframe.

Jenna crashed down from her handstand, bouncing to her feet in one fluid motion. "Are we going somewhere? Is it cake? Did you buy a pony?"

"No pony," Paige laughed. "But I have keys, and I have twenty dollars. We're going to the mall."

"Emerald Creek?" Jenna gasped, her eyes widening. "Can we go to the arcade? Can we go to the pretzel place? I want to see the new sparkly notebooks at the stationery store!"

Sarah looked up from her bears. She didn't bounce. She looked at the clock on her nightstand. "It’s 1:14 PM. If we go now, traffic will be moderate. But is the car clean?"

"It's clean enough," Paige promised. "Come on, Sarah. We’ll get you a smoothie. A symmetrical one."

Sarah considered this. She looked at her bears, checked the alignment one last time, and nodded. "Okay. But I get the window seat behind the driver. It’s statistically safer."

"Deal," Paige said.

The drive to Chesapeake was a short but intense experience. Paige was a careful driver, gripping the wheel at ten and two, checking her mirrors every fifteen seconds. She took her responsibility as the transport pilot seriously.

In the backseat, the energy differential was palpable.

"Look at that truck! It's huge!" Jenna shouted, pointing out the window. "Oh, look, a dog! Two dogs! Paige, can we play the license plate game? I see a Florida! I see a New York!"

"Jenna, volume," Freddie murmured from the passenger seat. He was clutching the door handle, not because Paige was driving badly, but because being in a moving vehicle always messed with his vestibular system.

"Sorry, Freddie!" Jenna whispered, a volume that lasted exactly three seconds before she saw a billboard for a movie. "Oh my gosh, *Action Force 3*! We have to see that!"

Next to her, Sarah was silently counting the telephone poles passing by, tapping her finger against her knee in groups of four. She checked the door lock. Then she checked it again.

"Locked," Sarah whispered to herself. "Safe."

Freddie watched them in the rearview mirror. He saw Jenna’s leg bouncing like a piston. He saw Sarah’s rhythmic tapping. They were just like him—governed by their own internal wiring, their own unique operating systems.

"We're here," Paige announced, using her turn signal to pull into the sprawling parking lot of Emerald Creek Mall.

The mall was a monolith of beige concrete and glass, a temple of commerce rising out of the heat. To most people, it was just a place to buy clothes. To the Avery kids, it was a neutral zone—a place where the air conditioning was freezing, the smells were artificial, and for a few hours, they didn't have to worry about the silence at home.

"Operation Birthday Protocol is in effect," Paige said, putting the car in park. "Jenna, stay with the group. Sarah, I have hand sanitizer in my purse if you need it. Freddie, you have point."

"Copy," Freddie said, unbuckling his seatbelt.

Jenna burst out of the car like a majestic, energetic firework. "MALL!"

Sarah stepped out carefully, closing the door and checking the handle to make sure it locked behind them. "Door secured," she confirmed.

Paige looked at Freddie over the roof of the car. She smiled, a tired but genuine expression. "We got them out."

"We did," Freddie agreed. He offered his arm to Paige, not for balance this time, but as a gesture of partnership. They were the captains now.

"Let's go get some pretzels," Paige said, locking the car. "Before Jenna vibrates into another dimension."

### Part 3: The Route

The beige sedan sat vibrating in the driveway of 1852 Delaney Street. The engine idle was rough, shaking the dashboard slightly and making Freddie’s teeth chatter if he didn't clench them.

Paige sat in the driver’s seat, staring intensely at the tachometer. Her left foot was doing something complicated with a pedal Freddie didn't understand, and her right hand was gripping the gear stick like it was the controls of a fighter jet. It was a manual transmission—a "stick shift"—which to Freddie meant the car spent a lot of time lurching before it decided to move.

"Radio check," Paige muttered, reaching for the dial.

She twisted the knob until the static cleared and a heavy, distortion-filled guitar riff filled the cabin. A gruff voice announced: *"You’re tuned to the erratic heartbeat of Hampton Roads... Rock 107, The Wolf."*

"Classic rock," Freddie noted, approving of the structured, predictable chord progressions coming from the speakers. It was a nice counter-balance to the engine noise, which was currently whining in a high pitch that hurt his ears.

"Hang on," Paige said, wrestling the stick into what Freddie assumed was Reverse. "Here comes the lurch."

The car jerked backward, then smoothed out as she backed out of the driveway. She stopped, stomped on the brake, and shoved the stick forward.

"Belt secure," Sarah announced from the back. "Tension is optimal."

"Copy," Paige said. The car bucked once—Freddie grabbed the door handle reflexively—before catching the gear and rolling forward.

They made a left onto Pope Street. The engine roared louder and louder until Paige moved the stick again, and the roar dropped to a comfortable hum. Freddie didn't know *why* she did it, only that he was grateful when the noise stopped.

"Are we getting pizza?" Jenna shouted from the back seat, leaning forward so far her seatbelt locked. "I want the pepperoni with the little cups of grease! Or maybe the Chinese place with the chicken on a stick? No, wait! The cookie place!"

"I require a pretzel," Sarah stated calmly, though she was tapping her window button to check that it was locked. "Salted. No dip. Dip is messy."

"We can get pretzels *and* cookies," Paige promised, her eyes glued to the road. She made a sharp right onto Brandon Boulevard. The suspension groaned, but she held the line.

"Right turn in 500 feet," Freddie murmured, reading the street signs. He might not know how to operate the machine, but he knew the grid. He was the navigator.

"Thanks, map man," Paige said. She signaled right for Kempsville Road.

As they cruised down the main artery, the backseat conversation dissolved into a high-speed negotiation of birthday demands.

"If we go to the arcade," Jenna said, vibrating with kinetic energy, "I'm playing the motorcycle game. You have to lean on it, Sarah. You have to actually lean!"

"That game is unsanitary," Sarah countered. "Thousands of hands touch the handlebars. I will play the claw machine. It involves precision."

"The claw machine is a scam!" Jenna yelled. "It's rigged! The claw is loose!"

"Volume," Freddie whispered, pressing his head back against the headrest. The classic rock on the radio was fighting a losing battle against Jenna’s excitement.

"Jenna, bring it down a notch," Paige commanded from the pilot’s seat. "Crossing city limits. Entering Chesapeake."

"Fun density increasing!" Jenna cheered, bouncing in her seat.

Paige navigated the turns—Volvo Parkway, then Eden Way North—her hand moving the stick shift back and forth in a rhythm Freddie couldn't follow. Emerald Creek Mall rose out of the asphalt sea like a fortress.

"Turning onto the ring road," Paige narrated. "Drop to Second gear."

The engine revved high again as they slowed down. On their left, the massive, blocky facade of **Pennyworth’s** loomed, its red logo distinct against the grey sky.

"Don't park here," Jenna warned. "It's too far. My legs will fall off."

"We aren't parking at Pennyworth's," Freddie observed, watching Paige steer them past the loading docks. "She is aiming for the tower."

They swung around the back of the complex toward **Silverman’s**. Rising between the brick walls of the mall and the side of the department store was the beacon.

Vertical white letters ran down the center of the dark glass: **FOOD COURT**.

"Elevator entrance," Paige confirmed. "Neutral. Handbrake."

She pulled the car into a spot three rows back. It was 1:35 PM. She did something with her feet, moved the stick to the middle, and yanked the parking brake lever up with a ratchet-like *crrr-click*.

"Engine off," Paige said, killing the ignition. The classic rock faded into silence, instantly replaced by the sound of Jenna unbuckling her seatbelt.

"WE'RE HERE!" Jenna shouted.

"Door check," Sarah whispered, waiting for the distinct *click* of the unlock mechanism before opening her door.

They spilled out onto the asphalt. The air was thick and humid, smelling of rain and car exhaust.

"Okay, listen up," Paige said, rounding the hood of the car. "We go in through the glass doors. We take the elevator up. That drops us right at far end of the Food Court. Stay together."

"I know how elevators work!" Jenna scoffed, already skipping toward the building. "I'm pressing the button!"

Freddie looked up at the Food Court tower. The dark glass reflected the grey clouds. It was a good entry point—direct access, low traffic, and a straight shot to the food.

"Let's go," Paige said, linking her arm with Freddie's. "I promised Sarah a symmetrical smoothie."

They walked toward the double doors, the "Avery Corporation" moving from the stifling heat of the parking lot into the cool, conditioned promise of the mall.

### Part 4: The Sugar Rush

The elevator doors slid open with a cheerful *ding*, and the noise hit them like a physical wave.

The Food Court at Emerald Creek Mall was a cavernous hall. The high, tent-like ceiling trapped every sound—screaming toddlers, clattering trays, the sizzle of woks from the Chinese counter—and bounced it around the room. It wasn't an open atrium; it was a contained ecosystem of noise and neon.

Freddie flinched, his shoulders hiking up toward his ears.

Paige didn't hesitate. She stepped slightly in front of him, breaking the visual wall of the crowd, and hooked her pinky finger through his belt loop. It was a subtle anchor, a tactile reminder that he wasn't drifting in the sea of noise alone.

"Target acquired," Paige said, her voice pitched just loud enough for him to hear over the din. "Twelve o'clock. **Auntie Mae's**."

The smell hit them next—a warm, buttery cloud of yeast and cinnamon that cut through the smell of floor wax. It was the only thing that made the noise bearable.

"Cinnamon sugar!" Jenna chanted, bouncing on her toes as they joined the line. "Cinnamon sugar! Cinnamon sugar!"

"Jenna, you are vibrating," Sarah observed, checking her watch. "And we haven't even ingested the sucrose yet. Your baseline energy is concerning."

"It's birthday energy!" Jenna corrected, spinning in a circle until she bumped into a velvet rope.

They reached the counter. The glass case was filled with rows of golden-brown pretzels, glistening with butter and toppings.

"Welcome to Auntie Mae's," the teenager behind the counter droned. She looked bored, wearing a visor that was slightly too big for her head.

Paige stepped up, pulling the crisp twenty-dollar bill from her pocket like it was a diplomatic credential.

"Four cinnamon sugars, please," Paige ordered. "And two large lemonades. No ice."

"No ice?" the girl blinked.

"More volume per cup," Sarah whispered to Freddie. "Ice displaces approximately 30% of the liquid. It is a scam."

"Smart," Freddie whispered back.

The girl punched the keys. "That’ll be twelve-fifty."

Paige handed over the twenty. The transaction was smooth. The return of change—seven dollars and fifty cents—meant the arcade was still a go.

They moved to the pickup counter. A moment later, the tray arrived: four hot, paper-wrapped pretzels radiating heat, and two massive, wax-paper cUDS of yellow lemonade, filled to the brim.

"Secure the cargo," Paige instructed.

They bypassed the crowded tables in the center of the room. Freddie needed a defensible position. They found a high-top table in the far corner, tucked against the brick wall near the windows that looked out over the rainy parking lot.

It was the perfect spot. No one could walk behind them. Freddie sat with his back firmly against the wall, his field of vision covering the entire food court entrance without being *in* it. Paige sat right next to him, their knees touching under the table.

"Okay," Paige said, distributing the goods. "One for the birthday monsters. One for the pilot. One for the navigator."

She placed the massive lemonade cUDS in the center. "We share. Jenna and Sarah, you guys are on cup two. Freddie and I are on cup one."

Jenna tore into her pretzel immediately. Sugar crystals exploded onto the table, onto her shirt, and onto the floor. "It burns! But it's so good!"

Sarah opened hers methodically, folding the wrapper into a neat square to catch any falling debris. She took a small, precise bite. "Acceptable texture," she decided. Then she looked at the lemonade cup she had to share with Jenna.

"We need a straw protocol," Sarah stated, eyeing her twin. "You backwash."

"I do not!" Jenna protested, mouth full of dough.

"You do. We will use the 'sip and release' method," Sarah decreed. "Five sips for you. Wipe the straw. Five sips for me."

On the other side of the table, Freddie took a bite of his pretzel. The warmth spread through his chest. The sugar was gritty and sweet, and the dough was soft. He chewed slowly, letting the sensory input of the food override the sensory overload of the room.

He felt Paige lean against him. She took a long drag from their shared straw, then nudged the cup toward him.

"You doing okay?" she asked, her voice low.

Freddie looked out at the chaotic room—the teenagers flirting near the pizza place, the moms wrestling strollers. Then he felt the solid brick wall behind his back and the pressure of Paige’s knee against his.

"The noise is static," Freddie said, taking the straw. "But the signal is good."

"Good," Paige smiled, wiping a speck of cinnamon off his chin. "Eat up. We have seven dollars and fifty cents burning a hole in my pocket, and Jenna is eyeing the arcade."

"The claw machine," Sarah reminded them, wiping her hands on a napkin. "I have calculated the grip strength. Today is the day we win."

### Part 5: The Calculation

The arcade at Emerald Creek Mall was named **The Starcade**. It was located just past the food court restrooms, a dark cave illuminated by the flashing CRTs of attract screens and the neon glow of Lane-Master lanes.

To Freddie, the Starcade was a paradox. It was louder than the food court—a cacophony of 8-bit explosions, synthesized voices shouting "RELOAD!", and the clatter of tokens hitting metal trays. Yet, unlike the chaotic noise of the crowds, this noise was *designed*. It was computerized. It followed rules.

Paige stopped at the change machine. She fed the remaining bills into the slot.

*Whirrrrrr-clink-clink-clink-clink.*

The machine spit out a heavy stream of brass tokens into the metal cup. Thirty tokens.

"Okay," Paige said, dividing the loot. "Seven for Jenna. Seven for Sarah. Eight for Freddie because he's the navigator. Eight for me because I'm the pilot."

"Go!" Jenna shouted, grabbing her handful. She sprinted immediately toward a game called *Torrent-X*, vibrating with the need for speed.

Sarah held her seven tokens in a neat stack. She didn't run. She walked past the noisy shooters and the flashing racing games until she reached the back wall.

There it stood. **The Claw**.

It was filled with generic plush animals—neon bears, unauthorized cartoon characters, and weirdly shaped pillows. But Sarah didn't care about the prize quality; she cared about the physics.

"Target identified," Sarah whispered. She pointed to a blue bear that was sitting loosely on top of the pile, its tag snagged on a red pillow. "The friction coefficient is favorable."

Freddie walked over, standing behind her. He loved watching Sarah work. When she focused, her OCD wasn't a burden; it was a supercomputer.

"Do you require vector assistance?" Freddie asked.

"Negative," Sarah said, slotting a token. "I need you to block the glare from the overhead light. It interferes with my depth perception."

Freddie stepped to the side, raising his hand to cast a shadow over the glass. "Glare blocked."

Sarah gripped the joystick. She didn't jerk it. She tapped it—*tap, tap, tap*—moving the metal claw into position. She leaned forward, pressing her forehead against the glass, checking the X-axis. Then she moved to the side of the machine to check the Y-axis.

"Aligning," she muttered. "Drop zone calculated."

She hit the big red button.

The claw descended. It spun slowly, the metal fingers opening. It hit the pile.

*Clunk.*

The fingers closed around the blue bear’s head.

"Grip is weak," Sarah observed instantly. "But the tag loop is engaged."

The claw lifted. The bear dangled precariously by its ear tag. The machine rattled as it moved toward the chute. The bear swung—left, right, left...

"Steady," Freddie whispered.

The claw opened over the chute.

*Plop.*

The blue bear tumbled down the plastic slide and landed in the retrieval bin.

"VICTORY!" Jenna screamed, appearing out of nowhere. She had already blown through four tokens in three minutes and was looking for action. "SARAH WON! SARAH BEAT THE SYSTEM!"

Sarah reached into the bin and pulled out the blue bear. She inspected it for defects, straightened its ribbon, and then—for the first time all day—a genuine, wide grin broke across her face.

"The physics were sound," Sarah declared, handing the bear to Freddie. "Hold this. I have six tokens left. I am going for the red pillow."

Paige leaned against a pinball machine nearby, watching her siblings. Jenna was jumping up and down. Sarah was calculating her next attack vector. Freddie was holding the blue bear with a look of serious pride.

For a moment, the tension of the house, the "spiky air" of their parents' marriage, and the uncertainty of the future didn't exist. There was just the neon light, the smell of ozone, and the victory of the blue bear.

"You guys good?" Paige asked, walking over.

"We are operational," Freddie said, handing the bear back to Sarah so she could clutch it. "And Sarah is lethal."

"Good," Paige smiled. "Burn the rest of the tokens. Then we ride."

### Part 6: The Collapse

The high of the Starcade lasted exactly until the front door of 1852 Delaney Street clicked shut behind them.

The house was not quiet. The silence Freddie had feared earlier had been replaced by something worse: the sharp, staccato rhythm of an argument.

Jenna and Sarah, sensing the atmospheric pressure change, immediately retreated. Jenna grabbed her new blue bear and bolted for the stairs. Sarah followed, her face blank, disappearing into the safety of their room.

That left Freddie and Paige standing in the foyer, the "captains" of a ship that was suddenly taking on water.

"I’m just saying look at the numbers, Robert!" their mother’s voice came from the kitchen. It was pitched high, tight with frustration. "I picked up the catalog from VDU today. The nursing program is two years. If I start now—"

"And pay for it with what?" their father’s voice cut in. It wasn't angry; it was exhausted. It was the sound of a man who had given up. "We have eight kids, Ellen. I’m working fifty hours a week. The job pays the bills. The lights are on. The fridge is full. Why isn't that enough?"

"Because 'enough' is suffocating!" Ellen snapped. "I don't want to just survive, Robert. I want to build something. And you should too. They have a management track. You could—"

"I am forty years old!" Robert shouted. The volume spiked, a sudden red line on the equalizer. "I am not going back to school to sit in a classroom with a bunch of twenty-year-olds and pretend I have a future! I am tired, Ellen! I just want to come home and sit down!"

The shout hit Freddie like a physical blow.

He was standing near the coat rack, still holding his half-empty cup of lemonade. The sudden spike in decibels scrambled the signal. His brain, already fatigued from the sensory input of the mall, couldn't process the aggression.

The "Architecture"—the carefully built grid of logic and safety—shattered.

It started in his hands. They began to shake, not a little, but violently. The lemonade cup slipped from his grip. It hit the hardwood floor.

*SPLAT.*

The lid popped off. Yellow liquid and ice exploded across the foyer, soaking Freddie’s socks and splashing onto the wall.

The sound of the cup hitting the floor stopped the argument in the kitchen.

"Freddie?" Ellen called out, her voice shifting from angry to startled.

But it was too late.

Freddie wasn't there anymore. He was vibrating apart. The noise in his head was a white-hot static, drowning out thought, drowning out logic. He dropped to his knees, not because he wanted to, but because his legs simply stopped receiving commands.

He brought his hands up to his ears, pressing hard, trying to physically crush the sound out of his head. He began to rock back and forth, a desperate, rhythmic motion to try and find a center that didn't exist. A sound tore out of his throat—a low, guttural keen that rose into a high-pitched scream of pure, unfiltered agony.

"Make it stop!" Freddie screamed, squeezing his eyes shut. "The air is breaking! Stop the breaking!"

Robert and Ellen rushed into the foyer. They froze when they saw him—curled on the floor in a puddle of lemonade, rocking violently, hitting the side of his head with his palms.

"Freddiey..." Robert started, reaching out.

"DON'T!" Paige yelled.

She threw herself between her parents and her brother. She wasn't the scared little girl anymore; she was the pilot, and she was furious. Her face was flushed, her eyes blazing with an anger that shocked both of them.

"Don't touch him!" Paige hissed, shoving her father’s hand away. "Look at him! Look what you did!"

"We were just talking..." Ellen stammered, pale.

"You weren't talking! You were screaming!" Paige shouted, tears of rage spilling over. "You do this every day! You pick at each other and you yell and you don't care who hears it! He can't handle it! His wiring can't take it!"

She turned her back on them, dropping to her knees in the lemonade puddle without a second thought. Her voice dropped instantly from a scream to a soothing, low murmur.

"Freddie. Freddie, I'm here. I've got the signal."

Freddie was hyperventilating, his chest heaving. "Too loud. Too sharp."

"I know," Paige whispered. She grabbed his wrists, gently pulling his hands away from his head before he could hurt himself. She replaced his hands with her own, cupping his ears firmly, blocking out the world. "I'm blocking the frequency. It's just me. Just static."

She looked back over her shoulder at her parents. Robert looked defeated, staring at the floor. Ellen looked horrified, her hand over her mouth.

"We're going to our room," Paige said, her voice cold and hard. "Do not come in. Do not knock. Just... stop."

She turned back to Freddie. "Up, soldier. We're aborting the mission. We're going to the bunker."

It took a minute to get him to stand. His legs were locking up, the spasticity firing wildly in response to the stress. Paige took his entire weight, wrapping her arm around his waist, letting him drag his feet.

They shuffled past the stunned parents, up the stairs, and down the hall.

Paige opened their bedroom door, shoved them inside, and locked it. Then she went to the window and pulled the blackout shades down, plunging the room into a soothing, grey twilight.

"Bed," Paige ordered gently.

Freddie collapsed onto the mattress. He curled into a tight ball, facing the wall, shaking.

Paige didn't hesitate. She climbed onto the bed behind him. She wrapped her arms around him, pulling his back against her chest. She hooked her legs over his, creating a human straightjacket of deep, firm pressure.

## Chapter 2: The Severance

Outside in the den, the TV flickered on the face of a man who realized, far too late, that he had lost his family long before he lost the house.

"And then Mrs. Gable said the prize pot was only fifty dollars, can you believe that? For a blackout round!"

In the passenger seat, Ellen was checked out. She wasn't sleeping, but she was in "Power Saving Mode," staring blankly at the passing trees, letting her sister’s voice wash over her like white noise.

In the back, a separate universe existed. Freddie sat behind the driver. Paige sat behind the passenger. But the middle seat was not a barrier; it was a bridge. Freddie leaned toward the center. Paige did the same. Their knees pressed together—a solid, constant point of contact. Paige reached over and took Freddie’s hand, lacing her fingers through his. She rested their joined hands on the seat between them.

"The Queen bed," Freddie whispered, so low that Aunt Linda couldn't hear him over her story about the cat. "Variable confirmed," Paige whispered back, squeezing his hand. "No more Twin. Maximum surface area." "And the Decoy," Freddie noted. "Security through obscurity." "Exactly," Paige smiled.

Freddie looked out the window. The mountains of the Shenandoah Valley were receding in the distance, fading into the blue haze. He was leaving the Iron City. He was leaving the Terminal and the Stomp. He was going back to the flat, humid, noisy reality of Virginia Beach. But he wasn't going back the same. He had a diagnosis. Autism. He had a manual. And he had a Queen bed waiting for him.

"Look at that truck!" Aunt Linda exclaimed, pointing at a massive Iron-Haul semi passing them on the left. "That thing is huge!" "Class 8 Heavy Duty," Freddie murmured automatically. "Turbo-diesel. High torque." Paige rubbed her thumb over his knuckles. "We're good," she whispered. "We're good," Freddie agreed.

The station wagon sped down I-64, carrying the Avery unit back to the coast, ready to face the divorce, the graduation, and the future.
