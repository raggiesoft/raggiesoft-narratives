# Part 7: The Connection

The air conditioning of the family van was a sanctuary they were reluctant to leave, but the digital clock on the dashboard read 12:55 PM.

"Go," Ellen said, unlocking the doors. "Hydrate. And ignore the girl."

"We will," Freddie promised.

Jessica hopped out of the front seat. She wasn't staying for the afternoon block—her clarinets didn't report until Wednesday—but she smoothed her oversized t-shirt and adjusted her glasses with the focused demeanor of someone clocking in for a shift.

"I'm walking you in," Jessica said, slinging her purse over her shoulder. "I need to talk to Panoff about the section leader binders for the woodwinds."

The three of them walked into the band room together. The chaos had returned, the room vibrating with the sound of fifty teenagers digesting fast food and slamming locker doors.

Heidi was standing near the marimba, holding her clipboard like a shield. She looked ready to pounce. Her eyes locked onto Freddie’s drafting chair, and her mouth opened, likely to make a comment about how it was taking up too much space or ruining the aesthetic of the setup.

Then, she saw Jessica.

Heidi froze. Her eyes darted from Freddie, to Paige, to Jessica, and then back to Freddie. It was a visible calculation. A connection was being made in real-time.

"Jessica?" Heidi said, her voice pitching up into a tone Freddie recognized immediately. It was the "Teacher Voice" used by students who wanted extra credit. "I didn't know you were coming in today!"

"Just dropping off the twins," Jessica said coolly, placing a hand on Freddie’s shoulder. It was a claiming gesture. "And I need to check the roster with Panoff."

Heidi’s face went through a rapid transformation. The scowl vanished, replaced by a wide, plastic smile that didn't reach her eyes.

"The twins?" Heidi repeated, looking at Freddie and Paige as if seeing them for the first time. "Oh my gosh! I didn't realize they were *your* siblings! Avery! Of course! I should have put that together."

Freddie watched the performance with fascination. It was like watching a computer program crash and reboot into a different operating system.

"Yeah," Paige said, her voice dry. "We're the Averys."

"Well!" Heidi clapped her hands together, beaming at Freddie. "We are so lucky to have them in the Pit. Freddie has great hands! Really great texture." She looked at the drafting chair, the object of her rage only an hour ago. "And I'm so glad we got the seating sorted out. We want you to be comfortable, Freddie. Seriously, if you need a water break or if the sun glare hits you through the window, just let me know."

Freddie blinked. The falseness of it was almost physically painful. It was a frequency that grated on his nerves more than the snare drums. He preferred the hostility; at least the hostility was honest.

"I'm fine," Freddie said, looking at his mallets.

"Great! Just great," Heidi chirped. She turned back to Jessica. "So, are you excited for the woodwinds on Wednesday? I was telling the drumline, we really need to coordinate our sectionals this year..."

Jessica nodded politely, but her posture remained the unshakable foundation of the family. She saw right through it. "We'll see, Heidi. I need to catch Panoff before he gets sucked into a drill writing session. I'll see you later."

"Bye, Jessica!" Heidi waved enthusiastically.

As Jessica walked toward the office, the air around the marimba changed. Heidi was still smiling, but she looked nervous now. She busied herself with straightening a stack of music stands, giving Freddie and Paige a wide berth.

"She's terrified of her," Paige whispered, picking up her mallets.

"She should be," Freddie whispered back, adjusting his height on the chair.

He watched Jessica knock on the glass door of Mr. Panoff's office. Panoff looked up, saw her, and waved her in immediately, smiling. It was clear who held the actual respect in the room.

Freddie took a breath, feeling the tension in his shoulders dissolve. The social architecture of the band room had just revealed its load-bearing walls, and fortunately for him, his sister was one of them.

"Okay, Pit!" Heidi announced, though her voice was significantly less shrill than it had been at 8:00 AM. "Let's run the warm-ups. From the top!"

Freddie hit the first note of the exercise. It rang out clear and true. He was safe.
