# Part 1: The Downbeat

The red numbers on the digital alarm clock flipped from 6:59 to 7:00 AM, accompanied by the harsh, electronic buzz of the radio springing to life. It wasn't music; it was a static-filled Morning Zoo commercial for a local car dealership, shouting about low financing rates.

Freddie groaned, burying his face into the pillow. The sound felt like sandpaper against his eardrums. He tried to retreat into the darkness behind his eyelids, clinging to the phantom logic of a dream he was already forgetting, but the physical world was demanding entry.

Beside him, the mattress shifted. A hand, warm and steady, placed itself gently in the center of his back. It didn't pat him; it just pressed down, a firm, grounding weight that seemed to hold his spine together.

"Time to get up, Freddie," Paige whispered. Her voice was the opposite of the radio—soft, devoid of sharp edges.

Freddie turned his head, squinting against the morning light filtering through the blinds. Paige was already sitting up, her hair a messy halo of sleep. They were fourteen years old, far too old for siblings to be sharing a bed by normal standards, but "normal" had never really applied to the Avery house. Nights were difficult for Freddie. The silence of the house magnified the ticking of clocks and the settling of the foundation until it sounded like gunfire. Paige was the only thing that made the noise stop. She was his white noise.

"It's summer," Freddie muttered, his voice thick with sleep. "Summer is for sleeping."

"Not anymore," Paige said, sliding out of bed. She stretched, her silhouette mirroring his own frame, just softer. "Today is the day. High school doesn't wait."

Freddie sat up, his legs heavy. His cerebral palsy—spastic diplegia, though he just thought of it as 'bad wiring'—always felt worse in the morning. His muscles were tight, like rubber bands left in the freezer. He rubbed his calves, trying to wake them up.

"Do I have to?" he asked, though he already knew the answer.

"Yes," Paige said, tossing a pair of gym shorts at him. "We’re in the Pit. We don't have to march. We just have to hit things with other things. It’ll be fine."
