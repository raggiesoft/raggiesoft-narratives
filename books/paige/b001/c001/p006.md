# Part 6: Rocket Burger

The tree Paige had found offered shade, but it didn't stop the humidity. The air was a wet blanket, heavy with the drone of cicadas and the distant rattle of snare drums. Freddie sat at the base of the oak, legs stretched out to prevent cramping, while Paige unpacked a sandwich.

A car horn honked—two short, authoritative bursts.

Freddie looked up. Idling at the curb of the bus loop was the family van. The passenger window rolled down, and Jessica leaned out. She was sitting in the front passenger seat, wearing sunglasses that made her look impossibly cool for sixteen. Behind the wheel was their mother, Ellen.

"Get in," Jessica called out. "We're busting you out."

"Busting us out?" Freddie asked, grabbing Paige’s hand so she could hoist him up. "We aren't allowed to leave campus. It's a closed camp."

"It's closed unless a parent signs you out," Ellen said, leaning across Jessica to smile at them. "I have the day off, and I am not letting you eat warm bologna on your first day. Get in. We're going to Rocket Burger."

Freddie and Paige climbed into the backseat, the blast of the van's air conditioning hitting them like a blessing. As they pulled away, Freddie saw the other freshmen watching them with envy. The rule was strict—once you were dropped off, you stayed until five—but the "Parental Override" was the one loophole in the system.

Five minutes later, they were sitting in a red vinyl booth inside Rocket Burger. The smell of grease, salt, and sanitizer was comforting. It was a sensory environment Freddie understood perfectly.

"So," Ellen said, unwrapping her burger. She looked at Freddie with that scanning, maternal gaze that checked for injuries or distress. "How was the morning? Did the noise get to be too much?"

"The noise was fine," Freddie said, dipping a fry into a puddle of ketchup. "The people were the problem. Specifically, a girl named Heidi."

Ellen’s eyebrows shot up. "Heidi? Is that the section leader?"

"She tried to take my chair away," Freddie said, the indignation flaring up again in his chest. "I walked in, and she told me I had to stand because of 'visuals.' She said it wasn't fair that I got to sit."

Ellen stopped chewing. Her face went hard—the same expression she wore during IEP meetings when administrators tried to cut corners. "She did what?"

"It's okay," Freddie added quickly, seeing the storm gathering. "Mr. Panoff stopped her. He called her into his office and showed her the file from Pope Middle. He told her the chair was non-negotiable."

Ellen relaxed, but only slightly. "Good. That file is there for a reason. I don't want you hurting yourself just to look like everyone else."

Jessica, who was stealing fries from Freddie’s tray despite having her own, let out a loud snort. "Mom, you don't have to worry about Heidi. Everyone hates her."

"Everyone?" Ellen asked.

"Everyone," Jessica confirmed. "The clarinets hate her. The trumpets hate her. Even the other percussionists can't stand her. She’s been bossy since freshman year, but ever since she got the Section Leader badge, the power went straight to her head. She thinks she’s the band director."

"Why did Mr. Panoff give her the job if she's terrible?" Freddie asked. It didn't make sense. The architecture of leadership should be built on competence and respect, not tyranny.

"Because it was the only way to get her to shut up," Jessica explained, pointing a fry at him for emphasis. "If she wasn't Section Leader, she would be complaining from the back row every five seconds. Panoff figured if he gave her a title, she'd be too busy micromanaging you guys to bother him. It’s a containment strategy."

"So, she's a problem he delegated to us," Freddie muttered.

"Exactly," Jessica said. "But now you know. She’s not powerful, Freddie. She’s just loud. And the rest of the band is on your side. You just have to survive her."

Freddie looked at Paige, who was quietly eating her cheeseburger, content to let the others talk. Survival. That was the theme of the family.

"I can do that," Freddie said, taking a bite of his burger. The "Spoons" were returning to his body with every calorie. "As long as I have my chair, she can yell all she wants."
