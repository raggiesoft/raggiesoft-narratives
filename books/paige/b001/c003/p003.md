# Part 3: The Clinic

The bell for fourth period lunch rang, a jarring electronic shriek that signaled the migration of two thousand students toward the cafeteria. The hallway, momentarily empty during class, was about to become a stampede.

Freddie flinched at the sound, curling tighter into himself. His stomach gave a painful, hollow growl—he hadn't eaten breakfast due to nervous excitement about giving Courtney the photo—but the thought of the cafeteria was impossible. The clattering trays, the yelling, the smell of industrial pizza... it was a sensory assault he could not survive right now.

He just wanted to stay here, anchored to the floor by Paige’s arms.

"I can't go," Freddie whispered, his face pressed against Paige’s shoulder. "It's too loud."

"We aren't going there," Paige promised, shifting her weight to shield him from the first wave of students exiting the classrooms. "We're staying right here until the hall clears."

"Report," a voice said from above them.

It was Jessica.

She didn't look like a student; she looked like a crisis manager arriving on the scene. She stood over them, her backpack slung over one shoulder, her expression shifting from confusion to immediate concern as she took in the tableau: Freddie on the floor, Paige guarding him, and the box of rejected pens lying on the linoleum.

"He's crashing," Paige said, looking up at their older sister. Her eyes were wide with confusion and anger. "It was Courtney. She... she just exploded."

Jessica dropped to a crouch, ignoring the skirt of her winter outfit touching the dirty floor. She looked at the abandoned gift, then at Freddie. "What happened? What did she say?"

"I don't know," Freddie choked out, his voice thick with tears. "She threw the pens back. She said I was a liar."

"A liar about what?" Jessica asked, her "Matriarch" instincts kicking in as she tried to find the logic in the situation.

"She didn't make sense," Paige explained, shaking her head. "She was screaming that she 'knew what he did' and that he was 'bragging,' but she wouldn't say about what. She just kept saying he was gross and to stay away."

"Bragging?" Jessica frowned. "Freddie doesn't brag. He barely talks to anyone but us."

"I know," Freddie whispered. "I don't understand. I just wanted to give her the picture."

"It's a mystery," Jessica muttered, her eyes narrowing. "But whatever she *thinks* she knows, she didn't bother to ask you about it first."

She reached out and placed her hand on Freddie’s back, right between his shoulder blades. Freddie leaned into the touch. Now he had bookends—Paige on the front providing deep pressure, Jessica on the back providing stability.

"I know it hurts," Jessica said softly. "I know it feels like the end of the world. But if she is going to believe some mystery story without giving you a chance to speak, then she wasn't the one, Freddie."

Freddie sniffled, lifting his head slightly. "Why is she so mad?"

"We'll find out," Jessica promised, her voice turning steely. "But right now, we need to deal with your legs."

She glanced down. Freddie’s knees were drawn up tight to his chest, trembling violently. The emotional stress was firing his spastic diplegia into overdrive, locking his muscles into painful knots.

"You're seizing up," Jessica observed. "We need to get you to the clinic. You need your medication before the spasms get worse."

"I can't walk," Freddie said, panic rising again. "Everyone is looking."

"Let them look," Jessica said. "But we aren't going to make you walk the gauntlet. Paige, help me get him up. We're going to the nurse."

Paige nodded. Together, the sisters coordinated the lift. Paige took his left arm, Jessica took his right. They hoisted him up, taking the weight off his stiff legs.

Freddie felt heavy, drained of all energy, but being sandwiched between his sisters felt like armor. They walked him down the hall, ignoring the stares of the students rushing to lunch. They moved as a phalanx—the Avery unit in defensive formation.

The school clinic was quiet. It smelled of rubbing alcohol and peppermint. The nurse, Mrs. Gable, knew the Averys well. One look at Freddie’s pale face and his sisters' grim expressions, and she pointed immediately to the cot in the back room.

"Lay down," Jessica instructed, helping him swing his stiff legs onto the paper-covered mattress.

Mrs. Gable brought a small paper cup with water and his mid-day muscle relaxer. Freddie swallowed it, his throat tight.

"I'm sorry," Freddie whispered, looking at his sisters standing by the cot. "I ruined lunch."

"You didn't ruin anything," Paige said, sitting on the edge of the cot and taking his hand. "We're better off here. Less noise."

"We aren't going anywhere," Jessica said, pulling up a chair on the other side. She took his other hand. "We're going to sit right here until the bell rings. We'll figure out what Courtney is talking about later. For now, just breathe."

Freddie closed his eyes. The medicine would kick in soon, loosening the knots in his legs. But the confusion remained—a cold, heavy weight in his chest. He didn't know what he was accused of, only that he had been convicted.
