# Part 3: The Dinner Table

The transition from the quiet, emotional triage of the bathroom to the kitchen was like stepping out of a submarine and into a riot.

As Freddie, Paige, and Jessica walked into the kitchen, the back door flew open.

"I'm home!" Ellen Avery announced, dropping her heavy nursing bag on the counter with a thud. She looked exhausted, her scrubs slightly wrinkled from a twelve-hour shift, but her energy was instantly maternal and commanding. "And I am starving. Who started the water? Please tell me someone started the water."

"I got it, Mom," Jessica said, moving smoothly past her to grab the big pot. "Water is on. Sauce is in the pantry."

The kitchen was already filling up. The "Satellite" sisters were converging on the food source.

Sarah and Jenna (age 10) were at the table, arguing over homework.

"You can't use a marker for math!" Sarah insisted, trying to snatch a purple marker from Jenna’s hand.

"It makes the numbers louder!" Jenna shouted back, dodging her twin.

Harper (age 7) was sitting under the table—her preferred spot when the noise got too high—tying the shoelaces of unsuspecting siblings together. The toddlers, Amanda and Elara (age 4), were banging plastic cups on the high chair trays in a rhythmic demand for juice.

Freddie stood in the doorway. The noise hit him—a wall of shouting, clattering, and humming appliances. Usually, this level of decibels would make him reach for his earplugs.

But today, it felt different. It felt... safe.

This noise wasn't malicious. It wasn't Josh Riley laughing. It wasn't Courtney screaming. It was just the operational hum of the Avery Corporation.

"Freddie?" Ellen asked, pausing as she washed her hands. She squinted at him. "Your eyes are red. Are you okay?"

Freddie froze. He felt Paige step closer to his right side, her shoulder pressing against his arm. Jessica paused at the stove, turning her head slightly to monitor the interaction. The Shield and the Anchor were active.

"Allergies," Paige lied smoothly. "The pollen count is crazy today."

"And the wind," Jessica added, dumping pasta into the boiling water. "Dust everywhere."

Ellen looked at Freddie. She saw the puffiness. She saw the way he was leaning slightly against Paige. She suspected "allergies" was code for "high school is hard," but she also knew her son. If she pushed him now, he would shut down.

"Well, take a Claritin after dinner," Ellen said briskly, drying her hands. "Set the table, Freddie. We need nine spots."

"Affirmative," Freddie whispered.

He moved to the cupboard. He grabbed the stack of plates. The ceramic was cool and heavy in his hands.

One. Two. Three...

He counted them out. He placed them around the long wooden table. The rhythm of the task grounded him.

Plate. Napkin. Fork. Plate. Napkin. Fork.

"Move your elbow, Jenna," Freddie said quietly as he placed a plate near the math homework war zone.

Jenna looked up. She saw his red eyes. She didn't make a joke. She moved her elbow.

"Thanks, Freddie," she said.

He finished the circuit. He sat down in his spot—the corner seat, where he was protected on two sides by walls.

"Food!" Jessica shouted. "Deployment!"

She brought the pot of spaghetti to the table. Aunt Linda (who had slipped in the side door moments ago to help) followed with a bowl of salad.

They all sat down. The noise level spiked again as food was passed, drinks were poured, and arguments were settled.

Freddie looked at his plate. The steam from the pasta rose up, smelling of tomato and garlic.

He felt a hand on his knee under the table. It was Paige. She gave him two quick squeezes. I'm here.

He looked across the table. Jessica caught his eye and gave a nearly imperceptible nod. You're safe.

He looked down at the end of the table. Harper crawled out from underneath, climbed into her chair, and immediately looked at him. She held up her fork like a weapon.

"I'm eating the big meatball," Harper announced, pointing at the serving bowl. "I called it."

"It's community property," Sarah argued.

"I claimed it!" Harper yelled.

Freddie picked up his fork. He stabbed a noodle.

"Harper gets the meatball," Freddie ruled, his voice quiet but audible over the din. "She provided... structural support today."

Harper beamed. "See? The Captain says so."

Ellen looked at Freddie, confused but smiling tiredly. "Structural support? What did you build?"

"A wall," Freddie murmured, taking a bite of spaghetti. "We built a wall."

He chewed. The food was warm. The kitchen was loud. Courtney was gone, and Josh was a snake, but the Avery Unit was eating dinner.

And for tonight, that was enough.
