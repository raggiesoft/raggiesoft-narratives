# Part 1: The Guidance Summons – 1:15 PM

The intercom speaker mounted high on the cinderblock wall crackled. I felt the sharp burst of static in my teeth a split second before the administrative voice actually spoke.

"Pardon the interruption. Will Tobias Mercer please report to the guidance office?"

My math teacher paused her lecture, looking over at my desk with a warm smile. She grabbed a neon-pink hall pass, filled it out, and handed it to Mrs. Higgins.

Mrs. Higgins was my district-assigned 1-to-1 aide. She was a perfectly nice woman whose job was strictly logistical: pushing my heavy manual wheelchair through the chaotic middle school hallways, managing the heavy cafeteria doors, and assisting with physical transfers when I needed the restroom. Because I am legally a minor and she is just a district employee, she doesn't have the clearance to make academic or medical decisions for me. But she was required to be attached to my chair anytime I moved.

She unlocked my brakes and wheeled me out of the classroom. With every rotation of the wheels, a cold, heavy spike of dread began to drive itself into my chest.

I knew exactly who my assigned 8th-grade guidance counselor was. Mr. Harrison. He was a man.

I had successfully avoided his office for the entire academic year. My IEP meetings were handled by the special education department head—a woman. My teachers were women. Outside of the school walls, my entire world was an impenetrable, trauma-informed matriarchy. The fortress had a strict "no men" boundary for a reason. Being summoned into a small, enclosed office with a male authority figure felt like walking directly into a tripwire.

When we reached the guidance suite, the receptionist pointed us toward the back. Mrs. Higgins wheeled me into the office, maneuvering my chair perfectly so I was seated directly across from the heavy oak desk. Once my brakes were locked, she took a quiet seat in the corner by the door, pulling a paperback novel from her tote bag. Her job was mobility; whatever was about to happen was between me and the school system.

Mr. Harrison was practically vibrating. He was a younger guy, wearing a tie that didn't quite match his shirt, and he had a thick manila folder sitting dead center on his desk.

I immediately dropped my gaze to the wood grain of his desk. I forced my breathing to slow, mentally visualizing the complex spatial geometry of the band room risers just to keep my autonomic nervous system from redlining. *Calculate the angles. Measure the distance. Keep your eyes down.* "Toby," Mr. Harrison said, leaning forward. His voice was too loud, too sudden. I flinched, my shoulders jumping against the canvas backing of my chair.

He didn't notice. "I’m going to skip right to the chase because I am just too excited. The district finalized the standardized testing matrix for the entire city this morning. Every middle school, including the magnet programs."

He paused, waiting for me to react. I didn't. I just kept my eyes locked on the desk, waiting for the interaction to end.

"Toby, you didn't just score well," he beamed. "You broke the curve. You are the highest-scoring eighth grader in the entire Virginia Beach public school system. Nobody even came close."

The math had been easy. I spent most of my evenings sitting in the safety of my heavily fortified house, running equations in my head to keep my brain occupied while Samantha anchored my nervous system. Of course the tests were easy.

Mr. Harrison tapped the manila folder. "Because of these numbers, I have the absolute privilege of telling you this before the official letter even goes out in the mail. Toby, the district wants you at the Math & Science Academy at Ocean Lakes High School next year. You’re in."

He sat back, smiling like he had just handed me a winning lottery ticket.

To the district, Ocean Lakes was the crown jewel. It was the elite program, the place where they funneled all their funding and prestige. To be invited as a freshman was the highest academic honor the city could bestow.

But I didn't care about prestige. I cared about survival. I cared about the plan I had been looking forward to all year: going to Northwood High School, sitting in the band room, and being exactly fifty feet away from my sister at all times.

I raised my right hand, my wrist curling slightly inward due to the CP, and began typing on the heavy, dynamic-display speech board mounted to my lap tray. It took me a few seconds to punch in the specific string of words. I hit the execution button.

The synthesized, digitized voice of the machine cut through the quiet office.

***"Is Samantha going to Ocean Lakes, too?"***

Mr. Harrison’s enthusiastic smile faltered for a fraction of a second. He let out a soft, patronizing chuckle, completely misunderstanding the gravity of the question.

"Well, no, Toby," Mr. Harrison said gently, resting his elbows on his desk. "Samantha is a rising junior over at Northwood. And Ocean Lakes is in a completely different zoning district down off Dam Neck Road. But that’s the beauty of this! This is about *your* future. Your potential."

I stopped typing. My breathing hitched slightly, a small warning sign from my central nervous system.

"I know you and your sister are close," Mr. Harrison continued, adopting his best counselor voice. "But high school is about finding your independence. You'll have an assigned district aide, just like Mrs. Higgins, to help you get around the Ocean Lakes campus. You are going to be surrounded by the smartest kids in the city. You don't need to follow your big sister around anymore."

He had absolutely no idea.

He didn't know that when my chest muscles violently lock, stopping my respiratory drive, a district aide isn't legally allowed to apply the deep-pressure crush-grip required to restart my breathing. He didn't know about the father and the uncle, or the violent extraction two years ago, or the fact that my mind only functions because Samantha, Abbey, and Sarah maintain a literal fortress around me. He had no idea how terrifying it was for me just to be sitting in his office right now.

He didn't see a trauma survivor who had finally found enough safety to excel. He only saw a data point. A test score that belonged at Ocean Lakes.

I looked down at my speech board. I didn't type anything else. There was no point in explaining the fortress to a man who thought safety was just an assigned aide and a shiny new campus. I just let the silence stretch, the heavy reality settling over me: the school system was completely blind.

Mr. Harrison took my silence for teenage shock. He smiled wider, leaning back in his squeaky desk chair and opening the manila folder.

"I know it's a lot to take in, Toby. It's a huge deal. The Math and Science Academy operates completely differently than a traditional high school. We are talking about university-level biology and chemistry labs. They have an actual robotics engineering bay. You'd be taking advanced calculus classes by your sophomore year."

He slid a glossy, tri-fold brochure across the desk toward my lap tray. The cover featured a stock photo of teenagers in white lab coats looking intently at a microscope.

"And I know transportation to the other side of the city sounds daunting," Mr. Harrison continued, waving his hand dismissively. "But the district has that totally streamlined. You won't take the regular neighborhood bus. You'll be assigned to a specialized magnet bus. It picks up all the academy kids from a localized depot in the morning and takes you straight to the Ocean Lakes campus. It's highly organized."

I stared at the brochure. A specialized district bus. An assigned aide. An entire campus filled with strangers, miles away from the Northwood band room. Miles away from Samantha.

If my vocal cords worked, I would have screamed. A district bus without my sister and cousins was a rolling death trap. My brain began to rapidly calculate the sheer number of variables that could trigger a catastrophic neurological event in that scenario, and the zero percent chance a district employee would know how to physically intervene.

"Here is what happens next," Mr. Harrison said, tapping a pen against his desk, clearly excited to outline the bureaucracy. "By Friday, a large, official envelope is going to arrive at your house. It is addressed to your grandmother, since she is your primary legal guardian of record. Inside will be your official acceptance letter and the 'Intent to Enroll' form."

He leaned forward, lowering his voice conspiratorially, as if we were plotting something brilliant together.

"Now, the district gives you two weeks to return the form. But I want you to go home, tell your grandmother the good news, and have her check the 'Yes' box and sign it immediately. We want to secure your seat before the weekend is over. Can you do that for me?"

I looked up from the brochure. I looked at this man who meant well, but whose institutional ignorance was actively threatening my life. I didn't reach for my AAC device. I didn't need to.

I just gave a single, slow blink.

"Excellent," Mr. Harrison beamed, clapping his hands together and standing up. He had successfully delivered his pitch. He looked over at the corner of the room. "Mrs. Higgins, you can go ahead and take Toby back to math class. Great job today, Toby. We are all so proud of you."

Mrs. Higgins marked her page in her paperback, stood up, and moved behind my chair. She unlocked the brakes. As she pulled me backward, spinning the heavy chair toward the door, the glossy brochure slid off my lap tray and fluttered to the carpet.

I didn't try to stop it. I let it fall.

As Mrs. Higgins wheeled me out of the guidance suite and back into the chaotic noise of the middle school hallway, my heart rate finally began to slow. The panic receded, replaced by a cold, absolute certainty.

Mr. Harrison was waiting for my grandmother to check the 'Yes' box.

He had absolutely no idea who Eleanor Whitmore was.
