# Part 1: The Midnight Protocol – 1:55 AM

The heavy, narcotic sleep was dreamless, acting as a thick chemical wall between my brain and the trauma of the day. As the heavy sedatives began to cycle through my system, the distant hum of the hospital slowly pulled me back into a groggy, hazy awareness.

I blinked against the dim light of the hallway monitors. The blinding agony in my lower half was still safely buried under the IV medications, leaving only a dull, heavy ache.

Grandmother Eleanor was not asleep. She was sitting in the vinyl recliner pulled close to my bed, her posture impeccably straight despite the late hour. When she saw my eyes flutter open, she leaned forward, resting one hand gently on the plastic bedrail.

She didn't offer empty platitudes. Because of my profound autism, she knew that predictability and routine were the only ways to keep my nervous system from redlining.

"Tobias, listen to me carefully," Eleanor said, her voice quiet but carrying her signature, precise authority. "It is almost two in the morning. In a few minutes, a nurse is going to knock on that door and come in. Because you are on heavy pain medications, hospital protocol mandates that they check your vitals every two hours."

A wave of deep annoyance rippled through my chest. I hated having my sleep broken. I hated strangers touching me.

"I know," Eleanor murmured, reading the frustrated furrow of my brow. "It is an incredibly disruptive schedule, but it is not a surprise, and it is not a threat. She will check your oxygen with a plastic clip on your finger, and she will check your blood pressure with a cuff on your arm. It will squeeze tightly, and then it will be over. I will be right here the entire time."

Knowing exactly what was coming didn't make the sensory intrusion pleasant, but it gave my brain a concrete schematic to hold onto. The terrifying unknown was eliminated.

Right on cue, at exactly 2:00 AM, there was a soft knock on the heavy wooden door.

The door clicked open, and an overnight nurse stepped in, pulling a rolling vitals machine behind her. She reached for the main overhead light switch, but Eleanor was immediately out of her chair, raising a sharp hand to stop her.

"Leave the overhead lights off, please," Eleanor commanded quietly. "The monitor glow is sufficient."

The nurse paused, her hand dropping from the switch. "Yes, ma'am. I just need to get his two-hour vitals."

"He is awake and prepared," Eleanor stated, stepping directly to the side of my bed to establish the physical perimeter. She leaned over the plastic bedrails and placed both of her hands firmly and flatly against the center of my chest, applying a steady, grounding downward pressure. "Remember his charts. My grandson has severe cerebral palsy and profound autism. Move slowly, and tell him exactly what you are doing before you do it."

The nurse nodded, clearly intimidated by the sharply dressed, 72-year-old matriarch holding court in the dark. She stepped up to the opposite side of the bed.

"Hi, Toby," the nurse whispered. "I'm going to put this little red clip on your finger to check your oxygen now."

I felt the plastic pinch my index finger. I was profoundly annoyed by the disruption, but because Eleanor had established the schematic, my brain didn't sound the alarm. My muscles remained relatively relaxed against the sheets.

"Now the blood pressure cuff," the nurse explained, wrapping the fabric around my bicep. "It’s going to give your arm a tight squeeze."

The velcro ripped, loud and abrasive in the quiet room. As the machine pumped air, the cuff constricted with brutal, mechanical pressure. My spastic muscles instinctively hated the sudden restriction, my arm twitching in protest, but Eleanor instantly pressed down slightly harder on my chest.

"Hold the line, Tobias," Eleanor murmured firmly, her thumbs gently stroking the fabric of my hospital gown. "It is exactly what we discussed. Almost over."

I squeezed my eyes shut, enduring the frustrating mechanical squeeze until the machine finally beeped and the pressure instantly deflated.

"Vitals are perfectly stable," the nurse said softly, quickly removing the cuff and the finger monitor. She backed away toward the door. "I'll be back at four."

"We will be expecting you," Eleanor replied, her tone leaving absolutely no room for deviation.

The nurse slipped out, the heavy door clicking shut behind her.

Eleanor finally lifted her hands from my chest. She looked exhausted, the deep lines around her mouth pronounced in the shadows, but she smoothed my blanket with pure, grandmotherly devotion. We had survived the first sensory intrusion, and we had the playbook for the rest of the night.

"Go back to sleep, Toby," she whispered, turning to walk back to her uncomfortable vinyl chair. "Two hours to go."
