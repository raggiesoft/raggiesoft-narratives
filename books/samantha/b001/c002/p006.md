# Part 6: The Sentries – 9:45 AM

We emerged from the Hampton Roads Bridge-Tunnel, bursting out of the tiled, fluorescent descent and back into the gray, overcast daylight of the Norfolk side. The rain had completely stopped, leaving the coastal air heavy and damp.

As Sarah guided the sedan down Interstate 64 East, the suffocating bottleneck of the tunnel gave way to open road. The massive, concrete HOV gates in the median were open for westbound traffic, heavily congested with a sea of cars rushing toward Naval Station Norfolk for the morning shift. But on our side, heading eastbound toward Virginia Beach, the lanes were mercifully clear. Sarah pressed the accelerator, and the heavy car surged forward, chewing through the final miles.

From the passenger seat, Abbey turned around to face us. The exhaustion was etched deeply into the lines around her eyes, but her voice was pure, uncompromising granite.

"When we get inside," Abbey dictated, her tone leaving absolutely zero room for negotiation, "Sarah and I are stripping the queen mattresses off the frames in both bedrooms. We are putting them in the dead center of the living room floor and pushing them together."

"You and Toby take the middle," Sarah added from the driver's seat, keeping her eyes locked on the road. "Abbey and I will take the flanks. We are piling every pillow and heavy comforter we own on top of you."

"Standard privacy rules are suspended indefinitely," Abbey continued, her dark eyes locking onto Samantha's. "Until that permanent perimeter is built, we sleep in a phalanx. You do not go into another room alone. You do not close a door behind you. Sarah and I are your sentries now, and we promise you, on our lives, we will never, *ever* let either of you out of our sights again."

If I had a voice, I would have sobbed in gratitude. Before Kentucky, the idea of four young adults crammed onto floor mattresses in the middle of the living room would have seemed absurd, a total violation of personal space. Now, it was the only thing I wanted in the entire world. The thought of being sandwiched between the overwhelming, physical density of my cousins, boxed in by the people who had just shattered industrial timber to protect me, was the ultimate salvation.

Samantha didn't argue. She just tightened her deep-pressure grip around my chest and nodded, burying her face into my shoulder. "Thank you," she choked out. "Please."

"Done," Sarah said firmly.

The reflective green signs for Exit 286B approached rapidly. Sarah smoothly navigated the heavy sedan onto the off-ramp, merging us onto Indian River Road eastbound.

The sprawling concrete of the interstate disappeared, replaced by the familiar, tree-lined suburban grid of Virginia Beach. I watched the strip malls and neighborhood brick walls glide past the window. My shattered nervous system clung to the profound, mundane normalcy of it all.

Sarah hit the brakes, guiding the car into a smooth right turn onto Centerville Turnpike. A few minutes later, she engaged the turn signal again, making a left onto Brandon Boulevard. We were deep in our own territory now. Every oak tree and faded street sign was a physical tether pulling me back to reality.

She took a final left onto Rueger Street.

We rolled past the first block in silence. Sarah eased off the accelerator as we approached the intersection at Simon Street, gently rolling through the stop sign. She counted exactly two houses down on the left.

The heavy sedan turned into the driveway of 1693 Rueger Street, the tires crunching softly against the concrete. Grandmother Eleanor's house stood before us, quiet and entirely undisturbed. Sarah shifted the car into park.

The engine cut off, and for the first time in over twenty-four hours, there was only silence. We were home.
