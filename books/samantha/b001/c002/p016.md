# Part 16: The Shift Change – 11:45 PM

The trauma bay had been swapped for a quiet, dimly lit pediatric recovery room. The chaotic energy of the ER was gone, replaced by the steady, rhythmic beeping of my heart monitor. The IV medications were keeping the acute agony at bay, but the heavy sedatives were making it difficult to keep my eyes open.

A new nurse entered the room, carrying a clipboard. She spoke in a quiet, apologetic tone.

"Visiting hours ended at nine," the nurse explained, looking at the four women surrounding my bed. "Hospital policy for the pediatric floor is incredibly strict, especially given the nature of the police report. Only the sole legal guardian is permitted to stay overnight. We have a pull-out recliner for you, ma'am," she said, nodding to Grandmother Eleanor.

The nurse looked sympathetically at my sister. "Minors cannot stay overnight under any circumstances. And extended family members have to leave."

A spike of raw panic shot through my chest. My spastic muscles twitched violently under the heated blanket, fighting against the heavy muscle relaxers. The thought of my physical armor leaving me in this strange, sterile place was terrifying.

I couldn't speak, but my left hand shot out. I bypassed Grandmother Eleanor completely, my rigid fingers desperately grabbing a handful of Abbey's dark navy t-shirt. Abbey was the one who had carried me inside. She was my blast shield. If Samantha had to leave, I needed Abbey to stay.

Abbey immediately covered my small hand with her massive one, her stoic face fracturing with a look of profound heartbreak. She looked at the nurse, her incredibly broad shoulders tensing. "He wants me to stay."

"I'm so sorry," the nurse said gently but firmly. "If you don't have legal guardianship paperwork, hospital security will not allow it. He needs his legal guardian."

Grandmother Eleanor stepped forward. She didn't argue with the nurse, because Eleanor knew the law better than anyone in the building.

"This is exactly what I was talking about this afternoon," Eleanor said quietly to Abbey and Sarah, her voice laced with cold, bitter frustration. "The state does not care how physically strong you are. On paper, you have no authority. Tonight, I am the only one the law allows to protect him. This is why you are signing those joint and several guardianship papers the second they are drafted."

Abbey swallowed hard, her jaw clenching. She understood the tactical reality. She couldn't fight hospital policy without becoming a threat, and she refused to compromise my safety.

But my nervous system was misfiring, sending panicked, erratic signals through my compact, four-foot-eleven frame. I was beginning to thrash slightly against the pillows. Abbey saw the escalating panic and knew exactly what I needed before they could leave.

She looked at Samantha and nodded.

Samantha stepped up first. Because of my injuries, she couldn't share the bed to provide her usual deep-pressure regulation. Instead, she carefully lowered the plastic bedrail just enough to lean completely over me. She wrapped her arms securely around my upper back and pressed her grounded, five-foot-five frame firmly across my chest and shoulders. The sudden, heavy pressure was an anchor. My rapid breathing instantly hitched, and I buried my face into her shoulder as she squeezed me as tightly as she safely could, entirely avoiding my lower half.

"I'm sorry I have to go," Samantha whispered, her voice cracking as she held the deep pressure for a long, grounding moment. "I love you. I'll be back as soon as the sun is up."

When Samantha finally pulled back, wiping her eyes, Abbey took her place.

Abbey leaned her towering, six-foot-four frame over the mattress. She didn't just hug me; she used her staggering physical mass to blanket my upper body. She slid one massive, dense arm securely beneath my shoulder blades and draped her incredibly broad chest and shoulders directly over mine. The sheer, immovable weight of her was like a physical vault locking around me. It was the safest feeling in the world. She applied her terrifying level of physical restraint, using her elite-caliber strength strictly to provide the absolute, metronomic pressure my shattered nervous system required to reset.

"The perimeter here is secure, Toby," Abbey murmured, her deep voice vibrating against my chest. "Grandmother is holding the legal line. Sarah and I are going back to secure the fortress for when you come home. We've got you."

Abbey held the heavy, utilitarian pressure for several long seconds before slowly stepping back.

Sarah was the last to step forward. She leaned over the bed, her heavy denim jacket brushing against my hospital gown. She mirrored her twin's exact geometry, applying the same calculating, massive weight across my upper chest and shoulders to seal the sensory regulation.

"Visiting hours open at 8:00 AM," Sarah stated quietly, pressing her cheek firmly against the top of my head. "We will be standing exactly in that doorway at 7:59. Not a minute later."

I looked at my sister and my towering cousins, taking in the sheer mass and fierce devotion that had saved my life today. They weren't abandoning me; the law was simply forcing them to shift to a different post. My muscles finally went entirely slack against the mattress.

Without another word, the three of them turned in unison. Abbey and Sarah escorted my sister out of the room, their heavy boots making quiet, measured thuds against the linoleum until they disappeared down the hallway.

The room felt terrifyingly empty without their tank-like frames boxing me in.

Grandmother Eleanor sighed heavily. She set her legal binder down on the bedside table—the absolute proof of her authority—and settled into the vinyl recliner in the corner. She pulled a thin hospital blanket over her lap.

"I know I am not the muscle, Tobias," Eleanor said softly in the quiet room, her aristocratic features softening. "But I have the watch. Go to sleep."
