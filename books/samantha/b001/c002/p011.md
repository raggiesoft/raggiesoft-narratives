# Part 11: The Iron Perimeter – 7:30 PM

After the staggering amount of food we had just consumed, the house settled into a heavy, quiet lull. But Abbey and Sarah weren't built to simply sit still.

Abbey pushed my custom manual wheelchair through the utility door and out into the garage. This was the twins' domain, the industrial heart of the fortress where they built and maintained the immense physical mass required to act as my human armor.

There were no mirrors on the walls, no bright neon signs, and no complicated, heavily-marketed corporate fitness machines. The space was strictly utilitarian. The floor was covered in thick, shock-absorbing black rubber. The room was dominated by heavy, bolted-down metal cages, thick steel bars, and towering stacks of massive iron discs. To me, it was just the physical architecture of my safety.

Normally, Samantha and I loved coming out here to watch them. There was something profoundly grounding about the repetitive, metronomic clanking of the heavy iron and the terrifying, explosive physical power my cousins generated. But since Abbey and Sarah had just taken in a massive volume of clean calories to fuel their frames, tonight was not the time for them to exert force.

Tonight was about maintenance.

Sarah grabbed a large spray bottle of industrial cleaner and a stack of clean microfiber rags from a metal shelf. She tossed a rag to her sister, handed one to Samantha, and kept one for herself.

"Start with the low benches," Sarah instructed quietly.

Standing at a towering six-foot-four, the twins moved through the garage with absolute, calculated efficiency. They began wiping down the thick metal frames and the heavy, dense iron plates they used to maintain their size. Even just doing light chores, their massive arms strained the seams of their dark navy t-shirts.

Samantha, standing at her grounded five-foot-five, stayed entirely within my physical perimeter. She sprayed down the thick, padded leather of a flat bench right next to my chair. She was my exclusive safe person, fiercely protective and constantly vigilant.

She handed me a soft, dry rag. My hands were still a bit stiff and uncoordinated, a lingering side effect of the severe autonomic spasticity that constantly misfired through my compact, four-foot-eleven frame. Samantha didn't rush me. She gently wrapped her warm hand over my rigid fingers, guiding my arm in slow, rhythmic circles to help me wipe down the edge of the leather pad.

I didn't mind the work. I actually loved it. The sharp, sterile smell of the cleaning spray mixed with the faint, metallic scent of the heavy iron was incredibly soothing.

There were no loud noises, no unpredictable variables, and absolutely no men allowed in this space. It was just the four of us, engaged in a quiet, shared routine. The rhythmic wiping of the metal, the quiet breathing of my cousins, and the steady pressure of my sister's hand over mine acted as a physical anchor, pulling my shattered nervous system further and further away from the nightmare in Kentucky.

When the last of the heavy iron plates had been wiped down, Sarah gave a quiet nod, signaling that the maintenance was finished.

It was time to head back inside. Abbey pushed my custom manual wheelchair out of the garage and into the living room, but as we approached the center of the floor, the twins stopped. During our grueling eight-hour nap earlier in the day, the two heavy queen mattresses had shifted, drifting a few inches apart and creating a small, uneven gap right in the middle of our perimeter.

Abbey and Sarah found this completely unacceptable.

Sarah immediately walked back toward the utility closet. She returned a moment later carrying a handful of thick, industrial elastic cords with heavy metal hooks—the kind usually used to strap down heavy cargo in the back of a truck.

Working with absolute, synchronized precision, the twins moved to opposite sides of the beds. With effortless mechanical power, Abbey lifted the edge of the heavy mattresses with one hand, while Sarah stretched the thick elastic cords underneath, hooking them tightly to the fabric handles and the reinforced stitching. They ratcheted the beds together in three different places, completely sealing the gap. The two mattresses were now bound into one monolithic, immovable block. The structural integrity of the fortress was restored.

While they worked, I sat quietly in my wheelchair. And as I sat there, the absolute last lingering fumes of adrenaline finally burned completely out of my system.

In its place, a searing, agonizing pain in my rear end came into sharp, horrifying focus.

During the violent rescue in Kentucky and the frantic, redlining drive across the Appalachian mountains, my brain had been too overloaded with sheer terror to fully process the physical damage. My body had been in pure survival mode. But now, in the quiet safety of Grandmother Eleanor's living room, the physiological shields dropped. The brutal reality of what my father and uncle had done to me began radiating through my lower half in waves of excruciating, localized agony.

Because of my severe cerebral palsy, the extreme spike in physical pain caused my autonomic spasticity to go into overdrive. My muscles began to violently misfire, locking my compact, four-foot-eleven frame into rigid, useless angles. Every tiny, involuntary twitch of my legs sent a blinding flash of pain through my rear end.

I couldn't speak, but Samantha saw the sudden, rigid shift in my posture. She instantly looked at Abbey.

Abbey stepped over to my wheelchair, her towering frame blocking out the overhead light. She looked at my tightly clenched fists and the pained geometry of my posture, and she understood exactly where I was hurting without anyone having to say a single word.

She completely altered her lifting mechanics. Instead of her usual, efficient scoop, she slid one massive, dense arm securely behind my shoulder blades, and carefully guided the other directly under my lower thighs. When she lifted me, she used her staggering physical mass to bear one hundred percent of my weight, completely bridging the gap so that my hips, pelvis, and rear end were entirely suspended in the air.

She carried me to the mattress block with agonizingly slow, calculated precision.

Samantha was already waiting. She had rapidly built a structured mound of soft pillows. As Abbey gently lowered me down, Samantha guided my rigid body so that I was positioned securely on my side, with the pillows supporting my knees and lower back. It entirely eliminated the direct gravitational pressure on my rear end.

I let out a ragged, trembling exhale. The pain didn't vanish, but shifting the pressure turned the blinding spike into a dull, manageable throb.

Samantha immediately slid in closely beside me. She wrapped her arms securely around my upper body, applying her grounding, deep-pressure hold entirely to my chest and shoulders. She stayed completely away from my lower half. As the twins took their sentinel positions on the outer flanks, their immense physical presence sealing us in, I buried my face into my sister's shoulder, finally allowed to feel the physical toll of my survival.
