# Book 12: The East Coast

## Chapter 1: Outbound to ORF (Saturday, December 22, 2001)

### Part 1: The First Class Algorithm – 6:00 AM

(Note: The following section shifts the narrative perspective to Rachel’s point of view, focusing on her sensory experiences and internal processing.)

The hydraulic brakes of the TAPS Route 194 bus exhaled a loud, sharp hiss, echoing violently against the concrete drop-off curb of the Seattle-Tacoma International Airport.

I hated that sound. It was sudden and unpredictable, an acoustic spike that made the muscles in my neck completely lock up. But as the bus doors opened, spilling us out into the freezing, gray Pacific Northwest morning, I didn't cover my ears. I couldn't.

Both of my hands were clamped tightly around the textured rubber push-handles of Jessica’s wheelchair.

Jessica did not need me to push her. Her upper body strength was formidable, and she was fiercely independent when navigating the friction of the adult world. She could easily propel her manual chair up the concrete ramps and through the automatic doors.

But today, my hands on her chair was a mandatory, non-negotiable rule.

"You are the engine today, Rachel," Jessica had instructed me the moment we stepped off the bus. "This airport is going to be a sensory nightmare. But if your hands are firmly on my chair, the crowd cannot physically separate us. You are my anchor, and I am yours. We move as a single unit."

It was a brilliant algorithm. Pushing the heavy chair provided deep, regulating proprioceptive feedback into my shoulders and wrists, grounding my nervous system. And Jessica was right: as we moved through the sliding glass doors into the ticketing lobby, the sheer volume of human bodies was terrifying.

It was a sea of chaotic, overlapping motion. People were rushing, shouting, and dragging heavy, squeaking luggage over the hard tile floors. National Guard soldiers stood near the pillars, holding heavy black rifles.

I kept my head down, staring directly at the back of Jessica's jacket. If I looked at the crowd, the visual input would overwhelm my processor. So, I just looked at Jessica, focusing on the mechanical resistance of the wheelchair tires rolling over the grout lines in the tile.

"Meridian Airlines, Rachel," Jessica said, her voice calm and authoritative, cutting right through the ambient roar of the lobby. "Turn us toward the red and blue signs."

I adjusted my grip and pushed the chair to the right, navigating us toward the massive ticketing counter.

My breathing was shallow. My chest felt tight. I was surviving the environment, but the ghost of my last airport experience was heavily overlaying my reality.

In September, Dad had dragged me through an airport just like this. He had pulled me by the wrist, his heavy, angry strides completely ignoring my panic. He had shoved me into a tiny, suffocating middle seat in Coach. It smelled like stale coffee and his angry, nervous sweat. I had been trapped between two strangers, completely unmedicated, vibrating with a terror so deep I couldn't breathe. And the worst part, the absolute most agonizing parameter of that September flight, was the trajectory. Every single mile that airplane flew took me further away from my brother.

But today, the trajectory was reversed.

I pushed Jessica up to the Meridian Airlines desk. The ticket agent, a woman with a strained, exhausted smile, looked down over the high counter.

"Good morning," Jessica said politely, handing over our identification and the heavy cardstock itineraries. "Checking in for the 8:00 AM flight to Chicago O'Hare, connecting to Norfolk."

The agent typed rapidly on her keyboard. "Alright, Ms. Ragsdale. I see you both here. Two bags to check?"

"Yes," Jessica confirmed.

The agent tagged our heavy suitcases and placed them on the conveyor belt. Then, she printed our actual boarding passes and handed them down to Jessica. The paper was stamped with a large, bold **ZONE 1**.

"Your First Class seating is confirmed all the way through to Virginia," the agent smiled. "You can use the priority security lane just down the hall."

I blinked, my brain snagging on the data. First Class.

I didn't know much about the logistics of commercial aviation, but I knew Dad always complained about the cost of tickets and how we had to sit in the very back of the plane.

As I turned the wheelchair away from the counter, headed toward the security checkpoint, I leaned down slightly. "First Class?" I rasped, my vocal cords tight.

Jessica looked over her shoulder at me, a highly calculated, protective smile on her face.

"I remember what Dad put you through in September, Rachel," Jessica said quietly, ensuring only I could hear her. "I know how much this environment hurts you. So, I fixed the parameters."

She tapped the bold lettering on her boarding pass.

"First Class means we board the airplane before anyone else. We don't have to stand in a crowded aisle while people hit us with their bags. The seats are wider, so no strangers will touch you. And we are at the very front of the plane, completely isolated from the noise of the main cabin. It is a controlled environment."

I stared at the boarding pass in her hand, profoundly overwhelmed by the sheer scale of her protection. She had weaponized her Quantum salary to buy a physical, acoustic barrier between me and the neurotypical world. She had engineered a sanctuary at thirty thousand feet.

I gripped the rubber handles of her wheelchair tighter. The noise of the Seattle airport was still deafening, and the long, winding security line ahead of us looked exhausting, but the panic in my chest began to recede.

I wasn't being dragged. I was the engine. And at the end of this algorithm, waiting for us on the other side of the country, was Michael.

### Part 2: The Priority Perimeter – 6:45 AM

The regular security line was a terrifying, winding labyrinth of exhausted, frustrated people. I ran the mathematical visual in my head. Based on the density of the crowd and the speed of the single metal detector, I calculated that the people at the very back of that line were going to be standing on the hard tile floor for at least four hours.

If Dad were here, we would be at the back of that line. He would be sweating, complaining about the rules, and his escalating anger would have completely short-circuited my nervous system by hour two.

But Jessica’s algorithm bypassed the friction entirely.

I kept my hands locked on the rubber push-handles of her wheelchair, steering us past the massive crowd and directly into the Meridian Priority Lane. There were only six people ahead of us.

We rolled up to the gray security podium. A uniformed contractor held out his hand.

"Boarding passes and photo IDs, please," he said, his voice flat and robotic.

Jessica handed over her Washington State Identification Card. Because she couldn't drive, she didn't have a standard license, but the heavy plastic card carried the exact same legal weight.

I reached into my coat pocket and pulled out my own matching piece of plastic. Last month, Jessica had taken me to the Department of Licensing to secure my official state ID. I stared at it for a second before handing it over. Printed right below my picture was the Federal Way apartment address.

It wasn't just a card; it was a legally binding shield. It proved to the adults in uniforms that I wasn't a runaway from Virginia. I belonged in King County. I belonged with my sister.

The officer checked the names against the heavy cardstock boarding passes, handed the plastic cards back, and unhooked the velvet rope.

We moved forward to the X-ray belts. I initiated the protocol, dropping my heavy laptop bag, my coat, and my shoes into the gray plastic bins.

"I've got this part," Jessica said quietly.

I released the rubber push-handles. Jessica didn't need my help to navigate the physical space. She grabbed the metal push-rims of her wheels and propelled herself forward with practiced, powerful thrusts, moving expertly toward the bypass gate while I walked through the standard metal detector.

I waited on the sterile side of the checkpoint, watching carefully as a female security officer ran the back of her hands over Jessica’s pockets and visually inspected the frame of the wheelchair. Jessica endured the physical pat-down with silent compliance.

Once she was cleared, Jessica rolled forward to join me at the end of the X-ray belt. I grabbed my laptop bag and slung it over my shoulder.

I reached out to take the push-handles again, but before my fingers could make contact, a female airport employee wearing a bright yellow Meridian Airlines vest stepped up to us. She offered a polite, customer-service smile and reached out toward the back of Jessica's chair.

"Good morning, ma'am. I can take you to your gate from here," the employee offered.

"Good morning," Jessica replied, her tone perfectly pleasant and equally polite. "Thank you for the offer, but we are fine. We have it handled."

"Oh, it's really no trouble," the employee insisted gently, her hand closing over the metal push-handle. "It's actually airport policy. Just for liability and navigating the crowds, we push passengers needing assistance. Your daughter can just follow along behind us."

My chest instantly tightened. My hands froze in the air.

The employee had just attempted to overwrite our perimeter. She was trying to break the physical connection between us in the middle of a crowded concourse, and she had done it by fundamentally misidentifying our pack.

The pleasant, polite customer-service aura radiating from Jessica vanished in a millisecond.

Before the employee could move the chair a single inch, Jessica's hand shot out and clamped firmly over the woman's wrist.

"She is my sister," Jessica corrected, her voice dropping into the absolute, terrifying register of a CEO terminating a contract. "And under the Air Carrier Access Act, it is a federal violation for an airline employee to mandate an escort or restrict my independence. Your internal policy does not supersede federal aviation law."

The employee blinked, utterly startled, pulling her hand back as if the metal of the wheelchair had suddenly burned her.

Jessica didn't raise her voice, but she commanded the space completely. She unzipped the heavy canvas tote bag resting on her lap, revealing the thick, raised-seal court documents sitting right on top.

"Furthermore," Jessica continued, locking eyes with the employee, "I am her legally appointed guardian. We do not separate. When we reach the boarding gate, I will transfer to an aisle chair to get down the cabin, and I will gate-check this manual chair at the end of the jet bridge. My sister will push me so we remain a single visual and physical unit in this crowd."

Jessica let the heavy silence hang in the air for exactly two seconds.

"Do we have an understanding?" Jessica asked.

The employee looked at Jessica’s cold, unyielding expression, then looked at the thick stack of legal paperwork in the tote bag. She wisely took a large step back and waved us toward the concourse.

"Yes, ma'am. Have a good flight," the employee muttered, retreating quickly toward the checkpoint.

I let out a massive, shaky breath. The crisis had been neutralized with lethal, legal precision. I stepped forward and locked both of my hands firmly onto the rubber push-handles.

"Are you okay, Rachel?" Jessica asked, her voice instantly softening as she looked back at me.

"Yes," I rasped, my heart rate slowly returning to normal. "You didn't let her take the chair."

"Nobody takes the chair," Jessica promised. "Now. Let's find the lounge."

We navigated down Concourse A. The terminal was loud and echoing, the high glass ceilings doing nothing to dampen the noise of the holiday crowds. But midway down the hall, Jessica pointed to a set of heavy, frosted glass doors flanked by a polished wood podium. A discreet silver plaque read: *Meridian First Class Lounge.*

I pushed the wheelchair up to the podium. Jessica showed our boarding passes to the attendant, and the heavy glass doors hissed open.

I pushed us inside, and the acoustic assault of the airport instantly vanished.

It was a sensory miracle. The floors were covered in thick, sound-dampening carpet. The lighting was low and warm, completely devoid of the harsh, buzzing fluorescent bulbs from the terminal. There were massive, overstuffed leather armchairs arranged in quiet pods, and soft, instrumental music played in the background.

"They have a buffet in the back," Jessica whispered, pointing toward a marble counter filled with fresh fruit, pastries, and a cooler of unlimited bottled water. "Everything is free. It's included in the ticket."

I parked Jessica’s wheelchair next to two wide, empty leather chairs facing a massive window overlooking the runway. I sat down, sinking deep into the quiet, heavy leather.

I looked around the nearly empty room, then looked at my older sister. Dad's version of flying was a chaotic, terrifying punishment. Jessica’s version of flying was a highly regulated, perfectly insulated sanctuary.

I pulled my knees up to my chest, letting out a long, vibrating hum of absolute contentment. The algorithm was working flawlessly.

### Part 3: The Four-Hour Buffer – 7:00 AM

The Meridian First Class Lounge was an entirely different biome.

It didn't feel like an airport. The air smelled of dark roasted coffee and fresh citrus, completely masking the heavy, chemical scent of jet fuel that permeated the main concourse. The harsh, buzzing fluorescent lights had been replaced by warm, amber table lamps. But the most profound difference was the acoustic profile. The thick carpets and heavy velvet curtains absorbed the ambient noise, reducing the world to a soft, manageable murmur.

There were only about a dozen other people in the massive room. Most of them were businessmen in dark suits, reading newspapers in silence. Nobody was rushing. Nobody was yelling.

I parked Jessica’s wheelchair next to a low, polished mahogany coffee table, flanked by two oversized leather armchairs.

Jessica locked her brakes and looked at her watch. "It is seven o'clock," she stated quietly. "I engineered our timeline assuming we would be trapped in that security line for four hours. Because we bypassed it, we have a massive block of unscheduled time. Our flight to Chicago does not board until ten-forty-five."

For a neurotypical teenager, sitting in a waiting room for almost four hours might sound like a punishment. For my brain, it was a profound luxury. It was a guaranteed, uninterrupted block of processing time.

"I brought my laptop," I signed to her, tapping the heavy black bag on my shoulder.

"I know," Jessica smiled warmly. "And you have plenty of time to boot up Quantum OS and play your puzzle games. But first, we need to execute a breakfast protocol."

Jessica pointed toward the back of the lounge. There was a long, white marble counter glowing under soft recessed lighting.

"That is the buffet," Jessica explained. "It is entirely complimentary. I want you to go over there, select a plate, and bring breakfast back for both of us. Can you do that?"

My heart gave a tiny, sharp flutter of anxiety. She was issuing a solo objective in a public space.

I looked at the buffet, running a quick visual scan of the parameters. The marble counter was only fifty feet away. There was no line. There was no cashier to interact with, which meant zero requirement for verbal communication. The environment was completely contained and safe.

I gave Jessica a sharp, determined nod.

I slipped my laptop bag off my shoulder, setting it safely on the leather chair, and walked toward the food station.

The buffet was a masterpiece of predictable geometry. There were heavy, silver chafing dishes with domed lids, baskets woven with white linen napkins, and glass bowls perfectly aligned in a row. I picked up a heavy, warm porcelain plate. It felt solid and grounding in my hands.

I lifted the first silver dome. It was filled with scrambled eggs. I used the stainless steel tongs to transfer a perfectly calculated scoop onto my plate. I moved down the line, adding exactly three strips of crisp bacon. Next was the fruit station. I bypassed the unpredictable, messy berries and used a heavy silver spoon to select geometrically perfect, one-inch cubes of cantaloupe and honeydew melon.

I built a second plate for Jessica, mirroring the exact same nutritional ratios, and carefully carried them both back to our pod. I set them down on the mahogany table.

"Flawless execution," Jessica praised, picking up her fork. "Thank you, Rachel."

I sat down in my heavy leather chair, pulling my knees up and resting my plate safely on my lap. The food was warm and predictable. The leather chair wrapped around me like a protective shell. For the next three and a half hours, the universe was entirely contained within this quiet room. I ate my breakfast, booted up my laptop, and listened to the soft, rhythmic clinking of coffee cups.

### Part 4: The Aisle Transfer – 10:45 AM

The digital clock in the lower right corner of my laptop screen ticked over to 10:45.

Exactly on schedule, a female lounge attendant in a crisp navy blue Meridian uniform walked over to our pod. She didn't try to touch Jessica’s chair, and she didn't speak to me like a child.

"Ms. Ragsdale?" the attendant asked softly. "Flight 402 to Chicago is ready for priority pre-boarding. If you'll follow me, we can get you settled before they open the gate to the main cabin."

"Thank you," Jessica nodded. She looked at me. "Laptop away, Rachel. You're the engine again."

I quickly shut down my system, packed my bag, and grabbed the rubber push-handles of her wheelchair. We followed the attendant out of the sanctuary of the lounge and back into the bright, noisy reality of the terminal.

The gate area was crowded, packed with people waiting to board the massive Boeing 757. But we didn't stop. The attendant swiped our boarding passes and opened the glass door leading directly down the jet bridge.

The jet bridge was steep, sloping downward toward the airplane door. I leaned back slightly, using my body weight and the friction of the rubber handles to keep Jessica's chair rolling at a slow, controlled pace.

When we reached the end of the tunnel, stopping right outside the open door of the aircraft, the parameters changed.

Waiting for us was a piece of specialized aviation equipment called an aisle chair. It looked like a standard wheelchair that had been violently compressed; it was incredibly narrow, with tiny wheels and no side armrests. Because commercial airplane aisles are only about twenty inches wide, a standard manual wheelchair physically cannot fit down the cabin.

"Alright," Jessica announced, locking the brakes on her everyday chair.

This was the vulnerable part of the algorithm. Jessica had to leave her custom-fitted, highly mobile chair and surrender to the airline's equipment.

Two baggage handlers were waiting. They were polite and efficient. Jessica unbuckled her waist strap, planted her hands on the armrests of her own chair, and smoothly pivoted her body, transferring her weight directly into the narrow aisle chair.

The handlers immediately strapped her in, securing tight nylon belts across her waist and chest to keep her from sliding out of the armless seat.

"Rachel," Jessica instructed, looking up at me as she was strapped in. "Grab the canvas tote bag from my chair. Then take your laptop bag, and follow right behind me."

I nodded, grabbing the heavy bags and slinging them over my shoulders.

The baggage handlers tilted the aisle chair back onto its rear wheels and carefully pushed Jessica through the aircraft door. I followed immediately behind her, stepping out of the cold jet bridge and into the warm, pressurized cabin.

We didn't have to go far. Because Jessica had weaponized her budget, our seats were in the very first row. Seat 1A and 1B.

The handlers parked the aisle chair next to the wide, plush blue leather seats. Jessica unbuckled the restrictive straps and executed a second physical transfer, sliding herself gracefully from the narrow transport chair into the deep comfort of Seat 1B.

"I've got the chair, ma'am," the handler said, pulling the aisle chair back. "We'll stow your personal wheelchair in the cargo hold and have it waiting for you on the jet bridge in Chicago."

"Thank you," Jessica nodded, officially taking command of our new environment.

I slid into the window seat, Seat 1A. The chair was massive. My shoulders didn't touch Jessica's, and my knees were nowhere near the bulkhead wall in front of us.

A flight attendant immediately appeared beside us, holding a silver tray with crystal glasses. "Pre-departure beverages? Champagne, water, or orange juice?"

"Orange juice for my sister, please," Jessica requested, taking a glass of water for herself.

I took the cold glass, wrapping both hands around it. The heavy, chaotic crowd of the main cabin hadn't even begun to board yet. We were already safe, seated, and insulated behind the heavy curtain that separated First Class from the rest of the plane.

I looked out the thick plastic window at the gray Seattle sky. In just a few minutes, the engines would start. The trajectory was locked. We were going east. We were going to Michael.

### Part 5: The Cabin Sanctuary – 11:45 AM

The massive twin engines of the aircraft roared, pushing us back into our plush leather seats as we accelerated down the runway.

I closed my eyes and gripped the wide armrests. Takeoffs were always an intense sensory event, but the physics of First Class completely changed the equation. In the back of the plane with Dad, the turbulence had violently rattled my bones, and the terrified energy of a hundred cramped passengers had suffocated me.

Up here in row one, the climb was smooth and heavily insulated. The thick leather absorbed the vibration of the floorboards. But the most profound difference was the spatial geometry of the seat itself.

I opened my eyes as the nose of the plane leveled out and the loud hum of the engines dropped to a quiet, steady drone.

I looked down at my legs. I had inches of empty space on either side of my hips. The center console separating me from Jessica was so wide that our elbows didn't even touch. Nobody was encroaching on my perimeter. Nobody was bumping my shoulder when they walked down the aisle. I was perfectly contained within my own designated, mathematically precise zone.

"Comfortable?" Jessica asked, looking over at me. She had already retrieved a thick textbook from her tote bag and rested it on her tray table.

"It's huge," I rasped quietly, running my hand over the soft, dark blue upholstery. "No strangers can touch me."

"Exactly," Jessica smiled. "That is the point of the premium cabin. We buy the physical space so your nervous system doesn't have to."

The heavy curtain separating us from the main cabin snapped shut, sealing off the noise of the boarding crowd. A moment later, the flight attendant approached our row. She rolled a sleek metal cart down the wide aisle, offering a warm, professional smile.

"Good morning, ladies," the attendant said smoothly. "Now that we are at cruising altitude, I can offer you a beverage before our meal service begins. We have fresh coffee, juice, and I can also pour you a mimosa or a glass of champagne if you'd like to start the holiday early."

Jessica offered a polite, practiced smile. "Thank you, but I'm actually twenty, and my sister is fourteen. So we will pass on the champagne."

The flight attendant didn't miss a beat, flawlessly executing a customer-service pivot. Her smile never wavered.

"Of course! My apologies," the attendant said cheerfully. "In that case, we have a wonderful hot chocolate, freshly brewed decaf or regular coffee, assorted juices, cola, ginger ale, and bottled water."

"Hot chocolate sounds perfect," Jessica replied. "We'll both take a hot chocolate, please. And we'll also need two glasses of water."

"Right away," the attendant nodded, pulling down two thick, ceramic mugs and two heavy crystal glasses.

I watched the interaction with quiet awe. There was no friction. There was no argument. Dad would have made a sarcastic comment or gotten annoyed at the interruption, but Jessica just stated the parameters, and the world adjusted around her.

The attendant placed the steaming mugs and the glasses of water onto our tray tables on small linen napkins.

"Enjoy," she said before moving to the row behind us.

Jessica reached into her tote bag and pulled out two small, orange plastic bottles. She opened the first one, shaking a white tablet into her palm, and then passed the second bottle to me.

"Medication protocol," Jessica instructed quietly.

I unscrewed the cap and tapped my daily pill into my hand. In September, Dad had purposefully packed my medication in the checked luggage, forcing my brain to navigate the terrifying environment of the airport completely unshielded. That single variable had caused my entire system to crash.

Jessica never made that mistake. She kept the logistics tightly controlled.

I placed the pill on my tongue and took a long drink from the crystal glass. The cold water washed it down, locking my chemical baseline into place. Jessica took her own medication with a sip of water, managing the chronic nerve pain in her legs.

Once the medical protocol was complete, I wrapped both of my hands around the thick ceramic mug. The heat radiated through the porcelain, warming my palms. I took a slow sip. The hot chocolate was rich and sweet, melting away the last lingering traces of tension from the Seattle security checkpoint.

Shortly after, the attendant returned with our lunch. Instead of the chaotic, crinkling foil wrappers I remembered from coach, the food was served on warm porcelain plates with heavy, solid utensils. It was chicken and rice, predictable and easy to process.

I ate my meal in complete silence, occasionally sipping my hot chocolate and looking out the window at the thick blanket of white clouds below us. The cabin was quiet. The food was warm. My sister was right beside me, completely in control of the environment.

The algorithm was perfect. And every second that the massive engines pushed us forward, we were getting closer to Michael.

### Part 6: The Prismatic Corridor – 4:30 PM (CST)

(Rachel's POV)

The transition from the insulated, premium sanctuary of the aircraft cabin to the massive, sprawling reality of the Chicago transit hub was a severe shock to my system.

We retrieved Jessica's manual wheelchair on the jet bridge. She transferred back into her custom seat, instantly regaining her mobility and her armor. I took my position behind her, locking my hands onto the rubber push-handles.

"Alright, Rachel," Jessica said, looking up at the massive, illuminated signs hanging from the high ceiling of Concourse B. "The flight to Norfolk departs from Concourse C. According to the map, Concourses B and C are parallel. We have to take an underground pedestrian tunnel beneath the concrete apron to cross over."

I nodded, adjusting my grip. A tunnel sounded mathematically efficient. A straight line from point A to point B.

We navigated toward the center of the concourse, joining a massive current of holiday travelers moving toward the downward escalators and elevators. I steered us into the glass elevator, completely unaware that we were descending directly into an unmapped sensory trap.

The elevator doors opened at the sub-level.

The acoustic assault hit me before the visual did. It wasn't the ambient, echoing roar of a normal airport. It was music. Loud, pulsating, synthesized music echoing violently against curved, subterranean walls.

I pushed Jessica forward around the corner, and the entire world exploded.

We were standing at the entrance of the Prismatic Corridor. It was a massive, underground tunnel, and its entire ceiling was covered in thousands of kinetic neon-light tubes.

It wasn't just brightly lit; it was moving. Massive waves of rainbow light strobed and chased each other down the length of the tunnel in rapid, erratic sequences. The reflective glass walls bounced the flashing colors back and forth, completely destroying any sense of spatial geometry. And the synthesized music—a heavy, ethereal, ringing tone—vibrated aggressively against my ribcage.

But the floor wasn't a standard corridor. It was mechanized. Stretching out in front of us were parallel moving walkways—horizontal escalators made of heavy, grooved metal pallets. There were four massive belts running side-by-side in this first section, two pulling traffic toward Concourse C, and two bringing traffic back.

Meridian Airlines had commissioned the entire space as a flagship art installation. For my neurodivergent brain, it was a weaponized sensory gauntlet.

My lungs seized. The flashing lights felt like physical strikes against my eyes. I couldn't process the depth of the tunnel, and the echoing music mixed with the mechanical *clack-clack-clack* of the metal walkways completely scrambled my audio processing.

"Rachel," Jessica's voice cut through the synthesized noise, sharp and alarmed. She felt my hands lock up on her push-handles. The chair had stopped moving.

Jessica looked up at the ceiling, then down the massive, strobing length of the corridor. Her analytical brain instantly identified the catastrophe. She hadn't known about this. Her logistical supremacy had failed because this wasn't an operational parameter; it was an artistic one.

"I didn't know," Jessica said, her voice laced with a rare, furious guilt. "I am so sorry, Rachel. I didn't know this was here."

I squeezed my eyes shut, but it didn't help. The neon lights were so intense they flashed bright red and blue right through my eyelids.

"Disco tunnel," I managed to rasp out, my throat tight with panic. It was the most derogatory term my brain could generate for the horrific, flashing nightmare.

"It's a disco tunnel," Jessica agreed, her tone turning cold and tactical. She reached back, placing her hands over mine on the rubber grips. "We cannot turn around. We have to cross it to get to Michael. Look exactly at the back of my jacket. Do not look at the walls. Do not look at the ceiling. I will steer the wheels. You are just the engine. Push."

I opened my eyes, locking my vision entirely onto a single, dark seam on the back of Jessica's winter coat.

I pushed.

We moved forward, and Jessica skillfully angled her front casters onto the right-hand moving walkway. There was a sharp, jarring bump as the wheelchair transitioned onto the moving metal pallets. I stepped on right behind her, my hands still in a death grip on the handles.

Being on the mechanized belt was terrifying. Once we were on it, we were trapped in the flow. The metal plates vibrated beneath my shoes, adding a deep, physical hum to the strobing lights above. I just stared at the dark fabric of my sister's coat, counting the rhythmic mechanical clicks of the belt to stay grounded.

Click. Click. Click.

After what felt like an eternity, the walkway ended. The belt fed us back onto solid tile.

I gasped, looking up for a fraction of a second, hoping it was over. But we were only halfway. The tunnel broke in the middle, a wide, open plaza area providing access to a set of subterranean restrooms.

Beyond the restrooms, a second, identical set of four moving walkways stretched out toward the distant elevators of Concourse C. The lights were still flashing. The synth music was still ringing.

My knees buckled slightly, the sheer volume of sensory input threatening to shut my system down entirely.

"Do not stop, Rachel," Jessica commanded, her voice an absolute, immovable anchor in the chaos. "Onto the second belt. Right now."

I gritted my teeth, locked my eyes back onto her jacket, and pushed her onto the next grooved metal walkway.

It felt like it took an hour to cross the second half of the tunnel. By the time the walkway finally spit us out at the Concourse C elevator bank, my breathing was ragged, and a cold sweat had broken out on the back of my neck.

I pushed us into the elevator, and as the doors slid shut, cutting off the neon lights and dampening the horrific music, the heavy, metallic vibration finally stopped.

"We're out," Jessica said immediately. "We are out. It's over."

When the elevator reached the main concourse level, Jessica didn't direct me toward our gate. She pointed to a quiet, dimly lit alcove near a closed storefront.

"Park me there," she commanded.

I pushed the chair into the shadows, out of the flow of pedestrian traffic. I immediately dropped to my knees on the thin airport carpet, pressing my forehead hard against the metal side-frame of Jessica's wheelchair. I needed the deep, unyielding pressure of the cold aluminum against my skin to reset my nervous system. I let out a long, distressed hum, my hands gripping the metal spokes of her wheel.

Jessica didn't tell me to get up. She didn't tell me I was overreacting. She engaged the brakes, reached down, and rested her hand firmly on the back of my head, providing a heavy, grounding weight.

We stayed in the alcove for twenty minutes while my processor slowly rebooted.

As my breathing finally stabilized and the phantom flashing lights faded from my vision, Jessica looked up at the massive Meridian departure board hanging in the center of the concourse. Her jaw was set in a hard, unforgiving line.

"I am logging a permanent rule in the system," Jessica stated, her voice quiet but possessing absolute, terrifying finality. "We are surviving this return trip because we have no other choice. But after January, we are never flying through this city again. I will rebuild our entire transit protocol from scratch before I ever let an airline subject you to that again."

I kept my forehead pressed against the cold metal of her chair, letting out a soft, exhausted sigh. I hated the disco tunnel. I hated Chicago.

But as the heavy, grounding pressure of Jessica's hand remained on my head, I knew one mathematical certainty: when Jessica made a logistical vow, it was absolute. Meridian's massive Midwestern hub was officially dead to us.

### Part 7: The System Crash – 4:45 PM (CST)

(Rachel's POV)

The elevator ascended, the heavy metal doors shielding me for a few desperate seconds from the flashing horror of the underground tunnel. My breathing was ragged, my hands shaking violently where they gripped Jessica's push-handles.

I thought the worst was over. I thought returning to the main level would mean returning to the predictable, manageable friction of a standard airport.

But as the doors slid open onto Concourse C, the reality of the holiday travel hub crashed into me like a physical wall.

It was a sea of absolute chaos. The concourse was packed shoulder-to-shoulder with thousands of delayed, exhausted travelers. The ambient roar of overlapping voices, the screech of hundreds of rolling suitcases, and the sharp, blaring electronic tones of the public address system all slammed into my already shattered auditory processor.

My system couldn't take it. The delayed trauma of the Prismatic Corridor collided with the sheer density of Concourse C, and my internal algorithms simply failed.

My hands slipped off the rubber grips of the wheelchair. My knees gave out.

"Rachel," Jessica gasped, feeling the sudden loss of my weight against her chair.

She spun her casters around, her eyes darting frantically across the massive, sprawling concourse. We had been separated by court orders, airports, and the terrifying, oppressive shadow of Dad. Being back together, curled up on this terrible, lumpy sofa bed, felt like a dream. We were all terrified that if we closed our eyes, we would wake up back in our separate, isolated realities.

"It's not a dream," I promised them, my voice a low, steady rumble. "I'm right here. I'm not going anywhere. The perimeter is secure."

Rachel let out a long, shuddering sigh, her grip on my shirt tightening slightly.

"Northern Star," Rachel murmured into my shoulder.

I smiled in the dark, recognizing the reference to the song we had heard in Aunt Janet's car. It was our designated, unpunishable loophole. It was the absolute, undeniable truth.

"Yeah," I agreed, resting my chin against the top of Rachel's head. "You found the light. You made it home."

Slowly, agonizingly, the adrenaline finally burned out of our systems. The heavy, rhythmic breathing of my sisters began to synchronize with my own. I kept my arms locked securely around them, holding the perimeter tight, until the Virginia night finally surrendered, and we fell asleep.

## Chapter 3: The New Baseline (Sunday, December 23, 2001)

### Part 1: The Three-Hour Shift – 8:30 AM

The winter sunlight bleeding through the living room blinds was entirely too bright, but I didn't dare move a single muscle.

I was completely pinned to the center of the lumpy fold-out mattress. On my left, Rachel had her face buried so deeply into my shoulder that all I could feel was her warm breath against my collarbone. Her arm was thrown heavily across my chest, and her leg was tangled securely with mine. On my right, Jessica was sleeping just as deeply, her head resting against my arm, her breathing slow and perfectly synchronized with our younger sister's.

I looked up at the digital clock on the television stand. It was 8:30 AM.

For my internal clock, and for Mom out in the kitchen, the morning was well underway. But for Rachel and Jessica, their biological rhythms were still heavily anchored to the Pacific Northwest. To their exhausted, travel-worn processors, it was only 5:30 in the morning.

I let my head fall back against the pillows, listening to the quiet sounds of the house.

From the kitchen, I could hear the rhythmic sizzle of bacon hitting a hot cast-iron skillet and the soft clinking of ceramic coffee mugs.

There was another sound, much closer. A heavy, rhythmic panting.

I turned my head slightly to the left. Resting his massive, heavy black chin directly on the edge of the mattress, mere inches from Rachel's face, was Baby. The giant Labrador let out a soft, low whine, his dark eyes staring at us with pure, unadulterated devotion. He wanted to climb onto the bed, but he knew the rules.

Right behind him, pacing a tight circle on the carpet, was Archie. His cloudy blue eye was wide with morning energy, and clamped firmly in his jaws was a frayed, saliva-soaked plush squirrel. He kept nudging Baby out of the way, desperate to show off his prize to the girls.

"Not yet, boys," I whispered as quietly as I could. "They're still on Seattle time."

Baby let out a heavy sigh, his breath ruffling Rachel's hair, but he stayed perfectly still.

My own biological needs were starting to override my role as the human anchor. My bladder was full, and my muscles were incredibly stiff from sleeping perfectly rigid all night. I needed to execute an extraction protocol.

Moving with agonizing, millimeter-by-millimeter precision, I slowly slid my right arm out from under Jessica. She shifted slightly, letting out a quiet murmur, but didn't wake up.

Rachel was the harder puzzle. I had to gently lift her heavy arm off my chest, immediately replacing my body weight with a thick, folded blanket so her nervous system wouldn't register the sudden loss of pressure. I slid out from under her leg and carefully rolled off the end of the mattress.

Rachel let out a soft, distressed vocalization, her hands blindly grabbing the blanket and pulling it tight against her chest, but she stayed asleep.

I quietly grabbed my clothes and headed for the downstairs bathroom.

When I emerged ten minutes later, dressed in sweatpants and a clean shirt, the smell of breakfast had completely saturated the first floor. I walked into the kitchen.

Mom was standing by the stove, flipping eggs.

"Morning," Mom said softly, offering a warm, tired smile over her shoulder. "Did they sleep through the night?"

"Like rocks," I confirmed, leaning against the kitchen counter. "Their internal clocks are entirely reversed right now."

Mom nodded, setting a plate of bacon on the table. She poured two glasses of orange juice and a cup of coffee for herself.

I stood in the kitchen, just listening to the ambient noise of the morning. And as I stood there, a profound, undeniable realization hit me.

The house was quiet. But it wasn't the terrifying, suffocating silence we used to endure.

When Dad lived here, silence was just the volatile pause before an explosion. We used to tiptoe across the linoleum, terrified that a dropped fork or a loud laugh would trigger a violent, screaming lecture. Every single morning used to begin with a strict, oppressive calculation of Dad's mood.

But today, the silence was just... peace. The baseline of fear was completely gone. Dad wasn't sitting at the head of the table radiating anger. He wasn't glaring at Rachel for struggling to speak. He wasn't barking orders at Jessica.

He was gone. And absolutely none of us missed him.

"It feels different, doesn't it?" Mom asked quietly, catching the analytical drift of my gaze. She leaned against the counter next to me, her hands wrapped around her warm coffee mug.

"It feels safe," I answered honestly.

Mom closed her eyes for a fraction of a second, a profound relief washing over her face. "I'm glad. That's all I want for you three."

A loud, mechanical squeal from the living room broke the quiet moment. Jessica was awake, and she had just unlocked the brakes on her manual wheelchair.

A moment later, Jessica wheeled backward into the kitchen archway. Her hair was messy, and her eyes were heavy with sleep, but her pragmatic radar was already fully online.

Right behind her chair, looking like a complete zombie, was Rachel. She was dragging her feet, her oversized sleep shirt hanging off her shoulders, but her hands were locked securely onto the rubber push-handles of Jessica's chair.

"It is entirely too early," Jessica announced, letting out a massive yawn. "But the smell of sodium and cured meat pulled us out of stasis."

"Good morning, girls," Mom laughed, pulling out chairs at the dining table. "Sit down. Breakfast is ready."

Rachel didn't look at the food. She bypassed the table completely, walking straight past Mom, and stepped right into my personal space. She wrapped her arms around my waist, pressing her face into my chest, her eyes squeezed tightly shut against the bright kitchen lights.

"Morning, Rachel," I rumbled softly, wrapping my arms around her shoulders and resting my chin on her head. "Welcome to the East Coast."

She let out a long, vibrating hum of contentment, completely ignoring the fact that Baby and Archie were currently circling her legs, their tails thumping loudly against the kitchen cabinets.

We eventually migrated to the table. I sat in the middle, naturally, with Rachel on my left and Jessica parking her chair on my right. We ate in a comfortable, groggy haze. Archie occasionally rested his chin on Rachel's knee, offering his soggy plush squirrel as a tribute, which Rachel happily accepted and placed on the table next to her plate.

Nobody screamed. Nobody told Rachel to sit up straight or look people in the eye. Nobody complained about the dogs.

It was just Mom, me, Rachel, and Jessica. The perimeter was perfectly secured, the food was warm, and the monster was officially evicted from our reality.
