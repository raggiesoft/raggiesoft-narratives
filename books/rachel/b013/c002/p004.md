The standard-hardware world was almost entirely built on red tape, invisible prerequisites, and corporate holding periods. Independent, mom-and-pop logistics were infinitely better, but they still operated on the basic physics of banking.

Mom set her purse down on the small kitchenette counter and pulled out her checkbook.

"First month's rent and the security deposit," Mom said, her pen scratching quickly across the paper. She ripped the check free and handed it across the counter. "I assume you need to run a background check before we finalize?"

Dave took the check, looking at the numbers, and gave a dismissive wave of his hand.

"We don't bother with corporate background checks," Dave said, folding the check and sliding it into his shirt pocket. "My wife and I run this building on a pretty simple philosophy. If you're honest with me, and you don't tear up the walls, we’ll get along fine. I trust my gut, Ellen, and I like these two. Let's get the paperwork signed."

He carried a worn leather portfolio under his arm. He unzipped it and pulled out a standard, two-page residential lease agreement. There were no fifty-page addendums or predatory clauses. It was just a straightforward, literal contract.

Mom signed on the guarantor line. Ashley and I stood side-by-side at the counter, each taking the pen to sign our names on the tenant lines.

I waited for the physical handover. I watched Dave’s pockets, waiting for him to produce the brass keys to the deadbolt so we could lock the door and seal the perimeter.

Instead, Dave zipped his portfolio back up and gave us an apologetic, practical smile.

"Alright, the paperwork is locked in," Dave said, tapping the leather folder. "Now, I do trust my gut, but I also have to trust the bank. As soon as this check fully clears—which usually takes about two or three business days—the keys are yours. I'll give you a call the second my bank gives the green light, and you can start moving in."

The Gray Area Freeze instantly locked my feet to the carpet.

My processor lagged violently as it ran the new variable. *Two or three business days.* That meant we didn't have access to Unit A5 tonight. It meant we had to walk back out the front doors.

It meant we had to go back to 849 Baldwin Avenue.

My breathing immediately turned shallow. The absolute dread of Greg's cramped condo—the impossible Catch-22 of the job hunt, the claustrophobia of the galley kitchen, and the terrifying, frictionless trap of the tile shower—crashed back down on my shoulders. I had spent the last twenty minutes believing I had successfully escaped it.

"We have to go back," I whispered, the panic causing my right leg to spasm sharply against the counter.

Mom saw the physical flinch. Her maternal instincts flared, but before she could try to negotiate a standard-hardware compromise with the landlord, Ashley stepped into the gap.

The Sentinel didn't panic. She analyzed the math.

"It's just a holding pattern," Ashley stated, her voice projecting the cold, flat, undeniable authority she used to override my internal static. She looked directly at me. "The perimeter is legally secured. Our names are on the contract. We aren't trapped at the condo anymore, Michael; we are just waiting for the extraction vehicle."

I swallowed the heavy lump in my throat, forcing my brain to process her logic.

She was right. The parameter of Greg's condo had fundamentally changed. Yesterday, it was an indefinite prison sentence. Today, it was just a temporary, seventy-two-hour waiting room.

Dave gave us a friendly nod, completely unaware of the massive psychological algorithms running in front of him. "I'll call you by Thursday or Friday at the latest, Ellen. You folks have a good afternoon."

He walked out of Unit A5, pulling the wooden door shut until the deadbolt clicked.

We stood in the empty hallway, staring at the closed door of our new Base Camp. We couldn't get inside, but we knew exactly where it was.

"Three days," Mom said quietly, wrapping her arm around my shoulders as we walked toward the heavy rubber steps of the lobby. "You can survive Greg's rules for three more days. And while we wait for the bank, we can start planning the logistics of the move."

Ashley walked right beside me, our shoulders brushing as we pushed through the glass doors and back out into the blistering Virginia heat.

"We hold the line for seventy-two hours," Ashley promised, her voice a low, unyielding anchor. "We bypass the architectural friction, we ignore the job hunt lectures, and we pack the canvas bags. The second that check clears, we are gone."

I let out a long, shuddering breath as we reached the sedan. The physical Base Camp was delayed, but as I looked back at the brick building on Greenway Court, my processor finally stabilized. I could survive seventy-two hours in hostile territory. I just had to watch the clock.
