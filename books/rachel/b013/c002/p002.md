The automatic sliding doors of Best Tech parted, and the oppressive, ninety-degree heat of the Virginia afternoon slammed into us like a physical wall.

Ashley didn't slow down. She kept her vice-grip locked on my forearm, navigating us through the crosswalk and into the sprawling asphalt parking lot. She didn't let go until we reached the passenger side of Mom’s sedan.

I leaned heavily against the hot metal of the car door, my chest heaving. The forced, rapid walk and the massive spike in my internal telemetry had sent the Cerebral Palsy spasticity in my legs into a violent flare. My right knee locked tight, the muscles vibrating with a deep, burning ache.

But the physical pain was secondary to the catastrophic system crash happening in my processor.

"I don't understand," I stammered, my breathing shallow and erratic as I looked between Mom and Ashley. "The math was perfect. He said it was a digital system. He said I wouldn't have to stand behind a register. Why did we run?"

Mom unlocked the doors with her key fob. She looked across the roof of the car, her expression a mix of lingering fury and deep maternal guilt.

"Because he was lying, Michael," Mom said softly.

"He didn't lie about the parameters," I argued desperately, my rigid brain fighting to hold onto the perfect workaround. "He said B2B e-commerce. He said asset-building. It was a logistical solution to the transit grid."

"He was feeding your processor malicious code," Ashley translated smoothly, standing right beside me to block the glare of the sun. She spoke in the exact mechanical terms my brain needed to hear to override the Gray Area Freeze. "It’s a social engineering hack. The company is called Nexar, but Ellen is right—it's just a shell for Ameri-Way. They don't have a real web portal, and they don't have hourly jobs. They manipulate you into buying their own products, and then force you to trick your family and friends into doing the same."

My jaw dropped slightly. The flawless, algorithmic sanctuary I thought I had just found shattered into jagged, worthless pieces.

"He wasn't offering you a job," Mom added, walking around the front of the sedan. "He was trying to make you his customer. They wander around retail stores specifically looking for people who look tired, overworked, or vulnerable. They are predators."

I slumped against the window glass, the realization hitting me with a wave of profound, exhausting nausea.

The standard hardware world wasn't just inaccessible. It wasn't just filled with physical barriers, frictionless showers, and managers who visually disqualified me. It was actively hostile. There were people out there wearing crisp button-down shirts and friendly smiles who had perfectly reverse-engineered my desperation just to exploit it.

"He was using the wrong terminology anyway," I whispered bitterly, my brain latching onto the technical data as a defense mechanism against the emotional betrayal.

"What do you mean?" Ashley asked gently.

"When he approached me, he asked about running heavy B2B server requests through a standard residential router," I explained, staring down at the melting asphalt. "It was a nonsense query. The firmware would completely bottleneck."

I thought of Jessica, sitting 2,800 miles away in Washington State.

She wasn't just living out there for the scenery. She was working as a Junior Technical Program Manager at Quantum Corporation. She spent her days coordinating enterprise-level architecture, forcing different development teams to talk to each other, and managing actual network protocols. My big sister built digital perimeters for a living.

"He was just throwing around network buzzwords to sound like an authority figure," I realized, the puzzle pieces finally snapping into place. "He didn't know anything about e-commerce or routing. If Jessica had been standing in that aisle, she would have dismantled his entire network topology in sixty seconds."

A fierce, incredibly sharp spike of homesickness pierced through my chest.

I desperately wanted the pack back together. I wanted Jessica to stand in front of me and tear that recruiter apart with pure, undeniable technical data. I wanted Rachel to stand beside me so we could silently judge his ridiculous button-down shirt.

But they were gone. And I was trapped in Norfolk, leaning against a hot car, utterly exhausted by the endless parameters of surviving in a world I didn't understand.

"He was a predator," Ashley repeated softly, reaching out and placing a steadying hand on my shoulder. "But your perimeter held. We didn't let him install the malware."

"I have no job, Ash," I whispered, the crushing weight of Greg’s Catch-22 settling heavily back over my shoulders. "And the transit grid is destroying my legs. I don't know how to solve the math."

"We'll run a new algorithm," Mom said firmly, opening her car door. "Get in. Let’s get the air conditioning on, and let's go find you a real Base Camp. We aren't giving up."

I opened the passenger door and carefully folded my aching, spastic legs into the seat. The digital mirage was gone, but as Ashley climbed into the back and Mom put the car in gear, I knew I still had one Sentinel left to watch the grid.
