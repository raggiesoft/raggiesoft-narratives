The extraction plan started flawlessly but quickly deteriorated into another logistical dead end.

By Tuesday afternoon, Mom’s sedan had traced a meticulous grid pattern through the tree-lined streets of Ghent and West Olney. We were completely ignoring the corporate apartment locators, scanning instead for handwritten "For Rent" signs staked in the small front lawns of independent duplexes.

But the math simply wasn't on our side. The few places that were actually available either required navigating steep, narrow flights of exterior stairs that my CP-affected legs couldn't handle, or they were completely disconnected from the CVTA transit grid.

By one o'clock, the silent, heavy dread of Greg's impossible paradox was creeping back into the car.

"We'll regroup," Mom sighed, tapping her fingers against the steering wheel as we hit a red light. "I need to grab a new networking cable for the computer anyway. Let's stop at Best Tech, get the cable, and we'll map out a new neighborhood for tomorrow."

I nodded, staring blankly out the window.

When we walked through the sliding glass doors of Best Tech, the blast of industrial air conditioning and the familiar hum of the massive electronics retailer acted as a brief sensory reset. Mom headed toward the peripheral cables, and Ashley and I naturally drifted toward the PC software and networking aisles.

This was my environment. Surrounded by the neat rows of Vertex Creative Studio boxes and the latest Quantum OS iterations, my processor felt grounded.

Ashley was looking at a display of blank CrimsonBurn Ultra CD-Rs when a man stepped into our aisle. He looked to be in his mid-forties, dressed slightly too sharp for a Tuesday afternoon in a big-box store—wearing a crisp button-down shirt and polished shoes.

He stopped a few feet away from me, staring intently at the back of a high-end router box, before turning with an easy, practiced smile.

"Hey, man," he said, his voice casual and friendly. "Between you and me, I'm trying to figure out if this model supports the bandwidth for a heavy B2B e-commerce setup. Any thoughts?"

My rigid, literal brain didn't register a threat. It registered a query outside my current database.

"I don't know," I answered factually, my posture stiffening into its usual guarded stance. "I just know standard residential hardware. If you are running heavy B2B server setups, you would have to ask my older sister. That is her domain."

The man’s smile froze for a fraction of a second. A brief, inexplicable flash of annoyance crossed his face—as if the mere suggestion of consulting a girl about networking hardware offended his standard-hardware sensibilities. But because my processor didn't read social subtext, I just logged it as anomalous facial data.

Before I could process it further, the expression vanished. He swallowed whatever comment he wanted to make and dialed his smile back up, clearly not wanting to lose my attention.

"Well, you seem like a really sharp guy regardless," he said, pivoting smoothly and taking a step closer. "Actually, it's funny I bumped into you. Are you keeping your career options open right now?"

My internal telemetry spiked. *Career options.*

"I am currently looking for employment, yes," I replied, my highly literal cadence betraying my desperation.

"Perfect timing," the man said smoothly, his voice dropping into a conspiratorial, mentoring tone. "I'm working with a group of local entrepreneurs. We're expanding a massive e-commerce web portal. It’s all digital, all system-based. We're looking for ambitious people who want to build passive income assets, rather than just trading their time for an hourly paycheck standing behind a register."

The pitch hit my processor like a jolt of pure, algorithmic salvation.

*No standing behind a register.* That meant my legs wouldn't be in agony. *System-based.* That meant explicit, predictable rules. *Digital e-commerce.* That meant no visual disqualification from neurotypical managers who took one look at my gait and threw my application in the trash.

It was the perfect workaround. To my desperate eighteen-year-old brain, this man wasn't a stranger; he was the mathematical solution to Greg Lawson's impossible Catch-22.

"I am very good at following systems," I told him, my heart rate accelerating with genuine hope. "I would be interested in applying for the portal."

"That's great," he nodded smoothly, pulling a sleek pen from his pocket. "It’s a proven, step-by-step process. We actually have a required reading list to get you started—we use *The Passive Principle* to help our team understand the asset-building mindset. I'd love to get your phone number so we can set up a sit-down meeting with my mentors."

"He doesn't have a phone number for you," Ashley's voice cut through the aisle like a physical blade.

She stepped directly between me and the recruiter, her Sentinel posture fully deployed. Her shoulders were squared, and her eyes were locked onto the man with cold, analytical precision. While my autistic processor had been swept up in the logical relief of the pitch, Ashley had been tracking the psychological data.

"What's the name of the company?" Ashley demanded, her tone completely devoid of standard-hardware politeness.

The man blinked, his smooth smile faltering again. "Like I said, it's an expanding e-commerce network. It's highly visual, which is why we do a sit-down—"

"What is the hourly wage?" Ashley interrupted, her voice rising just enough to assert total dominance over the conversational space. "Legitimate companies have names, and they have an hourly rate. Give me the data."

"We focus on passive income, not a traditional wage structure," he deflected, his tone hardening slightly as he tried to look past her to me. "Look, if your friend here wants to take control of his financial future—"

"Are you pitching *The Passive Principle*?"

The new voice belonged to Mom. She walked into the aisle holding a coiled blue ethernet cable, her eyes narrowing as she took in the recruiter’s crisp shirt and the defensive posture of the Sentinel.

Mom stepped up beside Ashley, her maternal instincts instantly aligning with Ashley's threat detection. She looked the recruiter dead in the eye.

"That's Ameri-Way," Mom stated flatly, stripping away the entire digital illusion in three words. "You're trying to recruit my son into a pyramid scheme."

The recruiter's friendly, mentoring facade completely shattered. The transition was so violent and immediate it made my chest hitch. The smile vanished, replaced by an aggressive, cornered arrogance as he realized his fishing lure was being ripped away by two women who refused to play along.

"We are *Nexar*!" the man snapped, stepping forward and jabbing a finger aggressively toward Mom. "We are an affiliated B2B web portal! We are *not* Ameri-Way! You people clearly don't understand the scope of the modern digital asset—"

"We understand it's a scam," Mom interrupted coldly. She didn't flinch at his raised voice. "Back off."

"It's a trap, Michael," Ashley hissed fiercely.

She reached out, wrapped her hand in a vice-grip around my forearm, and physically hauled me backward. She braced her weight to accommodate my stiff legs, dragging me out of the software aisle while Mom stood firmly as the rearguard, blocking the recruiter from following us.

The Gray Area Freeze locked my vocal cords as the terrifying reality of the corporate camouflage sank in. As we moved toward the safety of the main registers, I looked back over my shoulder. The man was already turning his back on us in disgust, smoothing his crisp shirt, his aggressive demeanor dissolving instantly as he reset his smile to hunt for the next target.

He hadn't seen my intelligence; he had seen my exhaustion. He had perfectly mirrored the exact logistical data I was desperate to hear just to extract my money.

Ashley didn't let go of my arm. She kept her grip locked tight, a rigid, physical tether grounding me to reality as the beautiful, algorithmic illusion of the digital sanctuary completely collapsed into ash.
