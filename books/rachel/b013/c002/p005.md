Because the logistics of Greg’s condo had changed from an indefinite prison to a strictly defined, seventy-two-hour holding pattern, my processor could handle a strategic retreat.

Instead of going straight back to Baldwin Avenue, Mom drove us directly to the mini-storage facility off Virginia Beach Boulevard, where the dismantled remains of 1852 Delaney Street were locked behind a rolling corrugated metal door.

When Mom pushed the heavy door up, the stifling, dusty heat of the unit rolled out over the asphalt. The space was packed floor-to-ceiling with the physical artifacts of my childhood. But my internal telemetry didn't redline. I wasn't mourning the house anymore; I was executing a tactical inventory.

"Alright," Mom said, wiping a bead of sweat from her forehead. "We just saw Unit A5. We know exactly how small that footprint is. We need to be realistic about what will actually fit through the door. No holding onto things just for sentimentality. We run the math."

"Understood," I nodded, my autistic processor immediately thriving on the rigid categorization task.

We started with the sleeping arrangements. I looked at the massive, heavy oak frame of Mom and Dad's old master bed.

"Too big," I stated factually. "It would consume seventy percent of the floor space."

Mom nodded and crossed it off her mental list.

I looked at the disassembled frame of my old twin bed.

"Too small," Ashley chimed in from beside me, instantly vetoing it.

I looked at the disassembled metal-tubing frame of Jessica and Rachel's old bunk bed leaning against the far wall. It was tactically viable for a small studio footprint, and the vertical efficiency was mathematically sound. But it was fundamentally flawed for our current operational needs.

The sensory math of my Cerebral Palsy required absolute, unrestricted deep pressure to prevent my legs from locking up at night. Ashley slept flush against my back to act as a thermal and physical anchor; separating us into an upper and lower metal bunk would completely break the perimeter.

"The bunks won't work," I said quietly, looking at Ashley.

She smiled, her eyes tracking toward the back corner of the storage unit. "No, they won't. But that will."

Tucked under a heavy canvas drop cloth was the standard, slightly faded living room sofa from Delaney Street. It wasn't just a piece of furniture. It was the physical barrier from the Monday Wars. It was the exact piece of architecture we had used to build the original Base Camp when the world got too loud.

"The sofa," my chest hitched, a massive wave of restorative relief washing over me. "It pulls out into a queen-sized mattress."

"It serves a dual function," Ashley pointed out to Mom, translating the emotional victory into standard-hardware logic. "It acts as seating during the day to maximize the studio space, and a full-sized bed at night."

"Perfect," Mom agreed, completely unaware that she had just authorized the relocation of our most sacred sanctuary. "We'll pull it out to the front so it's ready to load when Dave calls."

We moved on to the dining logistics. The massive wooden table from the old dining room was an immediate failure.

"I'll buy a small garden patio table and two folding chairs," Mom pivoted smoothly. "It will fit perfectly in the kitchenette corner without blocking the traffic flow to the bathroom."

"I need my old dresser," I added, pointing to the tall, narrow wooden chest of drawers. "The dimensions are vertically efficient."

With the core furniture selected, my brain shifted to the electronic infrastructure. A Base Camp wasn't secure without a digital terminal. I looked toward the center of the unit, where my heavy desktop computer tower was sitting safely inside its Aegis Transport Case. It had been banished to storage because there was simply no room for a massive PC setup in Greg's cramped condo. But at Greenway Court, it would be the nerve center.

"The desk from the girls' room completely fell apart during the move," Mom sighed, looking at the broken particle board stacked near the front. "I'll take you to the store tomorrow and we'll buy a new, compact computer desk to set the tower up on."

"We need the AV hardware, too," I said, scanning the boxes. I found the 17-inch television from Mom's old bedroom and the simple, black rolling TV stand. "And the VCR/DVD combo."

"You can take the TV, but I am not paying for cable right now, Michael," Mom warned me, setting a firm financial parameter. "The budget is tight with the new lease. You'll have to survive on rabbit ears."

"That is acceptable," I nodded.

I knew Greg was going to absolutely hate that arrangement. When Monday rolled around, I would inevitably drag myself over to Baldwin Avenue just to watch AWE Monday Night WAR on his living room television. But that was Greg's problem.

"I need to call Jessica tonight," I told Ashley as we carefully pulled the sofa toward the front of the unit.

"To tell her about the apartment?" Ashley asked, grabbing the other end of the heavy frame.

"To requisition the software," I corrected. "I need her to use her Quantum Blue Badge to buy a copy of Quantum OS XN Professional at COGS pricing and ship it to us immediately."

"Why the Professional edition?" Ashley asked, raising an eyebrow.

"Because we are establishing a secure perimeter," I explained, the rigid logic of the system comforting my frayed nerves. "I need the advanced User Accounts architecture. We are setting up two distinct, password-protected administrative profiles on the desktop. One for me, and one for you. And we are replacing the default Quantum icons with our actual photographs on the Welcome screen. When that computer boots up, I want undeniable, visual proof that the hardware belongs strictly to us."

Ashley’s expression softened into a fierce, protective smile. She understood exactly what I was doing. I was hardcoding her presence into the physical and digital architecture of the apartment.

By five o'clock, the inventory protocol was complete. We pulled the corrugated metal door down and locked the padlock.

The physical objects of our survival were queued up and ready for deployment. The digital mirage of the Best Tech ambush was completely behind us. All we had to do now was survive seventy-two hours of Greg Lawson's rules, and Base Camp would finally be back online.
