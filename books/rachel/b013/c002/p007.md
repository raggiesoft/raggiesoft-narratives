MegaMart was a sensory nightmare of fluorescent lights, echoing aisles, and chaotic foot traffic, but for the first time in weeks, my processor wasn't redlining.

I wasn't scanning the aisles for a predatory recruiter in a button-down shirt, and I wasn't agonizing over the Catch-22 of standard-hardware employment. I was executing a highly specific, mathematically sound provisioning run for my own perimeter.

Ashley pushed the dark blue plastic shopping cart while Mom operated as the logistical director.

"We only have the sedan trunk," Mom reminded us as we hit the home goods section. "So we are only buying what keeps you alive and functional until the moving truck tomorrow morning."

"Sleep architecture first," Ashley stated, steering the cart toward the sporting goods aisle.

We grabbed a queen-sized air mattress in a heavy cardboard box, and Mom explicitly checked the shelf for the electric plug-in air pump to spare my CP legs the repetitive stress of a manual foot-pedal. From the bedding aisle, we selected two firm pillows, a basic set of queen-sized sheets, and a thick, heavy comforter. The weight was essential to provide the sensory pressure my nervous system required.

"Hygiene and fuel," Mom commanded, pivoting us toward the grocery side.

We grabbed a four-pack of toilet paper, two basic bath towels, travel-sized toothpaste, and a generic bar of soap. I looked down at the soap in the blue cart. It was a mundane object, but to me, it represented an absolute architectural victory. When we toured Unit A5, I had immediately scanned the bathroom. It didn't have a tub; it had a dedicated standing shower. But crucially, the floor was a textured, high-grip acrylic pan. It wasn't the terrifying, frictionless tile trap of Greg’s condo. I could finally shower safely.

Finally, we grabbed a case of bottled water and a box of frosted brown sugar toaster pastries.

As we headed toward the registers, Mom stopped abruptly in the seasonal aisle.

"We need a flat surface," Mom decided, grabbing a long, flat cardboard box. "Small, round glass patio table. We can slide the box into the trunk right now." Ashley grabbed two matching metal folding chairs and stacked them onto the bottom rack of the cart.

By the time Mom paid and we loaded the heavy canvas duffel bags, the laptops, and the MegaMart supplies into the sedan, the sun was beginning to dip lower in the Virginia sky.

We drove back to 801 Greenway Court in exhausted silence.

When we unlocked the door to Unit A5, the space was exactly as we had left it: a bare, rectangular void. But this time, we had the hardware to boot up the system.

Mom set the box of toaster pastries on the kitchenette counter. The cooking space was incredibly tight—a literal operational bottleneck. There was a stainless steel sink, a four-burner gas stove, and a single, narrow square of countertop prep space located directly above an under-counter mini-fridge.

"We'll need to buy a standalone rolling cart for a microwave," Ashley observed, instantly running the spatial math of the kitchen.

"Agreed," Mom nodded. She checked the stove dials. "And I'll call the utility company first thing tomorrow morning to get the natural gas turned on. No cooking tonight anyway."

Mom plugged the electric air pump into the wall outlet. The loud, mechanical whine of the motor filled the empty room as the blue plastic mattress slowly expanded, taking up the center of the carpet. Ashley moved efficiently, throwing the fitted sheet over the inflated rubber and spreading out the heavy comforter.

Mom looked at the makeshift bed, then looked at me. Her maternal worry was fighting a losing battle against the sheer relief radiating from my posture.

"I'll be here with a rental truck at nine o'clock tomorrow morning," Mom said softly, pulling her keys out of her purse. "We'll go straight to the storage unit, get the sofa and the TV, and get this place fully operational."

"Understood," I nodded.

She walked over, wrapped her arms around me, and squeezed tight. "I'm proud of you, Michael. You held the line today."

She pulled back, gave Ashley a warm, appreciative smile, and walked out the door. The deadbolt engaged with a heavy *thunk*.

The perimeter was sealed.

Ashley didn't hesitate. She grabbed the box cutter from her duffel bag and sliced open the flat cardboard packaging of the patio table.

For the next twenty minutes, we sat on the carpet in the fading evening light, threading metal screws and attaching the four steel legs to the circular glass top. We didn't need instructions; we just needed the repetitive, logical sequence of assembly.

When we flipped the table upright and set the two folding chairs opposite each other, the room instantly transformed from an empty box into a functional command center.

I unzipped my duffel bag, pulled out my laptop, and set it on the glass surface. Ashley did the same, placing hers right next to mine. We sat down in the folding chairs, opened a pack of brown sugar toaster pastries, and looked at the glowing screens of our hardware.

The gas was off, the fridge was empty, and the only furniture we owned was designed for an outdoor patio. But as I listened to the quiet, rhythmic tapping of Ashley's fingers on her keyboard, my processor finally went completely still.

Base Camp was online.
