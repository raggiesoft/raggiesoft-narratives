When the morning sunlight finally broke through the small front window of Unit A5, it illuminated a completely empty room, a glass patio table, and a blue air mattress. The apartment looked exactly the same.

But everything felt entirely different.

Last night had worked perfectly. In the span of twelve hours, the terrifying, unwritten maze of physical intimacy had been replaced by something safe and predictable. There was no judgment, no high school drama, and zero biological risk. There was only Ashley, making sure I felt safe. We woke up tangled in the heavy navy comforter, and in the quiet of the morning, we figured it out all over again.

By seven-thirty, though, reality caught up with us. Mom was scheduled to arrive with the moving truck at nine o'clock.

We had to transition out of our private bubble and back into the real world. Mom couldn't know what had just happened. We needed to be clean, dressed, and looking like two typical best friends ready for a day of heavy lifting.

We needed to shower. But as Ashley sat up on the mattress, a sharp, sudden spike of separation anxiety hit my chest.

Jessica and Rachel were thousands of miles away in Washington State. Ashley was the absolute last piece of my support system left in the immediate area. The thought of being separated from her, even just standing on opposite sides of a small bathroom door for ten minutes, made my chest tight.

Ashley recognized the shift in my posture instantly. She didn't tell me I was being irrational. She just adjusted the plan.

We stepped into the textured acrylic pan of the standing shower together. It wasn't about romance this time; it was just about staying close while washing off the sweat and exhaustion.

Twenty minutes later, we were standing in the main room, pulling fresh clothes out of our canvas duffel bags.

"How are your legs feeling this morning?" Ashley asked, pulling a clean t-shirt over her head.

"Not too tight," I said, lacing up my sneakers. The heavy comforter and the continuous physical contact had kept my muscles relaxed. "I'm good to help move the furniture today."

I grabbed my cell phone off the glass patio table and flipped it open.

"No signal," I sighed, staring at the screen. "I only have one bar inside the apartment."

Ashley checked her phone and frowned. "Same here. The brick walls must be blocking it. We need to call Ellen so she knows we're awake, but the call will drop in here."

"Let's go outside," I said.

I grabbed the heavy brass key off the table. We walked out the front door, pulling it shut behind us, and I turned the deadbolt. The heavy *thunk* echoed in the quiet hallway.

We walked down the exterior walkway and crossed the quiet street in front of Greenway Court. Just opposite the apartment complex was a small, open grassy field where a few people from the neighborhood were letting their dogs run off-leash in the morning air. There were a couple of slatted wooden benches scattered around the grass.

We found an empty bench and sat down.

The moment we stopped walking, I shifted my weight, leaning heavily against Ashley’s side. I curled my right arm into my chest and rested my head against her shoulder. She immediately adjusted her posture to absorb my weight, wrapping her arm around my back to give me that familiar, grounding squeeze.

"Do you think Mom is going to let Archie come stay with us?" I asked quietly, watching a golden retriever chase a tennis ball across the damp morning grass.

"I think she has to," Ashley reasoned, resting her chin on the top of my head. "Greg doesn't want him at the condo. Plus, you have a ground-floor apartment right across the street from a grassy field. It's the most logical place for him."

"I hope so," I mumbled, leaning further into her.

Ashley flipped her phone open, checking the screen. "Three bars out here."

She dialed Mom’s number and put the phone to her ear.

"Hey, Ellen," Ashley said brightly, her voice shifting back into that capable, responsible tone Mom relied on. "Yep, we're awake and dressed. We're just sitting on a bench across the street from the building."

I nudged Ashley’s side and pointed to my stomach.

"Actually, Ellen, we do have one request," Ashley laughed lightly into the receiver. "Those toaster pastries didn't really hold us over. Could we hit a drive-thru for some actual breakfast before we go to the storage unit?"

Ashley smiled, listening to Mom's response, and then snapped the phone shut.

"Nine o'clock," Ashley confirmed. "With a stop for breakfast sandwiches."

I closed my eyes, listening to the quiet morning sounds of the neighborhood and the dogs barking in the field. We had a place to live, we had breakfast on the way, and we were finally going to bring the Delaney Street sofa home.
