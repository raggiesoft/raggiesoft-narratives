Traffic on Interstate 264 was remarkably light for a Monday morning. Exactly forty-five minutes after locking the door to my childhood, I steered Mom’s sedan into the ground-floor parking garage of the Ghent Villas, pulling perfectly into the reserved stall exactly second from the right.

The physical transition into the new perimeter was immediately jarring. There was no familiar driveway, no sprawling front yard, and no quiet cul-de-sac. We grabbed our canvas bags from the trunk and walked up the wooden exterior decking, the hollow thud of our footsteps echoing under the awning until we reached Unit 2E.

I unlocked the door and pushed it open.

The architectural friction of the condo hit my processor instantly. The front door opened directly into the base of a carpeted staircase. There was absolutely no open foyer to decompress in.

But before the claustrophobia could trigger an operational freeze, a frantic, rhythmic clicking of claws on hardwood rushed down the narrow hallway.

"Archie!" Ashley dropped her heavy bag, a massive grin breaking across her face.

The one-eyed dog practically vibrated with excitement, his tail whipping so hard his entire back half shook. Right behind him, moving with the stiff, lumbering gait of an older gentleman, was Baby.

I didn't care about the bags or the cramped entryway. I dropped straight to my knees on the floor. Baby immediately pushed his massive, graying head directly into my chest, letting out a loud, contented grumble. I wrapped my arms completely around the senior Lab’s thick neck and buried my face in his fur. Archie squeezed in right beside him, aggressively licking Ashley’s hands as she knelt next to me.

They hadn't seen us in weeks during the chaotic transition of the move. The deep, heavy, unyielding pressure of Baby leaning his entire body weight against my chest was exactly the physical compression my fried nervous system was begging for. For three full minutes, we just sat in the tiny entryway, letting the undeniable loyalty of the dogs bleed the panic out of my muscles.

"Okay, boys, let them breathe," Mom’s voice echoed gently from down the hall.

I gave Baby one last squeeze and let Ashley haul me to my feet. The spasticity in my legs was flaring fiercely from the emotional toll of the morning, so I kept one hand firmly anchored on Ashley's shoulder for balance as we walked past the stairs.

To my right, a cramped galley kitchen felt more like a hallway than a room, complete with a tiny, apartment-sized stacked washer and dryer and a modern fridge with a bottom freezer drawer. Mom and Greg Lawson were standing near the island.

Greg was a practical, neurotypical guy. He wasn't overtly hostile like my father had been, but he was an adult who liked his space, and his space was currently being invaded by two teenagers and their heavy baggage.

"Morning, kids," Greg said, offering a tight but polite smile. "Leave the bags by the stairs for now. Come take a seat in the living room. We need to go over the logistics of how this is going to work."

Ashley and I moved to the end of the short hallway and stepped into the living room. It was painfully small, dominated by a television entertainment center and a standard sofa.

I stared at the sofa for a long moment. It didn't pull out into a bed. The physical, impenetrable barrier of the Monday Wars—the exact piece of furniture we had used to build the Base Camp—was permanently gone.

Ashley sensed the spike in my internal telemetry. She sat down on the sofa first, pulling me down right next to her. She immediately shifted her weight, pressing her shoulder and hip completely flush against mine, locking in the physical perimeter.

Mom and Greg pulled up two folding chairs across the small coffee table.

"I know this is a massive adjustment," Mom started, her voice laced with an exhausted, apologetic tone. "The house is officially closed. This is our home base now. But this condo is a fraction of the size of Delaney Street, and it belongs to Greg. So we have to operate on a different set of rules."

"It's just about traffic flow," Greg explained, leaning forward and resting his elbows on his knees. He spoke in clear, literal parameters, which my processor deeply appreciated, even if I hated the rules themselves. "There are four adults living in a two-bedroom unit. If we aren't careful, we are going to constantly be bumping into each other."

He laid out the operational realities of 849 Baldwin Ave.

The galley kitchen was a strictly one-person zone. If someone was cooking, no one else could be in there. The washing machine was too small for massive loads, meaning laundry had to be strictly scheduled.

"And upstairs," Greg continued, looking specifically at me and Ashley. "You two are sharing the room at the top of the stairs, directly across from the linen closet. It has an en-suite bathroom. But the walls are thin, and the floor plan is tight."

"We understand, Mr. Lawson," Ashley replied smoothly, her voice projecting absolute, mature competence. She kept her shoulder firmly pressed against mine, acting as my translator for the neurotypical adults. "Michael and I are used to operating in close quarters. We'll keep our footprint small. You won't even know we're up there."

"I appreciate that, Ashley," Greg nodded, visibly relieved by her neurotypical reassurance. "And Michael, I know this isn't what you're used to. But we're going to make it work. Just keep your routines tight, and we won't have any friction."

My chest hitched slightly, but I forced a stiff nod.

"Good," Mom smiled, though her eyes looked tired. "Why don't you two take your bags upstairs and get settled? We'll figure out dinner later."

We stood up. Ashley grabbed her heavy canvas bag with one hand and offered me her other. I took it, relying on her balance as my CP-tightened legs navigated the narrow, carpeted stairs.

As we hit the top landing and executed the tight U-turn into our new room, the reality of the briefing settled over me. Greg’s rules made perfect logistical sense for standard hardware adults. But to my autistic processor, the condo felt like a minefield of invisible tripwires.

I sat down heavily on the edge of the queen-sized bed, the room so small I could practically touch both walls at once.

"It's too tight," I whispered, the panic starting to hum under my skin again. "If I make a mistake in the kitchen, or if I break a rule... there's nowhere to hide here."

Ashley dropped her bag. She didn't offer empty platitudes. She just sat down directly beside me, wrapping both arms around my ribs and applying a massive, crushing squeeze to short-circuit the rising static in my brain.

"You don't need to hide," the Sentinel promised, her voice a low, unyielding anchor in the cramped room. "Because for the next five weeks, I am your perimeter. We'll navigate the rules together."
