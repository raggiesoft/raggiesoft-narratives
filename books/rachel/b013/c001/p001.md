I opened my eyes, the morning light filtering harshly through the plastic blinds.

I wasn't in my own room. I was lying fully dressed on the bottom mattress of the bunk bed in what used to be my sisters’ room. Beside me, Ashley Raybourn shifted, her arm draped heavily and protectively across my chest. The computer monitor on the desk in the corner was still glowing, a faint, humming rectangle in the quiet space, left on from whatever mindless task we had been using to distract our brains last night.

I sat up slowly, the shift in weight waking the Sentinel. My chest felt impossibly tight.

Hot, unprompted tears immediately started leaking from my eyes, tracking silently down my cheeks as the reality of the calendar processed in my mind. The time difference was a brutal, invisible wall. It was 7:30 AM here in Virginia, which meant it was 4:30 AM on the West Coast. I couldn't call Jessica in Seattle, and I couldn't call Rachel in Vancouver.

Ashley didn't say a word. She just sat up, wrapped both arms around my ribs, and applied a firm, grounding squeeze. I leaned into her, desperate for the deep pressure. She was the absolute last member of the pack left. After we walked out of this house today, she was riding with me to Norfolk. She had promised to spend the rest of the summer guarding my perimeter, but we both knew the clock was ticking. By the last week of August, she would be packing her car to drive west to Shenandoah Valley University.

"Let's do the final sweep," Ashley whispered, her voice rough with sleep but steady with purpose.

We pushed ourselves off the mattress and walked downstairs to get ready for the day. Breakfast was a dull, mechanical affair. The kitchen, usually the loud, chaotic center of the house, was completely dead. Even the rhythmic, comforting sounds of Archie and Baby were gone. The dogs had already been moved to Norfolk, waiting in the unfamiliar condo Mom’s boyfriend owned at 849 Baldwin Avenue.

I loaded my cereal bowl into the dishwasher and pushed the heavy door shut, locking the latch. I hit the 'Start' button, listening to the water cycle kick on for the absolute last time in this house.

Another tear rolled hot down my cheek. I wiped it away roughly with the back of my hand. Today was the final day. Mom had sold the house. The closing was finished, the boxes were moved, and this physical structure—the only real sanctuary I had ever known—would no longer belong to us.

We walked back upstairs, our footsteps echoing far too loudly on the bare wooden treads.

I pushed open the door to the girls' room again. Without the furniture Mom had already packed, the room looked massive and completely wrong. The absence of their things allowed all the phantom memories of our childhood to come flooding back, hitting my system like a physical wave. This house wasn't just wood and drywall. It was the only place we could run to where the world made sense. It had been our literal playground—the place where we mapped out our survival and built a fortress that no one was supposed to be able to destroy.

But the pack had been systematically dismantled. Jessica's departure for NPU in August of 2000 had been a planned, logical extraction. We had time to prepare for it.

Rachel's departure was a violent, catastrophic fracture. My chest heaved with a sudden, sharp sob as my processor flashed back to September 20th, 2001. As soon as the airspace had cleared after the terror attacks, Dad had forced her onto a flight out of ORF, dragging her all the way to a second-floor walk-up in Vancouver, Washington. The structural integrity of our family hadn't just broken; it had been forcibly torn apart by court papers and bitterness.

I walked over to the closet. Tucked all the way in the back, collecting a thick layer of dust, was the heavy plastic toybox from our childhood.

I knelt on the carpet and pulled the lid off. Inside was a chaotic, tangled collection of the past. There were the rigid, structured toys meant for little boys, mixed completely with the brightly colored dolls and ponies meant for my sisters.

I let out a sudden, jagged howl. The sound ripped out of my throat, bouncing off the empty walls.

I didn't care about the societal rules of what an eighteen-year-old high school graduate was supposed to be doing. The crushing heartbreak broke my executive function entirely. I suddenly, desperately wanted to sit on this carpet and play with my sisters. I wanted to map out the imaginary neighborhoods, set up the rigid rules of the game, and include my action figures in their dollhouse. I wanted to regress to a time when the three of us were an unbroken, impenetrable unit, hiding out in a world that no one else could touch.

My hands were shaking as I reached into the box. My internal telemetry was redlining. I needed a physical, tactile representation of the pattern I had lost.

I pulled out the plastic folding dollhouse we used to keep in Jessica's corner of the room. I opened it up on the carpet. I dug through the box and found one of the small, articulated boy dolls and two of the girl dolls.

Slowly, methodically, I set them up in the tiny plastic living room. I arranged them close together, shoulder-to-shoulder, exactly how we used to sit on the downstairs sofa bed when the world outside was too loud. I set them up as a perfect, unbroken little family. Me, Jess, and Rachel. Together.

The standard hardware world outside would never understand why an eighteen-year-old guy was experiencing a catastrophic system crash over a dollhouse in an empty room. But Ashley wasn't the outside world.

She didn't tell me to grow up. She didn't tell me to stop crying. She dropped to the carpet right beside me, wrapping her arms completely around my back and locking her hands over my chest. She pulled me flush against her, letting her physical weight act as a cargo strap to hold my fracturing nervous system together.

She reached her hand out, picked up a fourth plastic doll from the box, and gently set it right next to the other three in the plastic living room.

I looked at the four figures. My breath hitched.

"The perimeter is still intact, Michael," Ashley promised, resting her chin heavily on my shoulder. "It just covers a larger geographic grid now. Seattle, Vancouver, and Norfolk. We're still the pack."

I sat back on my heels, leaning my full weight into her. I reached into my backpack and pulled out the cheap disposable camera I had bought to document the move. I wound the plastic gear until it clicked, raised the viewfinder to my eye, and pressed the button. The bright flash illuminated the dark closet for a fraction of a second. I wanted one last, physical piece of proof that in this playground, we had belonged together.

We sat there on the floor for a long time, letting the deep compression bleed the panic out of my muscles. Finally, Ashley helped haul me to my feet.

I went into the bathroom to splash cold water on my face and clear the redness from my eyes. When I came back out, Ashley had my canvas bag slung over her shoulder.

We walked downstairs together and locked the front door of 1852 Delaney Street for the final time.

We walked out to the driveway where Mom’s sedan was idling. Ashley tossed her bag into the back and climbed into the passenger seat, keeping her promise to stand watch until SVU called her away.

I got into the driver’s seat and put the car in gear. The drive toward Norfolk was usually a mathematically predictable, uneventful route, but today, every mile marker felt impossibly heavy. I was heading into completely uncharted territory. The dogs were waiting for me, and they would be a comfort, but as I pulled onto the interstate and looked in the rearview mirror, watching 1852 Delaney Street disappear forever, I knew the hard truth.

The physical Base Camp was gone, and the long, grueling era of operating without my pack had officially begun.
