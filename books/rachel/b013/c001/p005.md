The suffocating Virginia heat was still radiating off the asphalt when the front door of Unit 2E clicked open at five-thirty.

I was leaning heavily against the tiny kitchen island, desperately drinking a glass of ice water, when Mom and Greg walked in. My physical chassis was completely failing. Hours of riding heavy CVTA transit buses, combined with the uneven walking and the constant, jarring vibrations of the road, had sent the Cerebral Palsy spasticity in my legs into overdrive. I wasn't just standing stiffly; my right knee was locked, and I was gripping the edge of the granite counter just to keep myself upright.

Greg set his briefcase down, his practical eyes immediately zeroing in on my rigid posture and the sweat soaking my shirt.

"Did you get any traction today?" Greg asked, loosening his tie as he stepped into the galley kitchen. "Or did you spend the whole afternoon riding the bus again?"

My jaw tightened. I desperately wanted to explain the sheer volume of parameters I had tried to navigate. I wanted to explain the physical agony of the transit grid. But my processor was redlining from the pain and the executive exhaustion. The Gray Area Freeze deployed, completely locking my vocal cords.

"He is hitting the targets, Mr. Lawson," Ashley stated smoothly, stepping out from the narrow hallway.

She walked over to the island and slapped her thick spiral notebook down onto the granite counter. She flipped it open to her meticulously hand-drawn spreadsheet, displaying the dates, the transit routes, and the store names.

"He hit the Admiral Center, the Little Creek corridor, and Glenrock Mall today," Ashley explained, operating in full Sentinel mode to intercept the neurotypical interrogation. "But the transit routes are incredibly taxing on his hardware, and when he finally gets in front of a manager, they visually disqualify him because of his legs. They aren't going to hire a cashier who can't stand for an eight-hour shift."

Greg leaned over the island, looking down at the columns of ink. He didn't look sympathetic; he looked mildly exasperated by the inefficiency.

"This is exactly why you shouldn't be taking the bus," Greg sighed, shaking his head at the spreadsheet. "It's a waste of the whole day. If you want to get hired, you need to be efficient. You need to drive to these interviews so you're fresh when you walk in the door."

I stared at him, my chest heaving, my brain completely short-circuiting at the blatant flaw in his math.

"He can't drive, Greg," Ashley pointed out, her tone tight but fiercely controlled. "Ellen has the sedan at work all day. There is no vehicle for him to use. The bus is the only option."

"Which is exactly why securing a job is the priority," Greg countered smoothly, completely missing the impossible paradox he had just constructed. He looked at me, delivering the parameters with absolute, neurotypical authority. "You get a job. You save up your paychecks. And the very first thing you do is buy a used car. Then you don't have to worry about the bus system ruining your legs."

He had just built a flawless, unassailable circle of flawed logic. I needed a car to get a job, but I needed a job to buy a car. I was trapped in an impossible loop, and the adult in the room refused to acknowledge the missing variable.

Greg shifted his attention, looking directly at Ashley.

"And speaking of cars," Greg continued, "why aren't your name and routes in this notebook, Ashley? If you two are going out every day, you should be dropping off applications just as fast as he is. You need to be saving for a car, too."

Ashley blinked, her own processor stalling for a fraction of a second at the sheer absurdity of the question.

"Mr. Lawson, it is the middle of July," Ashley said, stating the timeline as a rigid, undeniable fact. "I leave for Shenandoah Valley University at the end of August. No retail manager is going to hire someone who has to quit in exactly five weeks. It makes me entirely unhirable."

Greg waved his hand dismissively, brushing past the logistical reality as if it were just an excuse.

"That's a quitter's mindset," Greg lectured, leaning back against the fridge. "You don't tell them you're leaving in five weeks. You just get the job, put the money in the bank, and put your two weeks' notice in when it's time to pack for college. It's called playing the game."

The cramped condo went dead silent.

Mom looked down at the floor, offering absolutely no backup, quietly complicit in the standard hardware script. Greg looked at both of us, completely satisfied that he had solved our logistical problems with a motivational speech.

"Get some rest tonight," Greg instructed, picking up his briefcase. "Tomorrow is a new day. Hit the pavement again, and let's see some actual results."

He walked up the stairs, leaving the heavy, suffocating weight of his expectations hanging in the galley kitchen.

I stood there gripping the granite counter, the spasticity in my leg vibrating painfully against the cabinets. The rules of the adult world weren't just confusing; they were rigged. They demanded results based on tools we didn't possess and timelines that didn't make sense.

I looked at Ashley. She slowly pulled the spiral notebook off the counter and hugged it to her chest, her jaw tight with silent fury.

The physical Base Camp was gone. The Sentinel could log the data, and she could shield me from the worst of the sensory noise, but as I limped away from the kitchen and dragged myself toward the stairs, the terrifying truth of the Baldwin Ave condo settled over me.

There was no sanctuary here. And in five weeks, I would be facing this completely alone.
