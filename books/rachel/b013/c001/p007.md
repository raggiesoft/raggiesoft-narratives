With the apartment magazine tossed onto the floor and the plan to get out of the condo officially set for tomorrow, the adrenaline of the day finally evaporated, leaving behind a heavy, bone-deep exhaustion.

Ashley reached over and clicked off the small bedside lamp, plunging the cramped bedroom into shadows, illuminated only by the amber glow of the streetlights filtering through the blinds.

I laid back against the queen-sized bed, and for the first time all day, my physical chassis experienced genuine relief. The mattress in this guest room was incredibly soft. As I let my weight settle, the material completely yielded, sinking in and enveloping my back and shoulders. For my autistic processor, it was a flawless sensory environment; the mattress pressed up against my sides, acting like a massive, full-body weighted blanket that finally began to quiet the lingering spasms in my CP-affected legs.

Ashley climbed in next to me, the soft mattress shifting to accommodate her weight. She pulled the covers up, leaving a few inches of space between us to let my overheated system regulate, but staying close enough that I could feel the familiar, steady rhythm of her breathing.

She reached for the cordless phone we had brought up from the kitchen.

"It's 9:02," Ashley whispered into the quiet room. "Which means it's just past six o'clock in Federal Way."

My chest hitched. The geographic math always felt like a physical blow, but tonight, after the crushing failure of the standard hardware world and the hostility of Greg's condo, I desperately needed my family.

Ashley dialed the long-distance number, pressed the speakerphone button, and rested the handset on the soft mattress between our pillows.

The line rang twice before picking up.

"Hello?" Jessica’s voice came through the small speaker. The audio quality was slightly compressed by the 2,800 miles of copper wire, but just hearing the familiar cadence of her voice made my chest physically ache.

"Hi, Jess," I rasped, my voice sounding incredibly small and fragile in the dark room.

"Michael!" Jessica’s tone shifted instantly, the fierce, protective older sister kicking in the second she heard my voice. "Are you okay? You sound exhausted."

"I am," I whispered.

"He had a really bad day," Ashley added softly, leaning toward the phone. "Greg's condo is suffocating him. And trying to take the bus system everywhere is destroying his legs. We're going out tomorrow to find an apartment for him."

There was a heavy pause on the other end of the line. Then, the sound of a cabinet slamming in the background.

"Jess burned the garlic bread," Rachel’s voice drifted over the speaker. She sounded so close, like she was standing right next to the phone.

"I merely *crisped* the garlic bread, Rachel," Jessica fired back, though her tone was gentle. "And considering you just spent an eight-hour shift at The Spot dealing with customers, you can eat the crisped bread or make your own dinner."

Tears pricked the corners of my eyes, sliding hot and silent into the soft fabric of my pillow. Just hearing them bicker over dinner in the kitchen—hearing the mundane, everyday reality of their life together in Washington—shattered the last of my emotional defenses.

My baby sister wasn't trapped in Vancouver anymore. She was living safely in Jessica’s apartment. They were together, but I was 2,800 miles away, trapped in a tiny bedroom in Norfolk.

"I miss you guys," I choked out, a sudden sob ripping out of my throat before I could stop it. "I miss you so much. It's so loud here, and Greg doesn't understand anything, and the house on Delaney Street is gone."

"Oh, Michael," Jessica said, her own voice suddenly thick with emotion. "I know. I'm so sorry. Do you want me to fly back? I can talk to my professors at NPU. I can get on a plane tomorrow morning."

The offer was so genuine it physically hurt. But my processor knew the math. Pulling Jessica out of college would ruin the life she was building.

"No," I cried quietly, wiping my eyes with the back of my hand. "No, you have to stay there. Ashley is taking care of me. But it's just... it's really hard."

"It feels wrong," Rachel said quietly into the phone. "It feels wrong eating dinner without you. The map is just too big now."

"I know," I whispered, another wave of tears soaking the pillowcase.

I closed my eyes, letting the enveloping softness of the mattress hold my exhausted body. I listened to the faint, static-laced sound of my sisters breathing on the other side of the country. I wanted nothing more than to reach through the speaker, pull them into this room, and lock the door so the rest of the world could never touch us again.

"We're not hanging up," Jessica promised softly. "We'll just leave the phone on the counter while we eat. You just listen, okay? We're right here."

"Okay," I whispered.

Ashley reached over and gently pressed the mute button on our handset, leaving the connection open so the faint, ambient sounds of the Federal Way apartment—the clinking of silverware, the hum of their refrigerator, the murmur of their voices—could filter into our dark bedroom.

I turned my head, burying my wet face into Ashley’s shoulder. The Sentinel wrapped one arm securely around my back, applying that deep, grounding pressure while the soft mattress held us from below.

The family was shattered across 2,800 miles, but as I lay in the dark listening to my sisters on the open phone line, my fractured processor finally began to power down for the night.
