The entry-level job hunt of 2003 was not designed for someone running on my hardware, and the logistics of the Ghent condo made it infinitely harder.

Greg’s parameter had been clear: I needed a job to cover my own gas and expenses now that high school was over. Mom had tried to help by handing Ashley and me two freshly printed CVTA 30-Day Passes. They were small, magnetic-stripe cards, valid for unlimited rides for exactly thirty days from the first swipe. I respected the logic of the hunt, and I made it my absolute, hyper-focused mission.

But Mom needed the sedan for work during the day, which meant the protective, air-conditioned bubble of a personal vehicle was gone. I was entirely reliant on the sprawling Norfolk bus grid.

By 2:00 PM on Thursday, the coastal Virginia heat and the constant, jarring vibrations of the transit system had pushed my internal battery dangerously close to zero.

The walking-distance options around the condo had been eliminated on day one. Colley Avenue and the Redgate district were packed with restaurants, but my processor had hit a massive, insurmountable wall on the very first application.

*“Applicants are required to possess a valid City of Norfolk Food Handler's Permit.”*

To my literal, rule-abiding brain, a printed prerequisite was absolute law. I didn't know that getting the permit simply required sitting through a ninety-minute class hosted by the Department of Environmental Health. I just saw the parameter, recognized I didn't meet it, and locked up. The Gray Area Freeze deployed instantly. I crossed every restaurant off the list.

"Okay, scratch the food industry," Ashley pivoted smoothly. We were sitting side-by-side on the sticky vinyl seats of the CVTA Route 2 bus as it rumbled up Hampton Boulevard. "What about the university?"

We drove past the sprawling brick campus of Virginia Dominion University.

I looked out the smeared bus window at the massive VDU Bookstore and the Pro-Dine dining halls, my brain immediately running the literal parameters of the environment.

"I can't," I told her, my voice flat, resting my head against the vibrating glass. "It's the VDU Bookstore. I'm not a VDU student. You have to be enrolled to get those jobs."

It was a completely incorrect assumption. I didn't understand that the bookstore and the dining services were run by third-party corporate contractors. As far as my autistic operating system was concerned, the building said VDU, therefore the jobs were strictly reserved for VDU students. I lacked the neurotypical audacity to walk in and question the sign.

With food and the university out, we were forced to execute a brutal transfer.

We rode Route 2 all the way to Little Creek Road, stepping off the bus into the punishing afternoon sun right in front of Chapel's Chicken. My legs were already burning from the physical effort of stabilizing myself on the moving bus. Ashley stayed right at my side as we walked a block up Baylor Place to catch our connection.

When the Route 15 bus finally arrived, it initiated a grueling, agonizing hour-long ride across the city to Glenrock Mall.

I had done the logistical math earlier in the week: if I actually managed to get a job at the mall, I wouldn't take this route. I would take the Route 2 to Downtown Norfolk and transfer to the Route 20—a forty-five-minute ride that was far more efficient. But for the initial application sweep, we were stuck on the 15.

The physical toll of the system was devastating. The constant bumping of the bus floorboards and the heavy, uneven walking had caused the Cerebral Palsy spasticity in my legs to flare fiercely. By the time we walked into Pixel Stop near the mall's food court, my rigid posture and limping gait were glaringly obvious.

It made perfect, logical sense to apply there. I had the specs of the Nova 64, the MagnaByte ecosystem, and every current RPG completely memorized. I knew the inventory better than the people working there. But when I handed my paper application to the manager, her eyes immediately performed a prejudiced visual assessment.

"We're really looking for someone with prior cashiering experience," she said, looking sympathetically at my stiff legs. "And honestly, it's an eight-hour shift of standing perfectly still behind the register. We don't have stools."

My brain held all the correct data, but my physical chassis couldn't pass the inspection.

When we finally took the buses back to Ghent and walked up the wooden exterior stairs to Unit 2E, the crushing weight of the week finally caught up to me. The rejections, the physical pain in my legs, and the impossible neurotypical riddles on the application forms had completely drained me. I felt like a broken, defective machine.

As soon as we reached the safety of the tiny upstairs bedroom, I collapsed onto the edge of the queen-sized bed, burying my face in my hands. The system crash was imminent.

Ashley didn't say a word. She didn't try to give me a pep talk about how I would get the next one. She just dropped her heavy canvas bag, sat down right beside me, and wrapped her arms completely around my torso. She pulled me flush against her side, applying a massive, deep-pressure hug.

It was the ultimate physical countermeasure. The heavy, unyielding squeeze grounded my frantic nervous system, acting as a manual override for the panic.

While she held me together with her right arm, she reached into her bag with her left hand and pulled out a thick spiral notebook.

She opened it to a meticulously maintained, hand-drawn spreadsheet. It listed every single place we had walked to, every CVTA route we had taken, the transfer times, the name of the manager I had spoken to, and the exact reason for the rejection.

It was her Sentinel logbook.

Ashley knew exactly how Greg’s standard hardware brain worked. She knew that when he got home from work, he was going to look at me—sitting in his condo without a job—and assume I just wasn't trying. Because the autistic effort is invisible to the outside world, she was translating my physical and mental agony into hard, undeniable data.

"When he asks," Ashley promised, resting her chin on my shoulder and tapping the notebook, "I am going to show him the math. I am going to prove exactly how hard you are fighting."

I leaned my heavy head against hers, closing my eyes. I didn't have a job, and the physical Base Camp was gone, but as long as the Sentinel was holding the logbook, I wasn't completely defenseless.
