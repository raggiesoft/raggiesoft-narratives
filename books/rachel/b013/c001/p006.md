The suffocating tension from the kitchen had followed us upstairs.

Ashley and I were sitting cross-legged on the queen-sized bed, the door firmly shut against the rest of the condo. We hadn't spoken since we came upstairs. I was staring blankly at the wall, my processor locked in a dark, spinning loop of Greg’s impossible logistical paradox, while my right leg continued to twitch with residual CP spasticity.

At seven-thirty, there was a soft knock on the door.

Ashley shifted, her Sentinel posture instantly returning, but before she could answer, Mom pushed the door open.

She didn't look like the woman who had quietly stood by in the kitchen. She looked exhausted, guilty, and fiercely determined. She closed the door behind her and walked over to the bed, dropping a thick, glossy magazine onto the mattress between us.

The cover read: *The Hampton Roads Apartment Locator*. It was one of those free, heavy catalogs you could grab from the racks at Harvest Fresh or Skipper's Chicken.

I stared at the cover, my brain struggling to process the variable.

"This isn't working," Mom said quietly, keeping her voice low so it wouldn't carry down the stairs. "Greg is a good man, but he is completely blind to the reality of your hardware, Michael. And I was wrong to just stand there and let him corner you."

I looked up at her, my chest tightening.

"You need a Base Camp," Mom continued, her voice firming up. "A real one. You need a space where you control the perimeter and the rules, without having to tiptoe around someone else's expectations. So, we are going to find you an apartment."

My eyes went wide. "Mom, I don't have a job. The whole point of the fight downstairs was that I have zero income."

"I am paying for it," Mom stated, cutting off my panic with a rigid, non-negotiable parameter. "I'll sign the lease as the guarantor, and I will pay the rent until you secure a job that actually accommodates your legs. Ashley, you will stay with him and run the perimeter until it is time for you to drive to SVU."

Ashley let out a long, heavy exhale, the tight, defensive line of her shoulders instantly dropping. "Thank you, Ellen."

"Don't thank me yet," Mom sighed, sitting down on the edge of the mattress. "We have to actually find a place that works. Open the book."

I pulled the glossy catalog toward me. My analytical processor immediately booted up, shifting from despair to tactical logistics. Finding an apartment was a math problem, and I was exceptionally good at math.

We started flipping through the pages, but it quickly became apparent that the standard hardware world of Norfolk real estate was just as hostile as the job market.

"Okay, what about this one?" Mom pointed to a massive, sprawling complex near Ocean View, showing a picture of a large swimming pool.

I looked at the address and immediately cross-referenced the geographic location in my head. "No," I said flatly. "It's directly beneath the flight path for Naval Station Norfolk. The F-18s run drills all day. The acoustic static from the jet engines would trigger a system crash within twenty-four hours. It's a sensory nightmare."

Mom nodded, completely understanding, and turned the page.

"This one is on Military Highway," Ashley suggested, tapping a half-page ad. "The rent is well within the budget, and it's physically a much larger space than this condo."

I looked at the page, then ran the CVTA transit grid through my head. "The nearest bus stop for Route 15 is across the street from the complex," I calculated, my chest tightening slightly. "Military Highway is six lanes of high-speed traffic with no sidewalks and no crosswalks. My CP limits my mobility. If I have to dodge traffic on foot every single day just to reach the bus grid, I'm going to get hit."

"Cross it off," Ashley said immediately, vetoing it on physical safety parameters alone.

We spent the next hour turning pages, but every glossy advertisement represented a massive logistical failure. The high-rises in Downtown Norfolk were too crowded and required navigating elevators filled with strangers. The garden apartments near the university were completely disconnected from the transit routes I needed.

By eight-thirty, we had reached the back cover of the magazine.

Out of hundreds of listings, not a single apartment fit the exact, rigid parameters my neurobiology and physical chassis required.

I pushed the heavy catalog away, a familiar, heavy exhaustion settling back into my bones. "The book is useless. The math doesn't work for any of them."

"Then we don't use the book," Mom decided, standing up from the bed. She wasn't defeated; she was pivoting the strategy. "Tomorrow, while Greg is at work, I will take the sedan. The three of us are going to drive a grid pattern through the neighborhoods of Ghent and West Olney. We won't rely on corporate listings. We will look for independent 'For Rent' signs until we find a perimeter that is physically and structurally safe."

She leaned down and kissed the top of my head, then squeezed Ashley’s shoulder.

"We are getting you out of this condo, Michael," Mom promised. "Pack a day bag for tomorrow."

As she slipped out of the room, Ashley and I looked at each other. The physical friction of Baldwin Ave was still pressing in on us, but the heavy, suffocating dread was gone. We had an extraction plan.
