The heavy, crushing pressure of Ashley’s arms around my ribs slowly bled the buzzing panic out of my muscles. We sat on the edge of the queen-sized bed for a long time, the stifling heat of the Virginia summer pressing against the small bedroom window.

Eventually, the physical reality of the morning caught up with us. We were both covered in the heavy, suffocating dust of the Delaney Street attic, our clothes sticking to our skin from the sweat of loading the cars.

"We need to wash this off," I muttered, carefully shifting my weight to stand up. My legs were burning, the familiar, tight spasticity of my Cerebral Palsy flaring up after hours of heavy lifting and emotional redlining.

"Agreed," Ashley sighed, pushing herself off the mattress. She reached into her canvas bag and pulled out her toiletries and a clean t-shirt. "You go first. I'll take my turn after you're done."

It was the standard script. Two young adults sharing a space meant establishing a rotating schedule to maintain standard hardware privacy.

I nodded, grabbing my own towel, and took the three short steps into the en-suite bathroom.

I reached out to turn on the light switch, but as the small room illuminated, I stopped dead in the doorway. I didn't step inside. My eyes immediately locked onto the architecture of the shower, and my processor began running a rapid, terrifying sequence of spatial geometry and physical physics.

"What's wrong?" Ashley asked, stepping up right behind my left shoulder.

"It's a hazard," I whispered, my chest tightening as my internal telemetry began to spike all over again.

I pointed to the shower enclosure. It wasn't a standard setup. There was no fiberglass tub to step into, and absolutely no raised lip to contain the water. Instead, the floor was 100% smooth, glazed tile that sloped slightly inward toward a center drain. Worse, the walls were the exact same slick tile, completely devoid of any textured surfaces.

There were no grab bars. There was absolutely nothing to hold onto.

For Greg or Mom—people running on standard hardware with flawless equilibrium—it was just a modern, space-saving bathroom design. But for me, it was a frictionless trap.

"I can't do it, Ash," I said, my voice dropping to a harsh, panicked whisper. "My legs are already shaking from the CP. The second the soap and hot water hit that tile, it's going to turn into a sheet of ice. If my knee buckles or a muscle spasms, there's nothing to catch me. I'll hit the floor."

The operational freeze locked my feet to the carpet. The condo had already stripped away the physical perimeter of Delaney Street and the safety of the pull-out sofa. Now, it was actively threatening my physical safety over the simple act of trying to get clean.

Ashley didn't offer empty reassurances. She didn't tell me I was overthinking it or that I would be fine if I was just careful. She stepped past me into the bathroom, looking down at the sloping tile and running her hand flat against the smooth, unyielding wall.

She did the mechanical math, and she reached the exact same conclusion I did.

"You're right," Ashley said, turning back to face me. Her voice shifted instantly into the low, absolute register of the Sentinel. "It's a massive fall risk. So, we scratch the schedule. We don't take turns."

I blinked, my processor struggling to catch up. "What?"

"We shower together," she stated, her tone completely factual and entirely devoid of hesitation. "My balance is perfect. I don't have the spasticity. I can plant my feet on that tile and not move an inch. You use me as the grab bar."

In the outside world, a neurotypical young adult girl offering to step into a tiny, enclosed shower with a young adult guy would carry a massive, complicated web of romantic or sexual context.

But inside our perimeter, those standard societal rules simply did not exist. We had been best friends since we were toddlers. She had spent the last two years acting as a literal, physical cargo strap to hold my fracturing nervous system together whenever the world got too loud. To my brain, her physical body wasn't a complication; it was the ultimate, infallible safety mechanism.

"Ashley, you don't have to—"

"Michael, look at me," she interrupted gently, stepping back out of the bathroom and placing both hands firmly on my shoulders. She locked eyes with me, delivering the parameters with crystal-clear, non-negotiable logic to bypass my Gray Area Freeze. "We are operating in hostile architecture. I am not going to stand on the other side of that door and listen for you to crack your head open on the tile just because society says teenagers need privacy. Safety overrides the rules. Always."

I let out a long, shaky breath, the tension bleeding out of my jaw. The sheer, undeniable logic of her plan instantly stabilized my nervous system.

"I have to hold onto you the whole time," I warned her quietly, needing to explicitly define the physical boundaries of the workaround. "If I let go, I'll slip."

"Then you don't let go," she promised, her grip tightening on my shoulders. "I will anchor you. We wash the dirt off, we keep you upright, and we survive the condo. Understood?"

"Understood," I whispered, the crushing weight of the morning finally lifting just a fraction of an inch.

We didn't hesitate, and we didn't negotiate. We simply executed the workaround.

Ten minutes later, the tiny en-suite bathroom was completely filled with thick, suffocating steam. The exhaust fan hummed loudly overhead, but it couldn't compete with the deafening, chaotic roar of the scalding hot water hitting the slick tile floor.

The environment was exactly the frictionless hazard we had predicted. The soapy water turned the inward-sloping tile into a sheet of ice. The spasticity in my legs flared violently, my knees trembling as my CP-affected muscles fought a losing battle against gravity.

But I didn't fall.

Ashley stood directly in front of me, her feet planted wide and immovably on the tile. Because her neurobiology and physical balance were completely intact, she was incredibly stable.

I had my arms wrapped heavily over her shoulders, my hands locked tightly behind her neck. I surrendered my physical weight entirely to her, letting my center of gravity sag forward until I was resting flush against her collarbone. She had her arms wrapped firmly around my waist, acting as a literal, unyielding cargo strap to keep my hips locked in place.

It wasn't a hug. It was architectural scaffolding.

I squeezed my eyes shut, burying my face into the crook of her neck to block out the visual static of the small room. The scalding hot water beat down on my back, a massive, overwhelming sensory input that effectively drowned out the lingering hum of the morning's trauma. The intense heat washed away the sweat, the dust of the plastic toybox, and the terrifying chill of the empty Delaney Street house.

We stood there in the cramped, steamy shower, locked together in absolute silence. The outside world would have fundamentally misunderstood the mechanics of this perimeter. But as I leaned all of my broken, exhausted weight into my best friend, feeling her hold me flawlessly steady against the slick tile, I realized the transition to the condo hadn't defeated us.

The walls of Base Camp had changed, but my Anchor was still standing right beside me.
