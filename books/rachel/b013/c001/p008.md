We listened to the faint, comforting ambient noise of the Federal Way apartment for another thirty minutes. The static of the line was a tether, but as the clock ticked closer to ten, the exhaustion pulling at my physical chassis became impossible to ignore.

Ashley recognized the shift in my breathing. She reached over, gently un-muting our handset.

"Hey, Jess," Ashley said softly, breaking the long silence. "We need to let him sleep. His legs are starting to lock up."

"Okay," Jessica replied, the protective older-sister frequency softening her voice. "Are you going to be okay tonight, Michael?"

I swallowed the heavy lump in my throat, forcing my vocal cords past the Gray Area Freeze. "Yeah. I'm okay."

"We'll see each other soon," Rachel promised, her voice trembling just a fraction. "I don't know the math yet, but we will."

"We will," I echoed blindly. It was a promise built entirely on hope rather than logistical facts, but my processor needed it to be true. "I love you guys."

"We love you too," Jessica said quietly. "We're right here if you need us. Goodnight."

There was a soft click, and the West Coast connection severed. The dial tone buzzed harshly in the dark room before Ashley reached over and placed the handset back on the charger. The silence of the Baldwin Ave condo crashed back down on us, heavy and claustrophobic.

I tried to shift my weight on the mattress, but a violent spasm ripped through my right thigh. I let out a sharp hiss of pain. The hours on the CVTA transit grid had finally caught up with me. The spasticity in my CP-affected legs was redlining, the muscles locking tight and burning with deep, radiating cramps.

"Shower," Ashley ordered quietly, immediately pushing the covers back. "We need to get the heat on those muscles before they completely lock up."

We didn't hesitate or negotiate the logistics. We moved directly into the frictionless trap of the en-suite bathroom.

Once again, we bypassed the standard-hardware rules of privacy in favor of absolute survival mechanics. The tiny, 100% tile shower filled with suffocating steam. I surrendered my physical weight entirely to Ashley, locking my arms around her neck while she stood wide and immovable against the slick floor. The scalding water battered my back, providing a massive sensory override while the heat slowly forced the rigid muscles in my calves and thighs to release their agonizing grip.

When we finally turned the water off and dried the moisture from our skin, the physical reality of my condition became starkly clear.

The spasticity was still humming just beneath the surface. My sensory telemetry was so utterly fried that the mere concept of putting clothes back on—the friction of cotton against my hypersensitive skin, or the restrictive pressure of an elastic waistband against my hips—felt like an intolerable threat.

Ashley saw the rigid panic in my eyes as I looked at my sleep shorts. She understood the sensory math instantly.

"Leave them," she said gently, tossing her own t-shirt aside. "No barriers tonight."

We needed maximum thermal regulation and absolute, unrestricted deep pressure. We climbed back into the enveloping softness of the queen-sized bed completely stripped of fabric.

I curled tightly onto my side, drawing my aching knees up toward my chest to relieve the tension in my lower back. Ashley didn't just lie next to me; she pulled herself completely flush against my back, slotting her knees behind mine.

Without the friction of clothing to interfere, the physical mechanics of her proximity were flawless. Her skin radiated a steady, intense heat directly into my over-taxed nervous system, acting as a massive thermal battery for my cramping muscles. She draped her arm heavily over my ribs, locking her hand against my chest, pulling me into a suffocatingly tight, full-body hold.

It wasn't romantic. It was a perfectly executed sensory compression protocol.

The physical Base Camp on Delaney Street was gone, my sisters were 2,800 miles away, and the job hunt was a terrifying paradox. But as I lay in the dark, held together entirely by the unyielding grip and warmth of my best friend, my processor finally went offline, letting the exhaustion drag me under.
