The rhythmic, predictable *click-click-click* of Grandma Loretta’s wooden knitting needles had completely stabilized the acoustic environment of Room 412. Rachel and I remained perfectly anchored in our two-person perimeter next to the bed, listening to the quiet hum of the hospital's HVAC system.

The heavy wooden door swung open, breaking the silence.

Dan stepped into the room, bringing a rush of cold November air with him. He was wearing a heavy jacket, and slung over his right shoulder was a thick, black canvas laptop bag.

"Morning, Loretta. Hey, kids," Dan greeted us quietly, keeping his volume low to respect the sterile space. He walked to the side of the hospital bed, his eyes dropping to the massive, heavy plaster splints elevating Jessica’s legs. He winced slightly, the reality of the trauma hitting him, but he quickly forced a supportive smile and looked back up at her face. "How are you holding up, Jess?"

"I've been better, Dan," Jessica murmured, offering a weak, exhausted half-smile. The heavy dose of IV narcotics was relentlessly pulling at her eyelids, dragging her deep into a chemical fog.

"I figured," Dan said, unzipping the heavy canvas bag. "I was talking to Ellen this morning before she crashed. She said you were driving yourself crazy staring at the ceiling. I know you've been obsessing over that Quantum Catalyst Initiative deadline."

He reached into the bag and pulled out a monolithic, dark gray brick of a laptop. It was an older, heavy machine, thick as a dictionary, with a clunky plastic latch securing the lid. He set it gently onto Jessica’s rolling tray table.

"I don't use this one for work anymore," Dan explained, pushing the tray table closer to her chest. "It’s completely wiped clean. It's running Quantum OS 98, and I loaded a fresh install of Quantum Suite 97 on it. I know it's not much, but I figured it would at least give you a screen to stare at so you could start mapping out some ideas."

Jessica’s eyes widened slightly. She reached out, her hands resting on the heavy plastic lid. "Dan, thank you. This is exactly what I needed."

She popped the mechanical latch and lifted the screen. The heavy hinges groaned in protest. She hit the power button, and the machine whirred to life, the cooling fan immediately spinning up like a tiny jet engine.

As the blocky teal desktop of Quantum OS 98 rendered on the screen, Jessica’s pragmatic, systems-level brain immediately began running diagnostics.

"Does it have an IDE on it?" Jessica asked, her fingers hovering over the trackpad. "Any standard compilers? Quantum Developer?"

Dan shook his head, offering an apologetic shrug. "No, sorry, Jess. I never used it for coding. It’s strictly a consumer setup. Word processors, spreadsheets, that kind of thing. You aren't going to be able to compile any actual executables on it."

Jessica nodded, digesting the architectural limitation. Without a professional Integrated Development Environment (IDE) to translate her logic into machine code, she couldn't actually build the memory-allocation intercept she needed for the competition. The machine was functionally blind to C++ or x86 Assembly.

"That's okay," Jessica said, her voice tight with absolute determination. She clicked the mouse, opening a blank document in *Quantum WritePad*. "I don't need a compiler to build the blueprint. I can map the raw structural logic in plain text."

"Alright. I'll leave you to it," Dan said, giving her shoulder a gentle, encouraging pat. He turned to Grandma Loretta. "I'll swing back by around six to give you a ride home, Loretta."

"Drive safe, Dan," Grandma smiled, never missing a stitch with her green yarn.

When the door clicked shut, the true toll of the effort became instantly visible.

Without Dan in the room to maintain a brave face for, Jessica’s posture sagged against the thin hospital pillow. The glaring, unshielded backlight of the LCD screen was actively triggering her pain response. She pushed her tinted glasses up the bridge of her nose, squinting against the harsh light.

I watched my older sister fight a brutal, invisible war. The pulverized bones in her heels were radiating a white-hot agony that the heavy painkillers were barely keeping at bay. The synthetic narcotics were making the room spin, turning her usually razor-sharp thoughts into a sluggish, disjointed mess.

She stared at the blinking black cursor on the white screen.

*Memory Routing Protocol - Phase 1.* She typed the words slowly, her fingers heavy and uncoordinated on the mechanical keys.

She stopped, letting out a ragged, frustrated exhale. She closed her eyes, resting her head back against the pillow as a wave of chemical nausea washed over her.

"Jess?" Rachel whispered softly from my side, her dark eyes filled with deep, loyal concern.

"I'm okay, Rachel," Jessica slurred, keeping her eyes closed for another ten seconds to let the room stop spinning.

She was drowning in the fog of the hospital, entirely stripped of her physical independence, and trapped inside a heavily medicated body that was actively fighting her. But the protector of our pack simply refused to surrender.

She opened her eyes, forced her hands back onto the plastic keyboard, and continued to type. She couldn't write the code, but she was going to use every single ounce of conscious energy she possessed to lay the foundation for her empire, one painful, exhausted keystroke at a time.
