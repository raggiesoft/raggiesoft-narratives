My internal clock woke me with a sudden, quiet jolt. I blinked in the dark, my eyes slowly adjusting to the multi-colored glow of the Christmas tree illuminating the living room. The green digital numbers on the VCR clock read exactly 4:21 AM.

My first conscious thought didn't even register the holiday. My processor immediately locked onto a completely different timeline: *noon*. Just the realization that Bonnie was going to be standing on our front porch in a few hours sent a warm, electric hum straight through my chest.

I carefully shifted my weight, trying not to disrupt the heavy, grounding pressure of Rachel sleeping perfectly flush against my right side, or wake Jessica on my left. As I turned my head, my eyes swept past the front-wall brick fireplace and landed on the dark blue polyester carpet beneath the tree.

My breath caught in my throat.

Sitting directly in front of the neatly stacked, wrapped boxes from Mom and Dad was a massive, pristine pile of unwrapped magic.

"Bug," I whispered, gently nudging Rachel's shoulder. "Bug, wake up."

Rachel stirred, her dark eyes fluttering open. She blinked, confused for a microsecond, before she followed my gaze. She let out a tiny, sharp gasp, her fingers instantly digging into my t-shirt.

I reached over my left shoulder and lightly shook Jessica's arm.

"Jess," I breathed.

Jessica groaned, fighting through the heavy chemical weight of her nighttime medication. She squinted at the tree, pushing her glasses up the bridge of her nose. The moment her analytical brain registered the unwrapped payload, she went completely still.

"He did it," I whispered, completely awestruck.

To our hyper-vigilant, tactical processors, the evidence was absolutely staggering. The jolly old elf hadn't just bypassed the heavy brown painted wooden garage door and the locked deadbolts. He had completely evaded the biological threat-detection systems of Baby and Archie, who were still snoring softly at the foot of the mattress. And most impressively of all, he had stood mere *inches* away from our highly sensitive, tightly anchored perimeter to stage the entire drop zone without shifting a single floorboard or changing the air pressure enough to wake us.

Mom had been mathematically, flawlessly correct. Santa Claus was an absolute operational ninja. He was incredibly stealthy, and he was gone long before we even noticed.

"Do we wake them up?" Rachel whispered, her raspy voice vibrating with pure, unadulterated excitement against my chest.

Jessica looked back at the glowing green digits on the VCR clock, her pragmatic logic immediately taking over the Base Camp.

"No," Jess commanded softly, shaking her head. "It's barely four in the morning. If we wake Mom and Dad up right now, they are going to be exhausted and cranky. The drop is secure. We hold the perimeter until at least six-thirty. Just pretend to sleep."

It was going to be agonizing to wait, but Jessica's logic was structurally sound.

"Okay," I agreed.

I pulled the heavy winter quilts tighter over our shoulders. Rachel pressed her face back into my chest, her heart racing against mine. We weren't going to get any actual rest—our systems were far too charged with adrenaline and the impending magic of the morning—but we closed our eyes and held the line, lying perfectly still in the quiet, colorful glow of the Christmas tree until the adult world was finally ready to wake up.
