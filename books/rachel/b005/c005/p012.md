The space underneath the slatted wooden stairs of 1852 Delaney Street was completely insulated from the rising acoustic volume of the house.

For the last three hours, Jessica had been locked into an absolute, hyper-focused trance. The heavy mechanical clacking of her keyboard was a relentless, rhythmic drumbeat. On the glowing CRT monitor, her *Axiom* memory manager was taking shape in crisp, object-oriented C++. Without the invisible bloat of the compiler rearing its head yet, the code felt invincible. Every class structure she built, every QVirtualAlloc command she routed through the Q32 API, compiled cleanly. There were no memory leaks. There were no Bugcheck Screens.

She was in total control of the machine, completely unaware that the 16-bit CDOS foundation beneath her operating system was a ticking time bomb. For now, the C++ architecture felt like the perfect, elegant weapon to win the Catalyst Initiative.

"Jess, you need to save and exit," Mom announced, stepping out of the narrow galley kitchen holding a stack of ceramic plates and heading toward the dining room. "Everyone is here. We're sitting down to eat."

Jessica’s fingers hovered over the keyboard. She stared at the glowing lines of syntax, her jaw clenching with the reluctance of a developer being pulled out of the matrix. She let out a heavy sigh, hit Ctrl+S to save her workspace, and closed *Quantum Developer Studio 6.0*.

She reached down, unlocked the brakes on her manual wheelchair with a sharp, metallic *click*, and spun away from the desk to face the living room.

Base Camp had been entirely overrun.

The front door had been opening and closing for the last twenty minutes, letting blasts of cold Virginia winter air into the foyer as the perimeter expanded to its absolute maximum capacity.

Aunt Janet, Dan, and Grandma Loretta had migrated over from their afternoon gathering, bringing the chaotic energy of the four cousins—Janette, Maria, Katherine, and Michelle—with them. The sheer acoustic volume of the extended family bouncing off the dark blue polyester carpet and the vaulted ceiling was creating a massive auditory wall.

But the true shift in the room's geometry had happened five minutes earlier, when the Raybourns walked through the front door.

Jim and Joan had immediately bypassed the chaos of the living room, carrying covered dishes of food straight into the galley kitchen to assist Mom and Dad. Twenty-one-year-old Melissa hung her coat in the hallway closet, dodging the aggressively swishing tails of Baby and Archie before making her way over to talk to Jessica and Janette.

But fourteen-year-old Ashley didn't bother with the coats or the kitchen.

Our Honorary Sister stepped into the living room, her eyes immediately scanning the crowded space until she found exactly what she was looking for. Rachel and I were sitting on the edge of the deployed sofa bed, our shoulders pressed tightly together as we tried to weather the sensory assault of the overlapping adult conversations and the cousins’ laughter.

Ashley didn't say a word. She just marched straight across the room, bypassed the cousins, and climbed directly onto the mattress next to me.

She took her rightful place on my left side, wrapped her arms around my torso, and delivered a heavy, dead-weight pack squeeze.

Instantly, the glaring hole in our physical geometry vanished. With Rachel pressed against my right side and Ashley anchoring my left, the perimeter was flawlessly re-established. The deep-pressure grounding manually forced the buzzing panic in my chest to decelerate. The acoustic chaos of Dan laughing at something Uncle Jim said, and Michelle chasing Maria past the Christmas tree, suddenly felt miles away. We were sealed inside our own shared personal bubble.

"How was Grandma's house?" Ashley asked, resting her head against my shoulder and looking across me to Rachel.

"Loud," Rachel whispered, the single syllable carrying the exhausted weight of surviving the afternoon gauntlet.

"They interrogated him," Rachel added quietly, a faint, loyal smirk touching the corner of her mouth. "About Bonnie."

Ashley’s eyes lit up with immediate, tactical interest. She leaned forward, looking up at my face, which was already flushing bright red at the mere mention of my newly mapped romantic data.

"Did you survive?" Ashley grinned, completely devoid of the teasing malice the cousins had used. She was my wingman; she just wanted the mission report.

"Rachel sat on me," I mumbled, keeping my eyes glued to the dark blue carpet. "It blocked them out."

Ashley nodded in profound, absolute approval. She squeezed my side tighter, reinforcing the physical tether. "Good," she murmured, giving Rachel a fiercely protective nod. "Keep the perimeter locked."

"Alright, everyone, grab a seat or find a spot on the floor!" Dad’s voice rumbled over the noise of the room, carrying a stack of folding chairs out of the hallway. "Dinner is ready."

The adults began to herd the cousins toward the dining room threshold. Jessica expertly navigated her manual wheelchair into her designated, removed-chair docking position at the dark-stained wooden table, ensuring she had absolute tactical control over her space.

Rachel, Ashley, and I didn't move toward the table. We knew the Delaney Street protocol. With this many people in the house, the youth guard ate at Base Camp.

Mom walked out of the kitchen balancing three plates piled high with turkey, mashed potatoes, and stuffing. She handed them to us one by one. We balanced the warm plates on our laps, sitting cross-legged on the blue quilts of the sofa bed.

I looked around the room. It was loud, chaotic, and packed to the absolute structural limits. But sitting between my sister and my best friend, eating dinner on the floor with Jessica watching over us from the dining room table, the internal telemetry of my processor settled into a solid, unshakeable Green.

The perimeter was safe.
