At 11:45 AM on Christmas Day, the trauma of the Black Friday drop was temporarily pushed to the background. My internal processor was actively redlining over a completely different, infinitely more terrifying variable.

I was pacing a tight, rigid circle in front of the front-wall brick fireplace, carefully avoiding the expansive footprint of the fully deployed floral-print sofa bed that dominated the center of the room.

"I don't have the script for this, Ash," I muttered, my hands sweating as I ran the calculations in my head for the fiftieth time. "I know how to act at school. I know how to sit next to her on the bus. But she is coming *here*. Into our house. The parameters are completely different."

Sitting cross-legged on the edge of the mattress, Ashley let out a long, patient sigh. She reached out and grabbed the belt loop of my jeans as I paced past her, physically halting my momentum.

"Michael, stop pacing. You're going to burn a hole in the carpet," Ashley commanded, her voice dropping into her familiar, tactical Sentinel register. "We have gone over the algorithm. It is mathematically sound."

"But what if my processor freezes?" I asked, my chest tightening with buzzing panic. I looked over at Rachel, who was sitting quietly near the television, meticulously organizing a stack of newly opened *Nova 64* game cartridges. My little sister operated on absolute, literal routines, and the introduction of an outside civilian into our sanctuary was a massive disruption.

"Your processor isn't going to freeze because I am giving you the exact, step-by-step instructions," Ashley said, standing up from the mattress and placing her hands firmly on my shoulders to ground me.

She looked me dead in the eye, bypassing all the confusing, unwritten rules of standard-hardware teenagers.

"Step one," Ashley dictated clearly. "The doorbell rings. You open the door. Step two: You say, 'Merry Christmas, Bonnie.' Step three: You give her a hug."

My brain snagged on the third variable again, despite the physical drills we had just run. I frowned, the mechanical geometry of the action still fighting my default instincts.

"What kind of hug?" I asked, needing absolute, verbal clarity to lock the file. "I know we practiced, but... if my brain panics, do I default to the deep-pressure hold? Do I lock my arms around her ribs?"

"No!" Ashley laughed, though she quickly softened her tone so I wouldn't feel mocked. She understood exactly why I was asking. To me, a hug wasn't a casual greeting; it was a heavy, utilitarian cargo strap used to manually reset a crashing nervous system. I hugged Ashley and Rachel with bone-crushing force to keep the world from spinning out of control.

"Do not crush her ribs," Ashley corrected gently, giving my shoulders a light shake to reset my focus. "Remember the drill. This isn't a survival hug, Michael. It's a romantic hug. You keep it light. You just wrap your arms around her waist, hold her for two seconds, and step back. That's it. Repeat it back to me."

"Light pressure," I recited, running the specific code through my head. "Two seconds. Step back. It’s just to show her that I'm happy she is here."

"Exactly," Ashley beamed, stepping back and letting her arms drop. "You have the math. You just have to execute it."

From her manual wheelchair parked near the blue recliner, Jessica watched the coaching session with a quiet, observant smirk. Even dealing with the agonizing pain in her shattered heels, the older sister in her couldn't resist chiming in.

"Just remember to breathe, Michael," Jessica advised, adjusting her glasses. "Bonnie already knows we live in a triage unit right now. She knows I'm in the chair. You don't have to apologize for the state of the house or the giant bed in the middle of the room. Just be yourself."

I took a deep, shaky breath, nodding. *Open door. Merry Christmas. Light hug. Breathe.* The parameters were officially set.
