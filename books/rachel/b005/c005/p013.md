By 7:00 PM, the massive turkey dinner had been completely dismantled, and the adults had retreated to the living room with heavy mugs of coffee. But the dissipation of the meal didn't calm the house down; it merely opened up the physical floor plan for the younger cousins to exploit.

The architecture of the first floor of 1852 Delaney Street formed a continuous, unbroken circle. Without the bottleneck of people standing in the galley kitchen, the track was officially open.

"You can't catch me!" Michelle shrieked, sprinting out of the foyer.

She took a sharp left turn into the living room, blowing past the massive Christmas tree stationed in the front-left corner. The heavy branches shuddered, a few low-hanging ornaments clattering together as Maria and Katherine chased right on her heels. They sprinted through the wide threshold into the dining room, hooked a sharp right into the main hallway, and completed the circuit by bursting right back into the foyer.

They weren't running alone. The sudden, high-speed kinetic energy of the cousins had instantly activated the canine guard.

Baby, our massive, Great Dane-sized black lab, bounded joyfully after them, his heavy paws thudding like a localized earthquake against the linoleum and carpet. Archie was right beside him, a mangled plush reindeer clamped proudly in his jaws. His cloudy blue eye didn't slow him down in the slightest as he happily joined the chaotic, infinite loop.

Sitting dead center in the living room, perfectly entrenched on the deployed sofa bed, I watched the blur of cousins and dogs run their fourth lap. The acoustic volume was deafening, but my processor was completely insulated. Ashley hadn't moved an inch from my left side, and Rachel was pressed firmly against my right. Our shared personal bubble held the perimeter flawlessly.

The adults, however, were operating on standard-hardware hosting etiquette.

Aunt Janet stood near the blue recliner, sipping her coffee and watching us with a tight, disapproving frown. To a neurotypical outsider, a permanently deployed mattress taking up eighty percent of the main entertaining space wasn't a vital medical sanctuary—it was just messy.

"Ellen," Aunt Janet said, pitching her voice loud enough to cut over the screaming cousins. She gestured toward the sofa bed with her free hand. "Do you want Tom and Dan to help fold that thing up? It takes up so much space. The girls are practically tripping over the corner every time they run by."

The temperature in the living room seemed to drop ten degrees.

I felt Rachel immediately tense against my side. Her fingers gripped the dark blue quilts, her internal telemetry spiking. If the sofa bed was folded away, the Base Camp vanished. Our architectural zero would be gone, leaving us completely exposed to the chaotic geometry of the room. Ashley felt the shift, too. Our fourteen-year-old Sentinel narrowed her eyes, glaring directly at Aunt Janet, ready to deploy a fierce verbal defense.

She didn't have to.

Mom paused mid-sip. She looked at Aunt Janet, her expression hardening into absolute, unyielding stone. Despite the fracturing lines of their own marriage, Mom and Dad operated as a flawless, heavily armored united front when it came to the Pack's safety.

"No, Janet," Mom stated, her voice devoid of any polite, sisterly warmth. "The bed stays exactly where it is."

"I'm just saying, it would open up the room—"

"The bed stays out," Dad rumbled from his spot near the fireplace, his deep baritone completely terminating the discussion. He didn't offer a medical explanation. He didn't justify our trauma-informed logistics. He just established the iron-clad rule of the household.

Aunt Janet blinked, clearly offended by the blunt rejection, but she didn't push it. She took a stiff sip of her coffee and turned her attention back to the cousins running the loop.

Rachel let out a slow, quiet breath against my shoulder. The tension bled out of her frame, her processor accepting the parental override. The sanctuary was safe.

Across the living room, completely ignoring the family politics and the dogs running laps behind her chair, Jessica was initiating her own Paranoia Protocol.

She had docked her manual wheelchair back at the desk beneath the slatted stairs. But before she reopened *Quantum Developer Studio 6.0* to resume writing her C++ architecture, she reached into a plastic storage bin beside the desk.

She pulled out a fresh, unlabelled 3.5-inch high-density floppy disk.

She knew exactly what she was about to demand of this machine. To win the Catalyst Initiative, she was going to push the sluggish, aging clone processor and the fragile Quantum OS 98 architecture to their absolute breaking points.

As if sensing the impending violence, the 5400 RPM hard drive inside the beige tower let out a low, mechanical groan as Jessica booted the IDE back up.

She didn't flinch. She pushed the blank floppy disk into the tower's front drive until the plastic ejection button popped out with a sharp *click*. She didn't have the luxury of automated, high-capacity backups. The desktop was going to suffer, and Jessica was ruthlessly prepared to manually extract her raw code byte-by-byte to the 1.44MB physical disks when the platters finally shattered.

She cracked her knuckles, pushed her tinted glasses up her nose, and turned back to the glowing CRT monitor. The holiday was officially over. The crunch had begun.
