She sat back in her chair, the blue light of the monitor reflecting off her lenses. She was trapped between the absolute limitations of a cheap 1995 computer and the brutal ticking of a 2000 deadline.

Assembly was too slow to write. Standard C was too heavy to run.

Suddenly, her posture shifted. Her spine straightened, and her hands went back to the keyboard.

"I won't use Assembly for the whole thing," Jessica realized, her voice dropping into a razor-sharp register. "I'll use pure ANSI C for the logic. But I'm going to rip the engine out first."

"Rip what engine out?" I asked, watching her pull up the compiler interface.

"The C-Runtime Library," Jessica declared.

She navigated deep into the compiler's advanced settings, finding the linker configuration. With a few aggressive keystrokes, she flipped the compiler switches, permanently disabling the standard background library that came with the language.

By checking that box, she had just completely blinded the compiler. She could no longer use standard commands like printf or malloc. The safety nets were gone.

"If you kill the runtime, how does the program know how to start?" I asked, leaning closer.

Jessica opened her main source file. She deleted the standard void main() entry point that every programming student in the world is taught to use.

She replaced it with \_start.

"It doesn't," Jessica said, a fierce, predatory smile touching the corner of her mouth. "I have to tell it exactly how to wake up, and exactly how to talk to the Q32 API."

She began to type. It wasn't the slow, methodical coding of a student. It was the rapid, aggressive execution of an architect pouring concrete. Because she had severed the runtime, she was forced to use raw, direct Q32 API calls to build her patch.

Instead of asking a high-level library to find her some memory, she reached directly into the operating system's kernel.

*VirtualAlloc(NULL, memory_size, MEM_COMMIT, PAGE_READWRITE);*

"I'll build the structural logic in C," Jessica explained, her hands flying across the keys. "But for the surgical strikes—the exact moments where I need to intercept the corrupted enterprise leak—I'll use inline Assembly. I'll drop the bare-metal CPU instructions right into the middle of the C code."

She was creating a hybrid weapon. It had the speed of C, the absolute, microscopic precision of Assembly, and zero bloat to trigger the dreaded 64KB graphics crash.
