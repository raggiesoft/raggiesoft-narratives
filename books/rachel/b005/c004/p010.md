The heavy brown painted wooden garage door was closed, and by 7:50 PM, we were officially locked inside the perimeter of 1852 Delaney Street.

The transition from the cold car to our nightly routine was swift. Mom took control of the logistics immediately, wheeling Jessica past the living room and down the main hallway toward the Downstairs Bathroom. She helped Jessica navigate the tight space to take care of her nighttime routine: using the toilet, taking her nightly pain and muscle medications, and changing into her pajamas.

While Mom and Jessica were occupied on the first floor, Rachel and I bounded up the slatted wooden stairs to the second floor. We hadn't actively slept in our upstairs bedrooms since the Black Friday drop forced our migration to the living room, but all of our clothes were still kept in our respective dressers. I grabbed a pair of sweatpants and a t-shirt from my dark blue room, while Rachel ducked into her white-walled bedroom across the hall. We changed in record time and immediately rushed back down the stairs, eager to get back to the Base Camp.

The living room had already been converted. The floral-print sofa was pulled out into its expansive, flat configuration.

I climbed onto the mattress first, taking my designated spot dead in the center. A few moments later, Mom wheeled Jessica out of the bathroom. Jessica executed a smooth, practiced transfer from her chair directly onto the left side of the mattress. Rachel immediately scrambled onto the right side, pressing perfectly flush against me. The three of us curled up together, pulling the heavy winter quilts up over our shoulders, forming our tight, indestructible human wall.

The air in the living room was practically vibrating with our collective electric energy. Pacing nervously at the foot of the bed were Baby and Archie. Archie, his cloudy blue eye reflecting the glow of the Christmas tree lights, was aggressively squeaking a plush toy in his mouth. The dogs didn't understand the complex mythology of Santa Claus or the logistical anomaly of the night, but their biological threat-detection systems registered the massive spike in our excitement levels. They knew their pet humans were amped up about something, and it made their tails thump a chaotic rhythm against the dark blue polyester carpet.

Mom and Dad walked into the living room to initiate the final lockdown.

Dad walked over to the end table sitting next to the blue recliner and picked up the cordless phone.

"Alright, let's get a tactical update," Dad said, punching in a 1-800 number. He hit the speakerphone button so we could all hear.

We went dead silent. Even the dogs stopped pacing.

A few seconds later, the official, static-laced voice of a military operator from the NORAD Santa Tracker hotline filled the living room. To us, it was the ultimate, undeniable proof of the operation—a direct tactical update from the federal government.

"Current radar telemetry confirms Santa's sleigh has just departed Nova Scotia," the operator announced, delivering the briefing with absolute, military precision. "He is moving rapidly down the Eastern Seaboard. ETA to the Virginia coast is less than two hours. All children are advised to be asleep immediately."

Dad hung up the phone, giving the three of us a wide-eyed, urgent look.

"Alright, you three," Mom said softly, pulling the edges of the quilt a little tighter around Jessica's shoulders. "Time to go to sleep."

Dad walked over to the front-wall brick fireplace. Because there hadn't actually been a fire lit in the hearth all day, he didn't have to shovel any ashes or pour any water. Instead, he just reached his hand inside, giving the dark brick a thorough, serious inspection to make absolutely certain the perimeter was safe. It was a vitally important, non-negotiable security check he performed every single year to ensure the route was perfectly clear for Santa.

"Chimney is completely clear and cold," Dad announced, standing up and brushing his hands together with a satisfied nod. "It's ready for him. Goodnight, guys."

"Goodnight," we chorused back.

Dad turned off the main living room lamp, leaving only the multi-colored glow of the Christmas tree illuminating the dark room. He and Mom walked over to the slatted stairs, their footsteps fading as they headed up to the master suite on the second floor.

The absolute second we heard their bedroom door click shut upstairs, the stakeout officially began.

Rachel shifted against my right side, tilting her head up to look at me in the dim, colorful light.

"So," Rachel whispered, a rare, mischievous spark in her dark eyes. "Do we want to stay up and meet Santa?"

"Obviously," I whispered back, completely energized.

I looked over my left shoulder. "Jess, you in?"

Jessica didn't answer. I leaned over slightly, peering at her face. Her eyes were already fluttering, fighting a losing battle against the heavy, chemical weight of her nightly medication. The painkillers and muscle relaxers required to dull the agonizing ache of her shattered heels didn't respect holiday magic.

"I'm awake," Jessica mumbled, though her words were thick and slurred. She blinked slowly, trying to focus on the Christmas tree. "Just... resting my eyes for a minute."

Less than sixty seconds later, her breathing evened out into a deep, rhythmic cycle. The pragmatic protector of our pack was officially down for the count.

"It's just us, Bug," I whispered, wrapping my arm tighter around Rachel's shoulders.

Rachel squeezed me back, burying her face into my chest, utilizing the deep, crushing physical pressure to regulate her nervous system. We were absolutely determined to beat the clock.

We talked to each other in hushed, quiet murmurs, keeping our voices barely above a breath so we wouldn't wake Jessica. We whispered about the *Nova 64* games, about Ashley, about Brewster Gardens, and about what the jolly old elf might drop onto the carpet tonight. We hugged. We cuddled. We engaged every single tactile variable we possessed to keep our processors online.

But the sheer physics of our shared personal bubble were working against us. The intense, continuous physical contact manually forced our racing heart rates to decelerate, blocking out the adrenaline. The heavy, grounding weight of each other acted as an irresistible biological sedative.

Rachel's whispered responses grew shorter. The iron-clad grip she had on my t-shirt slowly loosened. I felt the exact moment her system powered down; her body went completely heavy against my side, her breathing synchronizing perfectly with Jessica's.

I was the last guard on the wall. I stared hard at the brick fireplace, my eyelids feeling like they were lined with lead. I tried to run mathematical calculations in my head to keep my brain firing. I tried to think about noon tomorrow. I tried to focus on the hum of the refrigerator in the kitchen.

But the warmth of the quilts, the hypnotic glow of the tree lights, and the absolute, unyielding safety of my sisters flanking me were too much. My processor finally surrendered, and I slipped into the dark, leaving the perimeter completely undefended for the magic to happen.
