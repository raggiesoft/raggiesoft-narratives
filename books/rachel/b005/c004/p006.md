At 5:42 PM, the heavy wooden front door finally swung open, letting a sharp blast of freezing coastal air into the crowded foyer.

My dad stepped over the threshold, his shoulders slumped in exhaustion. He was still wearing his Baja Taco uniform, smelling faintly of fast-food grease and the biting winter wind.

"Sorry I'm late," Tom sighed, shrugging off his heavy winter coat and hanging it on the rack. He rubbed his eyes, projecting the perfect image of a worn-out, working-class dad. "Traffic on Independence Boulevard was an absolute nightmare."

From the kitchen, Mom stepped out, wiping her hands on a dish towel. She locked eyes with my dad across the chaotic hallway. It was a fleeting, completely silent exchange—a barely perceptible, grounding nod from Tom, followed by a quiet, immediate drop in Mom's tense shoulders.

Sitting on the sofa, anchored under Rachel and Ashley, my processor didn't even flag the look as an anomaly. To me, it was just the standard, unspoken communication of two parents relieved that the holiday commute was finally over. The alibi was absolutely, mathematically flawless.

"Well, you made it," Grandma Loretta announced, clapping her hands together as she emerged from the kitchen. "Everyone grab a plate! It's time to eat!"

The logistical division of the house immediately commenced. Because Grandma Loretta's house was packed to its structural limits, dining was split into two distinct zones.

The adults and the older teenagers claimed the formal dining room. Grandma Loretta, Dan, Jim, Joan, Tom, Janette, and Melissa filled the chairs around the large wooden table, their voices blending into a loud, festive hum.

The rest of us were relegated to the living room, which had been transformed into a secondary mess hall. A wobbly, vinyl-topped card table had been unfolded near the center of the room with four folding chairs, while a stack of wooden TV tables was deployed around the two sofas.

Jessica bypassed the card table entirely. She rolled her manual wheelchair directly up to a TV tray near the blue recliner. While she was technically an adult and belonged at the main dining table, her logistical logic overrode the social hierarchy.

"It's just easier to dock here," Jessica noted, adjusting her glasses and locking her brakes. She didn't want to deal with the friction of squeezing her chair between the heavy dining room furniture.

Rachel, Ashley, and I managed to carefully untangle our three-person knot just long enough to grab our food.

It was a traditional, heavy Virginia Beach Christmas Eve dinner. We loaded our ceramic plates with thick slices of honey-glazed ham, massive scoops of sweet potato casserole, buttery yeast rolls, and the absolute regional staple—Grandma Loretta's rich, savory oyster stuffing.

We navigated our heavy plates back to the floral-print sofa. We didn't revert to the intense, stomach-to-stomach compression we used when the house was at maximum volume, but the perimeter remained tight. I took the middle cushion, sliding my TV tray directly over my lap. Rachel sat tight against my left side, her knee pressed firmly against mine, while Ashley anchored my right.

Katherine, Michelle, and Maria filled out the remaining spots at the card table and the opposite loveseat.

As the opening credits of the next holiday movie played on the heavy CRT television, the frantic, buzzing energy of the house finally settled into a contented, synchronized rhythm. We ate in the warm glow of the Christmas tree lights, surrounded by the indestructible walls of our pack. And as I ate, my mind kept happily drifting toward tomorrow. We still had the magic of Christmas morning to get through, but my internal countdown had already locked onto noon, when a certain girl was finally going to knock on our front door.
