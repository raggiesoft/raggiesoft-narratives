The coastal Virginia winter air had a sharp, biting chill to it by late afternoon. The sky over Delaney Street was already beginning to bruise into a pale, bruised purple as Mom backed her sedan out of the garage and idled it in the driveway.

The transition from the safe, strictly controlled perimeter of the living room to the unpredictable outside world was always a massive logistical hurdle.

Mom stood by the open passenger door, her breath pluming in the cold air. She wheeled Jessica carefully down the slight incline of the front walkway. Jessica was bundled in a heavy winter coat, her legs resting stiffly on the elevated footrests of the heavy manual wheelchair.

"Okay, Jess, lock the brakes," Mom instructed, stepping directly in front of the chair to act as the primary physical barrier.

Jessica reached down, snapping the metal brake levers into place with a sharp, practiced click. She leaned forward, gripping the frame of the car door with one hand and Mom's forearm with the other. With a swift, fiercely independent pivot, Jessica swung her body weight out of the chair and dropped safely into the front passenger seat.

She immediately reached down, using her hands to manually pull her legs inside the cabin. She grabbed her heavy winter boots, wiggling her toes and rotating her ankles in the footwell. She had full feeling and complete, flawless range of motion.

"See that?" Jessica pointed out to Mom, a fierce, determined spark of hope in her eyes. "The physical therapist said my rotation is improving. Once the bone grafts fully calcify, I'll be able to start bearing weight again."

Mom offered a tight, encouraging smile, shutting the passenger door. "You're doing great, sweetie. You'll be back on your feet."

Standing on the cold concrete of the driveway, I watched the exchange, my processor accepting the data exactly as it was presented. To all of us, the wheelchair wasn't a permanent reality; it was simply a temporary, tactical vehicle required to bridge the gap between the Black Friday drop and her eventual recovery. We fully believed the doctors were going to fix her.

But believing the chair was temporary didn't make the metal any lighter.

Mom walked around to the driver's side, leaving Rachel and me standing on the driveway with the empty, imposing piece of medical hardware.

"Okay, Bug," I muttered, my breath fogging in the cold. "Let's break it down."

Rachel stepped up beside me, her dark eyes glued to the chair. She didn't speak, but she didn't have to. We operated as a perfectly synchronized, silent unit.

I reached down and grabbed the center of the heavy canvas seat, pulling sharply upward to collapse the X-frame. The metal groaned and snapped together, but the chair was incredibly bulky. The cold air was actively aggravating my high-tone spasticity, causing my leg muscles to seize slightly and making my stance rigid on the slick concrete.

Rachel immediately recognized the shift in my mechanics. She stepped in, grabbing the large rear wheels to stabilize the massive frame, taking the awkward, shifting weight off my arms.

We waddled the collapsed chair to the back of the sedan.

"On three," I said, my grip tightening on the freezing metal bars. "One... two... three."

Together, we heaved the dead weight upward. My arms shook as my muscles fought the tension, but Rachel guided the bulky wheels perfectly over the rubber lip of the trunk, shoving the chair deep into the cargo space until it clattered against the back seats.

I slammed the trunk shut, my chest heaving slightly from the exertion.

Rachel immediately grabbed the back hem of my jacket, stepping directly into my shadow to block the cold wind. I let her use my body as a physical shield as we walked around to the rear passenger door.

We climbed into the back seat, the sudden blast of the car's heater thawing the chill in my fingers. The absolute second the doors were shut and the perimeter was sealed, Rachel slid across the upholstery, pressing her side flush against my arm and resting her head heavily on my shoulder.

I leaned my weight back against her, providing the heavy, crushing pressure her nervous system required. Up in the front seat, Jessica was adjusting the radio, completely unbothered by the fact that she couldn't feel the floorboards under her boots. The engine shifted into gear, and Mom pulled out onto the street, steering us away from our sanctuary and directly toward the chaotic, hostile architecture of Grandma Loretta's house.
