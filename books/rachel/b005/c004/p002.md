Mom moved efficiently out of the narrow galley kitchen, carrying four ceramic bowls of cereal to the dark-stained wooden dining table. She set one down in front of Jessica's docked wheelchair, slid one across to Rachel, placed mine down, and took the final seat for herself.

The sudden clatter of spoons against ceramic acted as an immediate, localized dinner bell.

Instantly, the heavy, rhythmic *thud-thud-thud* of canine footsteps echoed from the foyer. Baby and Archie rounded the corner and flanked the dining table, executing their daily, highly rehearsed psychological warfare.

Baby arrived at my side first. Despite being a purebred black Lab, he was an absolute genetic anomaly, built like a small Great Dane. He rested his massive, heavy chin directly onto my thigh. He didn't make a sound; he just looked up at me with huge, sorrowful brown eyes, projecting the absolute illusion of a neglected, starving animal.

Archie took up his post next to Jessica’s wheelchair. He looked up at her with his good eye—the other a cloudy, milky blue—while a mangled, squeaky plush squirrel was clamped firmly in his jaws. Archie’s defining personality trait was his absolute need to carry a toy in his mouth at all times to proudly show off, specifically whichever one you were currently looking at. He let out a muffled, pathetic whine around the stuffed squirrel.

"Don't let them guilt-trip you," Mom warned, pointing her spoon at the two Labs. "I filled their bowls in the kitchen ten minutes ago. They aren't starving."

It was an ironclad, non-negotiable parameter in our house: the dogs did not get human food. Baby and Archie knew the rule perfectly, but their biological algorithm demanded they test the perimeter anyway.

I reached down, burying my hand into the thick, dark fur behind Baby's ears. He leaned his massive weight into my leg. It was a comforting, heavy pressure. Even though he was just begging for cereal milk, to my processor, he was fulfilling his primary function: acting as a physical, biological anchor for the pack.

Rachel, sitting silently on my left, didn't offer the dogs any food either, but she quietly dropped her hand under the table to scratch Archie's head. He aggressively nudged his plush squirrel against her knee, proudly displaying it.

It was a completely standard, chaotic morning routine. But amidst the looming, stressful logistics of getting Jessica’s wheelchair loaded into a car and navigating the hostile architecture of Grandma Loretta's house, the predictable, dramatic begging of the dogs was exactly the kind of unshifting, safe data our nervous systems needed.
