Grandma Loretta’s house was operating at absolute, maximum sensory capacity.

From the kitchen, the rich, heavy smells of roasting turkey and baked ham collided with the sharp, metallic clatter of pots and pans as Grandma Loretta cooked. In the dining room, the sounds of silver clinking against ceramic echoed as Janette and Melissa set the large table. Woven through it all was the overlapping, chaotic chatter of Jim, Joan, Aunt Janet, and Mom discussing seating arrangements and holiday logistics over the steady background noise of the television.

For a standard-hardware brain, it was a festive holiday evening. For our processors, it was an overwhelming wall of unorganized, chaotic data.

To survive the environment, we executed our oldest, most deeply ingrained survival mechanic. We collapsed our physical space into a shared personal bubble.

I was sitting on the left side of the floral-print sofa. Rachel, completely overwhelmed by the volume of the house, had bypassed all neurotypical rules of personal space and climbed directly into my lap. She wrapped her arms around my torso, burying her face into my chest, and squeezed. I wrapped my arms around her back, pulling her in tight, providing the suffocating, heavy compression her nervous system desperately required to stay out of the Yellow zone.

Ashley was sitting on my immediate right, pressed perfectly flush against my side, her head resting on my shoulder. We formed an impenetrable, three-person knot of silent, grounding pressure.

Sitting on the opposite end of our sofa, Maria was flipping through a holiday catalog, mostly ignoring the chaos.

Directly across from us, on the matching loveseat, Katherine and Michelle were locked in a relentless, high-pitched war of neurotypical sibling bickering. They were arguing over who was going to get the new *Bella Doll* accessories tomorrow morning, their voices growing increasingly shrill as they kicked at each other's socks.

"Stop kicking me!" Katherine yelled, shoving her sister's knee.

"I didn't even touch you!" Michelle shrieked back.

Aunt Janet appeared in the threshold between the kitchen and the living room, holding a wooden mixing spoon and looking entirely exhausted.

"Katherine, Michelle, that is enough!" Aunt Janet scolded, her voice cutting through the noise. She pointed the wooden spoon directly at our sofa. "Look at your cousins. Why can't you two sit quietly and get along like Michael and Rachel?"

Katherine and Michelle paused their kicking, turning their heads to look at us.

Instead of taking the compliment, Katherine wrinkled her nose in profound confusion. To her standard-hardware brain, the physical geometry of our pack made absolutely no sense.

"Because it's weird," Katherine declared loudly, pointing at us. "Rachel is twelve. Why is she sitting on him like a baby? Why are they all squished together like that?"

My shoulders tensed instantly. The outside world constantly demanded explanations for the way we survived. I opened my mouth to say something, but before my brain could even load the script, the Sentinel intercepted the threat.

Ashley didn't uncross her arms, and she didn't lift her head from my shoulder. She just snapped her dark eyes toward Katherine, deploying an icy, terrifyingly flat glare that could freeze a room.

"She's sitting on him because he's her brother and it keeps her calm," Ashley stated, her tone making it incredibly clear that the subject was permanently closed. "Your mom told you to look at how they aren't fighting, Katherine. She didn't ask for your review. Turn around."

Katherine blinked, thoroughly intimidated by the older teenager's absolute lack of hesitation. She slumped back against the loveseat, muttering under her breath, and immediately resumed her low-level bickering with Michelle.

Rachel didn't react to the exchange at all. She just tightened her grip on my shirt, digging her fingers into the fabric. I squeezed my Bug back, dropping my chin to rest on the top of her head. Let them think it was weird. They didn't understand the physics of safety. In a house this loud, my lap was the only perimeter that actually worked.

From her docked wheelchair near the television, Jessica offered Ashley a quiet, approving nod. The shield was holding.

"When is Uncle Tom getting here?" Maria asked, looking up from her catalog, rubbing her stomach. "I'm starving."

"He should be here any minute," Mom answered from the kitchen. "He got off at 4:30, but Baja Taco traffic is always a nightmare on Christmas Eve."

I looked at the clock on the wall. It was 5:30 PM. My stomach gave a loud, hollow rumble, perfectly matching the hunger setting in across the room. We just had to wait for my dad to finally beat the holiday gridlock so we could eat.
