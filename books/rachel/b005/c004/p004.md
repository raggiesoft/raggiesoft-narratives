The drive to Grandma Loretta’s house was a tense, quiet calculation of holiday traffic and cold weather, but the moment Mom’s sedan turned into the driveway, a wave of profound relief washed over the car.

The rest of the family had deliberately left the prime spot at the top of the driveway, right next to the heavy wooden garage door, completely open for us. Dan was already standing in the illuminated garage, wearing a thick flannel shirt, his breath pluming in the freezing air as he waved us in.

Mom killed the engine. Before she could even unbuckle her seatbelt, Dan was already opening the passenger door.

"I got her, Ellen," Dan announced, his voice carrying that booming, easy confidence of an uncle who wasn't afraid of the new physical mechanics of our family.

He leaned into the cabin, wrapping his arms securely around Jessica's back and under her knees. With a smooth, practiced heave, he lifted my nineteen-year-old sister completely out of the car. Jessica wrapped her arms around his neck, holding on as Dan easily carried her up the single, elevated concrete step leading from the garage floor into the warm, brightly lit kitchen.

"You're up, Bug," I muttered to Rachel as we scrambled out of the back seat.

We popped the trunk and hauled the heavy, folded manual wheelchair out into the cold garage. Working with our established, silent synchronicity, we bumped the heavy rear wheels up the concrete step and rolled it straight into the kitchen.

The house was a wall of sound and heat. The smell of roasting ham, baked apples, and cinnamon completely overwhelmed my sensory processors in the best possible way.

We unfolded the chair next to the kitchen island, and Dan gently lowered Jessica into the canvas seat. Jessica locked the brakes, adjusted her glasses, and let out a long, victorious exhale. She unlocked the wheels and pushed herself forward, rolling seamlessly out of the kitchen, through the dining area, and into the crowded living room.

I followed right behind her, practically vibrating with a chaotic, electric energy I couldn't suppress. I was pacing, bouncing slightly on the heels of my sneakers, entirely unable to stand still.

"Look at him," Aunt Janet laughed from the sofa, pointing a finger at me as I walked into the living room. "He’s practically humming."

"Leave him alone," Janette, my cousin, teased from the floor, though her grin gave her away. "It’s a big deal. His *girlfriend* is coming over tomorrow."

"Oh, the girlfriend!" Grandma Loretta chimed in from her armchair, her eyes crinkling with absolute delight. "Bonnie, right? Are we going to meet this Bonnie?"

My face flushed a brilliant, burning crimson. I tried to cross my arms to look stoic, but my CP spasticity, mixed with the sheer adrenaline of my first real crush, made my shoulders twitch. "She's just coming over to drop something off," I mumbled, though the massive, goofy smile breaking across my face completely betrayed me. The teasing was relentless, but it was wrapped in so much familial warmth that it didn't trigger my anxiety; it just made the reality of tomorrow feel even more incredibly real.

The doorbell rang, cutting over the chaotic hum of my cousins and the television.

"I'll get it!" Katherine yelled, running toward the foyer.

A blast of cold air hit the hallway, followed immediately by the loud, booming laughter of Jim Raybourn. The Honorary Pack had arrived.

Jim, Joan, Melissa, and Ashley poured into the house, instantly expanding our perimeter to its maximum, indestructible capacity. Coats were thrown onto the bed in the guest room, and the house hit an absolute fever pitch of holiday volume.

Ashley pushed her way through the crowd in the living room. The second she saw me, her face lit up. She bypassed the casual side-hugs she had given my cousins and launched herself directly at me, wrapping her arms around my torso in a massive, heavy, deep-pressure pack hug. I squeezed back, the familiar, anchoring weight of my Sentinel instantly bleeding off the excess nervous energy vibrating in my chest.

"Perimeter secured," Ashley whispered in my ear, grinning as she pulled back. "You ready for tomorrow?"

"Yeah," I breathed, the panic officially replaced by pure excitement.

From her spot parked near the coffee table, Jessica suddenly grimaced, shifting uncomfortably in her chair. She looked down the narrow hallway toward the cramped powder room.

"Mom," Jessica called out, her voice tight. "The 1:00 PM water cutoff didn't work."

The living room quieted slightly. The logistical nightmare Mom and Jessica had argued about over breakfast had finally materialized. The hallway was entirely too narrow for the wheelchair to navigate the turn into the powder room.

I immediately stepped forward, my brain running the geometric calculations I had mapped out that morning. "I got the pivot. Jess, park parallel to the door. I'll lock my knees inside the frame and anchor you."

"Hold on, Michael. We got this," a deep voice rumbled behind me.

Jim Raybourn stepped up, clapping a heavy hand onto my shoulder. Dan materialized right next to him. In the hierarchy of our pack's survival mechanics, Jim and Dan were the ultimate physical lifters. They didn't even need to discuss the geometry.

"Clear the hall, girls," Dan instructed smoothly.

Jessica wheeled herself to the mouth of the hallway. Jim and Dan stepped in on either side of her. With effortless, synchronized strength, Jim scooped her up under the arms while Dan supported her legs. They carried her seamlessly down the narrow corridor, bypassing the architectural bottleneck completely. They helped her into the powder room, shut the door to give her privacy, and waited patiently in the hall to carry her back.

I watched them bring her back to her chair a few minutes later, a profound sense of relief settling over my processor. I didn't have to be the sole physical anchor today. The adults in our orbit actually understood the assignment. The logistical crisis was solved with zero friction.

"Alright, someone pop the tape in!" Joan called out from the kitchen, wiping her hands on an apron.

Melissa grabbed a VHS from a stack near the television and shoved it into the VCR. The tracking static flickered across the heavy CRT screen before the opening credits of *House Guard 2: Lost in Manhattan* began to play.

I sank down onto the carpet next to Rachel and Ashley, the three of us forming our tight, unbreakable Trio right in front of the television. The smell of the holiday dinner wafted over us, the movie dialogue echoed through the packed, chaotic room, and my mind drifted happily to the yellow disposable camera sitting on the desk back home, waiting for noon tomorrow.
