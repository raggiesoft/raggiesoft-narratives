# Book 10: The Divorce

## Chapter 3: The Broken Door (Tuesday, September 4th, 2001)

### Part 1: Rachel's Fury – 5:30 PM

The two-week reprieve was officially over. Mom was back from her work trip, the house was no longer ours, and the terrifying reality of the legal separation had finally arrived at the front door.

We were huddled together on the bottom mattress of the bunk bed in Rachel’s room, my arms locked tightly around her, when the shouting started downstairs. We had dragged the cordless phone upstairs with us, keeping Jessica on speakerphone, needing our older sister's presence to anchor us as the volume in the kitchen escalated.

Then, heavy footsteps pounded up the wooden stairs.

"I’m here to collect my daughter, then I’ll leave!" Dad’s voice boomed from the hallway.

The court order was finalized. The terms were set. Rachel was to live with Dad, and I was to stay with Mom. But sitting there on the bed, feeling Rachel shaking violently against my chest, we had already made our pact. Court orders be damned. We were not going to be separated.

Dad’s heavy fist pounded against the painted wood of the bedroom door. We had locked it the second he pulled into the driveway.

"Rachel, it’s time to go," Dad yelled through the wood.

Rachel didn't cower. The sheer, overwhelming terror in her nervous system suddenly flipped a switch, bypassing panic and igniting into pure, unadulterated fight-or-flight rage.

"I will not!" Rachel snarled angrily, her voice tearing from her throat.

A heavy, metallic scraping sound echoed from the hallway. I stared at the brass doorknob, my brain struggling to process the visual data. He wasn't just turning it. He had a screwdriver.

A second later, the brass doorknob completely detached, clattering heavily onto the carpeted floor. The mechanism gave way, and the door swung wide open.

The absolute violation of our safe space shattered whatever restraint Rachel had left.

Dad entered the room, his jaw set, and reached out to grab her arm to force her to come with him. He didn't even get his hand around her wrist.

Rachel snapped. Right before my eyes, moving faster than I had ever seen her move, my little sister lunged forward. She slapped our father squarely across the face with a loud, stinging *crack*.

"Get the fuck out of my bedroom!" Rachel screamed, her face flushed red with absolute, blinding rage.

Dad staggered back a half-step, completely shocked. At that exact moment, Mom rushed through the open doorway, reaching out to intervene. But Rachel’s system was entirely overloaded. She was lashing out at the source of the threat, unable to distinguish between the two adults tearing her world apart. Still full of anger and sheer panic, Rachel swung her hand again and slapped our mother, too.

The room dissolved into absolute chaos.

I jumped off the bed, putting my body directly between my sister and our parents. I puffed my chest out, ignoring the tightness in my legs, and screamed at the top of my lungs.

"Both of you, leave this house!" I roared, my voice breaking with fury. "Get out!!! Just get out!"

Behind me, the adrenaline suddenly burned out of Rachel's system. She collapsed onto the carpet, her knees giving out, and broke into heavy, gasping sobs. She couldn’t believe she had just slapped both of her parents, and she couldn't believe we had both just screamed at them. The rigid, predictable rules of our childhood had just been violently destroyed.

From the mattress, the small speaker of the cordless phone crackled to life.

Jessica had heard everything.

"Mom and Dad, you both are being fucking assholes," Jessica snarled over the phone, her voice shaking with protective fury. "Yes, I just called both of you 'assholes!' Get out of my sister’s room… now!"

Mom stood in the doorway, holding her cheek, looking down at us with a mixture of shock and exhaustion. "Rachel, can we be reasonable?"

But Dad was already gone. He had turned around and walked out, his heavy footsteps retreating down the stairs.

I decided right then and there that my sister and I were in no mood to talk to either parent. We were completely done.

"Get out of the house and leave us be!" I yelled at Mom, pointing toward the hallway.

Mom didn't argue. She turned around and slowly walked out. A few minutes later, I walked over to the bedroom window and looked down at the driveway. Dad’s truck was gone. Mom’s car was backing out into the street. They had both completely retreated, running away from the explosion they had caused.

I walked back to the bed and sat down next to Rachel. I looked at her, sobbing on the floor, and made a permanent decision. I didn’t care how bad the legal fallout would get, or what the courts said. From this second forward, I was going to be extremely protective of my sister. We loved each other too much, and we absolutely refused to be separated.

"They are gone, Jess," I said, picking up the phone. "They both went to work. It is just Rachel and I right now. And the slapping you heard... was Rachel slapping both of them."

"Good!" our older sister replied fiercely. "If I had money, I’d bring both of you to Seattle with me right now. I'd get an apartment and you'd never have to see them again." Jessica let out a frustrated, tearful sigh. "Now I need to get going because I have an evening class. If anything happens, report it to me immediately."

"Will do," Rachel and I sobbed together. We said our goodbyes and hung up the phone.

The house was dead silent, the broken doorknob still resting on the floor.

"Let's go downstairs and pour two quick bowls of cereal," I said quietly, trying to re-establish our coping routine. "When we get back upstairs, I’m going to quickly gather something to wear for tomorrow. Then I don’t mind sleeping with you tonight. Would you like to be in my room, since my door can still lock?"

"Please do," Rachel sobbed as we walked downstairs to the kitchen. "I’m afraid of being alone."

"That’s no problem," I said, setting the cereal boxes on the counter. "How about we take turns taking showers, then we will try to catch some sleep?"

"I think I need a bath," Rachel whimpered, her hands shaking as she tried to hold her spoon. "To relax my muscles. Everything hurts."

"That’s fine," I said gently. I knew the neurotypical rules of high schoolers and modesty, and I tried to apply them. "I can wait in my room, so I don’t invade your privacy. I’ll still be right here. If you need me, you can wrap a towel around yourself and call for me."

Rachel dropped her spoon into the ceramic bowl. She belted out the biggest, most agonizing wail I had ever heard. The sheer panic of abandonment ripped through her all over again.

"I don’t care about privacy!" Rachel cried, tears streaming down her face. "Please don’t leave my side!"

"Okay," I surrendered instantly. "If that’s what you really want."

To make absolutely sure I understood her, she reached out, grabbed my wrist, and physically dragged me down the hallway toward the bathroom.

I didn't fight her. The concept of privacy had been completely eradicated by the terror of the court order. I walked into the bathroom and immediately turned my back, staring at the closed door while she turned on the faucet. I listened to the rushing water and the heavy splash of half a bottle of bubble bath being poured in.

I waited until I heard the sloshing sound of her sinking into the tub.

"Okay," she sobbed.

I turned around. I sat down on the closed toilet lid, fully dressed in my t-shirt and jeans. Rachel was completely submerged up to her chin in a thick, white mountain of soap bubbles, her knees pulled up to her chest beneath the water, crying her eyes out.

She didn't want a bath for the hygiene. She wanted the heavy warmth of the water to regulate her overloaded sensory system, and she needed her Safe Person to anchor her to reality.

I leaned forward, resting my elbows on my knees, and locked my eyes firmly with hers. I didn't look anywhere else. I just held her gaze, offering the steady, undeniable proof that I was right there, keeping the world from dragging her away.

### Part 2: The Anchor – 6:00 PM

The bathroom was thick with warm, fragrant steam, turning the small, tiled space into an insulated, physical cocoon.

Rachel was submerged up to her chin, entirely hidden beneath a thick, impenetrable mountain of white bubbles. Her wet, dark hair was plastered against her cheeks, and she was trembling so violently that the water around her rippled continuously, sending small clusters of foam sliding down the porcelain sides of the tub.

She was completely exhausted, stripped of the explosive, defensive rage that had taken over in the bedroom. Now, there was just the raw, paralyzing terror of what the court order meant.

I sat on the closed toilet lid, leaning forward with my elbows resting heavily on my knees. I kept my eyes locked entirely on hers. I didn't look at the walls, the sink, or the mirror. I just stared directly into her dark, tear-filled eyes, serving as the physical, immovable anchor she desperately needed to keep from floating away into the panic.

Hanging on the towel rack directly opposite from where I was sitting was her heavy bath towel. It was perfectly positioned. Whenever she decided the water had done its job, all I had to do was reach out, pull it down, and hold it up for her like a shield. But we weren't there yet. Right now, she just needed the heat of the water to force her rigid muscles to uncoil.

"He... he has a screwdriver," Rachel sobbed, her voice echoing slightly against the tile. Her lower lip quivered, sending a small ripple through the bubbles just below her chin. "He broke the door, Michael. He broke the lock. What if he comes back tonight?"

"He’s not coming back tonight," I said, my voice low, steady, and completely absolute. "And if he does, I won't let him past the stairs. You saw him. He ran away when we yelled. He's a coward."

"The paper says I have to go," she whimpered, her eyes pleading with me to fix the math. The court order was a massive, terrifying variable that completely overrode our control of the house. "The judge signed it. They are going to make me go to Washington. I don't know Cathy. I don't know Vancouver. I don't want to go."

"You aren't going," I promised, never breaking eye contact.

"But the rules—"

"The rules are broken," I interrupted gently but firmly. "They broke the rules when they tore our family apart. So we don't have to follow theirs anymore. Our rule is that we stay together. That's the only rule that matters now."

Rachel let out a long, ragged exhale, the breath disturbing the foam around her neck.

"I'm so scared," she whispered, the fight completely leaving her voice.

"I know," I said. "I am too. But I'm right here. I'm not leaving this room. I'm not leaving this house without you. If he tries to put you on a plane, he's going to have to drag me onto it, too."

She sniffled, blinking away a fresh wave of tears. Slowly, the intense, violent trembling in her shoulders began to subside. The deep, surrounding heat of the bathwater was finally working, replacing the frantic, buzzing static of her nervous system with a heavy, soothing warmth.

But more than the water, it was the unbroken eye contact that was regulating her. She watched my face, reading the absolute certainty in my expression. I was her older brother. I had kept her safe at the mall, I had protected her from the crowds at band camp, and I had just thrown our parents out of the house. If I said she wasn't going to Washington, her brain could finally accept it as a factual truth.

"Okay," she whispered, her voice finally losing its frantic edge.

We sat there in the steam for a long time. The only sounds were the slow, steady drip of the faucet and the quiet popping of the soap bubbles. The outside world, with its lawyers, its broken doorknobs, and its terrifying ultimatums, was completely locked out. As long as I was sitting right there, holding her gaze, she was perfectly, completely safe.

### Part 3: The Push-Button Lock – 6:45 PM

The bubbles eventually began to dissolve, and the heat of the water slowly bled out into the humid air of the bathroom.

Rachel shifted in the tub, her muscles finally loose enough to move without trembling. "I'm ready," she whispered.

I stood up from the toilet lid instantly. I reached across the small space, grabbed the heavy cotton towel off the rack, and held it wide open like a physical wall, keeping my eyes fixed firmly on the ceiling.

I heard the water cascade as she stood up. The second she stepped over the porcelain edge of the tub, I wrapped the towel tightly around her shoulders, completely burying her in the thick fabric. She pulled the edges together, securing the cotton fortress around herself.

"Okay," I said, keeping my hand on her back to guide her. "Let's go."

We stepped out of the humid bathroom and into the much cooler hallway. As we walked toward my bedroom, we both deliberately kept our eyes aimed straight ahead, refusing to look at the dark, open doorway of her room where the broken brass doorknob still lay dead on the carpet.

We crossed the threshold into my room, and I immediately pushed the door shut.

I grabbed the metal doorknob and firmly pressed my thumb against the small, circular push-button lock in the center. The sharp, mechanical *click* echoed loudly in the quiet room. It wasn't a deadbolt. It was just a standard interior lock that could probably be popped with a paperclip, but it was a boundary that hadn't been violated yet. That tiny click meant the perimeter was secure.

Rachel stood shivering slightly in the center of the room, clutching the towel.

I went straight to my wooden dresser and pulled open the bottom drawer. I dug past the heavy denim and pulled out my softest, most worn-out clothes: a pair of gray cotton sleep shorts and a massive, oversized Northwood band nightshirt. The fabric had been washed a hundred times, stripping away any harsh textures or itchy tags.

"Here," I said, holding them out to her. "You don't have to sleep in a damp towel."

She took the clothes with a small, grateful nod.

I immediately turned my back, walking over to face the locked door so she had her privacy to change. The rustle of cotton filled the room as she dropped the towel and quickly pulled the clothes on.

"I'm dressed," she said quietly.

I turned back around. The oversized shirt completely swallowed her, the hem dropping past her knees, but she looked infinitely more comfortable. She climbed onto my twin bed, immediately pushing herself all the way against the wall to make room for me. I quickly changed into my own sleep clothes—a t-shirt and athletic shorts—and climbed in right beside her.

Thirty-eight inches of mattress was a ridiculously tight squeeze. There was absolutely no room to move. But the heavy, restrictive pressure of being wedged between the wall and my side was exactly what her nervous system needed. She pulled the heavy quilt up to our chins, resting her head firmly against my shoulder.

"Michael?" she whispered, staring blankly at the dark ceiling. "What happens tomorrow? They have the court paper."

My brain had been running the calculations on that exact problem since the second Dad broke the door. The court order was a massive, structural authority. We couldn't fight it with just shouting. We needed a structural authority of our own.

"The moment we get to school tomorrow," I said, my voice steady, laying out the logical steps of the plan, "we aren't going to the band room. We are walking straight into the main office, and we are demanding to see the guidance counselor."

Rachel shifted slightly against my ribs. "Will they help?"

"They have to," I reasoned. "Guidance counselors are mandatory reporters. If we tell them that Dad broke your door down with a screwdriver and tried to physically drag you out of the house, and that we are terrified for our safety, the school has to intervene. They can override the parents. They can call the authorities and pause the court order."

It was a desperate mathematical equation, relying entirely on the school's bureaucracy to shield us from the legal system, but it was a solid plan. It gave us a tangible, actionable step for the morning.

"Okay," Rachel breathed, the rigid tension in her jaw relaxing just a fraction. "First thing tomorrow. The counselor."

"First thing," I promised.

The plan was set. The door was locked. The house was finally completely silent. But as the hours ticked by, the darkness in the bedroom felt heavy and suffocating.

Every time the floorboards settled, or the wind rattled the windowpane, Rachel would flinch, her breathing hitching in her throat. My own heart was hammering a restless, anxious rhythm against my ribs. I kept my arm wrapped tightly around her, staring into the dark, my ears straining for the sound of tires in the driveway or footsteps on the stairs.

We had survived the explosion, and we were safe behind the push-button lock. But as we lay there, squeezed together on the narrow twin bed, neither of us could sleep. The war wasn't over; we were just waiting for the sun to come up so we could launch our counterattack.

## Chapter 4: The Inevitable Math (Wednesday, September 5th, 2001)

### Part 1: The Guidance Office – 6:30 AM

The loud, violent hiss of the school bus air brakes and the sharp smell of diesel exhaust usually signaled the start of our rigid daily routine.

Normally, stepping off the bus at 6:25 AM meant walking into Northwood High School and heading straight for the cafeteria to sit in our specific, predictable corner until the first bell rang at 7:15 AM. But today, we completely bypassed the cafeteria. Rachel’s hand was gripping the fabric of my backpack strap so tightly her knuckles were white as we navigated the sparsely populated, echoing hallways toward the administrative wing.

We walked into the guidance counselor's office. The room smelled like stale coffee and floor wax. Mrs. Gable, a soft-spoken woman with thick glasses, was just turning on her desk lamp and sorting through a stack of college brochures. She looked up in surprise as we stood in her doorway.

"Michael? Rachel?" she asked, frowning slightly at the clock on her wall. "You two are here early. Classes don't start for another forty-five minutes."

"We need help," I said. I didn't wait for an invitation to sit. I guided Rachel to the two rigid, plastic chairs across from the desk and sat down, my legs stiff and aching from the sleepless night.

I laid out the data exactly as I had rehearsed it in the dark. I kept my voice clinical and completely factual. I told her about the shouting. I told her about Dad breaking the locked bedroom door with a screwdriver. I told her about the physical altercation, the absolute terror, and his threat to forcibly drag Rachel out of the house to Washington state to live with a woman we had never met.

"You are a mandatory reporter," I stated, staring directly into Mrs. Gable's eyes, using the bureaucratic rule I was certain would be our shield. "We are reporting an unsafe environment. He broke down her door. You have to stop the court order."

Mrs. Gable’s expression shifted from polite morning confusion to deep, heavy sadness. She let out a long sigh, took her glasses off, and pinched the bridge of her nose.

"Michael," she started, her voice painfully gentle. "Did your mother call the police last night?"

"No," I admitted, my stomach tightening. "She just told us to be reasonable."

"Because she knows what the paperwork says," Mrs. Gable explained quietly. She opened a filing cabinet drawer, pulling a manila folder—our student files—onto her desk. "Your parents filed their legal separation agreement with the school district last week so we would know who to contact in an emergency. Dad has primary physical custody of Rachel. Mom has primary physical custody of you."

"But he broke her door!" I argued, the panic starting to spike. "He tried to grab her!"

"I hear you, and I am so incredibly sorry that you both had to go through that," Mrs. Gable said, looking between us. "It is awful. But from a strictly legal standpoint, taking an interior door off its hinges in a house he still legally owns, to collect a child he has legal custody of, is not going to trigger a child protective services intervention. Especially if your mother isn't contesting it in court."

The room went completely still. The ticking of the wall clock suddenly sounded like a hammer hitting an anvil.

"The school cannot override a judge's custody order, Michael," Mrs. Gable said, delivering the final, fatal blow to our equation. "We don't have that authority. If the court says Rachel belongs in Washington with your father... she has to go."

The math had failed. The ultimate authority didn't care about our survival mechanics.

Rachel shot up out of her plastic chair, her chest heaving. The sheer devastation in her dark eyes instantly morphed into blinding, white-hot fury.

"I can't go!" Rachel yelled, her voice echoing off the cinderblock walls of the small office. "I am in the band! We just mapped out the entire muscle memory for *The Mountain Melodies*! I know every single measure!"

"Rachel, I know how much the band means—" Mrs. Gable tried to soothe her.

"No, you don't!" Rachel snarled, tears of absolute rage spilling down her cheeks. She pointed a shaking finger at me. "I play the marimba right next to him! I need to be next to him! If I go to Vancouver, I lose the music! I lose the pattern! I lose my brother!"

She wasn't just losing a hobby. The marching band was the rigidly structured universe where she finally made sense. The mallet permutations, the 4/4 time signatures, the absolute safety of standing shoulder-to-shoulder with her anchor—it was the only thing keeping her grounded while her family collapsed. And this piece of paper was going to rip it all away.

"Time is up," Mrs. Gable whispered, her own eyes watering behind her glasses as she looked at Rachel's heartbreaking fury. "The stalling is over. I am so sorry, Rachel. But you have to start packing."

Rachel let out a jagged, suffocating gasp. The warning bell for first period suddenly shrilled through the hallways overhead, a harsh, grating sound that made us both flinch.

Rachel spun around, grabbed my arm with bruising force, and practically dragged me out of the plastic chair. She didn't say another word to the counselor. She just pulled me out of the office and into the crowded hallway, running entirely on a furious, desperate need to escape the room where our last hope had just died.

## Chapter 5: The Grounded Skies (Tuesday, September 11th, 2001)

### Part 1: The Eerie Silence – 4:00 PM

The Delaney Street house was suffocatingly quiet.

We had turned the television off the second we walked through the front door. The endless, repeating loop of the news footage, the scrolling red tickers, and the panicked voices of the anchors were producing a massive, overwhelming wave of sensory data that neither of our brains could process. The world outside was completely broken, and we needed to shrink our universe down to the absolute smallest possible perimeter just to survive it.

We were huddled together in the center of the downstairs sofa bed.

I was sitting cross-legged, and Rachel was curled into a tight ball, pressed entirely into my chest. I had both arms wrapped around her, locking my hands together over her ribs and pulling her in with a heavy, continuous squeeze. We were both trembling. It wasn't a violent, crying shake; it was a low, constant vibration vibrating through our muscles, a physical byproduct of an overloaded nervous system that just couldn't power down.

Living in Virginia Beach, we were used to the constant, background roar of aviation. Between the commercial jets taking off from Norfolk International and the fighter jets from the nearby naval bases, the sky was always humming.

But this afternoon, the sky was completely, terrifyingly silent. Every commercial plane in the entire country had been ordered out of the air.

"He found the house," Rachel whispered, her voice barely audible in the quiet living room. She kept her face buried against my t-shirt.

"I know," I said, resting my chin on the top of her head.

"The court paper said he could take me when he found a suitable house," she breathed, tracing the terrifying logic loop that had trapped us. "And he found it with Cathy. That's why he bought the tickets for tomorrow. The rule was met."

The structural, inevitable math of the separation agreement had clicked perfectly into place for Dad. He had the house, he had the legal authority, and he had the plane tickets in his pocket.

"But they said on the bus... there are no planes," Rachel continued, her fingers clenching the fabric of my shirt. "They grounded all of them. Everything."

"They did," I confirmed quietly.

It was a horrifying paradox to process. The absolute, overriding authority of the United States government had just slammed the door shut on Dad's itinerary. His confirmation numbers were suddenly worthless. The worst tragedy in modern history had just occurred, people were hurting, and the entire country was paralyzed with fear—but for Rachel, the immediate, physical result of that tragedy was a miraculous stay of execution. The countdown clock had stopped.

But the relief was completely overshadowed by the sheer terror of the unknown. Our brains relied on predictable patterns to feel safe. We needed to know what was going to happen next, and right now, nobody on the planet knew what was going to happen next.

"When will they fly again?" she asked, her voice hitching. "What if he makes me go when they turn the airports back on? I don't want to get on a plane, Michael. After what we saw on the TV... I'm so scared. I can't get on a plane now."

"You don't have to think about that right now," I promised, squeezing her tighter, trying to physically absorb her panic. "The airspace is closed. You don't have to pack. You don't have to look at the suitcases. You aren't leaving tomorrow."

She didn't ask any more questions. The variables were too massive and too dark to sort through. She just closed her eyes and let her body go heavy against mine.

I didn't let go. I sat there on the sofa bed, holding my little sister as tightly as my arms would allow, listening to the eerie, unnatural silence outside our windows. The court order was still hanging over our heads, and the world had permanently changed, but right here, in this exact moment, we were anchored together. And until the skies opened back up, that was the only thing that mattered.

### Part 2: The Wooden Barricade – 5:30 PM

The shadows in the living room were growing long, but neither Rachel nor I made a move to turn on the lamps. We stayed anchored to the center of the sofa bed, wrapped in the heavy, predictable silence of the grounded skies.

Then, the familiar, heavy crunch of tires on the driveway broke the quiet.

Rachel’s entire body went rigid against my chest. Her breathing stopped completely. We didn't need to look out the window to know the sound of Dad’s truck engine.

A car door slammed. Heavy footsteps marched up the concrete walkway, onto the wooden porch, and stopped directly on the other side of the heavy front door.

I tightened my arms around Rachel, pressing my chin firmly against her head. I kept my eyes locked on the brass deadbolt. I had engaged it the second we walked in from school.

The doorknob rattled violently.

"Michael! Rachel!" Dad’s voice boomed through the thick wood. It wasn't the triumphant, cheerful tone from his phone call a few days ago. It was tight, frustrated, and deeply stressed. The chaos of the world had collided with his rigid separation timeline, and he was losing control of the variables. "I know you're in there. Open the door."

We didn't move a single muscle. We didn't make a sound. The house was our fortress, and the deadbolt was the absolute boundary.

"Listen to me!" Dad shouted, pounding his fist against the wood. The heavy thud vibrated through the floorboards. Rachel flinched with every strike, her hands clutching fistfuls of my t-shirt. "The FAA grounded everything. The airports are locked down. The tickets for tomorrow are completely useless."

He paused, breathing heavily on the porch.

"I spent the last two hours trying to find a workaround," Dad yelled through the door, his frustration leaking out. "I called ARTS. They shut down the entire rail network today. Even if they get the trains running tomorrow out of Newport News, the route to Portland takes almost four days. I checked Pathfinder, too. Their bus terminals are evacuated. And even if a bus leaves Norfolk tomorrow, it's an eighty-hour ride."

He let out a bitter, exhausted sigh that carried through the weatherstripping.

"And frankly, Rachel," Dad snarled, the resentment in his voice crystal clear, "after what you pulled yesterday... I am not spending four days trapped in a train car or a Pathfinder bus with a teenager who hates my guts and is going to fight me the entire way. I'm not doing it."

Rachel pressed her face deeper into my chest, fresh tears soaking into my shirt. Hearing the raw, vocalized resentment from our father was agonizing, but the mathematical logic of his anger was our ultimate shield. The sheer length of the cross-country trip had saved her.

"So here is the new plan," Dad announced, his voice taking on a hard, authoritative edge. "We wait for the airports to reopen. I don't know if it will be Friday, or next week. But the second I can get us rebooked on a flight out of Norfolk, I am coming back. And you are going to be packed."

He hit the door one final time with the flat of his hand.

"Have your bags by the door, Rachel. Don't make me break another lock."

The heavy footsteps retreated across the porch. The truck door slammed, the engine roared to life, and he backed out of the driveway, the sound fading down Delaney Street.

The threat was gone, but the atmosphere in the living room was suffocating.

The reprieve was real. She didn't have to get on a plane tomorrow, and she didn't have to endure a four-day train ride. But the victory felt incredibly hollow. Dad hadn't surrendered; he had just pushed the pause button.

"He's going to come back," Rachel sobbed, her voice muffled against my chest. "Whenever they turn the planes back on, he's going to come back."

"I know," I whispered, resting my cheek against her dark hair.

The world outside was a terrifying, unknown variable, and the court order was a ticking time bomb waiting for the airspace to clear. I held her as tightly as I could, the deep pressure of our embrace the only defense we had left against the inevitable math of the divorce.

### Part 3: The Second Barricade – 6:30 PM

After the sound of Dad’s truck completely faded away down Delaney Street, the downstairs sofa bed no longer felt secure. Dad had proven he could walk right up to the front door. We needed a harder boundary, a space where the perimeter was entirely under our control.

Without a word, I stood up and pulled Rachel with me. We bypassed her bedroom entirely—the broken brass doorknob still lying exactly where it had fallen yesterday—and went straight into my room.

I pushed the door shut and pressed my thumb against the small, circular push-button lock. The mechanical *click* echoed loudly. We climbed onto my narrow twin bed, pulling our knees to our chests and pulling the heavy quilt over our shoulders.

An hour later, the front door downstairs opened and closed.

"Michael? Rachel?" Mom’s voice called out from the foyer.

She sounded exhausted. The entire country had just lived through a collective, world-shattering trauma, and I knew the neurotypical script called for families to cling to each other tonight. But that script didn't apply to us. The adult world had proven itself to be fundamentally unsafe, and our parents were the architects of that lack of safety.

We heard her drop her keys on the kitchen counter. A few moments later, her footsteps started up the wooden stairs.

She paused in the hallway, likely seeing the broken door to Rachel's room. Then, her footsteps moved to my door. The brass knob turned, caught against the internal locking mechanism, and stopped.

"Michael, open the door," Mom said, her voice muffled through the painted wood. "It's been a horrific day. I know you both are scared. I want to check on you."

Rachel squeezed her eyes shut and pressed her hands tightly over her ears. The sheer, overwhelming hatred she felt for the woman standing in the hallway radiated off her in physical waves. Mom was the one who had agreed to the "bargain." Mom was the one who signed the paper saying Rachel belonged to Dad.

"Go away!" I yelled, my voice harsh and completely devoid of respect.

"Michael, please," Mom sighed, the exhaustion heavy in her throat. "The whole world is falling apart today. The airports are closed. Your father called and told me he couldn't take her. Just open the door. We need to be together."

"We *are* together!" I screamed back, the blinding fury I felt toward both of our parents completely taking over. "Rachel and me! You aren't a part of this! You let him break her door down yesterday, and you didn't do anything! You just told us to be reasonable!"

"I am trying to follow a legal agreement!" Mom argued through the door, her own frustration spiking. "I didn't want any of this, but it is the law!"

"Then the law is stupid, and so are you!" I roared, completely uncaring about the consequences of disrespecting her. The survival of my sister was the only variable that mattered. "You traded her! You traded her so you could stay in this house! We hate you!"

The hallway went dead silent.

My chest was heaving, my lungs burning from the force of my shouting. Beside me, Rachel had lowered her hands from her ears and was clutching my arm, her face pale, but her dark eyes were hard and completely unforgiving. She agreed with every single word I had just said. We had formed an absolute, impenetrable alliance of two, and both parents had been permanently reclassified as the enemy.

"I brought home some fast food," Mom finally said, her voice tight, wavering on the edge of tears. "I'll leave it on the table downstairs. I'm going to my room."

We listened to her footsteps retreat down the hall. We heard the master bedroom door close with a soft *click*.

Neither of us moved. We didn't care about the food. We didn't care that Mom was probably crying in her room. We had successfully defended our perimeter.

I leaned back against the headboard, keeping my arm firmly locked around Rachel's shoulders. The world outside was grounded, the house was a hostile environment, but behind the push-button lock of my bedroom door, our sanctuary remained perfectly intact.

### Part 4: System Shutdown – 8:00 PM

The silence in the house was absolute, broken only by the faint, rhythmic ticking of the analog clock on my nightstand.

After the adrenaline of the shouting match completely burned out of my system, it left behind a massive, hollow crater of exhaustion. The sheer volume of catastrophic variables we had processed today—the planes on the television, the grounding of the skies, Dad’s fists on the front door, and the furious standoff with Mom in the hallway—was too much data for our brains to hold.

My CP-tightened legs were throbbing with a dull, heavy ache, and Rachel’s eyes were completely glazed over, staring blankly at the wall.

"I'm tired," she whispered, her voice completely flat and devoid of emotion.

"Me too," I said.

We didn't go through our normal evening routine. We didn't unlock the door to use the bathroom. We didn't go to the dresser to pull out the familiar, soft cotton of our pajamas. The executive function required to stand up, unbutton our jeans, and change clothes was entirely beyond our capacity.

Rachel just slid down until she was lying flat on the mattress, still fully dressed in her denim shorts and the t-shirt she had worn to school. She pulled her knees up slightly, making herself as small as possible on the narrow thirty-eight-inch mattress, and pulled the heavy quilt up over her shoulders.

I kicked my sneakers off, letting them hit the carpet with a dull thud, but I left my jeans and my t-shirt on. I lay down right next to her.

Downstairs, sitting on the kitchen table, was the fast food Mom had brought home. I knew exactly what it was; the faint, greasy smell of french fries was slowly drifting up the air vents. We hadn't eaten anything since lunch in the cafeteria before the world ended. My stomach gave a low, painful rumble, but the thought of opening the bedroom door and walking downstairs was physically impossible.

The food was a peace offering from the enemy. It was a neurotypical attempt to smooth over a massive, structural betrayal with a hamburger. To go downstairs and eat it would be accepting her apology, and we absolutely refused to do that. Furthermore, the first floor of the house was compromised. The only secure perimeter left in the entire world was the space behind this push-button lock.

We were not crossing that boundary for anything.

I shifted my weight on the mattress, pressing my side firmly against Rachel's back. The rough texture of our street clothes rubbing against the quilt was uncomfortable, a sensory input we usually avoided at all costs, but tonight, we didn't care. The heavy, restrictive pressure of being wedged together on the tiny bed overrode the discomfort of the denim.

Rachel reached behind her back, blindly finding my hand and lacing her fingers tightly through mine.

I squeezed her hand back, resting my forehead against her shoulder blade. Outside our locked door, the skies were empty, the court order was paused, and our parents were entirely locked out of our universe. The world had stopped spinning, and as we finally closed our eyes, completely exhausted and fully dressed, our nervous systems initiated a total, desperate shutdown.

## Chapter 6: The Compromised Perimeter (Wednesday, September 12th, 2001)

### Part 1: The Broken Routine – 5:45 AM

The harsh, electronic beeping of my alarm clock shattered the heavy silence of the bedroom.

I reached out blindly, my hand fumbling over the nightstand until I slammed my palm down on the snooze button. The sudden quiet rushing back into the room felt heavy and suffocating.

I was incredibly stiff. Sleeping wedged against the wall in my denim jeans and street shoes had left my CP-affected legs screaming with deep, throbbing cramps. Beside me, Rachel shifted on the narrow twin mattress. She hadn't taken her clothes or shoes off either. We had slept entirely braced for an attack that never came.

"Michael?" she whispered in the dark, her voice scratchy and thick with exhaustion. "Is it time?"

Normally, 5:45 AM was the absolute start of our rigid sequence. Alarm. Bathroom. Uniforms. Backpacks. The yellow school bus at the end of the block at exactly 6:25 AM. It was the predictable pattern that ordered our universe.

But as my brain booted up, the terrifying variables of yesterday came crashing back. The grounded airplanes. Dad on the front porch. And worst of all, the cinderblock walls of the guidance counselor's office.

"No," I said, staring up at the dark ceiling.

Rachel turned her head, looking at me. "The bus is coming. We have to get up for band."

"We aren't taking the bus," I stated, my voice low but absolute. "We aren't going to school."

Rachel’s breath hitched. Her eyes went wide in the dim light. "But... we have to. It's Wednesday. We have third-period geometry. We have attendance. If we don't go, they'll call home. We'll get unexcused absences. We'll be in trouble."

The sheer panic of breaking a major, structural rule was fighting a war against her terror of the divorce. We were good kids. We didn't cut class. We didn't cause trouble. The idea of willfully violating the school's attendance policy made my own chest tight with anxiety, but the survival math was undeniable.

"Rachel, listen to me," I said, shifting my weight so I could look directly into her eyes. "Think about the logic. The airspace is closed, which means Dad is stuck here in Virginia Beach. And Mrs. Gable told us to our faces that the school cannot stop him. If we go to Northwood High, and Dad walks into the front office... they will call you down over the loudspeaker, and they will hand you right over to him."

I watched her process the data. Her dark eyes darted back and forth as her brain mapped out the scenario. She pictured the fluorescent lights of the main office. She pictured Dad standing at the secretary's desk. She pictured the school security officer making sure she got into his truck.

The school wasn't a safe zone anymore. It was a trap.

"He knows exactly where we are from 7:15 to 2:10," I continued softly. "It's the most vulnerable place we could possibly be. The only perimeter we actually control is this bedroom."

Rachel swallowed hard, the deeply ingrained fear of getting in trouble at school completely evaporating, replaced by the absolute necessity of survival. "Okay," she whispered, her hands gripping the quilt. "We stay behind the lock."

Ten minutes later, we heard the floorboards creak in the hallway. Mom was awake.

We held our breath, going perfectly still on the mattress. Her footsteps paused outside my bedroom door.

"Michael? Rachel?" Mom called out, her voice flat and drained. The emotional toll of the national tragedy and our screaming match the night before had completely stripped her of her usual parental authority. "Your alarms went off. The bus comes in thirty minutes."

We didn't make a sound. I pressed my thumb firmly against the push-button lock on the knob, just to ensure it was still engaged.

Mom sighed heavily through the wood. She didn't have the energy to fight us. She didn't try the knob, and she didn't demand we answer. The entire country was in a state of paralyzing shock, and enforcing high school attendance was a battle she clearly couldn't fight today.

"There's food in the fridge," she said quietly. "I have to go to work. Don't open the door for anyone."

Her footsteps retreated. We listened to her walk downstairs, grab her keys, and open the front door. The lock clicked behind her, and her car engine started in the driveway.

We waited in total silence.

At exactly 6:25 AM, the loud, familiar squeal of heavy air brakes echoed from the end of Delaney Street. It was our bus, stopping at the corner, opening its doors, and waiting for us.

Rachel squeezed her eyes shut, pressing her forehead against my shoulder. For a rule-follower, hearing the bus leave without you was a physical ache. The engine roared as the heavy yellow vehicle accelerated down the block, leaving us behind.

We were officially playing hooky. But as the sound of the bus faded away, leaving the house completely silent and empty, the tight, suffocating knot in my chest finally loosened. We had broken the rules, but we were still together.

### Part 2: The Afternoon Siege – 11:30 AM

The morning stretched out in an agonizing, slow-motion crawl.

Without the rigid bells of the high school schedule to divide the hours, time felt heavy and formless. We stayed on my narrow twin bed, rationing the remaining fast food Mom had left on the kitchen table, which I had carefully retrieved during a frantic, silent dash downstairs. The cold french fries tasted like cardboard, but we needed the calories to keep our systems from completely crashing.

At 11:30 AM, the familiar, heavy crunch of tires on the driveway broke the silence.

Rachel dropped the half-eaten hamburger wrapper. Her entire body went completely rigid, her back pressing hard against the bedroom wall.

A truck door slammed. Heavy, fast footsteps marched up the concrete walkway. Dad didn't just walk onto the porch this time; he stomped.

The brass doorknob downstairs rattled violently, followed instantly by the heavy, rhythmic pounding of his fist against the wood. It was louder and much more aggressive than yesterday.

"Michael! Rachel!" Dad’s voice roared, echoing through the empty first floor. "Open the damn door!"

We didn't move. I pulled my knees to my chest, wrapping my arms tightly around my legs, my eyes locked on the push-button lock of my bedroom door.

"I know you're in there!" he shouted, hitting the door so hard the weatherstripping squeaked. "I just drove all the way to Northwood! I walked into the main office to get you, Rachel, and the secretary told me exactly where you are!"

Rachel let out a tiny, suffocated gasp, burying her face into my shoulder. The terrible logic of his anger was tracking perfectly.

"Your mother called the attendance office!" Dad yelled, his voice vibrating with absolute, blinding frustration. "She called both of you out sick! So I know you aren't at band practice. I know you aren't in geometry. I know you are sitting right upstairs!"

My stomach plummeted. The school hadn't just been a compromised perimeter; it had acted as an active informant. Mom’s attempt to protect us from the school's truancy rules had completely blown our cover.

"The airports are still closed!" Dad shouted through the wood, his patience entirely evaporated. "But the second they open, we are leaving! I am not playing this game anymore, Rachel! I have the court order, and I am not leaving Virginia without you!"

He paused, breathing heavily on the porch. We could hear the faint, muffled sound of his boots shifting on the concrete.

"I'm going back to the hotel," Dad snarled, issuing his final ultimatum through the door. "But I am calling the airline every hour. When that airspace clears, I am coming back with the police if I have to. Pack your bags."

The heavy footsteps retreated. The truck door slammed, the engine roared to life, and the tires squealed slightly as he backed out of the driveway much too fast.

The house plunged back into its eerie, suffocating silence.

Rachel was shaking violently against my side, her fingers digging painfully into my arm. The threat of the police was a massive, terrifying new variable. The authorities were the ultimate enforcers of the rules, and Dad had the legal paperwork on his side.

"He went to the school," Rachel whimpered, her voice cracking. "He looked for me."

"But he didn't find you," I said firmly, forcing my own panic down to serve as her anchor. "You weren't there."

"He said he's bringing the police," she sobbed, tears spilling hot onto my shirt. "They have guns, Michael. They can break the doors down. They won't care about our rules. They'll make me go."

"I won't let them," I promised, though the absolute certainty in my voice was a desperate lie. I was sixteen. I couldn't fight a police officer. I couldn't override a judge.

The push-button lock on my bedroom door suddenly felt incredibly fragile. The airspace was the only thing keeping the legal system at bay, and we had no idea when the planes would start flying again. We were completely trapped in a countdown we couldn't see, waiting for the skies to open and tear us apart.

## Chapter 7: The Loophole (Monday, September 17th, 2001)

### Part 1: The Bleeding Finances – 2:15 PM

The national airspace had technically reopened a few days ago, but the logistical reality was a complete, gridlocked nightmare. The news stations were showing endless footage of massive, chaotic lines at airport ticketing counters. The system was severely backlogged with tens of thousands of stranded passengers, and getting a new flight out of Norfolk International was mathematically impossible for anyone at the back of the line.

We were sitting on the floor of my bedroom, a heavy textbook open between us, trying to maintain some semblance of a routine, when the cordless phone on my nightstand rang.

I picked it up and immediately hit the speakerphone button, setting it on the carpet between us. We never answered without the other present anymore.

"Hello?" I said cautiously.

"I have had it," Dad’s voice barked from the small plastic speaker. He sounded completely ragged, the exhaustion of the last week wearing away any filter he had left. "I have been on hold with the airline for four hours. Every time I get a human, they tell me there are no seats to Portland until at least Thursday. Maybe Friday."

Rachel and I exchanged a sharp look. Thursday. We had three more days.

"My credit card is completely maxed out," Dad continued, his anger rising, the financial reality of his situation causing his temper to snap. "I budgeted for two nights at this hotel, Michael. I have been here for six. The daily rate is bleeding me dry, and I'm paying for room service because I don't have a kitchen."

I stared at the phone, feeling a cold, clinical detachment from his financial problems. He had chosen to sign the paper. He had chosen to tear our family apart. His credit card limit wasn't my responsibility.

"So here is what is going to happen," Dad demanded, his voice hardening into a direct order. "Rachel, I know you are sitting right there. Pack your bags. Since I am paying for this damn hotel room, you are going to come stay in it with me. I'm coming to the house in twenty minutes, and you are getting in the truck."

Rachel’s entire body went completely rigid.

The hotel. He wanted to pull her out of her safe environment, out of the predictable routine of the house, and trap her in a strange, generic room with the man she currently hated more than anyone else on the planet.

But as the panic spiked in her eyes, I watched her brain suddenly pivot. She didn't dissolve into terrified tears like she had in the bathroom. The sheer volume of trauma over the last week had hardened her defenses. Her brain instantly scanned the rigid, literal rules of the paperwork that was destroying our lives, looking for an error.

And she found one.

Rachel leaned forward, her face inches from the phone’s microphone. "I am not going to a hotel," she stated, her voice icy and completely devoid of fear.

"Excuse me?" Dad snapped. "I have physical custody, Rachel. You don't have a choice."

"The court order says you get custody when you have *suitable housing*," Rachel fired back, weaponizing the exact legal phrasing he had used against us. "A single hotel room with two beds and one bathroom is not suitable housing. It doesn't give a fourteen-year-old teenage girl any privacy. It is temporary lodging. You don't have a house here. So, I am not going."

### Part 2: The Living Hell – 2:30 PM

Dead silence fell over the speakerphone.

The mathematical logic of her argument was flawless. A judge had signed off on a house in Washington state with a woman named Cathy, not a cramped room at a local motel off the interstate.

"I am your father," Dad growled, though his voice wavered slightly against the iron-clad wall of her logic. "I can just call the police to enforce the order."

Rachel’s eyes narrowed into a dark, terrifying glare. The threat of the police had paralyzed her last week, but now, trapped in a corner, it just ignited her fury.

"Then call them," Rachel snarled, her voice dropping to a low, dangerous register. "Send them to the door. Drag me out of this house. But I promise you, Dad... the second we are locked in that hotel room, I will make your life a living hell."

I held my breath, my heart hammering against my ribs.

"You think yesterday was bad?" Rachel continued, her voice trembling with absolute rage. "If you force me into a tiny room with you, I will not stop screaming. I won't sleep, which means you won't sleep. I will break everything in the room. Remember what happened when you broke my door? Remember how my hand felt across your face?"

The memory hung heavily in the static of the phone line.

Dad had felt the sting of that slap. He had seen the blinding, uncontrollable meltdown that had consumed her. And his brain, as rigid and stubborn as it was, had to run the calculations of what she was threatening.

He was stuck in a hotel with paper-thin walls. He was already stressed, bleeding money, and exhausted. If he locked a furious, violently resisting, neurodivergent teenager in that room, she would explode. The noise would carry down the hallway. The hotel management wouldn't hesitate to call security, or the police, on a middle-aged man with a screaming teenage girl in his room.

It was a logistical and social nightmare, and Rachel had perfectly calculated the one scenario he absolutely couldn't control.

"You bring me to that hotel," Rachel promised, delivering the final, fatal blow to his plan, "and they will kick you out into the street by midnight."

Dad let out a long, heavy exhale through his nose. The sound of absolute defeat crackled through the speaker. He realized he was completely trapped. The airlines had him boxed in, his finances had him boxed in, and now, his fourteen-year-old daughter had him boxed in.

"Fine," Dad spat bitterly. "Stay at the house. But the second I have two confirmed tickets in my hand, I am coming for you, and we are going straight to the airport. Do not test me on Thursday, Rachel."

The line clicked dead.

Rachel stared at the phone for a long moment. Then, the adrenaline completely washed out of her system. Her shoulders slumped, and she collapsed sideways, resting her head heavily against my leg.

"He backed down," I whispered in disbelief, resting my hand on her shoulder.

"He did," she said, her voice shaking slightly now that the confrontation was over. "But he's right. I can't use the hotel loophole forever. We only bought three days."

"Three days is a long time," I reminded her, squeezing her shoulder firmly. "We'll figure something out."

We had successfully defended the perimeter again, using the adult world's rules against them. But the victory was temporary, and the countdown clock to Thursday was already ticking loudly in the back of my mind.

## Chapter 8: The Departure (Thursday, September 20th, 2001)

### Part 1: The Bright Idea – 8:00 AM

The two floral-print suitcases sat by the front door like heavy, physical gravestones.

The battle was officially over. The airspace was flowing again, the chaotic backlog of stranded passengers had finally cleared just enough for Dad to secure two seats out of Norfolk International, and the court order was absolute.

Rachel was sitting on the bottom step of the staircase, fully dressed for the airport in her most comfortable, texture-safe clothes. The furious, explosive fight she had displayed over the last few weeks had completely drained out of her system. She was pale, her dark eyes hollow and resigned, staring blankly at the brass lock on the front door. The inevitable math had finally crushed her.

I was sitting on the step right above her, my knees pressed firmly against her back to provide a steady, continuous pressure.

Dad’s truck pulled into the driveway. The heavy engine idled loudly.

Rachel blinked, pulling herself out of her blank stare. Suddenly, a desperate spark of logic lit up in her eyes. She twisted around to look at me, her hands grabbing my knees.

"I have an idea," she whispered rapidly, her voice breathless with a sudden, impossible hope. "You come too. You just get in the truck. You have your clothes packed in your backpack. You just come with me. He can't stop you from getting in."

The brass deadbolt turned. Dad pushed the front door open, immediately stepping into the foyer to grab the handles of the floral suitcases.

"Michael is coming too," Rachel announced to him, standing up from the stairs. "He's coming to Washington."

Dad froze, his hand tightening on the plastic luggage handle. He looked at Rachel, then looked up at me sitting on the stairs. His face was a hard, exhausted mask.

"No, he's not," Dad said flatly. "The court order is crystal clear. Each parent gets one child. Your mother gets Michael. I get you. That was the bargain."

"Then he's coming to the airport!" Rachel pleaded, her voice rising in panic as her loophole was violently closed. "He has to come to the airport to say goodbye!"

"Absolutely not," Dad snapped, his stress levels visibly spiking. "Have you seen the news? The airports are militarized zones now. The National Guard is at the doors. It's going to take us four hours just to get through the new security checkpoints, and non-ticketed passengers aren't even allowed near the gates anymore. I am not dealing with you two having a screaming, crying meltdown at the curb. We are saying goodbye here."

He turned his back, dragging the first suitcase out onto the concrete porch.

### Part 2: The Assumption – 8:15 AM

Mom walked out of the kitchen, clutching a crumpled tissue in her hand. Her eyes were red and swollen.

She stood in the hallway, looking at Rachel, the agonizing reality of the legal document she had signed finally playing out in front of her. When they had sat in the lawyer’s office and agreed to the split custody, the fundamental assumption was that Dad would just rent an apartment across town. Everyone thought we would still be in the same city. No one, not Mom, not Jessica, and certainly not us, had ever suspected he would use the paperwork to run away to the Pacific Northwest with a woman he barely knew.

"Rachel," Mom choked out, taking a step toward her.

"Don't," Rachel snarled, stepping backward until her spine hit the wooden banister. The resignation was gone, instantly replaced by the raw, bleeding betrayal she felt toward both of them.

Dad walked back in for the second suitcase.

"Why?!" Rachel screamed at him, the sound tearing through the quiet house. "Why didn't you just find an apartment here?! Why did you have to go to Washington?!"

Dad didn't look at Mom. He just gripped the handle of the suitcase. "Because I needed to get three thousand miles away from your mother," he said coldly. "And it's too late to argue about it. The tickets are bought. The house is waiting. It's time to go."

On the kitchen counter, the cordless phone was lying flat on its back, the speakerphone broadcasting the entire nightmare.

"You're a coward!" Jessica wept through the plastic speaker, her voice echoing from her tiny dorm room in Seattle. Our older sister was completely broken, forced to listen to her family be physically dismantled while she sat paralyzed in a wheelchair on the other side of the country. "You're tearing them apart!"

Everyone was crying. Mom was sobbing into her tissue. Jessica was weeping over the phone. Rachel's chest was heaving with heavy, jagged gasps.

But I didn't cry. My brain had bypassed the emotion and locked entirely into survival mode. I walked down the final step, stepping directly between Rachel and our parents.

### Part 3: The Calling Cards – 8:30 AM

Mom reached into the pocket of her cardigan. She completely ignored Dad, stepping around me to press a thick, heavy stack of plastic cards into Rachel's trembling hands.

"Put these in your pockets," Mom whispered fiercely, her voice dropping so Dad couldn't hear the exact details of the transaction. "Hide them. Do not let him see them."

I reached into my own jeans pocket, my fingers brushing against an identical, massive stack of plastic. Prepaid calling cards. Hundreds of hours of long-distance minutes.

It was our secret arsenal. Only Mom, Jessica, Rachel, and I knew about them. Dad had no idea how we planned to fund our survival. The one, singular grace of the judge's court order was a strictly written clause: *Michael and Rachel must be allowed unrestricted communication with each other.* Dad legally couldn't stop us from talking, but Mom wasn't going to give him the chance to use the long-distance phone bill as an excuse to cut us off.

Rachel shoved the stack of cards deep into the pockets of her cargo pants.

"Rachel, let's go," Dad called from the open front door, the engine of the truck revving impatiently.

Rachel looked up at me. The panic in her dark eyes was absolute. Her anchor was being ripped away, and she was about to be dragged into a terrifying, unpredictable world of airplanes, armed guards, and strange houses.

I didn't hesitate. I stepped forward and wrapped my arms around her.

It wasn't a normal hug. It was a desperate, crushing collision. I locked my arms around her ribs, squeezing with every ounce of physical strength I possessed, trying to imprint the heavy, grounding pressure into her nervous system so she could carry it with her through the metal detectors. She buried her face into my chest, her hands fisted so tightly in the fabric of my shirt I heard the cotton tear.

We had never used the neurotypical scripts for affection. We didn't do casual hugs, and we never, ever used the emotional buzzwords that normal families threw around. Our brains didn't need the verbal confirmation; we proved our bond through proximity and protection.

But the proximity was gone. The absolute worst-case scenario had arrived, and the situation demanded total, unfiltered clarity.

"I love you," I whispered fiercely into her hair, the words feeling heavy and monumental on my tongue. It was the first time I had ever said those words out loud to another human being.

Rachel sobbed, a violent, full-body shudder ripping through her frame. She squeezed me so hard my ribs ached.

"I love you too," she wept into my chest, her voice breaking on the vow.

"Call me the second you land," I ordered, my voice shaking. "I'll be right by the phone. Unrestricted communication. He can't stop us."

"I will," she promised.

She pulled away. She didn't look at Mom, and she didn't say goodbye to the empty house. She just turned around, walked out the front door, and climbed into the passenger seat of Dad’s truck.

Dad pulled the front door shut behind them.

The heavy lock clicked into place, and the house was suddenly, terrifyingly silent. I stood alone in the foyer, the phantom pressure of her hug still burning against my ribs, and listened to the truck back out of the driveway, taking my sister three thousand miles away.
