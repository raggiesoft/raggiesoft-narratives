title: "The Morning Call"
date: 2004-09-03
start_time: "07:00"
timezone: "ET"
pov: "Michael"
---
I woke up at exactly seven o'clock, the morning sunlight cutting sharply through the blinds of Unit A5.

The apartment was completely, heavily silent. But the silence had a different texture today. I sat on the edge of the pull-out sofa, my chest tightening with a sudden, hollow ache. It was the first morning without Archie. There was no rhythmic clicking of claws on the linoleum, no jingling collar, and no one-eyed dog waiting by the door with a frayed toy. The final physical anchor of my East Coast pack was gone, and the isolation was suffocating.

I pushed through the morning spasticity in my legs and walked over to the cramped kitchenette. I poured myself a bowl of cereal and milk, carrying it over to the glass patio table.

I set my silver flip phone right next to my bowl.

I was practically vibrating with a tense, anxious energy. I wanted to call Delores immediately. I wanted to lock in the logistics for the day so I could get out of this empty room. But my processor was completely stalled by the unwritten rules of standard-hardware social etiquette. Was seven in the morning too early to call? What if she was still asleep? If I woke her up, would it trigger annoyance? The fear of making a massive social error kept my hands entirely off the phone. I just sat there, mechanically chewing my cereal, my internal telemetry running in frantic circles.

At 7:20 AM, the phone suddenly buzzed and lit up against the glass table.

I practically dropped my spoon. The caller ID flashed Delores's name.

I flipped the phone open, my heart hammering a frantic rhythm against my ribs. "Hello?"

"Hey! Good morning," she answered. Her voice was bright, awake, and incredibly eager. "Are you still coming over today?"

A massive, euphoric wave of validation washed through my chest, completely overriding the sadness of the empty apartment. A girl—a girl who wasn't my sister, an honorary sibling, or a cousin—was actively seeking me out. She wasn't just tolerating my presence; she wanted to see me.

"Yeah, absolutely," I replied, forcing my voice to stay steady despite the rush of adrenaline. "I'm just finishing up a quick bowl of cereal for breakfast. Once I'm done and getting ready to head out, I'll call you when I'm on my way."

"Perfect," Delores smiled through the phone. "My mom already left for work, so just call me when you catch the bus, and I'll meet you at the stop."

I hung up the phone. The crushing isolation of the apartment suddenly didn't matter. I had a locked-in destination, a flawless transit algorithm to execute, and a girl waiting for me at the end of the line.
