The credits began to roll, and the house lights of the Roper Performing Arts Center flickered back on, breaking the heavy, polarized spell of the documentary.

As the crowd slowly started to filter out of the aisles, Robyn stepped down from the row behind us. She gently tapped her daughter's shoulder and offered another one of those quiet, encouraging murmurs.

Delores turned to me, pulling a pen from her purse. "Hey, you should give me your phone number," she said, her tone bright and easy.

I didn't hesitate. I pulled my silver flip phone from my pocket, and we quickly traded numbers in the clearing auditorium.

"You should come over tomorrow and spend the day with Delores," Robyn suggested, taking the lead with a warm, maternal hospitality that completely bypassed any standard-hardware awkwardness. "We'd love to have you."

"I don't have any plans tomorrow," I agreed, my schedule completely open now that the summer routines had ended.

"Perfect," Robyn smiled as we walked up the carpeted incline toward the lobby doors. "How are you getting home tonight?"

"I'm going to walk back up to Monticello and Charlotte to catch the Route 2 bus," I explained, running the familiar transit grid in my head. "It's only about a ten-minute ride from there."

"Where do you live?" Delores asked.

"In West Ghent," I replied. "Over near the Midtown Tunnel."

Robyn's face lit up with a convenient realization. "Well, we're heading home to Portsmouth, and we use the Midtown Tunnel to get there anyway. Would you like a ride? It's right on our way, it's really no problem."

My analytical processor instantly weighed the variables. The spasticity in my CP-affected legs was already flaring. I had spent the afternoon walking around the CCHR campus, sitting in a hard library chair, and navigating the downtown crowds. The thought of hauling my heavy backpack all the way back to the bus stop on Monticello Avenue felt physically daunting.

Robyn's offer was a flawless, highly efficient logistical bypass.

"I'd really appreciate that," I said, the relief evident in my voice. "Thank you."

We walked a few blocks to where they had parked and climbed into Robyn's dark blue minivan. I took one of the middle bucket seats, and Delores climbed in right next to me.

As Robyn navigated the downtown streets toward the water, the atmosphere inside the van was incredibly relaxed. Delores and I started making polite, easy conversation in the dark cabin. We talked about the city, the buses, and the movie. There was no pressure, no confusing social subtext to decipher, just a steady exchange of data. Sitting next to her felt completely comfortable.

A short while later, the minivan pulled into the familiar parking lot of 801 Greenway Court.

Delores and I both climbed out of the sliding door, standing on the asphalt near the front walkway of my building.

"Call me anytime in the morning, and we'll figure out when you should come over," Delores said, smiling as she pulled her jacket a little tighter against the evening breeze.

Robyn rolled down the driver's side window, leaning over slightly. "I'll be at work tomorrow, Michael, so it will just be you and Delores at the apartment," she reminded me with a gentle, reassuring smile. "But that's perfectly fine."

To my literal brain, an adult parent explicitly green-lighting an unsupervised visit wasn't just polite; it was the ultimate, undeniable parameter of safety.

"I will," I promised. "Thanks again for the ride."

"See you tomorrow," Delores said.

She stepped forward and wrapped her arms around my torso. It wasn't a brief, polite side-hug typical of a new acquaintance. It was a fierce, incredibly tight embrace. She locked her arms around me and squeezed with a sudden, intense physical weight.

My breath hitched. The deep, heavy compression hit the exact same physical receptors my nervous system relied on to regulate. It was the precise, anchoring pressure I associated exclusively with the safety of my sisters and Ashley. My brain instantly accepted the data, miscategorizing the embrace as a familiar, profound gesture of loyalty and protection.

I hugged her back, the tension in my spine melting away for a fraction of a second, before she finally let go and climbed back into the dark blue minivan.

I watched them pull out of the parking lot, then turned and walked up the three heavy rubber steps into my building.

The deadbolt clicked shut as I stepped into Unit A5. The apartment was completely, suffocatingly silent. I dropped my heavy nylon backpack onto the floor and walked over to the desk, pressing the power button on my computer tower.

The cooling fans whined to life, and the monitor glowed in the dark room. The *Quantum OS XN* welcome screen loaded, displaying the two established user profiles side-by-side: *Michael* and *Ashley*.

I stared at the screen, my eyes locking onto the Sentinel's icon.

A heavy, sudden ache swelled in my chest. I missed Ashley. The dopamine rush from the movie crowd, the easy conversation in the minivan, and the confusing, deeply familiar comfort of that tight hug had my processor running in frantic circles. I was standing in uncharted emotional territory, and the one person who always made the world make sense was 200 miles away.

I let out a slow, shaky breath in the empty studio, wishing more than anything that my best friend was sitting on the sofa bed behind me, ready to translate this new set of variables and tell me exactly how I was supposed to feel about Delores.
