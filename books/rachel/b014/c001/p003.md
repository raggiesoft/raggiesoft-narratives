After finishing my lunch at The Orbit Diner, I slung my heavy nylon backpack over my shoulders and headed back out into the sweltering heat of Downtown Norfolk. I had hours to kill, and I desperately needed a quiet, structured environment to burn through the afternoon.

The Martin Building—the main library and academic hub for the CCHR campus—was exactly one block from the mall's main entrance.

I exited the mall onto Monticello Avenue and waited for the light to cross the busy street. As soon as I hit the opposite sidewalk, I was at the back of the Martin Building. I navigated my way through the concrete plaza, walking around to the front entrance, pushed through the heavy glass doors, and found an isolated carrel desk tucked away on the second floor.

The library was a flawless sensory haven. Unlike the suffocating, heavy emptiness of Unit A5, the silence here was a mandated, communal rule. The low hum of the industrial air conditioning and the occasional, rhythmic rustle of textbook pages provided a predictable background static. I spent the next few hours working through my coursework, letting the rigid, absolute syntax of my assignments ground my lagging processor.

By the time my battery finally started to drain and my eyes grew tired of the heavy LCD screen, the sun was beginning to dip lower in the sky. It was early evening.

I packed my laptop back into my bag and walked downstairs toward the main lobby doors.

As I reached out to push the glass open, a brightly colored flyer stapled to the community bulletin board caught my eye.

**TONIGHT: Special Screening – Celsius 9/11** *CCHR Roper Performing Arts Center – 7:00 PM* *Free Admission with Valid Student ID*

I stopped, my processor running the logistical variables. I knew the Roper well. It was a beautifully restored, historic theater right on the edge of the downtown campus grid.

I looked through the glass doors toward the street, imagining the CVTA bus ride back to West Ghent. If I took the Route 2 home right now, I would be walking into a dark, completely silent apartment. It would just be me, the hum of the refrigerator, and the crushing realization that Ashley wasn't going to be sitting on the sofa bed.

The math was incredibly simple. A two-hour political documentary would eat up the rest of the evening. It would keep me occupied, surrounded by the ambient noise of a crowd, until my physical chassis was exhausted enough to just go home and fall straight to sleep.

I turned away from the lobby doors. I still had plenty of time before the theater opened for seating.

I adjusted the heavy straps of my backpack and decided to loop right back across the plaza to The Admiral Center. I could hit the third-floor food court, grab a quick, predictable dinner at Crown Burger, and then make the short walk over to the Roper. It was a solid, low-friction algorithm for a Friday night, and it kept the silence of the apartment at bay for just a little while longer.
