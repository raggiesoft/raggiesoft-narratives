The silence inside Unit A5 was heavy and absolute.

For the entire summer, this rectangular studio on Greenway Court had buzzed with the steady, predictable rhythm of a two-person operation. But now, the second half of that operation was sitting in a dorm room in Harrisonburg, Virginia. Ashley had packed her bags for her sophomore year at Shenandoah Valley University a week ago, and the sudden, echoing quiet she left behind was suffocating.

I pushed myself off the pull-out sofa, my legs stiff with the usual morning spasticity of my Cerebral Palsy. I grabbed the leash from the hook by the door. Archie, my one-eyed black Lab, was already waiting. True to his absolute biological programming, the dog born right from Aunt Chris's litter had a frayed toy clamped firmly in his jaws, ready to proudly show it off to the world.

We walked out the wooden door, navigated the three heavy, rubber-coated steps in the lobby, and crossed the quiet Norfolk street to the open grassy field. It wasn't a fenced-in dog park, just a simple patch of green, but it was our established routine.

When we got back inside and the deadbolt clicked shut, sealing the perimeter, the emptiness of the apartment hit me again.

I walked over to the computer desk where my heavy tower hummed quietly. Sitting next to the monitor were two framed photographs. I stared at the first one: me, Jessica, and Rachel. I missed my sisters with a dull, constant ache. They were 2,800 miles away in Washington State, and the map just felt entirely too big.

I looked at the second picture: me, Jessica, Rachel, and Ashley. I missed having Ashley living in this apartment with me. With her gone, my daily processor was lagging. The silence was too loud, and I was struggling to maintain the flawless domestic algorithms she had helped me build over the summer.

Archie dropped his toy at my feet. His cloudy, milky-blue eye looked up at me, his canine radar instantly sensing the drop in my emotional baseline. He stepped forward and leaned his solid, sturdy weight directly against my leg, offering silent, deep-pressure doggie comfort.

I reached down and gave him a heavy scratch behind the ears before walking over to the cramped kitchenette. I poured a scoop of kibble into his metal bowl, then poured myself a bowl of cereal and milk. I sat down at our small glass patio table to eat.

The exact moment Archie finished his kibble, he trotted over and sat right next to my folding chair, staring intently at my cereal bowl. He started whining softly, begging for a bite, even though he knew perfectly well that I never shared my breakfast.

I set my spoon down, staring at the loyal, one-eyed dog.

"Well, Archie," I whispered, the knot in my throat tightening. "It’s just you and me. We're the absolute last of the pack on the East Coast."

He tilted his head, his good eye locked onto mine.

"But Mom is coming to get you today," I confessed, my voice cracking in the quiet room. "I'm struggling to take care of you on my own right now. The executive function is just too heavy without Ashley here to help me run the grid."

I slid out of the folding chair and dropped to my knees on the carpet. I wrapped my arms completely around his thick, furry neck, pulling him into a tight, desperate hug.

"You'll be with Mom and Baby," I murmured into his fur, fighting back a heavy sob. "I promise I'll visit. But Mom can give you a much better life than I can right now. I just can't keep up."

I held him tightly, burying my face in his dark fur to ground my fracturing nervous system.

A heavy, familiar knock on the wooden door pulled me out of the static.

I wiped my eyes roughly with the back of my hand, stood up, and twisted the deadbolt. Mom was standing in the hallway, offering a quiet, exhausted, but entirely sympathetic smile. She understood exactly how heavy the architectural silence of the apartment was on my processor.

"You got his gear?" Mom asked softly, stepping inside just far enough to pet Archie's head as he happily wedged himself against her legs.

"Yeah," I nodded, grabbing the plastic bag of kibble and his leash.

"Come on," Mom said, turning back toward the hallway. "You're riding with us to the condo to drop him off. Baby knows you're coming, and he's going to be absolutely miserable if you don't walk through that door with Archie."

I didn't argue with the logistics. The prospect of seeing the older dog was an immediate, vital draw. I locked Unit A5, and the three of us walked out to her idling sedan in the parking lot.

The drive to the Ghent Villas was brief. We navigated into the ground-floor garage and walked up the hollow wooden exterior decking to Unit 2E. The second Mom pushed the front door open, the cramped, carpeted entryway was filled with the frantic clicking of claws.

Baby didn't have Archie's erratic, bouncing energy, but his sheer, massive size more than made up for it. The senior black Lab lumbered forward, completely bypassing Mom and Archie to push his heavy, graying head directly into my stomach.

I dropped my bags and sank to my knees on the floor. Baby let out a low, contented grumble, leaning his entire body weight flush against my chest. The deep, heavy, unyielding pressure was flawless. I wrapped my arms around his thick neck, closing my eyes as the immense physical compression manually forced my redlining heart rate to decelerate.

I stayed on the floor of Greg's narrow hallway for several minutes, letting the two dogs absorb the grief of the transition.

"I'll drop you back off at Greenway Court on my way out," Mom promised, watching me give Baby one last, firm squeeze before standing up. "Are you still going downtown tonight?"

"Yes," I replied, the rigid structure of a scheduled event providing my processor with a necessary anchor. "I'm taking the bus into Downtown Norfolk to grab food before the movie at the Roper."

"Okay," Mom nodded, handing me the condo keys so I could lock up behind us. "Call me if you need anything."

An hour later, Mom's sedan idled briefly in front of 801 Greenway Court. I stepped out, walked up the three heavy rubber steps, and unlocked the door to Unit A5.

The silence inside was completely deafening without Archie's collar jingling or Ashley's quiet typing at the desk. I was entirely alone in my perimeter. I sat down at my computer, pulled up the transit schedule, and began calculating the exact timing for the CVTA bus routes into downtown. I focused entirely on the math of the evening, locking my processor onto the rigid structure of the schedule so I wouldn't have to think about the empty room.
