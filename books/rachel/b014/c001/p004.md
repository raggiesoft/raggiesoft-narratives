The lobby of the CCHR Roper Performing Arts Center was packed, buzzing with the loud, echoing hum of college students and downtown locals waiting for the theater doors to open.

I stood near the back of the line, the straps of my heavy nylon backpack digging slightly into my shoulders. I kept my hands shoved deep into my pockets. I was flying solo tonight, and the sheer volume of the crowd was making my internal telemetry spike just a fraction. I missed Ashley. I missed having my Sentinel standing right beside me to act as a physical buffer against the chaotic, unpredictable energy of the standard-hardware world.

Directly in front of me stood an older woman and a girl who looked to be around my age.

As the line stalled for what felt like the fifth time, I caught the mother shifting her weight. She glanced over her shoulder, her eyes briefly landing on me. She didn't look suspicious or guarded; she had the warm, familiar expression of a parent scanning the room. She leaned in close to her daughter, murmuring something quiet and encouraging, giving her a gentle, almost imperceptible nudge with her elbow.

The girl turned around.

"Are they ever going to let us in?" she asked, offering a slightly exasperated, casual smile. "I swear we've been standing in the exact same spot for ten minutes."

It was the ultimate, standard-hardware social opening. A harmless, frictionless observation designed simply to pass the time.

"It doesn't open until seven," I replied smoothly, my social scripts engaging without a single error code. "The Roper is an active classroom during the day. We're just waiting for the drama majors to finish up their sessions and clear out."

She nodded, looking toward the front of the lobby. We stood in a comfortable, quiet silence for another minute before the mother leaned over and nudged the girl again, whispering something else into her ear.

The girl blinked, turning back to me with a sudden, slightly embarrassed smile.

"Oh, right, where are my manners," she laughed softly, extending her hand. "I'm Delores. Delores Mahone."

"And I'm her mother, Robyn Peterson," the older woman chimed in, offering a polite, welcoming smile.

"Michael Ragsdale," I said, offering a respectful nod to both of them.

I didn't mind the small talk. To my literal brain, the mechanics of the situation were perfectly straightforward. Robyn was just a mom passing the time, and Delores was just a girl bored in the lobby. The conversation followed the strict, predictable rules of waiting-in-line etiquette, so I just ran the standard social scripts. It was an easy, low-friction interaction.

A sudden cheer went up near the front of the lobby. The heavy wooden doors to the auditorium finally swung open.

The line surged forward. We stepped up to the ticket takers. Delores and I both flashed our CCHR student ID cards for free admission, while Robyn handed over her one-dollar guest ticket. We walked through the threshold and down the sloped, carpeted aisle of the historic theater together.

As we scanned the rows for open seats, Robyn stopped and patted her daughter's shoulder.

"Why don't you two sit together?" Robyn suggested easily, gesturing to a pair of empty seats near the center aisle. She pointed to the row immediately behind them. "I'll grab a seat right back here."

It was a perfectly practical, polite logistical maneuver.

"Is that okay?" Delores asked, looking at me.

"Yeah, that works," I agreed.

We filed into the row and took our seats. The heavy velvet curtains were drawn back, and the massive screen loomed in front of us. The atmosphere in the auditorium was incredibly dense. It wasn't the relaxed, casual energy of a standard Friday night blockbuster; the room was charged with a high-voltage, polarizing political electricity.

The lights dimmed, and *Celsius 9/11* began to play.

It didn't take long for the documentary to completely commandeer the room. The narrator's voice was sharp, polished, and relentless. It fed the audience a constant stream of aggressive, black-and-white framing, painting the world into a neat grid of obvious heroes and obvious villains.

My analytical processor naturally craved those kinds of clear, literal parameters. When footage flashed onto the screen showing the President sitting in an elementary school classroom, staring blankly ahead while the narrator explicitly branded him as paralyzed and incompetent, I didn't question the data. I just absorbed the rigid narrative I was being handed.

Delores leaned over in the dark, her shoulder brushing against mine.

"Look at him just sitting there," Delores whispered, her voice laced with intense, absolute certainty. "He's pathetic."

"He doesn't know what to do," I whispered back, completely agreeing with her.

As the movie progressed, the simmering anger in the auditorium only amplified. During a particularly scathing montage, a guy sitting somewhere near the back rows suddenly stood up.

"BUSH SUCKS!" he roared into the dark theater.

The room erupted. Cheers, heavy applause, and loud shouts of agreement echoed off the acoustic paneling of the Roper.

Delores clapped her hands, looking over at me with a bright, energized smile. The sheer, overwhelming unity of the crowd was infectious. I found myself clapping along with her, a sudden, powerful dopamine hit flooding my system. For the first time all day, the heavy, crushing silence of my empty apartment was completely gone. I wasn't sitting alone in the void left by my sisters and Ashley; I was part of a collective, aligned with a girl who saw the exact same clear-cut rules of the world that I did.
