I couldn't sit in the silent apartment anymore.

I walked over to the desk and grabbed my heavy nylon backpack. I unzipped the main compartment and slid my laptop inside—an Apex Voyager 2500. It was a thick, heavy slab of early-2000s plastic, but it held my digital life, and I desperately needed to take my sanctuary with me. I tossed in the power brick, my mouse pad, and the wired USB mouse, zipping it shut and slinging the straps over my shoulders.

I stepped out of Unit A5, ensuring the deadbolt clicked securely behind me.

My transit algorithm engaged automatically. I turned left onto Greenway Court, took a right onto Redgate Avenue, and finally a left onto Hampton Boulevard. The brutal, humid weight of the Virginia September afternoon pressed down on me as I crossed Redgate to reach the westbound bus stop on SR-337.

Ten minutes later, the heavy pneumatic doors of the CVTA bus folded open. I climbed aboard, paid my fare, and took a seat near the window.

The rumble of the diesel engine vibrated up through the floorboards. It was a short, familiar ride—ten to fifteen minutes straight into the heart of Downtown Norfolk—but the empty seat next to me felt massive. For the last three months, Ashley had been sitting right there, our knees touching, acting as my physical shield against the standard-hardware world. Now, I was forced to run the perimeter entirely on my own.

The bus navigated the downtown grid, the air brakes hissing as we pulled to a stop on Charlotte Street at the corner of Bank. I stood up and stepped off onto the concrete. As the bus idled at the curb, I glanced up at the digital destination sign above the windshield. The amber lights flickered, seamlessly changing from *2 DOWNTOWN NORFOLK* to *45 VICTORY CROSSING*. It was a flawless, scheduled interline that my processor always appreciated.

I turned and walked a block down Bank Street, heading straight for the North Parking Deck entrance of The Admiral Center.

The massive, multi-level shopping mall was buzzing with the chaotic, echoing noise of the Friday lunch rush. A wall of ambient sound—chattering crowds, echoing footsteps, and retail music—hit my ears the second I walked through the glass doors. Without the Sentinel to run interference, my internal telemetry spiked slightly, but I forced my posture to remain rigid. I was getting used to navigating the grid by myself. I just had to keep moving.

I headed straight for the Terrace Court elevators and hit the 'Up' button.

My stomach gave a sudden, sharp rumble. I was incredibly hungry, but I had no idea what I actually wanted to eat. The paradox of choice in a massive food court usually triggered a Gray Area Freeze, so I needed a literal, straightforward option.

The elevator chimed, and the heavy doors slid open on the third floor.

Directly across the bustling hall, sitting like a polished, neon oasis in the middle of the mall, was The Orbit Diner. The retro, chrome-and-red-vinyl restaurant usually had a line spilling out the door on a Friday, but right now, there was hardly anyone waiting at the host stand.

I didn't overthink the math. I gripped the straps of my backpack, stepped off the elevator, and walked straight toward the diner. A hot burger and a quiet booth sounded like the perfect, predictable algorithm to reset my system and kill some time.
