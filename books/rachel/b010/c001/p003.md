The bubbles eventually began to dissolve, and the heat of the water slowly bled out into the humid air of the bathroom.

Rachel shifted in the tub, her muscles finally loose enough to move without trembling. "I'm ready," she whispered.

I stood up from the toilet lid instantly. I reached across the small space, grabbed the heavy cotton towel off the rack, and held it wide open like a physical wall, keeping my eyes fixed firmly on the ceiling.

I heard the water cascade as she stood up. The second she stepped over the porcelain edge of the tub, I wrapped the towel tightly around her shoulders, completely burying her in the thick fabric. She pulled the edges together, securing the cotton fortress around herself.

"Okay," I said, keeping my hand on her back to guide her. "Let's go."

We stepped out of the humid bathroom and into the much cooler hallway. As we walked toward my bedroom, we both deliberately kept our eyes aimed straight ahead, refusing to look at the dark, open doorway of her room where the broken brass doorknob still lay dead on the carpet.

We crossed the threshold into my room, and I immediately pushed the door shut.

I grabbed the metal doorknob and firmly pressed my thumb against the small, circular push-button lock in the center. The sharp, mechanical *click* echoed loudly in the quiet room. It wasn't a deadbolt. It was just a standard interior lock that could probably be popped with a paperclip, but it was a boundary that hadn't been violated yet. That tiny click meant the perimeter was secure.

Rachel stood shivering slightly in the center of the room, clutching the towel.

I went straight to my wooden dresser and pulled open the bottom drawer. I dug past the heavy denim and pulled out my softest, most worn-out clothes: a pair of gray cotton sleep shorts and a massive, oversized Northwood band nightshirt. The fabric had been washed a hundred times, stripping away any harsh textures or itchy tags.

"Here," I said, holding them out to her. "You don't have to sleep in a damp towel."

She took the clothes with a small, grateful nod.

I immediately turned my back, walking over to face the locked door so she had her privacy to change. The rustle of cotton filled the room as she dropped the towel and quickly pulled the clothes on.

"I'm dressed," she said quietly.

I turned back around. The oversized shirt completely swallowed her, the hem dropping past her knees, but she looked infinitely more comfortable. She climbed onto my twin bed, immediately pushing herself all the way against the wall to make room for me. I quickly changed into my own sleep clothes—a t-shirt and athletic shorts—and climbed in right beside her.

Thirty-eight inches of mattress was a ridiculously tight squeeze. There was absolutely no room to move. But the heavy, restrictive pressure of being wedged between the wall and my side was exactly what her nervous system needed. She pulled the heavy quilt up to our chins, resting her head firmly against my shoulder.

"Michael?" she whispered, staring blankly at the dark ceiling. "What happens tomorrow? They have the court paper."

My brain had been running the calculations on that exact problem since the second Dad broke the door. The court order was a massive, structural authority. We couldn't fight it with just shouting. We needed a structural authority of our own.

"The moment we get to school tomorrow," I said, my voice steady, laying out the logical steps of the plan, "we aren't going to the band room. We are walking straight into the main office, and we are demanding to see the guidance counselor."

Rachel shifted slightly against my ribs. "Will they help?"

"They have to," I reasoned. "Guidance counselors are mandatory reporters. If we tell them that Dad broke your door down with a screwdriver and tried to physically drag you out of the house, and that we are terrified for our safety, the school has to intervene. They can override the parents. They can call the authorities and pause the court order."

It was a desperate mathematical equation, relying entirely on the school's bureaucracy to shield us from the legal system, but it was a solid plan. It gave us a tangible, actionable step for the morning.

"Okay," Rachel breathed, the rigid tension in her jaw relaxing just a fraction. "First thing tomorrow. The counselor."

"First thing," I promised.

The plan was set. The door was locked. The house was finally completely silent. But as the hours ticked by, the darkness in the bedroom felt heavy and suffocating.

Every time the floorboards settled, or the wind rattled the windowpane, Rachel would flinch, her breathing hitching in her throat. My own heart was hammering a restless, anxious rhythm against my ribs. I kept my arm wrapped tightly around her, staring into the dark, my ears straining for the sound of tires in the driveway or footsteps on the stairs.

We had survived the explosion, and we were safe behind the push-button lock. But as we lay there, squeezed together on the narrow twin bed, neither of us could sleep. The war wasn't over; we were just waiting for the sun to come up so we could launch our counterattack.
