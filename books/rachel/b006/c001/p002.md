The heavy, black Quantum box sat on the concrete of the front porch, daring us to open it.

Jessica gripped the rubber push-rims of her wheelchair, leaning forward slightly, but her face tightened. At only five months post-accident, the mechanics of navigating her manual chair were still a daily, exhausting physical calculation. Bending down to haul a heavy package off the ground from a seated position required core balance she hadn't entirely mastered yet.

"I've got it, Jess," I said immediately, slipping past her chair and stepping out into the brisk April air.

I bent down and scooped the box up. It was incredibly heavy, packed dense with paper and hardware. As I stepped back over the threshold, Baby and Archie were practically vibrating in the foyer. Baby’s massive, Great Dane-sized tail thumped a chaotic, deafening rhythm against the drywall, while Archie pranced in tight circles around Jessica's wheels. They didn't understand the concept of a corporate scholarship, but they inherently understood the soaring, electric joy radiating from my older sister.

"Dining room," Jessica directed, her voice breathless with excitement as she backed her chair up to clear the hallway. "Let's open it on the table."

I carried the box into the dining room, setting it down dead center on the heavy oak table. Rachel practically skipped into the room behind me, pulling out a chair and bouncing on her knees to see over the edge.

Hearing the commotion, Mom walked out of the kitchen, wiping her hands on a dish towel.

"What on earth is all the screaming about?" Mom asked, a cautious smile touching her face as she saw the sheer elation on Jessica’s face.

"I won the Catalyst Initiative, Mom," Jessica said, her hands shaking slightly as she rested them on her armrests. "I got the grand prize. Full tuition. A job at Quantum."

Mom froze. The dish towel slipped from her hands, landing softly on the carpet. "Oh, my god. Jessica!" She rushed forward, throwing her arms around Jessica’s shoulders, tears instantly springing to her eyes.

At that exact moment, Dad walked into the dining room from the hallway, still wearing his work uniform. He looked at the black box, at Mom crying, and then at Jessica.

I reached out and pulled the flaps of the box open. Sitting on top of a stack of branded corporate tech gear was a massive, glossy portfolio folder stamped with the seals of Northwest Pacific University and Quantum Corporation.

I pulled it out and handed it to Jessica. She opened the folder, spreading the thick, heavy-stock documents across the wood for everyone to see. There were campus maps, internship onboarding forms, and a pristine, formal letter of acceptance.

"Seattle," Dad murmured, reading the letterhead. The word dropped from his mouth like a lead weight. His jaw set into a hard, rigid line of immediate frustration. "Washington State."

Jessica’s smile faltered just a fraction. "Yes. The main campus."

"Do you have any idea how much a plane ticket to Seattle costs, Jessica?" Dad groaned, rubbing his face with his heavy, calloused hands. The joyous, electric air in the dining room instantly turned suffocating and cold.

"Dad, they're paying for a six-figure education—" Jessica started.

"And they aren't paying to get you there!" Dad snapped, his anxiety over the budget completely overriding her monumental achievement. He gestured sharply at the wheelchair she was sitting in. "Even if we somehow buy the tickets, how are you going to do this? Who is going to take care of you out there? You can't even reach the top shelves in our own kitchen yet. How are you going to manage an entire life by yourself?"

The absolute, devastating lack of faith hit my processor like a physical strike. He wasn't looking at a prodigy; he was looking at a liability.

"Tom, stop it," Mom commanded. Her voice wasn't loud, but it carried a fierce, protective steel that instantly commanded the room.

Mom stepped around the table, placing her hands flat on the paperwork. She looked directly at our father.

"Look at what is sitting on this table, Tom," Mom said, her eyes flashing with a raw, painful honesty. "I am a college dropout. You didn't even finish high school. We scrape by every single month. And right now, the biggest, most powerful technology corporation on the entire planet is begging our nineteen-year-old daughter to come work for them. They are handing her a life we could never, ever afford to give her."

Dad crossed his arms, staring stubbornly at the wall, but he couldn't refute the math.

"She is going," Mom stated, laying down an absolute, non-negotiable parameter. "I will fly out with her myself. I will help her unpack, I will set up her room, and I will make sure she is situated before I come back."

Mom paused, looking down at Rachel and me. Her expression softened, a sudden, brilliant idea sparking in her eyes.

"Actually," Mom amended, her voice lifting with determination. "We have an entire summer to save up. We will all go. We can make a family vacation out of it. We will fly out to Seattle, see her campus, and send her off to this massive adventure together."

"A family vacation to Seattle?" Dad scoffed, his financial panic flaring again. "Do you know what four plane tickets and hotel rooms are going to cost?"

"We will figure it out," Mom promised fiercely, entirely refusing to let him crush the moment.

While the adults debated the financial logistics, Jessica remained perfectly silent at the head of the table. Her analytical brain had already moved past the argument and was actively processing the data in the NPU housing packet.

I watched her brow furrow. She flipped quickly through a stack of tri-fold brochures detailing the standard freshman dormitories. She pulled out the attached housing contract, her eyes scanning the fine print.

"Wait," Jessica murmured, her voice tight.

The argument between Mom and Dad ceased.

"What is it, Jess?" I asked, my own anxiety spiking at the sudden shift in her tone.

"There's no medical addendum in this packet," Jessica said, staring at the glossy paper. "The room assignment they tentatively drafted for me is on the third floor of a historic, unrenovated building."

She looked up at me, the terrifying, logistical reality dawning on her.

"When I submitted the initial application for the Catalyst Initiative online, it was back in February," Jessica explained, her mind racing. "I was still doing outpatient physical therapy. I didn't check the permanent disability box because the doctors hadn't given me a final prognosis yet. I didn't know I would be permanently bound to this chair."

She tapped her finger hard against the housing contract.

"They don't know," Jessica realized, her pragmatic processor instantly shifting into high gear. "Northwest Pacific University has absolutely no idea that I require an ADA-accessible dorm room, a roll-in shower, or elevator access. And standard university housing is notoriously backlogged."

The victory celebration was officially suspended. Jessica didn't wait for Dad to complain about the new complication. She pulled her wheelchair back from the table, grabbed the housing folder, and spun toward the hallway.

"The deadline to secure fall housing is in two weeks," Jessica commanded, wheeling toward the computer desk under the stairs. "I have to call their disability services office right now. I need an accessible perimeter, and I need to start fighting their bureaucracy today."
