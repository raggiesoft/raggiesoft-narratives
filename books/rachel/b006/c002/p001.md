After hours of highway driving, our charter bus finally pulled into the parking lot of a high-end hotel near the ATL airport.

Jessica, Dad, and I were the first to be let off. I took the handles of her wheelchair while Dad received a list of room assignments from Mr. Panoff.

"Dad, I need to use the restroom," Jessica said, exhausted from the long ride. "Could you check our names off so Michael can take me up to the room?"

Dad relayed the request to Mr. Panoff, who nodded. "Tom, my son is going to take my daughter up to the hotel room so she can use the restroom," Dad said.

Mr. Panoff thought for a moment, then replied, "Right, she can’t use the bus restroom. Check your children off the list and hand them their keys."

I wheeled Jessica to the elevator, bypassing the crowded lobby, and hit the button for the ninth floor. Once inside our assigned room, I dropped our bags on the bed and wheeled her straight into the accessible bathroom. At fifteen, the clinical routine of transferring my older sister from her chair to the toilet was second nature. It was an exhausting, heavy responsibility, but I did it because she needed me, and because our parents continually failed to manage it themselves. We finished the routine quickly, maintaining her dignity, and headed back out to meet Dad.

Dad was waiting by the door. "Does this room meet your accessibility needs?"

"It does," Jessica replied. "Now, I believe we have an hour until dinner."

"We do. I want you downstairs sooner rather than later to get loaded onto the bus," Dad replied, already distracted by the television.

"We’ll head down soon," Jessica said. She looked at him, her voice filled with hope. "Tomorrow we’re tourists, and the day after is the festival. But tonight, when we get back from dinner... I want to try going for a swim."

The logistical nightmare began immediately. When we got back down to the charter bus, the alarms sounded, indicating the wheelchair lift was being deployed—but the heavy metal ramp refused to budge. The bus operator tried everything, his face grim. "We don’t use them often enough, and they get old and rusted," he explained apologetically.

Jessica groaned, her frustration mounting. Other band members started to arrive. Brandon, a heavy-set guy from the drumline, stepped up. "The lift is broken," he announced to a few other athletic guys. "We need to lift Jessica onto the bus."

Mr. Panoff and the bus operator cleared it, and carefully, Brandon and the others lifted her up the narrow stairs. I sat next to her, immediately adjusting her arm so she could hold onto me without digging into my ribs.

"This is going to be annoying if we have to do this every time," she whispered, leaning her weight against my shoulder. "Still... please don't let this take away from your experience."

"I missed sitting next to you," I said honestly.
