The chaotic, post-competition adrenaline was finally wearing off, replaced by the deep, heavy exhaustion of a high school band trip. The marching band had completely taken over the *Crown Burger* located right at the edge of the hotel parking lot in Roanoke Rapids. The dining room was a loud, greasy sea of teenagers in half-unzipped uniform jackets eating fries and completely ignoring the curfew looming over their heads.

I was standing near the soda fountain, trapped in a physical dynamic I didn't fully understand and didn't particularly like.

Ashley Wiswell, a freshman who had actively pursued me for the last two weeks, was currently standing directly behind me. Her arms were wrapped tightly around my chest in a fierce, possessive embrace, her chin resting heavily on my shoulder.

This was her very first high school crush, and she was broadcasting it with aggressive, suffocating enthusiasm. I was only tolerating the physical contact because I had specifically run the scenario past Ashley Raybourn first. My honorary sister had cleared the perimeter, assuming a harmless freshman crush was a safe, low-stakes way for my processor to try dating again after the disaster of ninth grade.

But as I stood there, awkwardly holding my soda cup while Ashley Wiswell clung to me, the heavy glass doors of the *Crown Burger* violently swung open.

"Get off of my daughter!" a grown man's voice roared, echoing over the noise of the restaurant.

The entire dining room instantly went dead silent.

I looked up. Paul Wiswell was storming across the linoleum floor, his face red with fury. He was pointing a heavy, accusing finger directly at me.

"What the hell is wrong with you!?" Mr. Wiswell screamed, his voice booming with the aggressive, unchecked authority of an angry adult. "You are suffocating her! Get your hands off my daughter right now!"

My nervous system didn't even have time to process the sheer, illogical irony of the accusation. I didn't have my hands on her at all; my arms were pinned to my sides by her own embrace. But none of that mattered.

The second that angry, adult male voice echoed through the room, the year 2000 was completely vaporized.

The greasy smell of the *Crown Burger* vanished, replaced by the sterile, sweat-stained mats of a martial arts Dojang. The angry dad storming toward me wasn't Paul Wiswell; he was Curtis. My processor initiated a catastrophic, immediate hard-crash. My muscles locked. My vocal cords instantly paralyzed in profound situational mutism. The terror—the sheer, agonizing anticipation of being physically overpowered and stepped on—crushed my chest so tightly I couldn't draw a breath.

I was going to fail Rachel again. I was going to be broken again.

Ashley Wiswell let go of me and stepped back, looking terrified of her own father, but I couldn't move. I just stared at him, my eyes wide and entirely vacant, trapped in 1995.

Before Mr. Wiswell could close the distance and physically reach me, a solid, immovable figure stepped directly into his path.

"Is there a problem here, Paul?"

Jim Raybourn stood exactly between me and the screaming father. Jim was acting in his capacity as a chaperone, but the posture he took wasn't standard school-sanctioned protocol. His shoulders were set, his voice was deathly calm, and he was physically shielding me with his own body.

"The problem is this kid!" Mr. Wiswell shouted, pointing over Jim's shoulder. "He’s all over my daughter! He's a suffocating creep, just like every other high school boy!"

Jim didn't raise his voice. He didn't need to. "Paul, take a breath and look at the geometry. Your daughter is the one holding him. His arms were pinned."

Mr. Wiswell scoffed, his pride refusing to let him back down or admit his daughter had initiated the contact. "Do you have kids, Jim?" he sneered, trying to leverage the universal 'protective father' card. "Do you? Because I'd like to know what you would say if your daughters were involved with Michael!"

Jim's expression didn't change, but his tone dropped an octave, carrying the absolute, unshakeable weight of a man defending his own blood.

"I have two daughters," Jim replied evenly. "And I have known Michael since he was two years old. When he was a little boy, he spent four months confined to a sofa bed, unable to move, because he took the physical brunt of an adult's rage to protect his little sister. I know exactly what kind of man he is."

Mr. Wiswell blinked, momentarily derailed by the heavy, unexpected history.

"If either of my daughters brought Michael home, I would consider them incredibly safe," Jim continued, gesturing vaguely to where I was standing, completely frozen. "I trust him with my life. And right now, you are terrifying him over a situation your own daughter initiated."

"Boys are all the same at this age—" Mr. Wiswell started to argue.

"Were you a creep at fifteen, Paul?" Jim interrupted smoothly, flipping the script entirely. "Because Michael isn't. The only person acting out of line right now is the adult screaming in a fast-food restaurant. If you have a problem with your daughter dating in high school, you need to be talking to her and setting your ground rules. Do not project your fears onto a kid who hasn't done anything wrong."

Jim didn't wait for a response. He turned his back on Mr. Wiswell, definitively ending the argument, and looked at me.

Ashley Raybourn was already there.

She had materialized at my side the exact second the screaming started. She took one look at my wide, unblinking eyes and my shallow breathing, and she knew instantly that I wasn't in North Carolina anymore. I was seeing Curtis.

She didn't use words; she knew I couldn't hear them anyway. Ashley stepped firmly into my space and wrapped her arms securely around my ribs, applying a maximum, rib-crushing deep-pressure hug.

The heavy, utilitarian squeeze was a physical shock to my nervous system. It was the absolute baseline. It was the Base Camp tether dragging my processor back to reality. *I have you. He can't get to you. The perimeter holds.*

"I've got him, Dad," Ashley whispered fiercely over my shoulder.

Jim nodded, his eyes softening as he looked at me. He understood the trauma just as well as his daughter did. "Alright, pack it up. We're done here. Michael is coming with us."

"He's supposed to be in his assigned room—" one of the other chaperones started to say.

"Michael is sleeping in our room tonight," Jim stated, leaving absolutely no room for negotiation.

Ashley kept her arm firmly wrapped around my waist, physically supporting my weight as the adrenaline crash turned my legs to lead. We walked out of the *Crown Burger*, leaving Ashley Wiswell and her furious father behind forever.

Ten minutes later, the heavy door of the Raybourns' hotel room clicked shut and the deadbolt slid into place.

It was a mobile Base Camp. The room had two double beds. Jim Raybourn, the immovable shield who had literally stepped between me and my worst nightmare, took the bed closest to the door, standing guard.

I crawled into the second bed, still completely mute, shaking from the residual somatic terror. Ashley climbed in right next to me. She didn't hesitate or worry about how it looked; she just grabbed the heavy hotel comforter, pulled it over us, and locked her hand securely around my wrist.

As I lay there in the dark, the terrifying variable of dating was officially, permanently deleted from my processor.

I didn't want a girlfriend. I didn't want the unpredictable chaos of outside families. As Jim Raybourn's steady breathing filled the room and Ashley's rigid, sisterly grip anchored me to the mattress, the math finally settled into an absolute certainty. I had my Base Camp, and I was never leaving it again.
