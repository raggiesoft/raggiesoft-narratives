# Book 14: Delores

## Chapter 1: The Quiet Void (Thursday, September 2, 2004)

### Part 1: The Last of the Pack – 8:00 AM (EDT)

The silence inside Unit A5 was heavy and absolute.

For the entire summer, this rectangular studio on Greenway Court had buzzed with the steady, predictable rhythm of a two-person operation. But now, the second half of that operation was sitting in a dorm room in Harrisonburg, Virginia. Ashley had packed her bags for her sophomore year at Shenandoah Valley University a week ago, and the sudden, echoing quiet she left behind was suffocating.

I pushed myself off the pull-out sofa, my legs stiff with the usual morning spasticity of my Cerebral Palsy. I grabbed the leash from the hook by the door. Archie, my one-eyed black Lab, was already waiting. True to his absolute biological programming, the dog born right from Aunt Chris's litter had a frayed toy clamped firmly in his jaws, ready to proudly show it off to the world.

We walked out the wooden door, navigated the three heavy, rubber-coated steps in the lobby, and crossed the quiet Norfolk street to the open grassy field. It wasn't a fenced-in dog park, just a simple patch of green, but it was our established routine.

When we got back inside and the deadbolt clicked shut, sealing the perimeter, the emptiness of the apartment hit me again.

I walked over to the computer desk where my heavy tower hummed quietly. Sitting next to the monitor were two framed photographs. I stared at the first one: me, Jessica, and Rachel. I missed my sisters with a dull, constant ache. They were 2,800 miles away in Washington State, and the map just felt entirely too big.

I looked at the second picture: me, Jessica, Rachel, and Ashley. I missed having Ashley living in this apartment with me. With her gone, my daily processor was lagging. The silence was too loud, and I was struggling to maintain the flawless domestic algorithms she had helped me build over the summer.

Archie dropped his toy at my feet. His cloudy, milky-blue eye looked up at me, his canine radar instantly sensing the drop in my emotional baseline. He stepped forward and leaned his solid, sturdy weight directly against my leg, offering silent, deep-pressure doggie comfort.

I reached down and gave him a heavy scratch behind the ears before walking over to the cramped kitchenette. I poured a scoop of kibble into his metal bowl, then poured myself a bowl of cereal and milk. I sat down at our small glass patio table to eat.

The exact moment Archie finished his kibble, he trotted over and sat right next to my folding chair, staring intently at my cereal bowl. He started whining softly, begging for a bite, even though he knew perfectly well that I never shared my breakfast.

I set my spoon down, staring at the loyal, one-eyed dog.

"Well, Archie," I whispered, the knot in my throat tightening. "It’s just you and me. We're the absolute last of the pack on the East Coast."

He tilted his head, his good eye locked onto mine.

"But Mom is coming to get you today," I confessed, my voice cracking in the quiet room. "I'm struggling to take care of you on my own right now. The executive function is just too heavy without Ashley here to help me run the grid."

I slid out of the folding chair and dropped to my knees on the carpet. I wrapped my arms completely around his thick, furry neck, pulling him into a tight, desperate hug.

"You'll be with Mom and Baby," I murmured into his fur, fighting back a heavy sob. "I promise I'll visit. But Mom can give you a much better life than I can right now. I just can't keep up."

I held him tightly, burying my face in his dark fur to ground my fracturing nervous system.

A heavy, familiar knock on the wooden door pulled me out of the static.

I wiped my eyes roughly with the back of my hand, stood up, and twisted the deadbolt. Mom was standing in the hallway, offering a quiet, exhausted, but entirely sympathetic smile. She understood exactly how heavy the architectural silence of the apartment was on my processor.

"You got his gear?" Mom asked softly, stepping inside just far enough to pet Archie's head as he happily wedged himself against her legs.

"Yeah," I nodded, grabbing the plastic bag of kibble and his leash.

"Come on," Mom said, turning back toward the hallway. "You're riding with us to the condo to drop him off. Baby knows you're coming, and he's going to be absolutely miserable if you don't walk through that door with Archie."

I didn't argue with the logistics. The prospect of seeing the older dog was an immediate, vital draw. I locked Unit A5, and the three of us walked out to her idling sedan in the parking lot.

The drive to the Ghent Villas was brief. We navigated into the ground-floor garage and walked up the hollow wooden exterior decking to Unit 2E. The second Mom pushed the front door open, the cramped, carpeted entryway was filled with the frantic clicking of claws.

Baby didn't have Archie's erratic, bouncing energy, but his sheer, massive size more than made up for it. The senior black Lab lumbered forward, completely bypassing Mom and Archie to push his heavy, graying head directly into my stomach.

I dropped my bags and sank to my knees on the floor. Baby let out a low, contented grumble, leaning his entire body weight flush against my chest. The deep, heavy, unyielding pressure was flawless. I wrapped my arms around his thick neck, closing my eyes as the immense physical compression manually forced my redlining heart rate to decelerate.

I stayed on the floor of Greg's narrow hallway for several minutes, letting the two dogs absorb the grief of the transition.

"I'll drop you back off at Greenway Court on my way out," Mom promised, watching me give Baby one last, firm squeeze before standing up. "Are you still going downtown tonight?"

"Yes," I replied, the rigid structure of a scheduled event providing my processor with a necessary anchor. "I'm taking the bus into Downtown Norfolk to grab food before the movie at the Roper."

"Okay," Mom nodded, handing me the condo keys so I could lock up behind us. "Call me if you need anything."

An hour later, Mom's sedan idled briefly in front of 801 Greenway Court. I stepped out, walked up the three heavy rubber steps, and unlocked the door to Unit A5.

The silence inside was completely deafening without Archie's collar jingling or Ashley's quiet typing at the desk. I was entirely alone in my perimeter. I sat down at my computer, pulled up the transit schedule, and began calculating the exact timing for the CVTA bus routes into downtown. I focused entirely on the math of the evening, locking my processor onto the rigid structure of the schedule so I wouldn't have to think about the empty room.

### Part 2: The Solo Transit – 12:15 PM (EDT)

I couldn't sit in the silent apartment anymore.

I walked over to the desk and grabbed my heavy nylon backpack. I unzipped the main compartment and slid my laptop inside—an Apex Voyager 2500. It was a thick, heavy slab of early-2000s plastic, but it held my digital life, and I desperately needed to take my sanctuary with me. I tossed in the power brick, my mouse pad, and the wired USB mouse, zipping it shut and slinging the straps over my shoulders.

I stepped out of Unit A5, ensuring the deadbolt clicked securely behind me.

My transit algorithm engaged automatically. I turned left onto Greenway Court, took a right onto Redgate Avenue, and finally a left onto Hampton Boulevard. The brutal, humid weight of the Virginia September afternoon pressed down on me as I crossed Redgate to reach the westbound bus stop on SR-337.

Ten minutes later, the heavy pneumatic doors of the CVTA bus folded open. I climbed aboard, paid my fare, and took a seat near the window.

The rumble of the diesel engine vibrated up through the floorboards. It was a short, familiar ride—ten to fifteen minutes straight into the heart of Downtown Norfolk—but the empty seat next to me felt massive. For the last three months, Ashley had been sitting right there, our knees touching, acting as my physical shield against the standard-hardware world. Now, I was forced to run the perimeter entirely on my own.

The bus navigated the downtown grid, the air brakes hissing as we pulled to a stop on Charlotte Street at the corner of Bank. I stood up and stepped off onto the concrete. As the bus idled at the curb, I glanced up at the digital destination sign above the windshield. The amber lights flickered, seamlessly changing from *2 DOWNTOWN NORFOLK* to *45 VICTORY CROSSING*. It was a flawless, scheduled interline that my processor always appreciated.

I turned and walked a block down Bank Street, heading straight for the North Parking Deck entrance of The Admiral Center.

The massive, multi-level shopping mall was buzzing with the chaotic, echoing noise of the Friday lunch rush. A wall of ambient sound—chattering crowds, echoing footsteps, and retail music—hit my ears the second I walked through the glass doors. Without the Sentinel to run interference, my internal telemetry spiked slightly, but I forced my posture to remain rigid. I was getting used to navigating the grid by myself. I just had to keep moving.

I headed straight for the Terrace Court elevators and hit the 'Up' button.

My stomach gave a sudden, sharp rumble. I was incredibly hungry, but I had no idea what I actually wanted to eat. The paradox of choice in a massive food court usually triggered a Gray Area Freeze, so I needed a literal, straightforward option.

The elevator chimed, and the heavy doors slid open on the third floor.

Directly across the bustling hall, sitting like a polished, neon oasis in the middle of the mall, was The Orbit Diner. The retro, chrome-and-red-vinyl restaurant usually had a line spilling out the door on a Friday, but right now, there was hardly anyone waiting at the host stand.

I didn't overthink the math. I gripped the straps of my backpack, stepped off the elevator, and walked straight toward the diner. A hot burger and a quiet booth sounded like the perfect, predictable algorithm to reset my system and kill some time.

### Part 3: The Time Sync – 1:30 PM (EDT)

After finishing my lunch at The Orbit Diner, I slung my heavy nylon backpack over my shoulders and headed back out into the sweltering heat of Downtown Norfolk. I had hours to kill, and I desperately needed a quiet, structured environment to burn through the afternoon.

The Martin Building—the main library and academic hub for the CCHR campus—was exactly one block from the mall's main entrance.

I exited the mall onto Monticello Avenue and waited for the light to cross the busy street. As soon as I hit the opposite sidewalk, I was at the back of the Martin Building. I navigated my way through the concrete plaza, walking around to the front entrance, pushed through the heavy glass doors, and found an isolated carrel desk tucked away on the second floor.

The library was a flawless sensory haven. Unlike the suffocating, heavy emptiness of Unit A5, the silence here was a mandated, communal rule. The low hum of the industrial air conditioning and the occasional, rhythmic rustle of textbook pages provided a predictable background static. I spent the next few hours working through my coursework, letting the rigid, absolute syntax of my assignments ground my lagging processor.

By the time my battery finally started to drain and my eyes grew tired of the heavy LCD screen, the sun was beginning to dip lower in the sky. It was early evening.

I packed my laptop back into my bag and walked downstairs toward the main lobby doors.

As I reached out to push the glass open, a brightly colored flyer stapled to the community bulletin board caught my eye.

**TONIGHT: Special Screening – Celsius 9/11** *CCHR Roper Performing Arts Center – 7:00 PM* *Free Admission with Valid Student ID*

I stopped, my processor running the logistical variables. I knew the Roper well. It was a beautifully restored, historic theater right on the edge of the downtown campus grid.

I looked through the glass doors toward the street, imagining the CVTA bus ride back to West Ghent. If I took the Route 2 home right now, I would be walking into a dark, completely silent apartment. It would just be me, the hum of the refrigerator, and the crushing realization that Ashley wasn't going to be sitting on the sofa bed.

The math was incredibly simple. A two-hour political documentary would eat up the rest of the evening. It would keep me occupied, surrounded by the ambient noise of a crowd, until my physical chassis was exhausted enough to just go home and fall straight to sleep.

I turned away from the lobby doors. I still had plenty of time before the theater opened for seating.

I adjusted the heavy straps of my backpack and decided to loop right back across the plaza to The Admiral Center. I could hit the third-floor food court, grab a quick, predictable dinner at Crown Burger, and then make the short walk over to the Roper. It was a solid, low-friction algorithm for a Friday night, and it kept the silence of the apartment at bay for just a little while longer.

### Part 4: The Ticket Line – 6:45 PM (EDT)

The lobby of the CCHR Roper Performing Arts Center was packed, buzzing with the loud, echoing hum of college students and downtown locals waiting for the theater doors to open.

I stood near the back of the line, the straps of my heavy nylon backpack digging slightly into my shoulders. I kept my hands shoved deep into my pockets. I was flying solo tonight, and the sheer volume of the crowd was making my internal telemetry spike just a fraction. I missed Ashley. I missed having my Sentinel standing right beside me to act as a physical buffer against the chaotic, unpredictable energy of the standard-hardware world.

Directly in front of me stood an older woman and a girl who looked to be around my age.

As the line stalled for what felt like the fifth time, I caught the mother shifting her weight. She glanced over her shoulder, her eyes briefly landing on me. She didn't look suspicious or guarded; she had the warm, familiar expression of a parent scanning the room. She leaned in close to her daughter, murmuring something quiet and encouraging, giving her a gentle, almost imperceptible nudge with her elbow.

The girl turned around.

"Are they ever going to let us in?" she asked, offering a slightly exasperated, casual smile. "I swear we've been standing in the exact same spot for ten minutes."

It was the ultimate, standard-hardware social opening. A harmless, frictionless observation designed simply to pass the time.

"It doesn't open until seven," I replied smoothly, my social scripts engaging without a single error code. "The Roper is an active classroom during the day. We're just waiting for the drama majors to finish up their sessions and clear out."

She nodded, looking toward the front of the lobby. We stood in a comfortable, quiet silence for another minute before the mother leaned over and nudged the girl again, whispering something else into her ear.

The girl blinked, turning back to me with a sudden, slightly embarrassed smile.

"Oh, right, where are my manners," she laughed softly, extending her hand. "I'm Delores. Delores Mahone."

"And I'm her mother, Robyn Peterson," the older woman chimed in, offering a polite, welcoming smile.

"Michael Ragsdale," I said, offering a respectful nod to both of them.

I didn't mind the small talk. To my literal brain, the mechanics of the situation were perfectly straightforward. Robyn was just a mom passing the time, and Delores was just a girl bored in the lobby. The conversation followed the strict, predictable rules of waiting-in-line etiquette, so I just ran the standard social scripts. It was an easy, low-friction interaction.

A sudden cheer went up near the front of the lobby. The heavy wooden doors to the auditorium finally swung open.

The line surged forward. We stepped up to the ticket takers. Delores and I both flashed our CCHR student ID cards for free admission, while Robyn handed over her one-dollar guest ticket. We walked through the threshold and down the sloped, carpeted aisle of the historic theater together.

As we scanned the rows for open seats, Robyn stopped and patted her daughter's shoulder.

"Why don't you two sit together?" Robyn suggested easily, gesturing to a pair of empty seats near the center aisle. She pointed to the row immediately behind them. "I'll grab a seat right back here."

It was a perfectly practical, polite logistical maneuver.

"Is that okay?" Delores asked, looking at me.

"Yeah, that works," I agreed.

We filed into the row and took our seats. The heavy velvet curtains were drawn back, and the massive screen loomed in front of us. The atmosphere in the auditorium was incredibly dense. It wasn't the relaxed, casual energy of a standard Friday night blockbuster; the room was charged with a high-voltage, polarizing political electricity.

The lights dimmed, and *Celsius 9/11* began to play.

It didn't take long for the documentary to completely commandeer the room. The narrator's voice was sharp, polished, and relentless. It fed the audience a constant stream of aggressive, black-and-white framing, painting the world into a neat grid of obvious heroes and obvious villains.

My analytical processor naturally craved those kinds of clear, literal parameters. When footage flashed onto the screen showing the President sitting in an elementary school classroom, staring blankly ahead while the narrator explicitly branded him as paralyzed and incompetent, I didn't question the data. I just absorbed the rigid narrative I was being handed.

Delores leaned over in the dark, her shoulder brushing against mine.

"Look at him just sitting there," Delores whispered, her voice laced with intense, absolute certainty. "He's pathetic."

"He doesn't know what to do," I whispered back, completely agreeing with her.

As the movie progressed, the simmering anger in the auditorium only amplified. During a particularly scathing montage, a guy sitting somewhere near the back rows suddenly stood up.

"BUSH SUCKS!" he roared into the dark theater.

The room erupted. Cheers, heavy applause, and loud shouts of agreement echoed off the acoustic paneling of the Roper.

Delores clapped her hands, looking over at me with a bright, energized smile. The sheer, overwhelming unity of the crowd was infectious. I found myself clapping along with her, a sudden, powerful dopamine hit flooding my system. For the first time all day, the heavy, crushing silence of my empty apartment was completely gone. I wasn't sitting alone in the void left by my sisters and Ashley; I was part of a collective, aligned with a girl who saw the exact same clear-cut rules of the world that I did.

### Part 5: The Midtown Transfer – 9:00 PM (EDT)

The credits began to roll, and the house lights of the Roper Performing Arts Center flickered back on, breaking the heavy, polarized spell of the documentary.

As the crowd slowly started to filter out of the aisles, Robyn stepped down from the row behind us. She gently tapped her daughter's shoulder and offered another one of those quiet, encouraging murmurs.

Delores turned to me, pulling a pen from her purse. "Hey, you should give me your phone number," she said, her tone bright and easy.

I didn't hesitate. I pulled my silver flip phone from my pocket, and we quickly traded numbers in the clearing auditorium.

"You should come over tomorrow and spend the day with Delores," Robyn suggested, taking the lead with a warm, maternal hospitality that completely bypassed any standard-hardware awkwardness. "We'd love to have you."

"I don't have any plans tomorrow," I agreed, my schedule completely open now that the summer routines had ended.

"Perfect," Robyn smiled as we walked up the carpeted incline toward the lobby doors. "How are you getting home tonight?"

"I'm going to walk back up to Monticello and Charlotte to catch the Route 2 bus," I explained, running the familiar transit grid in my head. "It's only about a ten-minute ride from there."

"Where do you live?" Delores asked.

"In West Ghent," I replied. "Over near the Midtown Tunnel."

Robyn's face lit up with a convenient realization. "Well, we're heading home to Portsmouth, and we use the Midtown Tunnel to get there anyway. Would you like a ride? It's right on our way, it's really no problem."

My analytical processor instantly weighed the variables. The spasticity in my CP-affected legs was already flaring. I had spent the afternoon walking around the CCHR campus, sitting in a hard library chair, and navigating the downtown crowds. The thought of hauling my heavy backpack all the way back to the bus stop on Monticello Avenue felt physically daunting.

Robyn's offer was a flawless, highly efficient logistical bypass.

"I'd really appreciate that," I said, the relief evident in my voice. "Thank you."

We walked a few blocks to where they had parked and climbed into Robyn's dark blue minivan. I took one of the middle bucket seats, and Delores climbed in right next to me.

As Robyn navigated the downtown streets toward the water, the atmosphere inside the van was incredibly relaxed. Delores and I started making polite, easy conversation in the dark cabin. We talked about the city, the buses, and the movie. There was no pressure, no confusing social subtext to decipher, just a steady exchange of data. Sitting next to her felt completely comfortable.

A short while later, the minivan pulled into the familiar parking lot of 801 Greenway Court.

Delores and I both climbed out of the sliding door, standing on the asphalt near the front walkway of my building.

"Call me anytime in the morning, and we'll figure out when you should come over," Delores said, smiling as she pulled her jacket a little tighter against the evening breeze.

Robyn rolled down the driver's side window, leaning over slightly. "I'll be at work tomorrow, Michael, so it will just be you and Delores at the apartment," she reminded me with a gentle, reassuring smile. "But that's perfectly fine."

To my literal brain, an adult parent explicitly green-lighting an unsupervised visit wasn't just polite; it was the ultimate, undeniable parameter of safety.

"I will," I promised. "Thanks again for the ride."

"See you tomorrow," Delores said.

She stepped forward and wrapped her arms around my torso. It wasn't a brief, polite side-hug typical of a new acquaintance. It was a fierce, incredibly tight embrace. She locked her arms around me and squeezed with a sudden, intense physical weight.

My breath hitched. The deep, heavy compression hit the exact same physical receptors my nervous system relied on to regulate. It was the precise, anchoring pressure I associated exclusively with the safety of my sisters and Ashley. My brain instantly accepted the data, miscategorizing the embrace as a familiar, profound gesture of loyalty and protection.

I hugged her back, the tension in my spine melting away for a fraction of a second, before she finally let go and climbed back into the dark blue minivan.

I watched them pull out of the parking lot, then turned and walked up the three heavy rubber steps into my building.

The deadbolt clicked shut as I stepped into Unit A5. The apartment was completely, suffocatingly silent. I dropped my heavy nylon backpack onto the floor and walked over to the desk, pressing the power button on my computer tower.

The cooling fans whined to life, and the monitor glowed in the dark room. The *Quantum OS XN* welcome screen loaded, displaying the two established user profiles side-by-side: *Michael* and *Ashley*.

I stared at the screen, my eyes locking onto the Sentinel's icon.

A heavy, sudden ache swelled in my chest. I missed Ashley. The dopamine rush from the movie crowd, the easy conversation in the minivan, and the confusing, deeply familiar comfort of that tight hug had my processor running in frantic circles. I was standing in uncharted emotional territory, and the one person who always made the world make sense was 200 miles away.

I let out a slow, shaky breath in the empty studio, wishing more than anything that my best friend was sitting on the sofa bed behind me, ready to translate this new set of variables and tell me exactly how I was supposed to feel about Delores.

## Chapter 2: The Gateway (Friday, September 3, 2004)

### Part 1: The Morning Call – 7:00 AM (EDT)

I woke up at exactly seven o'clock, the morning sunlight cutting sharply through the blinds of Unit A5.

The apartment was completely, heavily silent. But the silence had a different texture today. I sat on the edge of the pull-out sofa, my chest tightening with a sudden, hollow ache. It was the first morning without Archie. There was no rhythmic clicking of claws on the linoleum, no jingling collar, and no one-eyed dog waiting by the door with a frayed toy. The final physical anchor of my East Coast pack was gone, and the isolation was suffocating.

I pushed through the morning spasticity in my legs and walked over to the cramped kitchenette. I poured myself a bowl of cereal and milk, carrying it over to the glass patio table.

I set my silver flip phone right next to my bowl.

I was practically vibrating with a tense, anxious energy. I wanted to call Delores immediately. I wanted to lock in the logistics for the day so I could get out of this empty room. But my processor was completely stalled by the unwritten rules of standard-hardware social etiquette. Was seven in the morning too early to call? What if she was still asleep? If I woke her up, would it trigger annoyance? The fear of making a massive social error kept my hands entirely off the phone. I just sat there, mechanically chewing my cereal, my internal telemetry running in frantic circles.

At 7:20 AM, the phone suddenly buzzed and lit up against the glass table.

I practically dropped my spoon. The caller ID flashed Delores's name.

I flipped the phone open, my heart hammering a frantic rhythm against my ribs. "Hello?"

"Hey! Good morning," she answered. Her voice was bright, awake, and incredibly eager. "Are you still coming over today?"

A massive, euphoric wave of validation washed through my chest, completely overriding the sadness of the empty apartment. A girl—a girl who wasn't my sister, an honorary sibling, or a cousin—was actively seeking me out. She wasn't just tolerating my presence; she wanted to see me.

"Yeah, absolutely," I replied, forcing my voice to stay steady despite the rush of adrenaline. "I'm just finishing up a quick bowl of cereal for breakfast. Once I'm done and getting ready to head out, I'll call you when I'm on my way."

"Perfect," Delores smiled through the phone. "My mom already left for work, so just call me when you catch the bus, and I'll meet you at the stop."

I hung up the phone. The crushing isolation of the apartment suddenly didn't matter. I had a locked-in destination, a flawless transit algorithm to execute, and a girl waiting for me at the end of the line.
