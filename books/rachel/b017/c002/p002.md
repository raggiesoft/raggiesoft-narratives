As the heavy diesel roar of the TAPS 565 faded into the drone of the morning traffic, Kristin and I stood together under the massive, sweeping concrete awnings of the transit island. The morning commuter rush was in full swing. Hundreds of tech workers and students carrying umbrellas hurried across the wet asphalt, funneling onto the dozen different TAPS buses idling in their respective bays.

I pulled the collar of my heavy winter coat up against the biting wind and checked the time on my newly activated silver flip phone.

"We have about forty-five minutes until First Continental Bank unlocks their doors," I said, my voice tight with nervous energy. "We should just stay under the shelter here until it's time to walk up to Northeast 8th Street."

"Agreed," Kristin said, her sharp eyes scanning the transit center. "No point in standing in the freezing rain outside a locked bank."

We backed up against the glass windbreak of the shelter, giving the rushing commuters plenty of space. Because my threat-detection system was already humming with the anxiety of the impending bank withdrawal, Kristin didn't leave me hanging. She stepped close, seamlessly linking her arm through mine. She leaned her weight comfortably against my shoulder, acting as a heavy, grounding physical anchor amidst the chaotic swirl of the transit hub.

Ten minutes passed.

A blue-and-white TAPS 564 bus, having traveled all the way up the corridor from Kent and Auburn, pulled into the bay directly across the asphalt from us. It hissed to a stop, opening its doors to discharge a handful of passengers before continuing its route up to the Overlake Transit Center.

Kristin’s posture suddenly went completely rigid against my side.

"Oh shit," Kristin breathed, her voice dropping into a deadly serious whisper. "Go. Get moving. Just get going!"

"What?" I asked, completely confused by the sudden spike in her adrenaline.

"Don't look," Kristin ordered, her fingers digging into the thick sleeve of my coat.

But my processor had already registered the anomaly. I looked across the wet asphalt.

Stepping off the TAPS 564, completely ignoring the pouring rain, was Delores.

My heart instantly stopped. The blood drained from my face, and a cold, paralyzing terror slammed into my chest like a physical weight. My lungs locked up. When she threw me out of the house in Kent, the contact was entirely severed. I had absolutely no idea she was even still on the West Coast.

As my frozen brain processed the transit math, a horrifying deduction clicked into place. The northbound 564 continued straight to the Overlake Transit Center. Delores hadn't planned to get off at Bellevue. She had been on her way to the Quantum campus to scream at Jessica. But she had looked out the window during the brief passenger loading pause, spotted me under the awning, and made a frantic, rage-fueled audible to jump off the bus.

She marched across the transit island, her eyes burning with toxic, unhinged fury. She fully expected me to cower. She expected me to drop my head, apologize, and fall right back into her trap.

But my perimeter was no longer empty.

Kristin didn't run. She didn't flinch. As Delores closed the distance, Kristin flawlessly executed the exact protocol she had silently promised the pack. She stepped slightly in front of me. At five-foot-nine, Kristin was significantly taller and sturdier than Delores. She literally eclipsed the abuser's line of sight.

Kristin wrapped both of her arms securely around my waist, pulling me flush against her side. She rested her head affectionately against my shoulder, projecting an aura of absolute, unbothered confidence and ownership of the space.

Delores stopped dead in her tracks just ten feet away.

The psychological short-circuit was violently visible on Delores's face. She recognized Kristin from the parking lot in Pierce County. But seeing this gorgeous, taller, fiercely protective young woman actively holding me, shielding me, and treating me like I was worth something completely shattered Delores’s delusion. I wasn't shivering in an alley. I was wearing clean clothes, a new coat, and I was being held by a woman who made Delores look entirely pathetic.

Delores's mouth opened, preparing to unleash a torrent of screaming, weaponized guilt.

"Get on the Seattle bus," Kristin commanded in a low, sharp voice, completely ignoring the monster standing ten feet away.

Without breaking contact, Kristin spun us around. An articulated TAPS 550 heading toward Seattle was idling in the adjacent bay, its doors wide open. Kristin kept her body between me and Delores, rushing us up the steps. She flashed her TAPS Horizon Pass to the operator, and I quickly flashed the paper transfer slip I had requested from the 565 driver.

"What is going on?" I rasped, my hands shaking violently as we hurried to the very back of the bus. "How the hell did she find us?!"

"Pure, terrible luck," Kristin said, sliding into the window seat and pulling me down next to her. "Duck down."

We crouched low in the seats. Kristin immediately pulled her new phone out of her pocket, dialing Jessica's 425 number.

"Jess, we have an emergency here," Kristin said, her voice tight but entirely focused. "Delores is in Downtown Bellevue. No, I don’t think it is a coincidence... she got off the 564 right across from us."

Through the rain-streaked window of the idling bus, I peeked over the edge of the seat. Delores was standing on the wet concrete, her fists clenched at her sides. She had spotted us boarding the 550, but she couldn't get to us.

"No, we are safe for now," Kristin told Jessica over the phone, keeping a hawkeye on the window. "We are hiding on the 550. I don’t think she saw us, but I do not want to chance it. I've got to go."

I watched as Delores furiously paced the concrete platform. She looked frantic, humiliated, and completely outplayed. Her territory was permanently gone. Realizing she had lost the trail and completely missed her connection to Overlake, Delores angrily stormed over to the southbound bays.

"That old bat," Kristin snapped.

We watched Delores angrily board a southbound TAPS 564 heading back toward South Hill and Kent. The threat was officially neutralizing itself.

Kristin let out a long, heavy sigh of relief, flipping her phone open again to type a rapid text message to Jessica: *We are safe – Delores gone. Heading to bank now.*

"Let’s get this account closed and get the money out before she does anything else," Kristin said, checking the time. It was five minutes until the bank opened.

Before the 550 could pull out of the bay and merge onto Interstate 90, Kristin grabbed my hand. We pushed through the rear exit doors, stepping safely back onto the concrete of the Bellevue Transit Center.

Kristin reached over, wrapping her arm securely around my shaking shoulders. "You're okay, Michael. She can't touch you. I've got you."

The heavy, paralyzing terror in my chest slowly began to recede, replaced by a profound, overwhelming wave of gratitude. I wasn't related to Kristin. We shared no blood. But as she held me in the freezing rain, projecting absolute safety, my trauma-wired brain finally began to process the truth.

The female body wasn't a weapon anymore. It was a sanctuary.

We left the transit center behind, making the short walk up to Northeast 8th Street together.
