My physical body walked out of the Quantum credit union, but my brain was entirely left behind on Nigel’s desk.

Fifty thousand dollars in a wealth management vault. Six thousand dollars in liquid cash. A brand-new digital perimeter.

For two years, my daily existence had been defined by absolute, suffocating scarcity. I was punished for the cost of tap water. Now, walking across the manicured corporate campus in the freezing rain, the sheer, astronomical scale of the abundance was physically crushing me. My chest felt tight. My breathing was growing shallow and rapid.

Jessica navigated her wheelchair effortlessly down the paved, tree-lined path. We approached a massive, multi-story glass structure marked as Building 34.

"This is Café 34," Jessica announced, leading us through the automatic double doors. "My office is right next door in 35, so this is my usual hub."

We stepped into the atrium, and the sensory input hit me like a shockwave.

It wasn't a standard, sterile corporate breakroom. It was the size of a shopping mall food court. The air was thick with the smell of a dozen different commercial kitchens—wood-fired pizza, a massive grill station, an international curry line, and fresh espresso. Thousands of brilliant, casually dressed tech workers in hoodies and jeans were swarming the floor, laughing, eating, and arguing over open laptops.

I stopped dead in my tracks, my boots glued to the polished floor. The volume, the smell, and the crushing weight of the fifty-six thousand dollars completely overloaded my circuits. I blew a fuse.

A sharp, jagged wheeze escaped my throat. I couldn't process it. It was too much. It was entirely too much.

Kristin noticed my shutdown instantly. She stepped directly into my line of sight, anchoring me. She didn't look overwhelmed by the massive facility; she had clearly been here with Jessica before.

"Hey," Kristin said gently, pulling me out of the main flow of foot traffic. "Look at me. Deep breaths."

Jessica wheeled up to a digital kiosk near the security desk, tapping her employee badge against the reader. She rapidly typed our names into the touchscreen. Two adhesive paper badges printed out from a slot below.

"Here," Kristin said, taking the badges from Jessica. She carefully peeled the backing off and pressed one onto the chest of my heavy winter coat, right over my rapidly beating heart. "You will need to wear these while in the cafeteria. We are officially authorized visitors."

"I... I can't," I stammered, my hands flying up to grip my head, the tears finally breaking through my defenses. I dropped to a crouch right there in the lobby, pulling my knees to my chest. "Jess, it's too much. I owe you my entire life. I don't belong here. I'm broken, I can't—"

"Michael," Jessica said softly, wheeling her chair in close. She didn't tell me to quiet down or care that people were walking by.

Before Jessica could begin the grounding protocol, a massive commotion erupted from a sprawling cluster of tables just inside the cafeteria entrance.

"Code off! Code off! Code off!" a group of about ten people started chanting, rhythmically pounding their fists against the tables.

I flinched, looking up through my tears.

Two guys in their mid-twenties were furiously typing on their laptops, sitting across from each other like they were locked in a gladiatorial arena. The rest of the table was cheering them on.

Jessica let out a warm, exasperated laugh. She looked at Kristin and me. "That would be my team. Quantum Live. We are running the matchmaking network stress tests for *Aegis Protocol* before the holiday launch. We've been in absolute crunch mode all month. They are blowing off steam."

One of the network testers, a guy wearing a faded band t-shirt, looked over and spotted Jessica’s wheelchair. He also spotted me, huddled on the floor, visibly crying and completely overwhelmed.

In the civilian world, a grown man crying in a lobby would be met with stares, whispers, or uncomfortable avoidance. But this was Quantum. These were Jessica's people.

"Jessica brought guests!" the guy called out, standing up from the table.

Instead of making it weird, the group immediately rallied. They didn't offer pity; they offered absolute, frictionless inclusion.

"Guest gets first crack at the pizza!" another tester named Tyler announced loudly, gesturing to the massive wood-fired boxes scattered across their table.

A few of Jessica's coworkers walked over to our perimeter. It was all in incredibly good fun. They knew exactly who I was, and they knew exactly what Jessica had been dealing with over the last week to execute my extraction. They didn't ask questions. They just aggressively welcomed me into the fold.

Kristin reached down, taking my hand and pulling me gently to my feet. "Come on," she whispered, a massive smile on her face. "You heard them. First crack."

Jessica reached around to her waterproof messenger bag and pulled out her piano-black corporate laptop. It instantly whirred to life when she opened the lid. She placed it on a specialized pillow-stand on her lap.

"Let’s show Michael and Kristin how a true Coding Challenge works," my big sister said, a wicked, highly competitive smirk crossing her face as she wheeled up to the table and booted up Quantum Developer Suite.

Naturally, the guy named Kyle pulled his laptop toward him, his eyes lighting up at the challenge from his boss. "Oh, it’s on! What are the rules, Tyler?"

"Quantum Sharp!" Tyler barked, acting as the referee. "Write a recursive method to generate a Fibonacci sequence up to 10,000, store only the even numbers in a dynamically sized array, and print the sum! Sixty seconds! Blind compile! Sudden death: first compiler error loses!"

"Go!"

The "Code Off!" chant began again, the entire team drumming the tables. I sat down next to Kristin, the heavy, suffocating anxiety completely burning away in the warmth of the room. I grabbed a slice of pizza. Kristin pointed toward a massive wall of open-access coolers.

"Go grab a drink, Michael," Kristin grinned, reading my mind. "The food costs money, but the drinks here are completely free. Unlimited. Take whatever you want."

I stared at the coolers, my mind blown all over again. I grabbed an ice-cold soda and sat back down just as Tyler shouted, "Time!"

Jessica and Kyle simultaneously hit Ctrl + Shift + B on their keyboards.

A split second later, a loud, obnoxious Quantum OS error *'ding'* erupted from Kyle's laptop.

Kyle threw his hands up in the air with just a fraction too much dramatic flair, groaning loudly as he read the red text at the bottom of his screen. "CS0266: Cannot implicitly convert type 'double' to 'int'. An explicit conversion exists... I forgot the damn cast!"

The table absolutely erupted.

"PIZZA! PIZZA! PIZZA!" the team chanted, pointing relentlessly at Kyle.

"Alright, alright, I yield! Lunch is on me for the whole group!" Kyle laughed, pulling out his wallet. He caught Jessica's eye for a split second—a microscopic, knowing look passing between them—before pointing a finger at her, grinning deviously. "And yes, before you ask, I'm ordering one with extra anchovies just for our fearless TPM."

"Don't you dare," Jessica shot back, laughing as she closed her laptop. "I will revoke your server access, Kyle."

I took a bite of my pizza and a long drink of the freezing cold soda, looking around the massive, buzzing cafeteria. For two years, I had been convinced the adult world was a cold, hostile place designed to break me, ruled by screaming and financial terror.

But sitting here, surrounded by my sister's brilliant, welcoming coworkers, watching a room full of highly paid engineers playfully torture each other over a syntax error, I finally realized the truth.

I wasn't broken. I had just been trapped in the wrong universe. And Jessica had finally brought me home.
