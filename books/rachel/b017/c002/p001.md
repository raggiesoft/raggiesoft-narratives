Wednesday morning arrived with a violent, blustery vengeance. The Pacific Northwest skies had opened up, dropping a freezing, sideways rain over Renton that battered against the windows of Jessica’s one-bedroom apartment.

But inside, the perimeter was warm, mobilized, and executing a highly coordinated tactical strike.

It was the day before Thanksgiving. By tomorrow, the banking grid would be completely locked down for the four-day holiday weekend. If we didn't sever the financial tether to the East Coast today, Delores would have four completely uncontested days to drain the remaining funds from the joint account I had been stupid enough to let her open in my name at First Continental Bank.

Jessica zipped her waterproof corporate messenger bag. "Alright, pack. We have a very tight window. Let's move."

We stepped out into the biting wind and made our way to the nearest TAPS bus stop. We boarded the Route 105, the heavy air brakes hissing as the bus pulled away from the curb and navigated the slick, rain-soaked streets toward the Downtown Renton Transit Center.

Sitting in the back row of the bus, Rachel leaned her shoulder heavily against mine, her dark eyes fierce and determined.

"I can’t wait to have your account shut down," Rachel rasped over the low hum of the diesel engine. "No more asshole in your life."

I smiled, wrapping my arm around her. "That's the plan, Bug."

It wasn’t a long ride to the transit center. We stepped off the 105 onto the covered concrete platforms. This was our first point of divergence. Rachel had to catch the \#560 bus to the airport, where she would connect to her final route down to Pierce County so she could clock in for her afternoon shift at *The Spot*.

Kristin and I, meanwhile, were tasked with following Jessica up the transit corridor toward Overlake and Bellevue to handle the financial extraction.

As Rachel stepped into the queue for the 560, I turned to look at Kristin.

Over the last few days, Kristin had completely shattered every traumatic, defensive wall Delores had built inside my brain regarding non-relative women. She had physically shielded me, anchored me through severe meltdowns, and respected my boundaries flawlessly.

I took a deep breath, stepping past my own hesitation. I reached out and wrapped my arms around Kristin, pulling her into a tight, deliberate hug. Because of her sturdy, five-foot-nine frame, it felt incredibly secure—like hugging a second Sentinel.

"Truth is, Kristin," I said softly, my voice muffled slightly against her damp jacket, "you are making a huge difference for me right now."

Kristin didn't freeze or make it weird. She just hugged me right back, applying that perfect, deep pressure my autistic nervous system desperately craved.

"I've got your back, Michael," Kristin said warmly. She looked up over my shoulder as a heavy bus pulled into the adjacent bay. "The 565 is here."

I released the hug, feeling my chest lighten. We said our goodbyes to Rachel, watching her safely board her bus, before Kristin, Jessica, and I stepped up to the open doors of the northbound 565.

Jessica and Kristin smoothly flashed their monthly TAPS Horizon Passes to the operator. Because the transit system only sold passes by the calendar month, it made zero mathematical sense to buy one for the last week of November. So, Jessica reached into her pocket and dropped a heavy handful of exact change into my palm.

I fed the coins into the mechanical farebox, the heavy *clink-clink-clink* echoing near the driver's seat, before following the women down the aisle.

As the bus merged onto the wet highway, speeding toward the massive corporate tech hubs of King County, Jessica leaned in close to deliver the final logistics.

"I'm putting the maximum tier Horizon Pass on my corporate card for you on December first, so you won't have to carry all that metal in your pocket much longer," Jessica whispered, giving me a reassuring smile. Her voice then dropped into its authoritative, technical register. "Bellevue Transit Center is coming up next. You two hop off there, and it is a very short walk to the First Continental Bank branch on NE 8th Street. You will get there about thirty minutes before they open."

"Got it," Kristin nodded, her posture straightening as she mentally assumed command of the escort detail.

"Do not wander. Stand at the doors," Jessica continued. "You two best get in there and get it done immediately, because she might be trying to do the exact same thing on the East Coast right now. Once the account is dead and you have the cash, come straight to the Quantum campus in Overlake. I will come down from my office, take you to the credit union to get your new account set up, and treat you both to lunch."

"I have the route memorized," Kristin assured her. "I'll have us take the 230 from the transit center. I will text you the second we board the bus leaving Bellevue."

"Perfect," Jessica said.

The bus began to decelerate as it approached the downtown Bellevue exits.

"Bye, Jess," Kristin and I said in unison as we stood up and moved toward the aisle.

Jessica gave me one last, fierce smile from her seat. "I will be at the Overlake stop waiting for you."

Kristin and I stepped off the bus onto the covered concrete platform of the Bellevue Transit Center, bracing against the rain. The doors hissed shut behind us, and the heavy vehicle lurched forward, carrying Jessica further north toward the Quantum campus.

I took a deep breath, my hands resting in my jacket pockets, feeling the heavy roll of spare quarters pressing against my thigh. I was about to walk into a bank and permanently cut off Delores's access to my life. I was terrified, but standing next to Kristin, I knew my perimeter was absolutely secure.
