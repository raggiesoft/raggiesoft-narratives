By the time we finished cleaning the Heavyweight Knockout Grill and putting away the leftovers, the exhaustion of the day had finally caught up to all of us. But we had a logistical bottleneck: Jessica’s Renton apartment was a one-bedroom.

"Alright, pack rules," Jessica announced, wheeling toward the open door of her bedroom. "Nobody is sleeping on the floor or the sofa. It’s a queen-size mattress. We cram."

We filed into the bedroom. In the civilian world, the logistics of four adults sharing a single bed would be a chaotic, awkward negotiation of boundaries. But we were operating on absolute pack trust.

Just like we had done on Sunday night at the Crown Regency before the Portland offensive, we stripped out of our clothes, shedding the heavy, defensive armor of the day. It was a profound, unspoken vulnerability protocol. For two years, my body had been a warzone, heavily guarded and constantly braced for impact. But stepping into that bed, returning to the absolute, bare vulnerability of skin-to-skin pack grounding, my processor logged zero threats.

We arranged ourselves into a tight, four-person puzzle under the heavy down comforter. Jessica and Rachel anchored one side, while Kristin and I took the other, our bodies pressed close together to make the perimeter work.

Before the lights went out, Jessica leaned over, pressing a warm, familial kiss to my forehead. Rachel immediately followed, kissing my cheek and throwing her arm over my chest to anchor herself.

Lying there in the dim light, feeling the absolute, unconditional love of my sisters, my mind flashed back to the East Coast. Specifically, to that freezing morning in Kent earlier in the week. I shifted slightly, looking over at my older sister.

"Jess?" I whispered into the dark.

"I'm awake, Michael," Jessica whispered back softly.

"I'm sad," I admitted, my voice tight with retrospective grief. "I was thinking about Thursday morning. When you kissed my cheek before you left for work. My processor was running a long-term escape algorithm. I was trying to figure out a slow, methodical way out. I didn't realize until today that you were already holding the door completely open."

Jessica's expression softened in the ambient light filtering through the blinds. She reached under the blanket, finding my hand and squeezing it tight.

"I was fully prepared to take you with me right then," Jessica told me, her voice thick with fierce, logistical love. "I had the route mapped. We would have walked to the Kent station and boarded the TAPS Commuter Rail together. Ridden it straight up into the city to Seattle King Street. Then a quick transfer to the 545 bus, and we would have stepped off at the NE 40th Freeway Station right next to my office. I had the transit fare in my pocket. All you had to do was step onto the train."

A heavy tear slipped down my temple, disappearing into the pillow.

"I wish my processor had caught it," I whispered. "But that kiss you gave me in Kent... it was the Kiss of Death. The exact second your lips touched my cheek, her empire was dead. The psychological tether snapped. I knew the relationship with Delores was permanently over. I just didn't realize my exit was standing right in front of me."

"It doesn't matter how long it took your processor to catch up," Jessica murmured, her thumb brushing against my knuckles. "You made it out. Think about where you were this past Monday. You weren't trapped in that squalor. You were sitting in a padded Ringsider folding chair in the front row of the Rose City Center watching AWE Monday Night WAR live. And this Sunday, you're sitting front row for *No Quarter* up here in Seattle. You are safe now."

"We've got you, Bear," Rachel added sleepily from my left, her grip tightening around my torso.

I took a deep, shuddering breath, the absolute truth of their words washing over me. I had survived the delay, and I would never doubt my sister's logistics ever again.

I turned my head slightly on the pillow, looking to my right. Kristin was lying on her side facing me, her blue eyes soft in the dark. She had listened quietly to the entire exchange, standing guard over the emotional perimeter.

"You okay?" Kristin whispered, her voice barely carrying over the quiet hum of the apartment's heater.

"Yeah," I whispered back.

I shifted my weight, closing the tiny remaining gap between us. I leaned in, and Kristin met me halfway.

It was our second kiss of the day, but the context had shifted entirely. Surrounded by the physical heat and absolute safety of my pack, the kiss was deeply, softly romantic. It was an exploration of trust. I felt the undeniable pull of desire logging in my processor—a heavy, genuine leaning toward wanting to open that door and experience full intimacy with her. I actively *wanted* it.

But I also knew the logistics. We were crammed into a single mattress with my two sisters sleeping inches away. Tonight was not the night for sexual intimacy, and my black-and-white brain was completely at peace with that boundary.

I pulled back slowly, resting my forehead against hers. Kristin smiled in the dark, her hand reaching under the blanket to rest gently against my chest, right over my heartbeat.

I closed my eyes, anchored by Rachel on my left and Kristin on my right. The processor finally powered down, and I fell asleep instantly, completely insulated from the rest of the world.
