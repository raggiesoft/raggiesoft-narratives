We didn’t move from the table for another hour. We just sat in the warm, ambient glow of the dining area, letting our bodies process the sheer volume of Thanksgiving dinner.

Eventually, Rachel let out a long, dramatic sigh, pushing her empty plate forward an inch. "I think I need pie now."

"Initiating phase two," Jessica agreed, wheeling over to the refrigerator.

The dessert course was a flawless execution of holiday logistics. There was a massive pumpkin pie, a warm apple crisp, and—in absolute defiance of Jessica's gourmet kitchen aesthetic—several cans of heavy aerosol whipped cream. We moved our slices back to the living room sofa to eat in front of the television, watching the final, absurd musical numbers of the *Winter Solstice Broadcast* bootleg.

By ten-thirty, the sheer volume of turkey, carbohydrates, and sugar had successfully immobilized the entire apartment. The kitchen was clean, the leftovers were packed away in Quantum-branded food storage containers, and a heavy, deeply peaceful exhaustion settled over the room.

Jessica wheeled to the center of the living room, clapping her hands once to establish command.

"Alright, pack," Jessica announced, looking at the three of us melting into the sofa. "Shift change. Rachel and I are claiming the living room. We are pulling out the extra blankets and passing out right here." She pointed a firm finger toward the hallway. "Michael, Kristin. You two take the bedroom. Shut the door and get some sleep."

My processor paused, instantly logging the massive shift in parameters.

For the last four nights—from the Portland hotel room to the cramped mattress in this exact apartment—I had been physically flanked by my sisters. They had acted as a human shield, an impenetrable physical barrier against the ghost of the East Coast. Sleeping behind a closed door, separated from Jessica and Rachel, was a massive test of my system's recalibration.

But as I waited for the heavy, suffocating spike of terror to hit my chest, I realized the hardest hurdle in the room wasn't my own trauma response. It was the girl sitting to my left.

Rachel went entirely rigid. She had severe separation anxiety when it came to me. For two years, I had been stolen from her, locked away in an abusive void where she couldn't reach me. Since the second we had slammed into each other at the Portland arena, she had been my shadow, refusing to let me out of her physical reach. Sleeping in a separate room wasn't just a test for me; it was an agonizing, terrifying ask for her.

I turned my head. Rachel was staring blankly at the hallway door, her hands gripped so tightly in her lap her knuckles were turning white.

The ambient noise of the television faded into the background as the closed-loop bond between us activated. I shifted closer, bumping my shoulder gently against hers to break the freeze response.

She looked up at me. Her dark eyes were swimming with a mixture of deeply rooted panic and fierce, protective love.

"I'm just on the other side of the wall, Bug," I whispered, keeping my voice incredibly soft, using our private language to anchor her. "I'm not going anywhere. I promise."

Rachel took a ragged, shaky breath. She looked past me, locking eyes with Kristin.

This was the deciding factor. If it had been anyone else, Rachel would have violently vetoed the logistics. But Kristin wasn't an outsider. She was Rachel's best friend. She was the Sentinel.

They held a silent, powerful exchange of absolute trust across the sofa cushions. Kristin didn't offer empty platitudes; she just gave Rachel a small, solemn nod, implicitly promising to guard the perimeter with her life.

Rachel’s rigid posture finally cracked. She turned back to me and threw her arms around my neck, burying her face in my shoulder. It was a deep, bone-crushing squeeze—a desperate holding on, followed by a deliberate, conscious letting go. She was actively choosing to lower her shield so I could finally learn to stand on my own.

"I know, Bear," Rachel whispered fiercely into my collarbone. "Go."

I kissed the side of her head, my throat tight with overwhelming pride.

I stood up and stepped forward, bending down to give Jessica a tight hug. There were no words exchanged, just the silent, fierce acknowledgment from my older sister that I was crossing a massive psychological threshold.

Kristin and I walked into the bedroom. I reached behind me and pushed the heavy wooden door until it clicked shut, sealing us inside. The ambient noise of the television in the living room vanished, leaving us in the quiet, dim light of the single bedside lamp.

For the first time in years, I was completely isolated in a bedroom with a woman. And for the first time in years, I wasn't terrified.

We changed into our sleepwear in comfortable, unhurried silence. When I finally slid under the heavy down comforter, Kristin climbed in beside me. There was no hesitation this time. I immediately closed the distance, wrapping my arms around her waist and pulling her flush against my chest.

She let out a soft sigh, tangling her legs with mine under the sheets. The heat of her skin and the steady rhythm of her heartbeat grounded me instantly.

I leaned down and kissed her. It was deep, heavy, and completely stripped of the tentative caution from this afternoon. The spark we had ignited on the couch flared into a brilliant, consuming fire. I shifted my weight, pressing her gently into the mattress, my hands mapping the curve of her waist, entirely lost in the beautiful, consensual rush of physical intimacy.

Kristin kissed me back with equal intensity, her hands sliding up into my hair. But after a long, breathless moment, she gently placed her hands flat against my chest, slowing the pace just a fraction.

She looked up at me, her blue eyes dark and heavy with desire in the lamplight, but her expression was fiercely protective. She knew exactly how my black-and-white brain operated. She knew I needed the logic explicitly laid out before we crossed the final threshold.

"Michael," Kristin whispered, her voice incredibly soft but completely unwavering. "Just to ensure your processor has every single data point logged before we go any further. I am on birth control. There are no biological traps here. There is zero risk. You are completely safe."

The final, lingering parameter clicked firmly into place. The logic was absolute. The perimeter was secure.

"I know," I breathed back, a profound, overwhelming wave of love and gratitude washing over me. "I'm safe."

Kristin smiled, a beautiful, brilliant expression of pure trust. She reached over my shoulder, her fingers finding the switch on the bedside lamp.

With a soft click, the room faded to black.
