The romantic gravity on the sofa was eventually broken by the sharp, metallic beep of the oven timer in the kitchen.

Kristin and I slowly untangled ourselves from the fleece blanket. My chest was still heaving, my heart hammering a joyous, frantic rhythm, but I felt lighter than I had in years. Kristin offered me a soft, brilliant smile, her hand lingering on my chest for one final, reassuring squeeze before we both stood up.

"Alright, everybody clear the deck!" Jessica commanded, wheeling backward as Rachel pulled the massive, twenty-pound golden turkey from the oven. "We are officially out of counter space. Everything goes straight to the table."

We spent the next ten minutes transforming Jessica's small dining area into a structural engineering marvel. Because the table was only designed for four people, but the food had been scaled for a massive family gathering, every available square inch was covered. There were massive bowls of mashed potatoes, green bean casserole, stuffing, and hot rolls.

We all took our seats. Kristin sat to my right, immediately shifting her chair closer so our knees pressed firmly together under the table.

Jessica picked up a carving knife and looked over the massive spread. She paused, her brow furrowing in perfectly feigned confusion. "Wait. Rachel, did you put the artisanal, fresh-cooked cranberry compote in the crystal dish like I asked?"

Rachel immediately froze. She slowly turned her head, fixing our older sister with a look of absolute, stone-cold betrayal.

My processor instantly aligned with Rachel’s. I dropped my fork. "Jessica, absolutely not," I stated firmly.

"What?" Jessica asked, her face completely deadpan. "It's a gourmet recipe. I spent an hour boiling it down with orange zest and cinnamon."

"If it doesn't have the ridges from the tin, it is not real," Rachel declared fiercely, crossing her arms and refusing to look at the center of the table.

"It's just jam at that point, Jess," I added, backing my little sister up completely. "You can't have Thanksgiving with jam. It has to retain the exact structural integrity of the cylinder. If it doesn't violently jiggle when you drop it on the plate, we aren't eating it."

Kristin looked between Jessica’s incredibly serious face and our absolute, unyielding panic. A second later, she caught the tiny, mischievous glint in Jessica's eye.

"Wait," Kristin laughed, pressing a hand to her mouth. "You didn't make any compote, did you? You're completely messing with them."

Jessica held out for three more seconds before she completely broke, laughing so hard she had to put the carving knife down. "I get them with it every single year. They never see it coming."

She reached under the table to a hidden grocery bag and pulled out a heavy metal can with a bright red and yellow label.

"You two are absolute heathens," Jessica grinned. She popped the top with a can opener, flipped it upside down over a small plate, and gave the bottom a sharp smack.

With a glorious, deeply satisfying *shhh-thwack*, a perfect, gelatinous, dark-red cylinder slid out onto the plate, completely retaining the ribbed texture of the aluminum can.

"Perfection," Rachel whispered, immediately grabbing a butter knife and slicing it precisely along the structural ridges. She placed a perfectly round disc on her plate, and I eagerly took the next one.

Once the annual cranberry crisis was averted, Jessica finished carving the turkey, and the meal officially began.

I loaded my plate with a massive, heavy portion of everything. But as I picked up my fork, my processor automatically initiated an old, defensive subroutine. For the last two years, food was never just food. It was a transaction. Delores used it as a reward for compliance, or she withheld it entirely as a punishment. When I was allowed to eat, I had to eat as fast as physically possible before she decided I hadn't earned it and took the plate away.

I took my first massive bite, my muscles tensing, bracing for the inevitable screaming to start.

But the screaming never came. The only sounds in the apartment were the quiet hum of the refrigerator, the distant, muffled dialogue of the *Winter Solstice Broadcast* continuing on the television, and the warm, easy laughter of the three women surrounding me.

Kristin felt the sudden, rigid tension in my arm. Under the table, she rested her hand gently on my knee, her thumb rubbing a slow, grounding circle against my sweatpants.

"Take your time, Michael," Kristin murmured softly, her voice pitched just for me. "Nobody is taking it away."

I let out a slow, shaky breath. I looked at the massive mound of food on my plate, and then at the twenty-pound turkey sitting in the center of the table.

There was no scarcity here. There was no transaction. This was unconditional, unlimited nourishment provided by my pack. I didn't have to earn my dinner. I just had to sit here and enjoy it.

I loosened my grip on my fork, the defensive tension finally draining out of my shoulders. I took my next bite slowly, actually tasting the rich, buttery potatoes and the savory gravy. It was the best thing I had eaten in two years.

Jessica raised her glass of sparkling cider.

"I originally bought this massive bird for a very different Thanksgiving," Jessica said, looking around the table. "I thought this table was going to be filled with the ghosts of the East Coast. Instead, the universe severed the dead weight."

She looked at Rachel, then at Kristin, and finally, her dark, fierce eyes locked onto mine.

"To the logistical overflow," Jessica smiled, her voice thick with pride. "And to the brand-new West Coast Pack. The perimeter is officially secure."

"Secure," Rachel echoed softly, tapping her glass against Jessica's.

"Secure," Kristin agreed, smiling warmly as she added her glass to the center.

I lifted my own glass, the heavy, gelatinous disc of canned cranberry sauce sitting proudly on my plate, surrounded by the absolute safest people in my universe.

"Secure," I whispered.

For the next hour, we ate until we couldn't physically take another bite. When the plates were finally cleared, I looked at the massive, mostly untouched carcass of the turkey sitting in the center of the table.

"We barely made a dent in that," I noted, doing the math on the sheer volume of meat remaining.

"That is exactly the point," Jessica said, wheeling over to the cabinet to pull out a massive stack of Quantum-branded food storage containers. "Thanksgiving dinner is just the prelude. The real holiday starts tomorrow. We have enough supplies here to build leftover turkey and cranberry sandwiches for the next five days."

My processor logged the data point, and a profound sense of peace settled over me. Tomorrow wasn't going to be a day of punishment or starvation. It was going to be a day of leftovers, safety, and the absolute certainty that I belonged exactly where I was.
