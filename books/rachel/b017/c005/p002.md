The ambient chill in the ground-floor apartment meant we had to move quickly. We stood in the center of the small bedroom and stripped out of our soft sleepwear.

In the neurotypical world, a twenty-two-year-old brother and a twenty-year-old sister would demand closed doors and strict privacy. But the trauma of our past had entirely eradicated those boundaries. Modesty was a luxury for people who didn't rely on each other to survive. We didn't turn away. We maintained our visual tether, an unspoken, continuous validation that we were both real, present, and completely safe.

Rachel pulled on her retail uniform for *The Spot*: crisp khaki pants, sturdy black shoes, black socks, and a bright red polo shirt. She clipped her plastic nametag to her collar, officially transforming into her customer-service persona. Since we were both exactly five-foot-seven, I folded her sleepwear and pulled my heavy denim jeans and winter sweater back on.

Rachel grabbed her backpack from the corner of the room, stuffing her sleepwear, her uniform for tomorrow, and a few toiletries inside. She zipped it up and handed the heavy bag to me.

"Take this to Renton," Rachel rasped, adjusting the collar of her red polo. "So it's ready for me when I get there tonight for the weekend."

"I've got it, Bug," I promised, slinging the strap over my shoulder.

We walked out into the living room. Jessica was already awake on the sofa, stretching her arms and running a hand through her hair.

"Morning, pack," Jessica yawned, transferring into her wheelchair. "Let's get some calories before we hit the grid."

We squeezed into the narrow space around the galley kitchen. Rachel pulled three ceramic bowls from the cabinet and grabbed a yellow box of Harvest Corn Squares from the top of the fridge. She poured the woven cereal into the bowls, then opened the refrigerator, pulling out a heavy plastic gallon jug of whole Vitamin D milk.

Rachel poured a generous splash into her bowl, then passed the heavy jug to me.

My processor instantly stalled. My hand hovered over the plastic handle, but my fingers refused to close around it.

For two years, milk was strictly contraband for me. Delores had explicitly declared it a luxury item, claiming it was too expensive for the grocery budget. But it was never really about the money; it was about absolute, suffocating control. She and her cousin, Brandi, would buy gallons of it for themselves, drinking it freely and pouring it over whatever they wanted. But if I dared to use a single drop for my own cereal, Delores would unleash a screaming, violent meltdown while Brandi snickered maliciously in the background. I was brutally conditioned to eat my food completely dry, punished for wanting basic nourishment while the two adult women in that squalid house gorged themselves on my dime.

I stared at the jug, the phantom echo of Delores's screaming and Brandi's cruel laughter ringing in my ears. I slowly pulled my hand back, my chest tightening. "I'll just... I'll eat it dry."

Rachel stopped chewing. She looked at my dry bowl of Harvest Corn Squares, then up at my face. Her hyper-observant radar instantly traced the origin of the hesitation.

Jessica wheeled up to the edge of the breakfast bar. Her dark eyes locked onto the gallon jug, and then narrowed with absolute, clinical disgust as she realized exactly which two monsters were still haunting my processor.

"Michael," Jessica said, her voice dropping into a tone of absolute, unyielding authority. "Look at me."

I forced my eyes away from the milk and met her intense gaze.

"Delores and Brandi's rules do not exist in this state," Jessica stated firmly, completely overriding the trauma programming. "If you want milk, you drink the milk. You can drink the entire gallon. I will buy you a dedicated dairy cow if that's what it takes to delete those parasites' parameters from your head."

Rachel didn't say a single word. She just reached out, grabbed the plastic handle of the jug, and aggressively tipped it over my bowl, flooding the Harvest Corn Squares with a massive, freezing cold splash of Vitamin D milk.

She set the jug down with a heavy thud, her dark eyes burning with fierce, protective defiance. She wasn't just pouring milk; she was actively pouring a massive, healing counter-measure directly over my trauma.

I looked down at the bowl. It was just a bowl of cereal, but the sheer, overwhelming abundance of it hit me like a physical weight. I picked up my spoon, scooped up a bite, and put it in my mouth. The cold, creamy texture and the crunch of the woven squares was incredible.

"Thank you," I breathed, taking another rapid bite, the suffocating fear evaporating entirely.

"Eat up," Jessica smiled warmly. "We have a bus to catch."

By 7:00 AM, our bowls were in the sink, our heavy coats were on, and Rachel had locked the deadbolt of the ground-floor apartment.

We walked out into the freezing, damp University Place morning, making the short walk to the transit stop on Bridgeport Way. The gray skies were holding off on the rain for now, leaving the air crisp and biting.

At exactly 7:15 AM, the teal and yellow TAPS Route 2—the South 19th-Bridgeport line—hissed to a halt at the curb. The operator lowered the front suspension and deployed the ramp for Jessica. Rachel and I followed her aboard.

We took our seats, and I pressed my shoulder flush against Rachel's red polo shirt as the heavy diesel engine roared. The bus pulled away from the curb, carrying us straight toward the Lakewood Transit Center so my little sister could clock in for the weekend retail war.
