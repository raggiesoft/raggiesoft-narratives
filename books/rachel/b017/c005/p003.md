The teal and yellow TAPS Route 2 bus pulled away from the curb in University Place, its heavy diesel engine rumbling through the floorboards. As we accelerated down Bridgeport Way West, I looked out the rain-streaked window. To our left, the massive, brightly lit facade of *Franklin's Market* slid past. Kristin was already inside, establishing her own perimeter for her opening shift.

Rachel sat perfectly flush against my left side, her shoulder pressing a steady, grounding weight against my ribs. Her dark eyes were tracking the familiar route, her transit-wired brain comfortably engaged in the predictable math of the morning commute.

We rode steadily for about ten minutes, crossing into Lakewood. As the bus approached the major intersection to make a right turn onto Gravelly Lake Drive SW, my analytical processor snagged on a glaring environmental discrepancy.

The bus didn't coast through a green light, and it didn't stop for a red. The intersection was operating as a chaotic, four-way stop.

I looked up at the traffic signal suspended over the road. It wasn't flashing yellow or red to indicate a programmed default state. The massive traffic light was completely, dead-black off.

"That's weird," I murmured, leaning slightly closer to the window.

"What is?" Jessica asked from her securement bay across the aisle.

"The traffic light," I pointed out as the bus carefully navigated the intersection and turned onto Gravelly Lake Drive. "It has absolutely no power."

Rachel frowned, her internal map instantly updating to account for the anomaly. We rode down Gravelly Lake for exactly three blocks before the bus made its scheduled left turn onto 100th Street SW.

The traffic light at that intersection was completely dead, too.

When the bus reached the end of the block and made a right turn onto 59th Avenue SW, I scanned the surrounding storefronts and streetlamps. Nothing was glowing. The neon signs were dark. The familiar, low-level hum of urban infrastructure was entirely missing. The entire grid was down.

"It's a massive blackout," Jessica observed, her corporate, logistical brain immediately assessing the scale of the failure. "We've passed three major arterial intersections without a single active signal."

The bus made a final left turn onto Lakewood Towne Center Boulevard SW, slowly navigating the darkened, unusually quiet commercial streets before pulling into the sprawling Lakewood Transit Center.

"Zone G," Rachel announced automatically as the air brakes hissed.

The operator deployed the ramp. I guided Jessica’s wheelchair down to the damp concrete of the transit island, and Rachel followed right behind us.

We didn't have to catch a connection. We just had to walk across the street. The massive, sprawling footprint of the Lakewood Towne Center retail complex loomed ahead of us. We crossed the asphalt together, heading toward the giant, red-painted exterior of *The Spot*.

As we walked down the wide, paved sidewalk bordering the complex, the reality of the power failure became starkly obvious. We passed the large, glass-fronted facade of *Harrison's* department store, and right next to it, the familiar green-and-white awning of *The Penny Post* dollar store. Neither building had a single interior light on. Even the emergency egress lights, which usually glowed faintly near the exits, were dark. The massive rooftop HVAC units, which usually provided a constant, droning white noise to the shopping center, were entirely silent.

"The whole grid is offline," I noted, my anxiety spiking just a fraction of an inch. An unpowered, unpredictable retail environment was a massive break in Rachel's routine.

We rounded the final corner into the massive parking lot of *The Spot*.

"Look," Rachel rasped softly, stopping dead in her tracks.

Standing on the sidewalk directly outside the main entrance of the superstore was a sea of bright red polo shirts and khaki pants. Dozens of employees were huddled together in the freezing morning mist, holding coffee cups and pulling their uniform jackets tight against the damp chill. The massive sliding glass doors of the store were pulled shut and locked.

For a neurodivergent brain that relied entirely on rigid, predictable schedules, showing up to work and finding the building locked and the routine shattered was usually a guaranteed catalyst for a severe meltdown.

Rachel’s breathing hitched. I immediately stepped forward, wrapping my arm securely around her waist to provide a deep, grounding physical anchor before the panic could spiral.

But Rachel didn't melt down. She looked up at the dark, silent building, and then looked down at me. Because her Bear was standing right next to her, the unexpected shift in parameters wasn't a threat. It was just a puzzle that needed solving.

"I need to check in," Rachel whispered, stepping into my chest and wrapping her arms around me for a quick, fierce hug. "I need to find my GSTL."

"We're right behind you," I promised, giving her a tight squeeze.

Rachel pulled back, adjusted her backpack, and walked confidently toward the crowd of red shirts. Jessica and I trailed a few paces behind, maintaining the perimeter while giving her the space to handle her professional logistics.

Rachel navigated through the cluster of employees until she found a man holding a heavy plastic clipboard and a walkie-talkie. It was Marcus, her Guest Services Team Lead.

"Marcus," Rachel announced her presence in her quiet, raspy voice. "I am here for my shift."

Marcus looked up from his clipboard, his face lined with the deep, frantic exhaustion of a retail manager dealing with a catastrophic facility failure.

"Rachel! I'm so sorry, I was just making my way down the phone tree to call you," Marcus sighed, clicking his pen and checking her name off his roster. "We had a massive substation failure just a few hours ago. It completely knocked out the grid for the Towne Center and the surrounding blocks."

"When does the store open?" Rachel asked, her mind needing absolute parameters.

"It doesn't," Marcus said, shaking his head. "The emergency generators only cover the basic egress lighting and the refrigeration units, and even those are struggling. We absolutely cannot boot the point-of-sale registers or the servers. Corporate just got off the phone with the utility company. They have to replace a massive piece of hardware. There is zero estimate for restoration, but they told us not to expect full power until sometime on Monday."

Rachel’s dark eyes went wide. "Monday?"

"Monday," Marcus confirmed. "The store is officially closed for the weekend. Anyone who is scheduled for today is being sent home. If you're scheduled tomorrow, consider it canceled until further notice."

"I have Sunday and Monday off anyway," Rachel noted softly, her logistical brain instantly running her calendar.

"Then you just got an extra day added to your weekend," Marcus offered a tired but genuine smile. "Go home, Rachel. Stay warm."

Rachel stepped back from the crowd. She turned around and walked back to where Jessica and I were waiting on the wet pavement. Her face was completely blank as she processed the massive data shift.

"What's the verdict, Bug?" I asked, my muscles tensing in preparation to comfort her through the broken routine.

Rachel looked at the locked, dark doors of *The Spot*. Then she looked at Jessica sitting in her wheelchair, and finally, she looked directly at me. The blank, processing stare completely melted away. A massive, brilliant, uncontainable smile broke across her face.

"The store is broken," Rachel announced, practically vibrating with sudden, intense joy. "I don't have to work today."

"Wait, really?" Jessica asked, a matching grin spreading across her face. "They sent you home?"

"Until Tuesday," Rachel confirmed, instantly stepping flush against my left side and wrapping her arm fiercely around my ribs.

My processor completely stalled, and then the sheer, mathematical perfection of the universe hit me like a shockwave.

We had successfully pulled me out of the terrifying squalor of Kent. We had survived the transit grid. We were supposed to spend the entire day sitting in Renton, waiting for her shift to end so the pack could finally be whole again. But a random, catastrophic failure of the municipal power grid had just entirely rewritten the schedule.

"You have the whole day off," I breathed, wrapping my arm tightly around her shoulders and pulling her into a heavy, crushing pack hug.

"I have the whole day off," Rachel rasped happily against my chest. She pulled back just enough to look at Jessica. "We don't have to separate. We can all go back to Renton together right now."

"That is exactly what we are going to do," Jessica laughed, spinning her wheelchair around toward the transit center. "Base Camp is officially expanding its hours. Let's go home."
