We walked back into the ground-floor apartment at Chambers Creek Estates, our arms loaded with the heavy plastic grocery bags from Franklin's Market.

The moment the front door clicked shut and the deadbolt engaged, sealing out the damp, gray University Place morning, the tension of the transit grid officially evaporated. It was time to process the supplies.

In the neurotypical world, putting away groceries is a tedious household chore. But for our pack, it was a highly satisfying exercise in spatial geometry and routine control. And because this was Rachel and Kristin's lease, Rachel was the absolute, undisputed commander of the operation.

"Okay," Rachel rasped, dropping her bags onto the small breakfast bar. Her dark eyes lit up with focused, logistical purpose. "The cereal boxes go on the top shelf of the pantry, aligned by height. The pasta goes on the second shelf."

I carried my bags into the narrow galley kitchen, setting them on the counter. "You're the boss, Bug. Just point."

Because the galley kitchen was a standard apartment build, it was entirely too narrow for Jessica to turn her manual wheelchair around in. She didn't try to force her way into the bottleneck. She parked her chair flush against the edge of the breakfast bar, establishing her command post right at the boundary line.

"Hand me the non-perishables that go in the lower cabinets," Jessica instructed, reaching over the counter. "I can reach the shelving under the island from here."

We formed a frictionless, three-person assembly line. I unpacked the bags, handing the heavy cans and dry goods to Jessica, who meticulously stacked them into the lower cabinets with corporate precision. Meanwhile, I handed the refrigerated items to Rachel. She organized the milk, the deli meat, and the vegetables, ensuring the labels all faced outward so her environment remained perfectly predictable.

It took exactly fifteen minutes. When the last empty plastic bag was folded and shoved into the dispenser under the sink, the kitchen was immaculate.

Rachel shut the refrigerator door with a soft, satisfying thud. She turned around, looking at the perfectly stocked, highly regulated sanctuary she had built with Kristin. She let out a long, heavy exhale. The adrenaline of the morning's power outage and the retail navigation had finally burned off, leaving behind a deep, hollow exhaustion.

She looked at me, her dark eyes entirely soft and vulnerable.

"I need to snuggle," Rachel whispered.

"Sofa," I agreed instantly, pulling my winter coat off and tossing it onto a barstool.

We migrated into the small living room. Rachel immediately climbed onto the heavy, dark-blue couch, pulling the thick fleece blanket off the backrest. I sat down right beside her. She didn't hesitate; she pressed her side flush against my ribs, throwing her arm securely over my chest and burying her face into my shoulder.

A second later, the squeak of rubber tires sounded behind us.

Jessica rolled up to the edge of the sofa and locked her brakes. "Make room," our older sister announced. "I want in on this."

She planted her hands on her armrests and smoothly transferred her weight out of the wheelchair, sliding onto the sofa cushions on my left side. The geometry locked into place flawlessly. I was sitting dead center, the massive, heavy anchor for both of them. Jessica wrapped her arm through mine, resting her head against my left shoulder, while Rachel clung fiercely to my right.

It had been years—not since the dark, freezing nights in the Delaney Street living room—since I had been sandwiched in a proper, three-way sibling cuddle. The deep, restrictive, bilateral pressure sent a massive wave of regulation straight through my central nervous system.

The quiet hum of the refrigerator in the kitchen was the only sound in the apartment.

I closed my eyes, leaning my head back against the cushions. I was entirely enveloped in safety. The walls of the apartment were solid. My sisters were holding me together. And because my processor finally recognized that the environment was absolutely, unconditionally secure, the heavy, iron vault door in my brain suddenly swung wide open.

The dam broke.

"She seemed so perfect at first," I whispered into the quiet room.

My voice was shaking, the words tumbling out of my mouth before I even fully realized I was speaking. Rachel tightened her grip on my shirt. Jessica didn't say a word; she just rested her hand flat against my chest, waiting for me to download the corrupted data.

"Like any bad relationship, it was a trap," I sobbed quietly, the tears suddenly spilling hot and fast down my cheeks. "When I met Delores, she seemed flawless. She actually respected that Rachel was the most important woman in my life. She pretended to understand."

I swallowed hard, the suffocating memory of the timeline closing in. "But it only took a couple of weeks for the isolation to start. She slowly started restricting my days. She made me feel guilty for calling you guys. And then, to celebrate one month of being together... we had sex."

Rachel didn't flinch at the admission, and Jessica just rubbed her thumb gently against my collarbone.

"She moved into my small studio apartment in Norfolk right after that," I choked out, my chest heaving. "And it was terrible. It felt completely oppressive. It wasn't like the Summer of the Sentinel."

A fresh, jagged wave of grief ripped through my throat as I said the words. The sudden, agonizing realization of how much I missed my childhood best friend hit me like a physical blow. When Ashley Raybourn had moved into my studio apartment in 2004, it had been a sanctuary. Because Ashley was my best friend, sharing a living space and practicing romantic intimacy felt incredibly safe, communicative, and secure.

"I thought Ashley was just practice," I wept, the tears soaking into the collars of my sisters' shirts. "I thought the suffocating, heavy feeling with Delores was what real adult romance was actually supposed to be. I put up with it because I felt like I had nowhere else to go. I thought I had to endure it."

"You didn't have to endure anything," Jessica whispered fiercely.

"And then Thanksgiving 2004 happened," I continued, my voice dropping into a disgusted rasp. "When you and Rachel visited her apartment in Portsmouth. Do you remember?"

"When she decided to cook the turkey completely naked," Jessica muttered, her voice dripping with absolute, clinical revulsion.

"Yes," I shuddered. "Beyond the practical concerns of hot oil spitting from a pan onto bare skin... did you and Rachel really need to see her naked? No! You did not! It wasn't about being comfortable. It was a massive, territorial display to make you guys uncomfortable so you would leave. And it just got worse and worse from there. She constantly degraded me."

I squeezed my eyes shut, the phantom smell of ammonia and squalor burning the back of my nose.

"And then it culminated a few days ago," I sobbed, my entire body trembling against them. "When she dragged me onto that cross-country Pathfinder bus. Kent was an absolute disaster. The house was filthy. There was cat feces everywhere on the floor. When we were waiting at the train station on Thursday morning, my gut instinct was literally screaming at me to ask you if I could just ride the train into Seattle with you."

Jessica let out a sharp, devastated gasp. Her hand tightened violently against my chest.

"Oh my god, Michael," Jessica wept, the rigid, pragmatic armor of the Senior TPM completely shattering. "I wanted you to. I was fully prepared to buy you a ticket right there on the platform. I was going to take you straight to the Quantum campus with me to get you away from her, and then I was going to figure out how to untangle you from the mess. The absolute second those pneumatic doors hissed shut and the train pulled away without you, I instantly regretted it."

Jessica buried her face against my neck, her own tears mixing with mine.

"I pulled out my Quantum Zephyr media player on the train to try and drown out the guilt," Jessica explained, her voice thick and broken. "And a song by Neon Horizon came on shuffle. It was called 'Before the Distance.'"

I knew the track. It was a heavy, dramatic 1980s pop-rock anthem that we had loaded onto our hard drives years ago.

"The lyrics," Jessica sobbed, reciting the exact, soaring words that had broken her firewall.

*I saw the shadow falling across your face*

*A quiet surrender in a crowded place*

*You wore a mask, but the edges were frayed*

*Lost in the rules of a game that we played*

*I should have reached out, I should have stood tall*

*But I let the silence echo down the hall*

*When the steel doors close and the platform clears*

*You can hear the truth grinding in the gears*

*Heed the warning hiding in his eyes*

*Before the distance leaves your soul behind*

*Trust the silence when he calls your name*

*'Cause once it's gone, nothing stays the same*

"It completely broke me," Jessica wept, clutching my shirt. "The song literally said, 'When the steel doors close and the platform clears.' I cried. Michael, I sat in that accessible bay and I cried all the way to Seattle King Street Station. I cried on the platform. And I cried all the way across the SR-520 bridge on the 545 express bus to work."

My processor stalled. Jessica *never* cried. The fact that the logistics of leaving me behind had caused her to weep openly on public transit for over an hour proved the absolute, terrifying depth of my danger.

"That's why I had Andromeda call you later that day," Jessica sniffled, trying to pull herself together. "I tried so hard to bring you to Quantum to talk you into leaving her. But you were still so blinded by the trauma bonding that you couldn't see the exit."

"I know," I whimpered, the darkest, heaviest part of the data finally rising to the surface. "And because I didn't listen to you... I got locked in."

Rachel went completely still against my right side. She sensed the massive, catastrophic shift in my gravity.

"That night," I whispered, the air completely leaving my lungs. "Behind closed doors. It was the ultimate violation."

It took every ounce of strength I had to force the words past the heavy, iron vault door in my throat. But I felt the deep, grounding pressure of my sisters holding me, and I knew I was safe.

"Delores didn't just keep me awake," I choked out, a raw, jagged sob ripping through the living room. "Her adult cousin, Brandi, was in the room with us. They cornered me. They wouldn't let me sleep. I was so exhausted I was hallucinating, and I couldn't fight back. They coerced me. It was an assault. They both... they both used me."

Rachel let out a horrified, muffled gasp into my shoulder. Jessica’s breathing stopped entirely.

"It wasn't a bad breakup," I wept, spelling out the exact, horrifying legal reality. "It was RCW 9A.44.050. Rape in the second degree, committed by a person who knew the victim was physically helpless or mentally incapacitated. They both did it. And then, a few hours later, Aunt Lynne threw me out into the morning rain and left me for dead."

The living room fell into an absolute, deafening silence, broken only by my heavy, frantic sobbing.

I was sitting on a comfortable sofa in my little sister's apartment, thousands of miles away from the squalor of Kent, crying my eyes out as I surrendered the absolute worst moments of my two years of misery.

Both of my sisters reacted instantly. They didn't ask questions, and they didn't demand details. They simply tightened their grips, locking their arms around me with a desperate, crushing, immovable force. They poured every single ounce of their physical strength into the hug, needing me to know, on a cellular level, that I was unconditionally safe.

"I'm so sorry, Bear," Rachel cried, her tears soaking my shirt. "I'm so sorry I wasn't there."

"They are monsters," Jessica snarled softly against my neck, her voice a lethal, vibrating promise of protection. "You survived them, Michael. You are here. You are with us, and you are with Kristin. You trust her, and Rachel trusts her, which means she is safe. We will never let anyone hurt you again."

I nodded weakly against their shoulders. "If we ever reconnect with Ashley... she deserves to know what happened to my baseline, too."

"She will," Jessica promised. "When the time comes, she will."

It took a very long time for my ragged breathing to slow down. But for the entire hour, Jessica and Rachel did not stop hugging me. They didn't loosen their grip for a single second. They understood the exact mathematical reality of my trauma: I was broken, and I needed my pack to physically hold the pieces together.

And as the afternoon sun faded behind the gray Washington clouds outside the apartment window, surrounded by the fierce, absolute safety of my sisters, I finally believed that I was going to be okay.
