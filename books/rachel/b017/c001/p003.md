The Route 7 dropped us off at the Franklin's Hypermarket transit shelter. We walked across the wet asphalt to The Riverway Diner. The air inside smelled of strong coffee, frying bacon, and maple syrup.

"Party of six, please," Jessica told the hostess. "We need a booth that can accommodate a wheelchair at the head."

The hostess led us to a large, spacious booth in the back corner of the restaurant. The table was designed to seat three people on each side. The second we reached the table, Jessica’s pragmatic, architectural brain took over. She didn't just tell us to sit down; she orchestrated the seating chart like a general arranging a battlefield.

She knew Dad would try to dictate the narrative, and she knew he hated the fact that Rachel relied on me for sensory regulation. He constantly tried to separate us, insisting that his teenage children shouldn't be so physically attached.

Jessica wasn't going to give him an inch of that leverage.

"Rachel," Jessica instructed, pointing to the booth bench against the far wall. "Inside seat. All the way against the window."

Rachel slid into the booth, her back pressing securely against the glass. It was the safest, most enclosed position in the restaurant, shielding her from the chaotic noise and movement of the diner.

"Michael," Jessica commanded. "Dead center. Right next to her."

I slid in, pressing my shoulder and hip firmly against Rachel's. I was the heavy, physical barrier between her and the rest of the table. Rachel immediately leaned her weight against my ribs, letting out a soft, grounded exhale. We were locked in.

"Kristin, take the aisle seat," Jessica finished.

Kristin slid in next to me, her posture tall and defensive. Our side of the table was a completely unified, impenetrable shield wall.

Jessica then wheeled her manual chair directly to the head of the table, locking her brakes with a sharp, mechanical click.

From her position at the head, she commanded the entire space. She could make direct eye contact with everyone sitting on our side, and she had a perfect, unobstructed view of the empty side of the booth—the two seats reserved for Tom and Cathy.

The psychological warfare of the seating chart was brilliant. When Dad arrived, he would be forced to sit opposite us. He would have to look at the undeniable, physical proof that his children were a fiercely united pack that he couldn't break, all while answering to his oldest daughter sitting at the head of the table paying the bill.

I rested my hands on the laminated tabletop, feeling the steady pressure of Rachel leaning against my side. The trap was set, the perimeter was secured, and all we had to do now was wait for the bell above the diner door to ring.
