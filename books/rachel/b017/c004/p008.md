Twenty minutes later, we were back out on the freezing street, walking the two short, flat blocks from the deli to the surface transit stop at the 2nd Avenue Extension.

The gray sky was threatening rain again as our regional express rounded the corner. The TAPS 594 was not a standard city bus. It was a massive, high-backed commuter coach designed exclusively for long-haul, high-speed highway travel.

As the colossal, boxy vehicle hissed to a halt at the curb, Jessica let out a sharp, deeply aggravated sigh, her hands dropping from her push-rims in sheer annoyance.

"I absolutely despise coaches," Jessica muttered, glaring at the side of the bus.

"Why?" I asked, pulling my collar up against the wind. "It looks comfortable."

"It's an architectural nightmare for accessibility," Jessica explained, her corporate logistics brain deeply offended by the design. "The local buses have low floors. They just drop a ramp, and I roll right on in three seconds. But these regional coaches are built high off the ground to store luggage underneath. Which means to get me on board, the operator has to stop everything, get out of the bus, and manually operate a mid-cabin hydraulic elevator."

She wasn't exaggerating. The operator secured the steering wheel, stepped off the bus, and walked halfway down the exterior. He had to unlock a specialized set of bay doors, unfold a heavy mechanical platform, and lower it completely to the sidewalk.

It took five agonizing minutes of standing in the freezing wind just to get Jessica lifted into the cabin. And once she was inside, the spatial geometry was equally inefficient. The operator had to physically fold up and lock three entire rows of heavy passenger seats just to clear enough floor space to create a single securement bay for her wheelchair.

"See?" Jessica grumbled as Kristin followed her through the mid-cabin doors to help secure the tie-downs. "It's an archaic, infuriating bottleneck."

Rachel and I boarded through the front doors, navigating the steep, narrow rubber stairs. The interior felt entirely different from a city bus. There were no wide, sideways-facing priority benches or open standing room. The seats were arranged in tight, forward-facing pairs, featuring thick, plush upholstery, overhead luggage racks, and individual reading lights.

It forced a slight adjustment to our perimeter.

Jessica was locked into the mid-cabin bay. Rachel immediately moved past the front rows, claiming the window seat in the row directly behind Jessica, pressing her forehead against the cool glass.

I stopped in the narrow aisle, analyzing the remaining seats to calculate the best protective configuration. Before my processor could finalize the math, Kristin slid smoothly into the aisle seat right next to Rachel.

"I've got the flank," Kristin said, her voice dropping into her low, protective Sentinel register. She looked up at me, offering a warm, reassuring smile. "Take the row across from us, Michael. You're right next to her."

I slid into the aisle seat directly across from them. I was exactly two feet away from my little sister. It wasn't the heavy, skin-to-skin compression she usually preferred in transit, but the layout worked flawlessly. Kristin was physically boxing Rachel in, shielding her from any passengers moving down the aisle, while I maintained a direct, unbroken line of sight to both of them.

Rachel reached her hand across the narrow aisle. I reached out, lacing my fingers firmly through hers. The physical tether bridged the gap.

The heavy coach doors hissed shut, sealing out the noise of the city. The massive diesel engine roared to life, vibrating powerfully through the floorboards as the bus merged onto the southbound lanes of Interstate 5.

I leaned my head back against the plush, high-backed seat, watching the towering skyscrapers of the Seattle skyline fade into the gray, overcast distance. My thumb gently traced the back of Rachel's knuckles as she hummed a soft, happy note, staring out her own window.

We were leaving King County and crossing the border into Pierce County. But this time, I wasn't being dragged south by Delores to be locked in a squalid, freezing room. I was riding a heavy, climate-controlled coach, holding my sister's hand, surrounded entirely by the women who had saved my life.

An hour later, the massive coach descended from the highway, pulling into the bustling Downtown Tacoma transit corridor at 10th and Commerce. We deployed off the 594, enduring the agonizing, five-minute reverse process of the mid-cabin hydraulic lift to get Jessica back onto the pavement.

We waited at the transfer hub for only a few minutes before our connection arrived.

The teal and yellow TAPS Route 2 pulled into the bay.

"Oh, thank god," Jessica sighed in pure relief as the heavy air brakes hissed.

The local bus was a low-floor model. The operator pressed a button, the front suspension knelt with a mechanical whine, and a motorized ramp flipped out directly onto the curb. The entire boarding process took less than ten seconds.

We transitioned from the heavy highway coach back to the standard transit seating. I immediately reclaimed my anchor position next to Rachel, pressing my shoulder flush against hers as the bus carried us out of the dense downtown grid and into the quieter, tree-lined streets of University Place.

"Getting close," Kristin murmured from the seat in front of us, checking her watch.

Rachel practically vibrated against my side. Her leg began a rapid, excited bounce. She had spent the last two years completely cut off from me, living in a separate county while I was trapped in Kent. The opportunity to physically bring her big brother into her own, personalized sanctuary was a massive milestone.

"Stop requested," Rachel announced, pulling the yellow cord as the bus turned down Bridgeport Way West.

The bus knelt to the curb at Chambers Lane West.

"Perfect execution, Bug," I said, stepping off the bus and helping Jessica navigate her wheelchair down the ramp.

Rachel's routing was absolutely flawless; we didn't even have to cross the busy street. Directly in front of us, nestled among towering, old-growth evergreens, was the sprawling complex of Chambers Creek Estates.

We walked up the paved entry path, the three-story apartment buildings looming quietly in the damp Pacific Northwest air. Rachel took the lead, practically skipping ahead with excitement, while Kristin walked at my side, her hand warmly laced through mine.

Rachel led us directly to a building near the center of the complex. She didn't head for a stairwell. She walked straight up to a ground-floor door, pulling her keys from her heavy coat pocket.

My architectural processor immediately logged the absolute, undeniable brilliance of the layout. By securing a first-floor unit with a completely flush threshold, Rachel and Kristin had ensured their apartment was instantly, permanently wheelchair accessible. Jessica could visit them anytime without fighting a single staircase or relying on a faulty elevator. It was a flawless, pre-calculated perimeter.

Rachel turned the deadbolt, pushed the door open, and stepped aside, gesturing wildly for me to enter.

"Welcome to our fortress, Bear," Rachel rasped, her dark eyes shining with absolute, unfiltered pride.

I stepped over the threshold, leaving the freezing dampness of University Place behind, and walked into my little sister's sanctuary.
