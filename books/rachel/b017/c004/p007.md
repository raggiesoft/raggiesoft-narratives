The freezing mist from the man-made waterfall was a brilliant sensory reset, but after forty-five minutes of sitting still in the November air, the biting Pacific Northwest chill finally began to penetrate our heavy winter coats.

Jessica checked her watch, unlocking the brakes on her wheelchair.

"Alright, pack. Our core temperatures are dropping, and we are burning calories trying to stay warm," Jessica announced, her voice slightly raised to carry over the roaring water. "It is time for lunch. And since we are already in Pioneer Square, we are keeping it simple, flat, and indoors."

We stood up from the metal patio chairs. Rachel immediately slotted herself against my left side, her hand finding its way back into my coat pocket to grip the fabric of my sweater. Kristin stepped up to my right, lacing her fingers through mine.

We walked back out through the wrought-iron gates, leaving the acoustic fortress behind, and merged back onto the wide, brick-lined sidewalks of Second Avenue South.

"There's a deli right on the corner of First and Yesler," Jessica instructed, navigating smoothly over the pavement. "No stairs, wide aisles, and they serve hot soup."

The word *soup* was a magic trigger for Rachel. Her dark eyes lit up, and she gently bumped her shoulder against my bicep. Hot, predictable, heavily textured food was her ultimate comfort when navigating the outside world.

We reached the deli a few minutes later. It was a classic Pioneer Square establishment—exposed brick walls, low lighting, and the rich, savory smell of roasting meats and melted cheese. Because it was Black Friday, the downtown office crowds were nonexistent, leaving the restaurant wonderfully quiet and empty.

We claimed a large, wooden booth near the back. Jessica parked at the head of the table, commanding the geometry, while Rachel pushed me into the center of the booth bench and Kristin boxed me in on the aisle side.

I didn't reach for my pocket. The trauma-wired panic over the check had been completely disabled in the food court. Kristin and Jessica handled the ordering at the counter, returning with two plastic trays loaded with hot sandwiches, massive bowls of creamy tomato soup, and cold sodas.

I dipped my sandwich into the hot soup, the rich, savory warmth instantly radiating down to my chest.

"So," Jessica said, wiping her mouth with a napkin and looking across the table at me. "You have officially survived the Black Friday retail spectacle. You've seen my apartment in Renton, and you've toured the Quantum campus in Overlake. But your map of our perimeter is still missing a massive, critical sector."

I paused, my analytical brain pulling up the regional geography. "Pierce County."

"Exactly," Kristin smiled warmly, taking a sip of her soda. "You haven't seen our apartment yet. We want to show you where we live in University Place."

Rachel practically vibrated against my side. Her leg began a rapid, excited bounce under the table. She had spent the last two years completely cut off from me, living in a separate county while I was trapped in Kent. The opportunity to physically bring her big brother into her own, personalized sanctuary was a massive milestone for her.

"Can we go today?" Rachel rasped eagerly, looking at Jessica. "Right now?"

"That is the plan," Jessica confirmed. "But since you are the resident transit authority, I need you to execute the routing. How do we get from Pioneer Square to University Place?"

Rachel didn't even need to blink. The TAPS grid was permanently burned into her hard drive.

"The Downtown Seattle Transit Tunnel is still completely closed for the light rail conversion," Rachel recited smoothly, her hands tracing the invisible route on the tabletop to accommodate the surface-level detours. "So we have to catch the express on the surface streets. We walk two blocks over to the 2nd Avenue Extension and catch the TAPS Route 594. It’s a heavy regional commuter coach. It gets on Interstate 5 and bypasses almost everything."

"Where does the 594 drop us off?" I asked, following her logic.

"Downtown Tacoma. Tenth and Commerce," Rachel answered rapidly. "From there, we transfer to the TAPS Route 2. It drops us off exactly at Bridgeport Way West and Chambers Lane West in University Place. We don't even have to cross the street. Chambers Creek Estates is right there on our side of the road."

"Flawless," Jessica praised, checking her watch. "The 594 runs every thirty minutes today. Finish your soup, pack. We are heading south."
