I woke up in the dark, my internal clock violently pulling me out of sleep just before five-thirty in the morning.

For the last two years, this exact time on the Friday after Thanksgiving meant being jolted awake by screaming. It meant being dragged out into the freezing East Coast weather to stand in chaotic, violent lines outside retail stores so Delores could extract high-value electronics and heavily discounted appliances using my credit.

But as my eyes adjusted to the dim, quiet light of the bedroom in Renton, the phantom anxiety evaporated. There was no screaming. There was just the soft, steady breathing of the woman sleeping beside me.

Kristin was lying on her stomach, the heavy down comforter pushed down to her waist, her bare back rising and falling in a deep, peaceful rhythm.

I lay there for a long moment, my processor running a complete, awe-striking diagnostic of the night before. I had crossed the ultimate threshold. It was the first time I had engaged in sexual intimacy since the sustained, terrifying assaults by Delores and Brandi. They had used my body as a weapon against me, turning physical connection into a nightmare of coercion and survival.

But last night with Kristin, every single broken wire had been reconnected. It had been passionate, respectful, and built on an absolute foundation of safety. She had asked for my consent at every step. She had let me set the pace. It felt incredibly, profoundly *right*. I wasn't permanently destroyed. The trauma conditioning had been completely incinerated.

I reached out, gently brushing a strand of blonde hair away from Kristin's shoulder. She stirred, letting out a soft, sleepy sigh, and turned her head to look at me. Her blue eyes were heavy with sleep, but as she focused on my face, a beautiful, brilliant smile broke across her lips.

"Morning," Kristin whispered, her voice husky.

"Morning," I whispered back, leaning over to kiss her gently.

The immediate rush of warmth was intoxicating, but my internal clock was still ticking, and a sudden wave of logistical anxiety hit my processor.

"Rachel," I said, pulling back slightly. "She’s been out there all night without me. I need to show her I'm okay."

Kristin understood instantly. She didn't take it as a rejection; she knew exactly how intense the separation anxiety was for my little sister. "Let's go," she agreed.

We got dressed quickly, pulling on jeans and heavy winter sweaters. Because my brain was still wired for the retail frenzy of the day, I automatically assumed we were going to be heading out to the stores. I laced up my boots, took a deep breath, and opened the bedroom door.

The living room was quiet, illuminated only by the streetlamps outside and a single lamp Jessica had turned on near the kitchen. Jessica was sitting in her wheelchair at the island, sipping a mug of coffee. Rachel was sitting cross-legged on the sofa, a heavy blanket wrapped around her shoulders.

The second the bedroom door clicked open, Rachel’s head snapped up.

She didn't wait for me to say a word. She threw the blanket off, crossed the living room in three rapid strides, and slammed into me. I caught her, wrapping my arms tightly around her shoulders as she buried her face in my chest. She was trembling slightly, the adrenaline of a full night of separation finally bleeding out of her system.

"I'm here, Bug," I murmured into her hair, rocking her gently. "I'm right here. Everything is perfect."

Rachel pulled back just enough to look at my face. She scanned my eyes, my posture, and the relaxed line of my shoulders. Whatever she was looking for, she found it instantly. The panic in her dark eyes vanished, replaced by a profound, overwhelming wave of relief. She looked past me to Kristin, offering her best friend a deep, tearful smile of absolute gratitude.

From the kitchen island, Jessica lowered her coffee mug. Her sharp, analytical eyes swept over me and Kristin. She took in the relaxed, undeniable glow radiating from both of us—the absolute lack of tension, the physical proximity we were still maintaining, and the quiet, fierce pride in Kristin's posture.

Jessica’s lips curved into a deeply satisfied, older-sister smirk.

"Well," Jessica noted dryly, raising her mug in our direction. "It looks like the independent perimeter was highly successful."

"One hundred percent secure," I confirmed, a massive, unstoppable smile breaking across my face.

Rachel squeezed my arm, leaning her head against my shoulder, completely validating the massive step in my recovery.

"So," Jessica said, shifting the logistical gears. "It's five-forty in the morning on Black Friday. The absolute maniacs who camped out in tent cities at *Broil's Electronics* and *MegaMart* for the 4:00 AM doorbusters have already stampeded the doors. Which means my question for the logistical anchor of this pack is: what are we doing today?"

I looked at the three of them. I didn't need to buy anything. Jessica had already secured my laptop, and I had my clothing. But hiding in the apartment felt like a concession to the ghost of the East Coast. Delores had made the holiday shopping season a living hell. I wanted to reclaim it.

"I want to go out," I said firmly. "I don't want to fight for a cheap television or a video game console. But I want to see the crowds. I want to walk through *Wellington's Department Store* or *The Spot* and just... exist. I want to see the holiday spectacle as a free person."

Rachel nodded immediately, her grip on my arm tightening in absolute solidarity. She was completely on board with whatever I decided.

"A tactical observation run," Jessica agreed, her smirk widening into a full, brilliant grin. "I love it. Let the early-morning brawls over cheap electronics finish up. We'll make some leftover turkey sandwiches for breakfast, let the sun come up, and then the West Coast Pack is deploying to the mall."
