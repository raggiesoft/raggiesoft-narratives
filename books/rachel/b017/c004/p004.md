We pushed out through the heavy, revolving glass doors of the Pine Street Center, stepping back out into the biting Seattle morning. The wind whipping off Elliott Bay cut right through my heavy winter coat, carrying that distinct, damp chill that seemed to seep straight into my bones. Having spent my entire life in the humid coastal air of Virginia Beach, my body was entirely unprepared for the sheer, aggressive grayness of a Pacific Northwest winter.

Directly across 4th Avenue loomed the massive, block-long stone fortress of the department store. High above the main entrance, suspended from the historic brickwork, was a brilliantly lit, four-story holiday star. But right above the heavy brass doors, bolted into the stone, was a shiny new corporate sign that read: *Hudson's*.

As we waited for the crosswalk signal to change, an older man in a heavy wool peacoat stopped next to us. He looked up at the *Hudson's* sign, took a sip of his coffee, and shook his head in absolute disgust.

"Corporate vultures," the man muttered to no one in particular. "Put whatever sign you want on the door. It's always going to be *The Grand Marché*."

Jessica immediately turned her wheelchair toward the man, giving him a firm, deeply respectful nod of solidarity. "Exactly, sir. Exactly."

"When did they change it?" I asked, pulling my collar tighter against the wind.

"Just last year," Jessica sighed, shaking her head. "It's a total tragedy. *The Grand Marché* was an institution out here. But, corporate acquisitions being what they are, they slapped the *Hudson's* name on it to standardize their national brand. It’s infuriating, but at least they kept the holiday star and the window displays."

The crosswalk chirped, and the four of us moved forward with the massive wave of pedestrians.

We didn't go inside. Jessica navigated her chair along the exterior perimeter of the massive stone building, steering us toward the legendary, street-level holiday window displays.

This was exactly what my brain needed. The food court had been a chaotic, deafening sensory assault. But standing on the freezing sidewalk, looking through the thick pane glass at the animatronic holiday scenes, the environment was entirely passive. I didn't have to navigate a crowd or calculate a transaction. I just had to stand there and watch the magic.

The first window featured a massive, intricate winter village. Tiny, animatronic ice skaters spun in circles on a frozen pond, while a miniature train chugged endlessly around a snow-covered mountain.

Rachel stepped up right next to me, her shoulder pressing flush against my bicep. She didn't say a word. She just stared at the mechanical train, completely mesmerized by the predictable, repetitive loop of the cars passing through the tunnel.

"This is nice," Rachel rasped softly, her breath pluming in the cold air.

"It is," I agreed, wrapping my arm securely around her shoulders.

I looked down to my right. Kristin had stepped up right beside me, slipping her hand naturally into mine. She didn't pull me closer or demand my attention; she just stood there, her fingers laced comfortably with mine, watching the animatronic skaters. The heavy, unshakeable tension that had lived in my chest for the last two years was completely gone, replaced by the profound, quiet realization that I was allowed to simply exist in this moment without waiting for a trap to spring.

"You surviving the cold?" Kristin asked softly, giving my hand a gentle squeeze.

"Barely," I laughed, my teeth chattering slightly. "The wind off that bay is brutal."

"You'll get used to it," Jessica promised, rolling up next to us. She looked at the four of us huddled together against the stone facade of the department store, a fierce, satisfied smile touching the corners of her mouth. "Alright. We've seen the windows. We've survived the cold. Where to next, Michael? You're calling the shots today."
