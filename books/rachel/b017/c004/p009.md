I stepped over the threshold, leaving the freezing dampness of University Place behind, and walked into my little sister's sanctuary.

The apartment was small, but it radiated an intense, perfectly curated warmth. The front door opened directly into a cozy, carpeted living room. A heavy, dark-blue sofa sat facing a modest CRT television, and a thick fleece blanket was neatly folded over the armrest. To the right, a narrow, galley-style kitchen was separated from the living room by a small breakfast bar with two wooden stools.

It wasn't the sleek, highly engineered corporate environment of Jessica's Renton apartment. It was a lived-in, distinctly twenty-something space, built entirely on the combined paychecks of a retail worker and a grocery store cashier.

And to me, it was the most beautiful piece of real estate on the planet.

Jessica rolled in right behind me. Because the apartment hadn't been retrofitted for ADA compliance, the architectural limitations were immediately obvious.

"Alright," Jessica announced, wheeling herself over to the edge of the breakfast bar and locking her brakes. "This is as far as the Quantum command center goes. I am officially establishing base camp in the living room. If I try to navigate that galley kitchen, I'm going to take the oven door off its hinges."

Rachel laughed, shedding her heavy peacoat and tossing it onto one of the barstools.

She turned to me, her dark eyes wide and vibrating with pure, unfiltered excitement. For two years, she had been forced to leave me behind in Kent. Now, the dynamic had completely shifted. She was the host. She was the one in control of the perimeter.

"Come here, Bear," Rachel rasped, grabbing my hand and pulling me away from the entryway. "I want to show you."

She dragged me down the narrow, carpeted hallway. We passed a small, standard-issue bathroom on the left, stopping at the two closed doors at the very back of the unit.

"Kristin's room is on the right," Rachel explained, pointing. "Mine is on the left."

She pushed her bedroom door open and pulled me inside.

The room was a masterclass in sensory regulation. The overhead light was turned off, replaced by the soft, warm glow of two small desk lamps. The window blinds were drawn tight, completely insulating the room from the gray Pacific Northwest afternoon outside. A heavy, weighted blanket was perfectly smoothed over her twin-sized bed.

But the centerpiece of the room sat on a small, sturdy desk against the far wall.

It was a heavy, industrial-grade laptop running Quantum OS XN Pro. The screen was dark, but a specialized accessibility switch was wired directly into the side of the chassis. Next to the computer sat a stack of neatly organized spiral notebooks and a heavy, battered Case Logic CD binder.

I stood in the center of the small room, my processor quietly absorbing the absolute safety of the space.

There was no clutter. There was no chaos. This was where Rachel retreated when the volume of the world became too loud. This was where she booted up Quantum Suite to type the words her vocal cords refused to say. It was a flawless, impenetrable bunker designed exactly for her specific neurotype.

"It's perfect, Bug," I whispered, the emotional weight of seeing her so safe and established hitting my chest like a heavy, warm wave. "You built a perfect perimeter."

Rachel beamed. She stepped into my space, wrapping her arms tightly around my waist, burying her face into my chest for a deep, grounding squeeze. I wrapped my arms around her shoulders, resting my chin on the top of her head.

"It's our perimeter now," Rachel whispered fiercely against my sweater. "No more Kent. No more Virgina. Just us."

"Just us," I agreed.

We held the pack-hug for a long moment before walking back out into the narrow hallway. As we stepped back into the living room, I found Kristin standing near the galley kitchen. She had already opened the fridge and was pouring two glasses of water.

She looked up, handing one of the glasses to Jessica at the breakfast bar, before turning her blue eyes to me.

"So, Michael," Kristin smiled warmly, leaning against the counter. "What do you think of our little corner of Pierce County?"

I looked at the narrow kitchen, the small television, and the woman leaning against the counter who had systematically dismantled my trauma over the last forty-eight hours.

"I think," I said, walking over to accept the second glass of water from her hand, "that it's the safest building in the entire state."
