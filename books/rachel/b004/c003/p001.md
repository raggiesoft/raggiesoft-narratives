The transition from deep, exhausted sleep back to consciousness was a slow, agonizingly stiff process.

The morning sunlight was just beginning to filter through the heavy curtains of the living room windows. I lay perfectly still in the dead center of the pull-out sofa bed, my processor slowly coming back online. Every single muscle in my body ached. My calves were throbbing from the cerebral palsy flare-up the night before, and my shoulders were stiff from hauling the massive concert bass drum around the amphitheater.

It was the ultimate coaster hangover.

Despite the physical exhaustion, the absolute safety of the geometry kept my heart rate perfectly level. Rachel was still fast asleep on my right side, her arm draped heavily across my chest. Ashley was curled up on my left, her head resting comfortably near my shoulder.

From the floor beside the sofa bed, the distinct rustle of heavy quilts broke the quiet.

Jessica sat up on her air mattress, rubbing her eyes and yawning quietly so she wouldn't wake our parents upstairs. She looked over at the sofa bed, her analytical eyes taking in the messy pile of pillows and the three of us tangled in the blankets.

"You guys literally didn't move an inch all night," Jessica whispered, a tired, fond smile on her face.

"Too sore to move," Ashley mumbled, her voice thick with sleep. She didn't open her eyes, but she tightened her arm around my waist, burying her face into my shoulder to hide from the morning light. "I think I left my legs in Germany."

The low hum of our voices was enough to break the final seal on Rachel's sleep. My little sister shifted, pulling her arm back and slowly sitting up. She rubbed her dark eyes, looking around the living room as if her brain was downloading the events of the previous day to establish her current reality.

She looked at me, then at Ashley, and then over at Jessica.

Suddenly, Rachel's processor encountered a massive, unresolved logical inconsistency. Her brow furrowed. She looked back down at me, her expression entirely serious, analyzing the missing data point with pure, unfiltered autistic logic.

"So," Rachel asked bluntly, her voice cutting through the quiet, groggy morning air. "When is Bonnie going to spend the night on the sofa bed with us?"

My internal processor immediately threw a catastrophic error code.

I froze, the lingering sleep vanishing instantly. My chest tightened in absolute panic. To Rachel, the math was incredibly simple. She was operating on a strictly literal, established algorithm: people who are in the pack spend the night in Base Camp. Ashley was in the pack, and Ashley slept on the sofa bed. Bonnie was now in the pack; therefore, Bonnie should sleep on the sofa bed. It was a flawless line of code in her head.

But my processor, working desperately to translate our trauma-bonded survival mechanics into the neurotypical rules of the outside world, knew that the social geometry was completely impossible.

Eighth-grade boys and eighth-grade girls did not have sleepovers. Bonnie’s parents would absolutely never allow it, and Mom and Dad would have a heart attack at the mere suggestion. The only reason Ashley was allowed to sleep over—the only reason she and I were trusted to be completely alone together in the dark—was because our families had twelve years of established history. Ashley wasn't a girlfriend; she was a legally recognized fixture of the house. She was practically blood.

Before I could even attempt to find the vocabulary to explain all of those complex, unwritten social rules to my sixth-grade sister without making her feel stupid for asking, the sentinel stepped in.

Ashley didn't laugh. She opened her eyes, propped herself up on her elbow, and gently adjusted the parameters of Rachel's algorithm.

"Different classification, Rachel," Ashley explained, her voice carrying that patient, protective tone she reserved specifically for our internal pack mechanics. "Bonnie is in the outer perimeter. The sofa bed is the inner core. Base Camp is strictly for family only."

Rachel blinked, processing the correction. She pointed a finger at Ashley. "But you sleep on the sofa bed."

"Because I'm his sister," Ashley stated with absolute, immutable certainty. "I've been here since you guys were in diapers. Bonnie is Michael's girlfriend. That means they hold hands at school, they sit together on the bus, and she'll hang out here for homework. But when the streetlights come on, she goes back to her house. The sofa bed is just for us."

"Ashley is right," Jessica chimed in from the air mattress, smoothly reinforcing the data to help Rachel's logic settle. "Girlfriends and boyfriends don't sleep over. It's an outside-world rule. Our living room is just for the blood pack and Ashley."

Rachel thought about it for a few quiet seconds. She looked at the boundaries of the mattress, then looked at the three of us. The logic finally balanced. The rules were clearly defined, and the new variable had a designated, permanent operational parameter that didn't threaten the sanctuary.

"Okay," Rachel decided, entirely satisfied with the data. She let out a small, contented sigh and flopped back down onto the pillows, resting her head against my arm. "I'm going back to sleep."

I let out a long, silent breath, my tense shoulders finally dropping back into the mattress.

"I've got you," Ashley whispered so quietly only I could hear it. She gave my ribs one last, reassuring squeeze before closing her eyes again.

I stared up at the ceiling, overwhelmingly grateful for my sisters. I didn't have to navigate the exhausting, confusing math of teenage dating alone. The Base Camp would forever remain a sanctuary for blood and Ashley alone, and the perimeter holding it together had never felt stronger.
