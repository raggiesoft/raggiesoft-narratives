The heavy, grounding pressure of Bonnie’s hand on my knee was exactly the physical anchor my processor needed to reboot.

I kept my eyes closed for another long minute, focusing entirely on the steady warmth of her palm. Slowly, the terrifying phantom echoes of the Dojang retreated back into the locked vault of 1995. The suffocating stillness of the booth dissolved, and the ambient noise of Josephine’s ice cream parlor finally filtered back into my ears.

I took a deep, shaky breath and opened my eyes.

Bonnie was still watching me, her expression a perfect mix of empathy and fierce, unwavering support. She didn't look scared, and she certainly didn't look like she was plotting an exit strategy to get away from the traumatized kid in the percussion section.

I managed a small, exhausted nod. I'm okay. Bonnie gave my knee one last, reassuring squeeze before sliding her hand back up to the table. Ashley let out a quiet sigh of relief, picking her spoon back up as if the massive, reality-altering trauma dump had just been a casual conversation about the weather.

"Eat," Ashley instructed gently, pointing her spoon at my melting waffle bowl. "We still have half a park to conquer."

I picked up my spoon. The ice cream tasted infinitely better now that the ghost was gone.

Fifteen minutes later, we tossed our empty bowls into the trash and stepped back out into the sweltering heat of the French hamlet. We needed another cooldown, which meant it was time to cross the bridge into the 'New France' section of the park.

Our target was The Timber Mill Plunge, the park's massive, sprawling log flume ride.

As we navigated the crowded, rustic pathways of New France, we spotted a highly satisfying scene unfolding near a turkey leg stand. Sloane was standing against a wooden fence, his chest puffed out in a desperate, defensive posture. But this time, he wasn't trying to impress older girls. He was currently surrounded by three serious-looking Brewster Gardens security guards in crisp white polo shirts.

"Well, well, well," Ashley smirked, not even slowing her pace as we walked past. "Looks like someone finally mouthed off to the wrong person."

"Just like we predicted," Bonnie agreed, shaking her head. "He’s definitely getting kicked out of the park."

None of us felt a single ounce of sympathy. Our perimeter was secure, and we joined the zigzagging wooden queue for the log flume.

Because the ride was a massive water attraction on a sweltering day, the line moved steadily. Soon, we reached the front of the queue, standing at the top of the wooden stairs that led down to the wet, revolving boarding platform.

"Okay, the logs hold four people," Ashley announced, instantly stepping back into her role as the tactical wingman. "Each row holds two. I am taking the back row entirely to myself."

"Are you sure?" Bonnie asked, looking back. "We can squeeze three—"

"Absolutely not," Ashley cut her off, her face lighting up with that same, buzzing, encrypted smirk she had been wearing for twenty-four hours. She was carrying a massive secret, and actively orchestrating this specific seating arrangement was clearly a major part of it. "I want the back row. You two take the front."

We stepped off the moving platform and climbed awkwardly into the floating fiberglass log.

The seating geometry of The Timber Mill Plunge was entirely unique. There were no individual bucket seats or lap bars. You simply straddled a long, padded bench. Because the space was so narrow, the person sitting in the front had to lean back directly into the chest and between the legs of the person sitting behind them.

I slid into the back of the front row, and Bonnie climbed in directly in front of me. Ashley hopped into the back row by herself, perfectly content.

"Clear!" the attendant shouted.

The log bumped against the fiberglass walls, drifting out of the station and immediately engaging with the first lift hill. The mechanics of the ride took over. The log lurched upward, the heavy, metallic clunk-clunk-clunk of the bumpy conveyor belt rattling our teeth.

Because the incline of the lift hill was so steep, gravity immediately forced Bonnie to slide backward, her back pressing flush against my chest.

For the first time all day, my processor didn't panic at the new physical data. The fear of being touched had been completely rewired at the ice cream parlor. She had heard the absolute worst, darkest part of my history, and she hadn't run away. She had anchored me instead.

As the log rattled violently up the conveyor belt, threatening to bounce us around the narrow fiberglass hull, I didn't hesitate. I reached both of my arms forward and wrapped them securely around Bonnie's waist, pulling her into a protective back-hug to stabilize her against the bumpy incline.

Bonnie gasped slightly at the sudden contact, but then she immediately relaxed. She let out a happy, breathless laugh, leaning her full weight back into my chest and resting her hands lightly over my arms.

It felt incredibly nice. It felt right.

We drifted through the winding, elevated water troughs of the logging camp, the back-hug remaining firmly intact until we reached the massive, plunging final drop. We plummeted down the waterfall, screaming as a massive wave of freezing water crashed over the front of the log, soaking us both.

When we finally drifted back into the station and climbed out, dripping wet and laughing, Ashley immediately bee-lined for the photo booth at the exit.

"Buying it," Ashley declared, slamming her money on the counter before we even saw the monitors.

When the picture flashed on the screen, I understood exactly why her wingman smirk was so wide. The camera was mounted at the top of the final plunge. In the photo, Ashley was in the back row, throwing a peace sign with a massive, victorious grin. In the front row, completely ignoring the camera, I had my arms wrapped securely around Bonnie’s waist, and Bonnie was leaning back against my chest, her eyes closed and a massive, radiant smile on her face.

It didn't look like a pack cuddle. It looked exactly like a couple.

Ashley collected the glossy 4x6 print, carefully sliding it into the dry plastic pocket of her wallet. "Best eight dollars I've ever spent," she muttered proudly, steering us back out into the heat.
