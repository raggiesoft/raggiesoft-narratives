Stepping out of the heavily air-conditioned fortress of *The Iron Aeronaut* and back into the Virginia sun was like walking headfirst into a physical wall of heat. The humidity was suffocating, hanging in the air like a wet wool blanket.

"Water. Now," Ashley declared, wiping a line of sweat from her forehead.

We dragged ourselves over to a nearby pretzel kiosk and asked the vendor for three paper cups of ice water. We drank them immediately, the freezing water providing a temporary, desperate shock to our overheated systems.

"Alright," I sighed, crushing the wax cup and tossing it into a trash can. "Next stop is Aquitaine. I want to drive the antique cars."

To get from the medieval village of Hastings to the French hamlet of Aquitaine, we had to cross through a densely wooded transitional section of the park called *The Merchant’s Pass*. For normal tourists, it was a scenic, shaded walkway. But for the locals, it had a very different, highly notorious reputation.

"Welcome to Heart Attack Hill," Ashley announced to Bonnie as we stood at the top of the paved path, looking down into the massive, plunging ravine.

Bonnie blinked, staring at the sheer incline. "You're kidding. We have to walk down that?"

"And then straight back up the other side," I confirmed grimly.

For an average eighth-grader, Heart Attack Hill was just an exhausting, sweaty nuisance. But for my body, it was a massive logistical nightmare. The spasticity of my cerebral palsy meant that my muscles didn't communicate smoothly with my brain. Walking down a steep incline was terrifying because gravity actively fought against my tight heel cords; I had to manually force my legs to act as brakes to keep from pitching forward. Walking back up the other side was pure, grinding muscular exhaustion.

I needed my anchors.

Ashley immediately moved to my left side. She didn't ask; she just reached out and locked her hand around mine in our standard, non-negotiable vice grip. Her wrist locked, her fingers clamped tight. It was pure utility—a rigid physical tether to help me maintain my balance against the gravity of the hill.

Bonnie stepped to my right side to provide the counterbalance. But as she reached for my hand, the data completely shifted again.

Bonnie didn't grab my wrist. She didn't lock her fingers down in a rigid vice grip. Instead, she slid her palm softly against mine, her fingers interlacing loosely with my own. As we started the slow, agonizing trek down the steep pavement, her thumb lightly, subconsciously traced the back of my knuckles.

It was a mobility assist, but it was wrapped entirely in an unmapped, electric friction.

My heart rate immediately spiked, pounding heavily in my chest. The humid air felt suddenly thinner. Because my processor still lacked the vocabulary for romantic affection, I desperately tried to categorize the physical response. I assumed it was just the brutal heat and the adrenaline of managing my cerebral palsy on the steep hill. But deep down, my system knew the difference. Ashley’s grip was survival. Bonnie’s grip was entirely something else.

We painstakingly navigated our way down to the bottom of the ravine, the lowest point of *The Merchant's Pass*. We were all sweating profusely, miserable and completely out of breath.

Resting on a wooden bench in the shade at the bottom of the hill, drinking a massive frozen lemonade, was Julia.

"Oh, you have got to be kidding me," Ashley muttered under her breath.

Julia lowered her lemonade cup. She looked exhausted from the heat, but the second she saw us, her sharp, irritatingly observant eyes zeroed right in on our linked hands. She saw the rigid, sisterly lock Ashley had on my left arm, and then she saw the soft, interlaced fingers and the gentle proximity between Bonnie and me on the right.

"Well, well, well," Julia singsonged loudly, her voice echoing over the quiet, wooded path. "Aww, look at the happy couple. Ashley looks like she's dragging him on a leash, but Bonnie's actually *holding his hand*."

Bonnie froze. Her face flushed a brilliant, panicked crimson. She immediately tried to pull her hand away, embarrassed that Julia had so accurately decoded the subtle difference in the touch.

But I didn't let her go. I just gently tightened my fingers, keeping Bonnie's hand securely laced in mine.

Julia wasn't finished. She stood up, a malicious, triumphant gleam in her eye. "And I heard you guys talking in line for *The Aeronaut*. You stayed over at his house last night, Ashley? And you all slept on the exact same pull-out sofa? You, Michael, and his little sister? You guys are in eighth grade. That is so entirely gross."

My chest tightened defensively. To us, the sofa bed was Base Camp. It was the absolute core of our survival and our pack dynamic. To hear Julia weaponize it and twist it into something weird made me see red.

But Ashley didn't even flinch. She stepped forward, physically putting herself between Julia and the rest of our pack.

"I am his sister, Julia," Ashley stated fiercely, her voice cold and completely unwavering. "Just because your family hates each other doesn't mean ours does. We protect our own. Now get out of our way."

Julia rolled her eyes, but the absolute, unshakeable confidence in Ashley's voice broke her momentum. She offered one last sneer before turning and walking away toward Hastings.

"Come on," Ashley said, her tone instantly softening as she turned back to us, taking her rigid grip on my left hand once more. "Let's conquer the rest of this stupid hill."

With Bonnie anchoring my right side and Ashley anchoring my left, we began the grueling, exhausting climb up the back half of Heart Attack Hill.

When we finally crested the top, the trees parted to reveal the beautiful, open architecture of Aquitaine. The blazing sun was still beating down on the pavement, but we had reached our destination: *The Aquitaine Motorway*.

The ride was a massive, winding track featuring scale-model, antique gas-powered cars. It was exactly what my exhausted legs needed. We waited in the short line until the attendant waved us forward. Instead of cramming into one car, we each claimed our own.

I slid into the leather seat of a shiny, cherry-red antique car, resting my tired legs. As the ride operator started the small engines, the smell of gasoline filled the air. Ashley was in the blue car directly in front of me, and Bonnie was in the green car right behind me.

I pressed my foot on the gas pedal, the car lurching forward onto the scenic track. The breeze whipped past my face as we drove, my hands resting on the steering wheel. We were driving our own separate cars, enjoying a rare moment of solo independence, but we were still moving as a perfect, unbroken convoy.
