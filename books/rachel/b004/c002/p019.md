The final three blocks down Morrison Street and onto Delaney Street were a pure, agonizing test of endurance.

My legs were practically dragging across the concrete, the residual muscle spasticity from my cerebral palsy flare-up making every single step feel like wading through wet cement. Ashley was walking on my left, matching my slow, exhausted pace perfectly, while Rachel kept her hand securely locked in my right hand, acting as a quiet, steady tether.

When the familiar, heavy front door of 1852 Delaney Street finally clicked open, the absolute, profound relief that washed over me was staggering.

We dragged ourselves into the front hallway, dropping our wallets and keys onto the side table. The house was blissfully quiet. There was no frantic scratching of paws on the hardwood, and no massive Labradors trying to tackle us at the door.

"Dogs are already upstairs," Mom whispered, stepping out of the kitchen with a warm, welcoming smile. She was wearing her bathrobe, holding a mug of tea. "Your sisters banished them so they wouldn't pop the plastic."

I looked past Mom into the living room, and my chest swelled with an overwhelming wave of gratitude.

Jessica and Rachel had completely transformed the room before Ashley and I had even gotten off the school bus. The heavy floral sofa had already been pulled out into a queen-sized bed. Jessica’s twin air mattress was fully inflated on the floor next to it. They had even taken the time to perfectly arrange our massive mountain of pillows and heavy quilts, setting up the exact, non-negotiable parameters of Base Camp so we wouldn't have to lift a finger.

"Thanks, guys," I murmured, leaning heavily against the archway.

"How was the trip?" Mom asked gently, stepping over to give me and Ashley a quick, careful hug, sensing exactly how physically depleted we both were. "Did you survive the heat?"

"We survived," Ashley smiled tiredly, her shoulders slumping the second she was safely inside the perimeter of the house. "And we won the Outstanding Percussion trophy. Superior rating for the band."

Mom's eyes lit up with pride. "That is amazing! Your dad is going to be so thrilled when he gets home. He's on the closing shift at Baja Taco tonight, so he won't be back until around three-thirty, but I’ll make sure to leave a note on the counter."

"Tell him we dropped Sloane," Ashley added, offering a weary but triumphant smirk. "Ms. Pitizer failed him and I took lead snare."

Mom chuckled softly, well aware of the year-long drama surrounding the percussion section. "I will absolutely tell him. Now, upstairs. All of you. Brush your teeth and go to sleep. You look like you're about to fall over."

The bathroom rotation was slow and highly methodical. We took turns trudging down the hallway to wash our faces, brush the stale theme-park sugar off our teeth, and finally peel off the clothes that had been soaked by the *Imperial Rapids* and dried stiff by the evening air.

When I finally pulled a clean, oversized t-shirt and a pair of soft flannel sleep pants on, my nervous system let out a massive, collective sigh of relief.

The four of us migrated back down to the dimly lit living room.

Jessica walked over to her air mattress on the floor. Before she sat down, Ashley, Rachel, and I lined up to exchange our standard, grounding goodnight hugs with our older sister.

"Goodnight, Base Camp," Jessica smiled, pulling her heavy quilt up to her chin. "I'm glad you guys had a good day. And I really like Bonnie."

"Me too," I admitted quietly, feeling a warm, exhausted blush creeping up the back of my neck.

"Goodnight, Jess," Ashley whispered, reaching over to click off the small reading lamp.

The room plunged into comfortable, familiar darkness, illuminated only by the ambient glow of the streetlights filtering through the front windows.

It was time to lock the perimeter.

I crawled into the absolute dead center of the pull-out sofa bed, my muscles practically melting into the mattress. Rachel climbed in immediately on my right side, and Ashley slid in on my left.

The transition was flawless. They both moved into my space, closing the physical distance. Rachel pressed her side flush against my ribs, throwing her arm securely over my chest to anchor herself. Ashley leaned her head heavily against my other shoulder, her arm draping across my waist.

I wrapped my arms around both of them, pulling them in tight to provide the deep, grounding pressure that regulated our entire ecosystem.

It was a profound contrast to the electric, heart-racing cuddle I had shared with Bonnie on the bus ride home. This touch didn't spark adrenaline or butterflies; it was the absolute baseline. It was the heavy, utilitarian gravity that manually lowered my heart rate and silenced the chaotic static of the outside world. It was the exact physical configuration that had kept us alive through the darkest months of 1995.

"Goodnight, Bug," I whispered, resting my chin lightly against the top of Rachel's head.

"Night, Bear," she mumbled, already half-asleep against my chest.

"Goodnight, Ash," I murmured to my left.

Ashley let out a long, contented sigh, her grip tightening securely around my ribs. "Best day ever, Michael. We did it."

We had. We had conquered the coasters, survived the heat, completely dismantled the toxic variables in our band, and successfully expanded the perimeter without breaking the foundation. As I stared up at the shadowed ceiling of the living room, listening to the synchronized breathing of my sister and my honorary sister, the math finally rested at zero.

I closed my eyes, and the Old World faded away into the safety of the dark.
