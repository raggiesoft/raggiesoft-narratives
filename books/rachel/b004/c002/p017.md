By the time the sun fully set and the massive amphitheater lights of *The Sovereign Palace Theater* flared to life, my internal processor was completely, blissfully offline.

The adrenaline reserves that had carried me through the performance had entirely burned out, leaving behind a heavy, bone-deep exhaustion. The hundreds of middle schoolers who had performed throughout the day were now packed into the stone seating bowl of the theater, waiting for the judges to finalize the scores.

We were sitting in the middle section, perfectly locked into our established geometry.

I was sitting in the absolute center. But unlike the hyper-vigilant, defensive posture I usually maintained in large crowds, my guard was completely down. I was leaning to my right, resting my heavy, exhausted head fully against Bonnie’s shoulder. She had shifted her seating angle specifically to accommodate my weight, her arm resting comfortably against my back. Our fingers were loosely, warmly interlaced in my lap.

On my left side, Ashley was sitting close, her knee pressed against mine. She was holding my left hand, but her grip was the familiar, utilitarian anchor—a solid, grounding tether to keep me from floating away into the exhaustion.

I was entirely surrounded by my pack. I didn't care who saw us, and I didn't care what Julia or anyone else thought. The math was perfect.

Down on the stage, the Master of Ceremonies stepped up to the microphone, holding a stack of heavy, gold-embossed certificates. Next to him stood a table filled with towering plastic trophies.

"Alright, ladies and gentlemen, settle down!" the MC’s voice boomed over the PA system. The chaotic chatter of a thousand teenagers immediately dropped to an anxious hush. "We will begin with the overall festival ratings for the Middle School Concert Band division."

In these competitions, bands were graded on a rubric and given a rating: Poor, Fair, Good, Excellent, or Superior.

Ms. Pitizer was sitting in the aisle seat of our row, nervously twisting her clipboard in her hands. Because of Sloane's constant tempo dragging all year, we had consistently scored in the 'Good' and 'Excellent' ranges at our regional assessments. We had never quite breached the top tier.

The MC read through the first few schools. Then, he looked down at the next certificate.

"For their performance of *Crown of the Vanguard*, *Whispers in the Valley*, and *The Locomotive Galop*," the MC announced, his voice echoing off the stone walls. "Morrison Middle School... Superior Rating!"

Our entire section of the seating bowl erupted. Ms. Pitizer actually jumped out of her seat, covering her mouth in shock. Ashley squeezed my left hand so hard my knuckles popped, letting out a deafening cheer.

"We got it!" Bonnie laughed, squeezing my right hand and turning her head to press a quick, incredibly warm kiss against the top of my hair.

My heart did a massive, happy flutter, but I was too tired to lift my head from her shoulder. I just smiled, letting the absolute victory wash over me.

"Settle down, settle down," the MC chuckled, waiting for the applause to die out. "We are now moving on to the Caption Awards. These awards are given to specific sections across all participating schools that demonstrated absolute excellence in their technical execution today."

He read off the award for Best Brass Section, which went to a school from Richmond, and Best Woodwind Section, which went to a local Williamsburg magnet school.

"And finally," the MC said, picking up a tall trophy topped with a golden lyre. "The award for Outstanding Percussion Section."

Ashley instantly stopped cheering. She leaned forward, her entire body going entirely rigid with anticipation. Bonnie tightened her arm around my back.

"This section demonstrated flawless tempo control, exceptional dynamic range, and incredible rhythmic precision, especially during the blistering pace of their finale," the MC read from the judge's notes. "The award for Outstanding Percussion goes to... Morrison Middle School!"

For a split second, there was total silence in our row as the sheer, undeniable irony of the universe processed in our brains.

And then, Ashley absolutely exploded.

She leaped out of her fiberglass seat, screaming at the top of her lungs, dragging me halfway up with her because our hands were still locked together. Bonnie was cheering just as loudly, her face glowing with pure, unadulterated joy. Even Julia Matthews, sitting a few rows ahead of us, turned around and pumped her fist in the air.

We had won the award for Best Percussion Section on the exact same day our so-called "lead snare" was kicked out of the park by security. The moment the toxic, faking variable was deleted from the equation, the true math of our section had proven itself to be absolutely flawless.

"Go get it, Ms. Pitizer!" Ashley yelled as our band director practically sprinted down the aisle toward the stage to collect the massive trophy and the plaque.

Ashley dropped back into her seat, breathing heavily, a wild, victorious grin plastered across her face. She looked at me, then looked at Bonnie.

"Best performance of the year," Ashley repeated, her voice thick with absolute, undeniable pride.

I let my head fall back onto Bonnie's shoulder, my eyes slipping closed as the ceremony continued on the stage below. The ghost of 1995 was locked in the vault. My sister was waiting for me at home. My honorary sister had orchestrated the ultimate victory. And the girl I liked was actively holding me up.

The Old World of middle school had been chaotic and exhausting, but as we sat in the cool Virginia night holding our Superior rating, the perimeter was entirely, permanently secure.
