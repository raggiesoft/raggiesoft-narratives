"Morrison Middle School, you are up! Go, go, go!" the stage manager barked, waving a glowing orange flashlight toward the wings.

The heavy, black acoustic curtains were pulled back, revealing the massive, open-air stage of *The Sovereign Palace Theater*. Stepping out from the cramped backstage hallway and into the cool, post-storm evening air was a massive relief. The amphitheater was cavernous, its stone seating bowl completely packed with parents, tourists, and other middle school bands waiting their turn.

At festival competitions, the logistics were ruthless. We were allotted exactly ten minutes to load onto the stage, perform our entire three-song program, and strike our equipment so the next school could take over.

The Morrison concert band swarmed the stage in a highly practiced, chaotic sprint.

Because my legs were still completely compromised, the pack took over my load-in. Bonnie carried my black folding chair and placed it perfectly in the back row of the percussion section. Ashley rolled the massive concert bass drum into position on my left, while Julia—surprisingly cooperative now that the musical hierarchy had been violently reset—dragged the suspended cymbal stand and the trap table to my right.

I sank into the folding chair, immensely grateful for the seating, and picked up my heavy, fleece-covered mallets.

Ms. Pitizer stepped up onto the conductor’s podium at the absolute front of the stage. She checked her watch, raised her baton, and the entire band brought their instruments to their mouths.

The downbeat hit, and we launched into our opener: *Crown of the Vanguard*.

It was a sharp, aggressive, brass-heavy concert march designed to wake the judges up, and it relied heavily on a driving, militaristic snare cadence. For the entire school year, this song had been a muddy, off-tempo disaster because Sloane couldn't keep a steady beat.

But tonight, the math was flawless.

Ashley stood tall behind the lead snare drum, her sticks flying with absolute, razor-sharp precision. She didn't just play the notes; she dictated the entire tempo of the band, laying down a perfectly rigid rhythmic grid that the brass and woodwinds could finally lock into. I sat in my chair, striking the massive concert bass drum on the one and the three, feeling the deep, resonant *boom* vibrate through my chest.

Without the dead weight dragging us down, the percussion section was a well-oiled machine. Up on the podium, the tight, frantic stress melted off Ms. Pitizer's face. She actually smiled.

As the final, echoing chord of the march rang out over the outdoor amphitheater, the audience erupted into applause. We didn't pause to celebrate. We immediately shuffled our sheet music to the second piece.

The second song, *Whispers in the Valley*, was a slow, lyrical, atmospheric piece designed to show off the woodwinds.

My job was to provide the ambient texture. I picked up a pair of soft yarn mallets and executed a long, sustained, seamless roll on the suspended cymbal, building a shimmering wave of metallic sound that swelled underneath the flutes.

I looked over at Bonnie. She was standing at the auxiliary station holding a silver triangle, counting her rests. She caught me looking, and even with the intense focus of the performance, she flashed me a quick, radiant smile. My chest swelled. I was sitting down to accommodate my cerebral palsy, making music with my honorary sister and my new girlfriend, and the perimeter was completely unbreakable.

The lyrical piece ended with a gentle fade to silence.

"Last one, let's go," Ashley whispered fiercely, flipping her music to the finale.

The third song was *The Locomotive Galop*, a frantic, high-speed technical piece that sounded exactly like a runaway train. It required every single percussionist to operate in absolute, synchronized lockstep.

I swapped my mallets for a heavy brass tambourine. Ms. Pitizer gave the final downbeat, and we took off.

The tempo was blistering. Julia proved why she deserved the snare two part, perfectly matching Ashley’s complex sticking patterns. I locked into the sixteenth-note tambourine rhythm, anchoring the chaotic speed. But the highlight of the song belonged entirely to Bonnie.

At the absolute climax of the piece, the entire band dropped out for one measure, leaving a massive, silent gap. Bonnie stepped forward, lifted the heavy, brass crash cymbals, and executed a flawless, deafening, perfectly timed crash that echoed off the stone walls of the amphitheater.

It was perfect. The brass section blasted back in for the final, triumphant chord, and Ms. Pitizer cut us off with a sharp slash of her baton.

The amphitheater exploded into cheers and a standing ovation. We had absolutely nailed it.

We didn't have time to bow. The stage manager was already pointing at his watch from the wings.

"Strike!" Ms. Pitizer ordered over the applause.

The pack sprang into action. Bonnie grabbed my chair, Ashley hauled the bass drum, and I grabbed my trap gear, my legs miraculously finding just enough residual adrenaline to carry me off the stage. We cleared the wings right at the nine-minute and forty-five-second mark, making way for the next middle school to take the stage.

As the heavy velvet curtains closed behind us, cutting off the noise of the crowd, Ashley dropped her sticks onto the drumhead and let out a massive, victorious cheer.

"That," Ashley announced to the section, high-fiving Bonnie and bumping her shoulder into mine, "is how you play the math. Best performance of the year."
