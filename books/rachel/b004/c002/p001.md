The sun wasn't even fully above the tree line when the living room officially woke up, but Ashley was already vibrating at a completely unmanageable frequency.

She practically bounced off the sofa bed, entirely bypassing the usual groggy teenage morning routine. That strange, encrypted energy she had been carrying since yesterday afternoon had amplified overnight. As I rubbed the sleep out of my eyes, I caught Ashley looking over at Jessica’s air mattress. Jessica was already awake, propped up on her elbows. Ashley shot my older sister a massive, barely contained grin. Jessica just rolled her eyes fondly and gave a subtle, affirming nod.

Whatever the secret was, it was taking every ounce of Ashley's willpower not to explode with it.

"Come on, Michael, get up!" Ashley ordered, throwing a pillow at my head. "The buses leave at eight! We have to move!"

The departure protocol initiated. We took turns cycling through the hallway bathroom, brushing our teeth and changing into our designated theme-park gear—comfortable sneakers, jeans, and lightweight t-shirts to survive the humid Virginia spring heat.

Once we were dressed, we headed into the kitchen for a rapid-fire breakfast of toast and cereal with Mom and Dad. Nobody lingered. The moment the bowls were in the sink, we moved back into the living room to tear down the base camp. Working as a highly efficient unit, Rachel, Ashley, and I stripped the quilts, hauled the massive pile of pillows back to the hall closet, and folded the heavy mattress back into the sofa with a loud, metallic squeak of springs.

At seven-fifteen, it was time to leave.

I gave Jessica a tight, grounding hug goodbye, then moved to the kitchen to hug Mom and Dad. When I turned back to the hallway, Rachel was standing by the front door. Her arms were crossed tightly over her chest, and she was wearing a massive, heavy pout.

Seeing Ashley and me buzzing with adrenaline while she was stuck in the sixth-grade reality of a normal Saturday was devastating her.

I didn't try to use logic from across the room. I walked right up to her, wrapped my arms around her shoulders, and pulled her into a heavy, deep-pressure Bear hug. She resisted for half a second before melting into the compression, wrapping her arms securely around my waist.

"I know it sucks, Bug," I murmured into her hair, holding the grounding pressure steady. "But I promise, the absolute second school is done for the summer, we are taking our season passes and going. Just the pack."

Rachel let out a muffled, reluctant sigh against my chest.

"And remember the math," I added, pulling back just enough to look her in the eye. "When you're an eighth grader, you'll get to go on this exact same trip with your friends. And I won't even be there. I'll be stuck doing homework at Northwood High."

The logic finally clicked into place. The math balanced. Rachel’s pout softened into a tiny, tired smile. I gave her one last, fierce squeeze before letting go.

"Have fun," Rachel mumbled, stepping back.

"We will," Ashley promised, giving Rachel a quick hug of her own. "We'll win you a giant stuffed animal."

Because the logistics of the band program dictated that our heavy instruments, sheet music, and stiff uniforms were already securely stored in the band room waiting for the chaperone's rented box truck, we didn't have to haul our massive backpacks. We just grabbed our wallets, shoving them into our front pockets. It felt incredibly strange, almost weightless, to walk out the front door without a heavy nylon strap digging into my shoulders.

The heavy deadbolt clicked shut behind us.

Stepping off the front porch, I immediately reached out my left hand. Ashley grabbed it, her fingers locking tightly around mine in our standard, non-negotiable vice grip. It wasn't romantic; it was our physical tether. It was the absolute baseline of our survival in chaotic environments.

We walked the three blocks down Morrison Street, the crisp morning air slowly warming up as the neighborhood woke.

When we reached Morrison Middle School, the front lobby was deserted. We bypassed the main entrance and walked around the perimeter of the brick building, heading straight for the far end where the heavy double doors of the band room opened directly out toward the bus loop.

The area was already buzzing with chaotic, loud eighth-grade energy. Two yellow school buses were idling by the curb, their diesel engines rumbling loudly. Near the double doors, a parent chaperone was standing with a heavy plastic clipboard, checking off names as students arrived.

Ashley and I walked up, still firmly holding hands to anchor each other against the rising volume of the crowd.

"Ragsdale and Raybourn," I told the chaperone, raising my voice slightly to be heard over the diesel engines.

"Got you both," the chaperone nodded, checking off two boxes with a blue pen. "Go ahead and wait by the grass. Ms. Pitizer will start boarding in ten minutes."

We turned to walk toward the designated waiting area, but we didn't make it three steps before a painfully familiar voice cut through the noise.

"Aww, look at the happy couple!"

I stopped, my shoulders instantly dropping in exhaustion. Julia was standing near the edge of the sidewalk with a group of clarinets, wearing a massive, irritating smirk. She pointed directly at our locked hands. "You guys are so cute. Are you going to hold hands on the rollercoasters, too?"

Both Ashley and I rolled our eyes in flawless, synchronized disgust. Julia had been running this exact same taunt since the third grade, and the sibling-style annoyance of it never faded.

"Shut up, Julia," Ashley fired back instantly, her tone dripping with absolute, unfiltered irritation. "He's basically my brother. You're gross."

I didn't even bother engaging. I just tightened my platonic grip on Ashley’s hand, completely unbothered by the accusation but entirely annoyed by the noise, and steered my best friend away from the clarinets to wait for the rest of our section to arrive.
