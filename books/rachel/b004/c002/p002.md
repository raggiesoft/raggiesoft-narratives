We didn't have to wait long. A minute or two later, Bonnie walked out of the heavy double doors of the band room.

"Reed," Bonnie said quietly, checking in with the chaperone holding the clipboard.

She stepped onto the grass and immediately started scanning the chaotic crowd of eighth graders. The social landscape of the percussion section was already out in full force. A few yards away, Julia was locked in a loud, aggressive squabble with one of the trumpet players, looking exactly like a bickering sibling. Over by the first bus, Sloane was puffing his chest out, acting completely macho and trying to look tough in front of a couple of guys from the trombone section.

Bonnie took one look at the toxic, exhausting middle school posturing, rolled her eyes, and naturally gravitated straight toward the quiet safety of our perimeter.

As she walked up, I instinctively reached out my right arm to greet her. My intention was to execute our standard, established baseline: a fierce, rib-crushing grounding hug designed to apply deep pressure and reset the nervous system.

I wrapped my arm around her, but the second the embrace connected, the physical data completely changed.

Bonnie didn't step into the heavy, static hold. Instead of wrapping her arms around my ribs to apply counter-pressure, her arms slid lower, wrapping lightly around my waist. She didn't press her weight into my shoulder; she just leaned her face close to my neck. And instead of pulling back immediately, she lingered for a fraction of a second too long.

It completely lacked the crushing weight of a pack hug. It wasn't an anchor; it was a spark. Instead of manually lowering my heart rate, this hug actively spiked it. A sudden, unfamiliar warmth flooded my chest. I hugged her back, utterly confused. The data was completely unmapped. I had hugged her every single day as a greeting, but my processor could not explain why this specific touch felt so entirely different.

Before I could even try to categorize the anomaly, a voice completely shattered the moment.

Julia had abandoned her argument with the trumpet player. She pointed a finger directly at us, her face lighting up with a massive, annoying smirk. "Ooooh! Look at the lovebirds!"

Bonnie instantly stepped back, her face flushing a brilliant, undeniable shade of crimson. She looked down at her sneakers, completely flustered.

I looked over at Ashley, expecting my best friend to fire back one of her usual, fiercely protective insults to chase Julia away. But Ashley wasn't yelling. Ashley was biting her lip, her own face flushing bright red, vibrating with that exact same encrypted, buzzing energy from yesterday. She was struggling with every ounce of her willpower to contain a secret.

"Alright, eighth grade! Listen up!" Ms. Pitizer shouted, clapping her hands loudly from the front of the line. "The buses are jammed packed today. We are sitting three to a seat! Boarding starts now!"

The crowd immediately surged toward the doors of the two yellow school buses.

Ashley, operating with flawless, tactical wingman precision, grabbed my arm. She quickly scanned the crowd, spotting Julia and Sloane both pushing their way onto the first bus. Without a word, Ashley smoothly steered me and Bonnie away from the chaos and straight onto the second bus.

We climbed the heavy rubber steps and found an empty, green vinyl bench seat halfway down the aisle. Because we had to cram three teenagers into a space designed for two, the geometry had to be precise.

Ashley orchestrated it instantly. She slid Bonnie into the window seat, then pointed to the middle for me. Ashley took the aisle.

I knew exactly what my best friend was doing. It had been exactly four years since Rachel and I were confined to the heavy pull-out sofa bed in our living room. Ashley remembered the layout of our survival perfectly. She knew that my safest, most grounded position was always in the absolute center, anchored by two solid physical boundaries. She put me in the middle so that if the sensory chaos of the packed bus disrupted my equilibrium, Ashley could be my shield on one side, and Bonnie could be my shield on the other.

As we squeezed into the tight space, my shoulders pressed firmly against both of them, I caught Ashley and Bonnie exchanging a look over my head. They both blushed. They were actively sharing an encrypted data point—a very deep secret that I was entirely locked out of.

The bus engine roared, the floorboards vibrating heavily beneath our sneakers.

Then, Bonnie shifted.

Instead of sitting up straight, she leaned in, initiating a cuddle. But just like the hug on the grass, the mechanics were entirely upgraded. It wasn't the dead, heavy weight I was used to from Rachel or Ashley when they needed a physical boundary. It was just proximity. Her shoulder brushed lightly against mine, her head tilting just slightly toward my arm. The light friction sent an electric, distracting warmth straight through my nervous system.

My heart rate spiked again. I stared straight ahead at the green vinyl seat in front of us, my brain working in overdrive. *It felt different.* The parameters of the pack were shifting, and I couldn't process the math. Was there something I needed to know?

"Listen up!" Ms. Pitizer’s voice boomed from the front of the bus, holding up her heavy plastic clipboard. She began calling out names one last time to verify the headcount, her voice fighting over the rumble of the diesel engine.

A moment later, the heavy folding doors hissed shut. The air brakes released with a loud sigh, the bus lurched forward, and we were officially off to Williamsburg.
