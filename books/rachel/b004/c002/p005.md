We bypassed the slower, scenic rides of the 'England' and 'Scotland' hamlets entirely, bee-lining straight for the towering yellow tracks of the park's most iconic coaster: *The Loch Serpent*.

Because the park had just opened, the queue winding through the wooden switchbacks was moving fast. As we navigated the line, Ashley and I—having spent practically every summer of our lives at Brewster Gardens—initiated the standard pre-ride briefing for our newest member.

"Okay, the cars on the train only have two seats per row," I explained to Bonnie over the roar of the coaster cycling overhead. "Because there are three of us, the math means someone has to sit by themselves in the row behind."

Before Bonnie or I could even offer to take the solo seat, Ashley immediately stepped back, her face lighting up with that same, buzzing, conspiratorial smirk she had been wearing since yesterday afternoon.

"I'm taking the back row," Ashley volunteered instantly, pointing a finger at the two of us. "You guys sit together."

I paused, my brain trying to process the social data. Usually, Ashley and I fought over who got to sit next to the newest person. But today, she was actively forcing the seating arrangement. Whatever massive, encrypted secret she was struggling to contain, my processor deduced it was directly related to making sure Bonnie and I shared a two-seater car.

Two minutes later, the air gates hissed open. Bonnie and I stepped into the third row, pulling the heavy, black over-the-shoulder restraints down until they clicked securely against our chests. Ashley climbed into the fourth row directly behind us, grinning like she had just won the lottery.

"Clear!" the ride operators called out, giving a thumbs-up.

The brakes released, and the train slowly rolled out of the station. Between the loading platform and the massive first chain lift, there was a tiny, two-foot dip in the track designed just to give the train enough momentum to engage the chain.

As the front car hit that tiny, insignificant drop, about half the train—including Ashley and me—let out a massive, exaggerated, theatrical scream, acting as if we were plunging down a hundred-foot drop. It was a legendary, unspoken tradition for the local kids who grew up at the park, entirely in good fun.

Beside me, Bonnie burst out laughing at the sheer absurdity of it, her tension instantly vanishing as the heavy *clack-clack-clack* of the chain lift took over.

The ride was a blur of adrenaline and wind. We plunged down the first drop, our stomachs dropping into our shoes, and tore through the interlocking loops. But the best part of *The Loch Serpent* was the cave.

The train dove straight into the dark mountain tunnel. The roar of the wheels echoing off the concrete walls was deafening as we were thrown into a massive, crushing triple helix. As we spun in the pitch black, a massive, glowing painting of the mythical serpent appeared on the left wall, illuminated by strobe lights.

We rocketed toward the exit of the cave. Right as the train burst back out into the Virginia sunlight, a hidden rig sprayed a thick blast of cold mist over the track—the "serpent's breath."

We hit the second chain lift, slowly climbing up for the final sequence.

"Get ready!" Ashley yelled from the row behind us. "Camera!"

Because Ashley and I had the entire track memorized, we knew exactly where the flash was. The camera was mounted at the absolute bottom of the steep drop immediately following this second lift hill.

The train crested the top and plunged. Right as we hit the bottom of the valley, Ashley and I threw our hands up and struck our standard mid-air poses. A blinding flash of white light exploded from the bushes, capturing us perfectly.

When the train finally hit the final brake run and rolled back into the station, we unbuckled our restraints and walked down the exit ramp toward the photo booth.

"I am buying that," Ashley insisted the moment we saw the monitors.

She slapped her money onto the counter. The glossy print was fantastic. Ashley was in the back row, her hands thrown up and a massive, wingman smirk on her face. In the row ahead, I was grinning widely, and Bonnie was captured mid-laugh, her hands gripping the restraint, looking absolutely thrilled.

As Ashley collected the photo, Bonnie looked up at the towering yellow loops.

"Okay, that was amazing," Bonnie breathed, her dark eyes shining with adrenaline. "I was a little worried it would be too tame compared to the hyper-coasters back home, but that helix in the cave pulled some serious positive G-forces."

Ashley and I both stopped dead in our tracks, turning to look at her.

"Wait," I said, my processor immediately locking onto the specific, technical vocabulary she just used. "Hyper-coasters? Positive G-forces?"

Bonnie offered a modest, slightly embarrassed smile. "Yeah. Since this is my first time at Brewster Gardens, I didn't want to brag. But back where I used to live? I rode everything. I'm a massive coaster nerd. I have a whole binder of track layouts and drop angles in my bedroom."

Ashley’s jaw dropped. Then, she looked at me, her blue eyes wide with the realization that she had just uncovered the ultimate compatibility data point.

Bonnie wasn't just a girl I had a crush on. She spoke the exact same hyper-fixated, technical language of adrenaline and physics that I did. We were both absolute rollercoaster fanatics.

"Well," Ashley grinned, wiping a bead of sweat from her forehead as the Virginia humidity began to rapidly bake the asphalt. "Since it's already getting sweltering out here, we're going counter-clockwise. Hastings is next."

"What's in Hastings?" Bonnie asked, fanning herself with the park map.

"Air conditioning," I answered, grabbing the edge of Ashley's sleeve to guide our pack through the thickening crowd. "We are going to ride *The Iron Aeronaut*."
