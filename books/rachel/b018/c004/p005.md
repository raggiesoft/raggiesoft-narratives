With no sofas or dining room chairs yet assembled, the geometry of the conversation was incredibly grounded. We formed a loose circle sitting directly on the carpet in the center of the empty living room.

Except for April.

The *Sunhoe Sabomnim* understood the delicate, deeply personal nature of a family trauma reunion. She intentionally excluded herself from the circle. Instead, April stood near the entryway, her back resting casually against the drywall beside the front window. She didn't intrude on the Raybourns' grief, but her posture was entirely tactical. She was physically placing herself between Ashley and the locked front door, establishing an impenetrable, human shield.

"I'll hold the perimeter," April said quietly, giving Jim a single, respectful nod.

In the center of the room, Joan was operating on pure, maternal instinct. She held both of Ashley’s hands, her eyes scanning her daughter's pale face.

"Ash," Joan whispered, her voice trembling slightly. "Did he hurt you?"

Ashley closed her eyes, a fresh wave of tears spilling over her eyelashes. She didn't answer verbally. Instead, she let go of her mother's hand and slowly pushed the left sleeve of her oversized sweater up past her elbow.

Dark, mottled purple and yellow bruises bloomed across her bicep and forearm in the unmistakable shape of a brutal, gripping handprint.

Rachel sucked in a sharp breath, her hand instantly tightening in mine.

"It wasn't just the isolation anymore," Ashley sobbed, pulling the sleeve back down as if trying to hide the shame. "He got angry last night. He threw me against the counter. That was it. I knew if I didn't get out today, I never would."

Jim stared at the bruises on his daughter's arm. The initial, heartbroken shock on his face instantly crystallized into a cold, terrifyingly focused parental fury. He didn't yell, and he didn't curse, which somehow made his anger even more imposing.

"Okay," Jim said, his voice a low, gravelly rumble. "Here is what is happening right now. We are packing up this laptop. You are coming home with us, and we are going straight to the Urgent Care clinic on Independence Boulevard."

Ashley looked up, panicked. "Dad, nothing is broken. I swear. It's just bruises—"

"I know, sweetheart," Jim interrupted gently, reaching out to stroke her hair. "But we need a medical professional to look at you, and more importantly, we need a paper trail. We need those bruises photographed and documented in a federal medical chart today. David's story about a dead wife is going to look incredibly stupid when I hand a judge a date-stamped medical report of his domestic battery."

Joan nodded fiercely, already zipping the laptop back into the canvas duffel bag.

Rachel and I recognized the mechanics of what Jim was doing. We had lived through custody battles and the weaponization of legal documents. Jim wasn't just protecting his daughter; he was actively preparing the battlefield to absolutely annihilate David Lawson.

Jim stood up, pulling Ashley to her feet.

Ashley turned to us. She didn't bother with words. She threw her arms around Rachel, hugging her fiercely, and then moved to me, burying her face in my shoulder.

"Thank you," Ashley whispered into my shirt. "Thank you for answering the message."

"You're a Sentinel," I replied softly, squeezing her back. "The door is always open."

Ashley pulled away and wiped her eyes. She looked over at the entryway, where April was already reaching out to unlock the deadbolt for them.

"Master April... thank you for driving," Ashley said awkwardly, clearly intimidated by the martial arts instructor she barely knew, but overwhelmingly grateful for the getaway car.

April offered a warm, steady smile that completely neutralized the intimidating aura. "Take care of yourself, Ashley. Let your parents do the heavy lifting now."

Jim and Joan guided Ashley out the front door and down the driveway toward their idling minivan.

We stood in the entryway and watched until the van's taillights disappeared around the corner of Canadian Arch. April stepped out onto her side of the concrete divider, turning back to look at us.

"I'm going to head inside and hit the shower," April said. Her eyes swept over the quiet neighborhood street one last time, her tactical assessment concluding that the immediate threat was gone. "Lock the deadbolt. You two did good today. I'll see you both later."

"See you, April," Rachel rasped quietly.

I pushed the heavy front door shut. The brass deadbolt clicked into place with a loud, absolute finality.

I turned around, my back resting against the wood of the door. The adrenaline of the Blackwater extraction was finally beginning to bleed out of my system. Rachel stood in the center of the foyer, looking around the empty, echoing townhouse.

It was late Monday afternoon. The living room was entirely devoid of furniture, the Command Center upstairs consisted of two metal folding tables, and our bed was a piece of green plastic filled with air.

But as I looked at my sister, the profound magnitude of what we had just accomplished settled over us. On our very first day of absolute independence, we hadn't just survived; we had mobilized, successfully executed a high-stress rescue, and defended our pack.

Rachel walked over to me, wrapping her arms around my waist and pressing her forehead against my chest in a deep, grounding hug.

The ghost of the Delaney Street pull-out couch was officially at peace. 3701 Canadian Arch was secure, and we were finally home.
