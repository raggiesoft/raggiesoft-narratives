The drive down into the deep, rural borderlands of Virginia Beach was an exercise in pure tension. Rachel and I sat shoulder-to-shoulder in the back seat of April’s little blue car, our hands locked together. April knew exactly what she was doing when she told us to both get in the back; we needed the unbroken, side-by-side physical contact to keep our neurodivergent systems regulated while rushing into an unknown, volatile environment.

The dense suburban sprawl slowly gave way to open farmland, thick tree lines, and marshy wetlands.

April slowed the car as we approached the very bottom of the city, right before the state line crossed into North Carolina. Up ahead, on the right side of Princess Anne Road, was the electronic security gate of the Blackwater Beach RV Resort & Cottages.

Standing on the public shoulder, clutching a single, overstuffed canvas duffel bag, was Ashley.

She looked pale and exhausted, her posture tense as she watched the passing traffic.

April pulled the car onto the shoulder, the tires crunching against the gravel. I immediately rolled down my window, leaning my face out so she could see exactly who it was.

Ashley let out a massive, shuddering gasp. The relief that washed over her face was absolute.

She ran to the car. Rachel slid over, pressing herself flush against the driver's side door to open up the middle seat. Ashley threw her duffel bag onto the floorboards and scrambled into the back, pulling the door shut behind her.

Instantly, the geometry of the pack shifted into a variation of our classic arrangement. Normally, I sat in the middle so I could act as the heavy, central anchor for both of them. But today, Ashley needed maximum compression. She sat dead center in the back seat. I pressed flush against her right side, and Rachel pressed flush against her left. We wrapped our arms around her simultaneously, locking the runaway Sentinel into a fierce, suffocatingly tight, two-sided pack hug.

"I've got you," I whispered, resting my chin on her shoulder.

"You're safe," Rachel rasped, her arm locked tightly around Ashley's waist.

Ashley burst into tears, dropping her head against my chest. "Thank you," she sobbed. "Thank you."

"We need to move," April stated from the front seat, her eyes scanning the rearview mirror with tactical precision. She pulled the car back onto Princess Anne Road, heading north.

Ashley reached into her pocket, pulled out her cell phone, and held down the power button until the screen went completely black. "He tracks my phone," she whispered, her voice trembling. "I can't turn it on."

I immediately pulled my phone from my pocket and handed it to her. "Use mine. Call Joan and Jim."

Ashley dialed her parents' number from memory. When her mother picked up, Ashley quickly and tearfully explained the extraction. Through the tiny speaker, I could hear Joan Raybourn's voice—steady, fiercely protective, and completely unsurprised that the Ragsdale siblings were the ones running the rescue.

"We're heading back to Canadian Arch right now," I called out toward the phone. "April says we should be there in about thirty minutes if traffic is clear."

"We are leaving the house right now," Jim Raybourn’s voice boomed over the line. "We will meet you at the townhouse, sweetheart. We know a very good lawyer. It's over."

Ashley hung up, handing my phone back. She leaned forward slightly, looking at the back of April's headrest. "Master April... David works in the Red Mill area. He could be on his lunch break driving around Princess Anne."

The *Sunhoe Sabomnim* didn't even blink. "Understood. We are going off the grid."

April hit the brakes, throwing her turn signal on. Instead of continuing straight up Princess Anne Road into the commercial heart of the Red Mill corridor, she ripped the steering wheel to the left, turning onto the two-lane rural stretch of Pungo Ferry Road.

As we accelerated toward the Pungo Ferry Bridge, my analytical brain suddenly registered the tactical brilliance of our current seating arrangement. By sitting directly in the middle, Ashley was sandwiched between my larger frame and Rachel. From the outside of the car, if David were to pass us in his truck, he would only see the silhouettes of a man and a woman in the back seat. Ashley was completely, visually hidden from the windows.

We crossed the Pungo Ferry Bridge, and April made a sharp right turn onto Blackwater Road.

We were deep in the backroads now. April navigated the winding, tree-lined asphalt with absolute focus, following Blackwater Road as it crossed over the Pocaty River, briefly slipping us over the city line and into Chesapeake. We followed it all the way to the end, turning onto Fentress Airfield Road for a short distance before merging onto SR-165—Mt. Pleasant Road.

"Hold on," April warned as she hit the accelerator.

We followed the curve of the road, the massive metal structure of the North Landing Bridge rising up ahead of us, carrying us back into Virginia Beach territory. The second our rear tires hit the solid pavement on the other side of the bridge, the warning bells began to ring.

I looked out the back window. The traffic gates dropped down, the red lights flashed, and the massive mechanical gears engaged. The two leaves of the drawbridge began to slowly swing open to the side to let a boat through.

We had made it across with mere seconds to spare. A literal, impassable physical barrier had just swung open behind us, completely severing us from the southern half of the city and any potential pursuit.

"Perfect timing," Rachel muttered, leaning her head against Ashley’s shoulder.

April didn't slow down. We continued northbound on SR-165, taking a sharp left turn onto Salem Road. It was a chaotic, twisting residential road that required intimate local knowledge to navigate efficiently, but April drove it like it was an extension of her own nervous system.

We hit Landstown Road, making a right, and followed it until it dead-ended at Dam Neck Road. As we made another right, the massive, sweeping lawns and parking lots of the *Tidewater Pavilion* amphitheater rolled past our windows.

"Almost there," I promised Ashley, squeezing her arm.

April executed a sharp left onto Rosemont Road. As we crossed the intersection of Rosemont and Celtic Drive, I looked out the window. Sitting just beyond the tree line, I could clearly see the recognizable roof of the wooden barn sitting in the backyard of Mom's house on Criollo Drive. We were officially back in our sector.

A left turn at the Buckner Boulevard signal brought the CCHR campus into view, and a final right turn put us onto the quiet, suburban pavement of Canadian Arch.

Because the townhouse community didn't feature street parking, the concrete driveways were the only place to stop. April steered her blue car directly into her own driveway at 3703.

Parked right next door, securely occupying one of the two spaces in the driveway of our unit at 3701, was a familiar minivan with the engine still running.

The moment April put her car in park, the doors of the van flew open. Joan and Jim Raybourn stepped out onto the concrete.

Ashley scrambled out of the back seat, practically falling into her father's arms in the space between the two driveways. Jim wrapped his daughter in a massive, crushing hug, burying his face in her hair while Joan rubbed her back, tears streaming down both of their faces.

I grabbed Ashley's duffel bag from the floorboards, and Rachel and I stepped out of the car. April killed the engine and joined us, her posture radiating absolute, impenetrable security.

"Let's get everybody inside," I said, looking at the Raybourns. "The perimeter is clear."

Jim nodded, keeping one arm securely around his daughter. We walked the few short steps to our front door. I unlocked it, and the entire group stepped over the threshold into 3701 Canadian Arch. The heavy brass deadbolt clicked shut behind us, and the hunt was officially over.
