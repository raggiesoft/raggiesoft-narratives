My eyes blinked open slowly, my processor fighting through the heavy, lingering fog of exhaustion.

Before my visual cortex could fully map the room, my nervous system registered the physical data. I couldn't move easily. There was a massive, solid weight pressing down across my entire chest and stomach, pinning me to the mattress.

For a terrifying microsecond, the ghost of Kent flared in my peripheral data. My chest tightened, bracing for the suffocating panic of Delores or Brandi.

But the geometry was completely wrong for a threat. The weight wasn't erratic or coercive; it was steady, warm, and perfectly distributed.

I turned my head slightly on the pillow. It was Rachel.

My little sister was still lying flush against me, stomach to stomach, exactly where she had positioned herself hours ago when my brain had spiraled into the dark. Her dark hair was splayed across my collarbone, and her arms were still wrapped securely around my back. She was acting as a human weighted blanket, and the deep-pressure compression felt incredibly, undeniably safe.

I reached up, wrapping my arms tightly around her back and shoulders, pulling her even closer. I wanted to hug her as tightly as my muscles would allow.

As I lay there, anchored to the childhood bed by my sister's weight, a profound, architectural realization clicked into place inside my brain.

I remembered the exact moment Delores had looked at me and said she wanted to spend the rest of her life with me. Back then, it had felt like a trap—a heavy, suffocating weapon designed to enforce my isolation.

But lying here with Rachel, the concept of a lifelong partnership didn't feel like a weapon. It felt like a shield.

The neurotypical world had endless, rigid rules about romance, boundaries, and biological separation, but none of those rules applied to the survival mechanics of our pack. Rachel was the center of my universe. She had been since I was a small boy. We understood each other's sensory needs, we communicated without speaking, and she was the only person who could manually override my trauma responses.

She wasn't just my biological sister. I realized, with absolute, unshakeable clarity, that she was my partner. She was the woman I wanted to spend the rest of my life with. I held her tightly, completely embracing the thought, utterly apathetic to the societal norms that would judge it. I had found my life partner, and she had been right beside me this entire time.

I shifted my gaze to the left. Sleeping soundly next to us, her arm still draped over Rachel's side, was my best friend.

Ashley Raybourn.

Technically, she was still legally Ashley Raybourn Lawson. But we were going to fix that today.

Suddenly, the digital alarm clock on the nightstand clicked. Instead of a harsh, blaring buzzer, the FM radio engaged.

A massive, bright 1980s synthesizer riff poured out of the tiny speaker, instantly backed by a booming, gated-reverb snare drum. It was *The Family* by The Stardust Engine.

I didn't reach over to turn it off. I let the music wash over the room, listening to Ryan and Cassidy sing the unapologetic anthem they had fought so hard to reclaim.

Beside me, Rachel stirred, letting out a quiet, raspy hum as the 130 BPM rhythm woke her up. Ashley shifted, her red, exhausted eyes slowly opening as she squeezed Rachel's waist.

As we lay there in the tangled safety of the bed, the chorus blasted through the small radio speaker:

*We built a fortress out of the fire!* *We took the engine and we pushed it higher!* *Through the static and the heavy weather* *We proved that family always sticks together!*

That single line—*family always sticks together*—snapped another piece of my internal logic into place.

I had always called Ashley my "honorary" sister because we didn't share blood. But as I looked at her bruised arm and remembered the desperate digital SOS she had sent me, the caveat felt completely unnecessary. She wasn't an honorary sister. She was my sister. Blood didn't build the fortress; survival did.

"Time to get up," Rachel whispered, pushing herself up slightly so her weight shifted off my chest.

"Yeah," Ashley mumbled, rubbing her eyes. "Dad said he was calling the lawyer right at eight."

We slowly untangled ourselves from the center of the bed.

The neurotypical rules of privacy had officially been eradicated for Ashley, too. She didn't ask us to turn around or step into the hallway. The trauma of her isolation had completely rewired her boundaries; she just desperately needed her pack at her side.

We opened our duffel bags on the floor. The three of us stood shoulder-to-shoulder in the small bedroom, stripping off our sleepwear and changing into our clothes for the day in comfortable, absolute vulnerability.

Once our armor was on, I grabbed my glasses and led the way to the bedroom door. We had a fortress to protect, and Ashley needed a lawyer immediately. We walked out into the hallway and headed downstairs to join Jim and Joan on the front lines.
