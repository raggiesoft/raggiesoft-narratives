We walked down the carpeted stairs of 1802 Rich Court, moving as a single, unified block. The smell of freshly brewed coffee and the low, murmuring voices of Jim and Joan drifted out from the kitchen.

When we walked in, Jim was standing by the counter, holding a mug of coffee and already dressed in a sharp, business-casual button-down shirt. He looked like a man preparing to go to war. Joan was at the table, setting down three massive ceramic bowls and a variety of cereal boxes.

There were no elaborate omelets or heavy breakfasts today. Joan knew exactly what her daughter's frayed nervous system needed: the frictionless, sugary comfort of childhood cereal.

"Sit down, eat," Jim instructed gently, his eyes softening the moment he saw Ashley. "The lawyer's office opens at eight. I want us out the door in twenty minutes."

We dropped into the kitchen chairs. Rachel and I flanked Ashley, naturally maintaining the physical perimeter even at the breakfast table. I poured a bowl of cereal, the mundane, mechanical action feeling strangely grounding after the chaos of yesterday.

Ashley stared into her bowl, her spoon pushing the brightly colored loops around in the milk. Her face was a complex map of conflicting emotions. The grief of her marriage officially ending was written in the dark circles under her eyes, but beneath that exhaustion was a profound, undeniable spark of relief. She was devastated that her life had been derailed, but she was incredibly glad to be out of David's suffocating grip. It was time to get her life back on track.

"Dad," Ashley started, her voice raspy and hesitant. She looked up at Jim. "I know we're going to the lawyer, but... how is this actually going to work? I've looked this up before when I was trapped in that RV. The law in the Commonwealth of Virginia says we have to be legally separated for a full year before a judge will even grant a divorce. He's not going to let me go for a year."

Jim set his coffee mug down on the counter. The soft, parental warmth in his eyes completely vanished, replaced by a cold, calculating legal precision.

"You're thinking of a *no-fault* divorce, sweetheart," Jim corrected her, his voice a low, steady rumble. "A no-fault divorce is for two people who simply drifted apart and mutually agree to separate. Yes, that requires a one-year waiting period."

Jim stepped over to the table and rested his heavy hands on the back of Ashley's chair.

"But we are not filing a no-fault divorce," Jim continued. "We are filing a *fault* divorce. You have an Urgent Care medical report documenting physical battery. And more importantly, you have undeniable, high-definition audiovisual proof of him committing adultery in your home. In Virginia, adultery is grounds for an immediate divorce. There is no one-year waiting period. We are going to drop a nuclear bomb on his life, and the judge is going to sever this marriage instantly."

Joan reached across the table, covering Ashley's trembling hand with her own. "He doesn't get to control the timeline anymore, Ash. We are taking the wheel today."

Ashley let out a long, shaky exhale, the tension bleeding out of her shoulders as her father's tactical reality settled over her. The trap was actually open.

She turned her head, looking at me, and then looking over at Rachel. Her eyes were pleading, filled with the vulnerable terror of a Sentinel who was about to walk into a sterile, intimidating legal battlefield.

"Will you two come with me?" Ashley asked softly. "Into the office? I don't want to sit in that room without you guys."

Rachel didn't even blink. She swallowed her bite of cereal and looked directly at Ashley. "We're not going anywhere, Ash. We are your perimeter."

"We're right beside you," I confirmed immediately. "Whatever you need."

Jim nodded in absolute approval. He understood that while he was providing the legal artillery, Rachel and I were providing the psychological armor.

"Alright," Jim said, checking his watch. "Finish your breakfast. The five of us are heading downtown."
