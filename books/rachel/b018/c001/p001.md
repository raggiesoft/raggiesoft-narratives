The heavy, mechanical thud of the landing gear deploying echoed through the cabin of the aircraft, followed minutes later by the jarring, high-speed screech of the tires hitting the tarmac at Norfolk International Airport.

As the massive jet engines roared into reverse thrust, pressing us firmly against our seatbelts, a complicated, heavy mixture of emotions crashed into my exhausted processor. I stared out the thick plastic window at the dark, familiar runway lights of Virginia.

"We’re home," I whispered, the words feeling dry in my throat as the plane began its slow taxi toward the gate.

Now that the cross-country flight was officially over, the geographic reality was finally starting to sink in. We were back on the East Coast. The suffocating, toxic nightmare of Kent was permanently behind me, and I was free. But as the adrenaline of the escape faded, a hollow, pulling ache settled deep in my chest. Part of me desperately wanted to turn the plane around. I wanted to be standing back in the damp Seattle air, pulling Jessica and Kristin into a massive, three-way pack hug.

"You’re torn," Rachel commented softly.

I didn't even have to explain. My little sister was twenty years old now, and after everything we had survived, her ability to read my nervous system was flawless. She unbuckled her seatbelt the second the captain turned off the overhead chime, leaned over the armrest, and wrapped her arm securely around my shoulders.

"You wish all of us were gathered in Renton right now, don’t you?" Rachel asked, her dark eyes completely empathetic.

I let out a slow, shaky breath, leaning my weight into her grounding pressure. "Yeah. I do."

On one hand, the relief of being back in Virginia—where the air smelled like salt and the geography made sense to my brain—was immense. But the fracture of our pack was agonizing. The memory of standing at the SeaTac security checkpoint twelve hours earlier was still burning in my mind. I had completely broken down, crying into Kristin’s shoulder while Jessica held my hand, terrified to sever the physical tether that had saved my life. They had both promised to keep in touch every single day, but the distance felt insurmountable.

Kristin and Rachel's lease down in Lakewood was up. Kristin was preparing for her new life at a public university in Washington, and I desperately needed to reset my life on familiar ground. I had never truly belonged in the Pacific Northwest; it had always been a temporary bunker.

My mind kept reminding me of the mathematical truth: Virginia Beach was my home. That’s where I was. I was home.

By the time we navigated the crowded terminal and claimed our heavy suitcases from the baggage carousel, the exhaustion was absolute. We pushed through the sliding glass doors into the chilly, damp March night air.

Mom was waiting for us at the curb in her sedan.

We loaded the bags into the trunk, and the second we climbed into the back seat, Mom's nervous, maternal energy completely filled the cabin. As she steered the car onto the interstate, she launched into a rapid-fire, highly administrative monologue.

"So, the closing paperwork is officially finalized," Mom explained, glancing at us in the rearview mirror. "We get the keys to the townhouse on Monday morning. I had my accountant look over everything, and doing it this way makes the most sense for tax purposes, Michael. We structured the deed so that..."

My brain instantly threw up a static wall. I couldn't process the financial variables. Between the time zone jump, the emotional devastation of leaving Jessica, and the sheer physical exhaustion of navigating two major airports, my executive function was completely offline. I just stared blankly at the back of her headrest.

Rachel sensed my system crashing. She didn't hesitate. She slid across the back seat, pressing her shoulder firmly against mine to anchor me, and stepped right into the line of fire to act as my shield.

"We are going home, Mom," Rachel interrupted, her voice dropping into a firm, pragmatic register that sounded exactly like Jessica. "It’s late, and we are both tired. The tax stuff can wait."

Mom blinked, slightly taken aback by the authoritative tone, but she nodded and quieted down.

Rachel turned her attention back to Mom, initiating a highly calculated logistical maneuver. "I think Michael and I can share a bed for one night if we’re going to be in the townhouse tomorrow anyway."

"Of course," Mom agreed softly, catching the exhaustion in our postures. "The spare room is set up."
