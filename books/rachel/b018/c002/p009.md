Mom’s sedan rolled to a stop alongside the curb of 3701 Canadian Arch just as April was turning to head back inside her unit. Mom rolled down the driver’s side window, leaning across the center console.

"April, before we head back," Mom called out, shifting straight into her administrative mode. "We need to get the tuition sorted. I want them fully registered before Monday."

April walked over to the car, resting her hands lightly on the window frame. She offered a warm, professional smile. "You don't need to do any paper forms, Ellen. I moved the entire registration system online. Just go to the academy's website and select the Adult Alumni tier. It covers the late Friday night closed-door sessions."

April recited the URL, and Mom quickly jotted it down on the back of a bank receipt from her purse.

"Got it," Mom confirmed. "I'll process the payment tonight."

"I'll see you both on Friday," April said, giving Rachel and me one last wave through the open window before walking back to her door.

Mom put the sedan in drive. The commute back to Mom's house was incredibly short. We navigated out of the townhouse complex, crossed over Buckner Boulevard, and drove the few minutes through the adjacent neighborhood until we pulled into the driveway of 3568 Criollo Drive.

The moment we walked through the front door, the excitement we had been containing completely bubbled over. Jimbo and Teddy were waiting for us in the foyer, their golden tails sweeping across the linoleum like metronomes. We dropped to our knees, burying our hands in their soft fur, completely ecstatic that the ghosts of the Dojang were dead. We were going back to the mats.

Mom didn't even take her coat off. She walked straight to the family computer in the den, booted it up, and navigated to the Lynnhaven Academy website. A few minutes of clicking and typing later, she hit print.

"Alright, it's official," Mom announced, holding up the confirmation page. "You are both fully registered, paid for, and on the roster. You return to the Dojang this Friday."

Rachel let out a quiet, raspy cheer, bumping her shoulder happily against mine.

But as the initial rush of the registration faded, the biological reality of the time zone shift crashed down on us. It was approaching nine o'clock on the East Coast, but our processors were completely drained.

"We need to go upstairs," I mumbled, pushing myself up from the floor and giving Jimbo one last, heavy pat on his golden side. Rachel stood up beside me, giving Teddy a final scratch behind the ears.

We walked to the bottom of the wooden staircase.

I looked up at the second floor, and a heavy, suffocating dread settled into my chest. The physical exertion of the day—the flights, the furniture store, the grocery shopping, and the emotional adrenaline of reuniting with April—had absolutely wrecked my lower body. My cerebral palsy was in full revolt. The spasticity in my calves felt like tight, burning cables of steel.

I grabbed the wooden banister with both hands, pulling my upper body weight forward, and forced my right foot onto the first tread. My knee trembled violently.

I dragged my left leg up to join it. It felt like I was wearing concrete boots.

Rachel, walking a step behind me, stopped. The joyful energy from the registration vanished, replaced by her sharp, hyper-observant radar. She watched me grit my teeth, my knuckles turning white on the railing as I fought a losing battle against gravity and my own misfiring neurology.

She didn't say a word, but I saw her dark eyes analyzing the biomechanics of my struggle. Her internal processor was actively logging the data: \*The stairs are getting harder for him. The physical toll is increasing.\* She stepped closer, hovering just behind me to act as a physical spotter, ready to catch my weight if my knees buckled.

It took two agonizing minutes to conquer the staircase.

When we finally reached the second-floor landing, I was panting, my back throbbing with a dull ache. We executed the neurotypical illusion for Mom, taking our separate turns in the hallway bathroom to brush our teeth before slipping into the spare bedroom.

Rachel pushed the door shut, and I reached out, twisting the lock with a solid \*click\*. The perimeter was secure.

But the room was miserable. Virginia weather was notoriously bipolar, and the coast was currently suffering through an unseasonably hot, humid March evening. The small spare bedroom, situated on the second floor, had trapped all the rising heat of the day. The air felt thick and stifling, completely devoid of any cross-breeze.

I sat heavily on the edge of the Full-sized bed, sweat already beading on the back of my neck. Rachel unzipped her canvas duffel bag on the floor, pulling out her oversized sleep shirt, while I stared at my flannel pajama pants.

The thought of putting heavy cotton over my overheated, exhausted skin was a sensory nightmare.

Rachel looked at her sleep shirt, then looked up at me. She felt the suffocating heat of the room, too.

"Screw this," Rachel rasped quietly, tossing the sleep shirt back into her duffel bag. "We're both overheating."

"Agreed," I sighed, kicking my shoes off.

The neurotypical rules of modesty simply did not exist inside our Base Camp. We didn't turn our backs, and we didn't look away. We were adults, and our shared physical space was built on absolute, unfiltered vulnerability that proved our environment was entirely threat-free.

We both stripped off our sweaty day clothes entirely, abandoning them in a pile near our bags.

I pulled the heavy comforter and the top sheet completely back, leaving only the cool, fitted sheet exposed. I climbed into the small, Full-sized bed, my bare skin instantly grateful for the cooler temperature of the mattress. Rachel climbed in right beside me.

Because the bed was so small, the geometry forced us perfectly together. We closed the distance immediately. Rachel pressed her side flush against my ribs, throwing her arm over my chest and tangling her legs with mine. I wrapped my arms around her, pulling her into a tight, skin-to-skin cuddle.

Without the barrier of heavy pajamas, the physical grounding was absolute. The direct contact radiated heat away from our cores while providing the massive, deep-pressure compression our nervous systems demanded.

I rested my chin against her dark hair, my racing heart rate instantly decelerating as I felt her pulse beat steadily against my side. The exhaustion of the day finally pulled me under. The stairs had been a brutal hurdle, and the heat was stifling, but lying there completely unfiltered and anchored to my sister, I fell asleep in minutes.
