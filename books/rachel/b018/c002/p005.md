The heavy, suffocating exhaustion of the morning’s shopping marathon finally burned off after a deep, two-hour nap in the spare bedroom.

When Rachel and I trudged back downstairs, our neurodivergent batteries were successfully recharged. Mom was waiting in the kitchen, her car keys already in hand.

"Since we have a few hours before dinner, I thought we could take a quick drive," Mom suggested, smiling as she grabbed her purse. "I know the lawyer doesn't hand over the official keys until Monday morning, but I figured you two might want to actually see the outside of your new townhouse in person."

Rachel’s eyes instantly lit up. She bumped her shoulder against my arm. "Yes. We want to see it."

We piled into the back seat of the sedan, the familiar, comforting weight of the dogs absent since they were staying home to rest. Mom navigated the Virginia Beach traffic with ease. As we drove down Buckner Boulevard, my analytical brain was actively mapping the geography, noting the traffic lights and intersections.

"Look," Rachel said, pointing out the passenger window as we approached a massive, sprawling campus directly to our right.

"Community College of Hampton Roads," I read the large brick sign aloud, a profound sense of relief washing over me.

"It’s literally right across the street from your new neighborhood," Mom confirmed, clicking the turn signal to pull into the residential complex.

Rachel and I exchanged a triumphant look. The logistics were flawless. We had already agreed to start classes at CCHR in the fall, and knowing that our campus was physically located just across Buckner Boulevard meant our daily commute would be entirely free of stressful highway driving or complicated transit routes. The perimeter was incredibly tight.

Mom steered the sedan through the quiet, tree-lined streets of the townhouse community, finally pulling to a stop alongside the curb.

"Here we are," Mom announced, putting the car in park. "3701 Canadian Arch. You have the end unit."

I looked out the window at the two-story brick and vinyl townhouse. It was perfect. Being on the end unit meant we only shared one single, structural drywall boundary with a neighbor, minimizing the unpredictable noise of the outside world.

As we stepped out of the car to get a better look, the low, rhythmic hum of a lawnmower caught my attention.

I looked over at the adjacent unit—3703 Canadian Arch. Someone was pushing a push-mower across the tiny, meticulously kept square of front grass. The person was wearing faded jeans, a plain gray t-shirt, and had a pair of protective safety glasses pushed up into their hair.

The engine of the lawnmower cut off. The person turned around to wipe their forehead, looking up at the sound of our car doors shutting.

My heart completely stopped in my chest.

Rachel gasped loudly, both of her hands flying up to cover her mouth.

It was April.

For the second time in a single day, the universe threw a massive, reality-altering coincidence directly into our path. April stood on the grass of 3703, her eyes darting from Mom’s sedan, to the end unit at 3701, and finally landing squarely on us.

"Michael? Rachel?" April asked, utterly bewildered, dropping the handle of the lawnmower.

We didn't hesitate. We sprinted across the small patch of grass separating the two properties. The grocery store hug had been restrained by the public aisles, but here, standing in the late afternoon sun, we held nothing back. We collided with her, pulling our Sabomnim into a fierce, suffocatingly tight, three-way pack hug right there on the lawn.

April let out a breathless, ringing laugh, wrapping her arms around our backs and squeezing us with that familiar, heavy, grounding pressure.

"What are you doing here?" April asked, pulling back just enough to look at us, her eyes wide with shock.

Rachel pointed a trembling, ecstatic finger directly at the front door of our end unit. "3701 Canadian Arch," she rasped, her voice vibrating with pure joy. "Mom bought it for us. We move in on Monday."

April looked at the brick facade of 3701, and then turned her head to look at the front door of her own house at 3703. The mathematical reality clicked into place.

"You're my new neighbors," April breathed, completely stunned. "I live right here. We share a wall."

The absolute magnitude of that fact hit my nervous system like a soothing wave of gravity. The townhouse wasn't just a home anymore; it was an impenetrable fortress. The only person in the world who shared a structural boundary with our Base Camp was the exact same woman who had protected us from the darkest horrors of our childhood.

"You're always allowed to visit," Rachel declared instantly, laying down the highest possible level of pack clearance. "You don't even have to knock. You are on the approved list."

"Forever," I confirmed fiercely. "The door is always open to you."

April’s eyes shined with unshed tears. She understood exactly how sacred that invitation was. To neurodivergent adults who guarded their environment with military precision, granting someone unquestioned access to the sanctuary was the ultimate proof of absolute trust.

"I accept," April smiled warmly. She looked past us to Mom, who was watching the reunion with a happy, tearful smile from the sidewalk.

"Ellen," April called out. "Why don't you leave these two with me for a bit? Since they don't get their keys until Monday, I can bring them inside my unit. Our floor plans are exact mirror images. They can get a perfect idea of what the inside of their house looks like."

"That would be wonderful, April," Mom agreed, waving her keys. "You kids catch up. Just call my cell phone when you're ready for me to come pick you up for dinner."

Mom climbed back into the sedan and pulled away, leaving the three of us standing on the grass.

"Come on in," April smiled, gesturing to her front door.

We followed her inside 3703 Canadian Arch. The moment the door clicked shut, the ambient noise of the neighborhood vanished. April’s home was a true sanctuary—minimalist, impeccably clean, with soft lighting and comfortable, sturdy furniture. It smelled faintly of jasmine and clean linen.

"So," April said, gesturing for us to sit on her plush living room sofa while she grabbed three bottles of cold water from the kitchen. "If this is a mirror image, your front door is on the opposite side, and your staircase will be right there."

Rachel and I sat shoulder-to-shoulder on the couch, our eyes tracking the architecture. We could already see our Base Camp taking shape. The living room was spacious enough for the old Delaney Street sofa, and the kitchen flow was perfectly logical.

April sat down in the armchair across from us, handing us the water.

"It has been a very, very long time," April said quietly, her professional demeanor melting into the deep, maternal warmth she had always carried for us. "The last time I saw you, Michael was driving you to the airport in 2001, and the world was falling apart."

"It fell apart," I admitted, taking a sip of the cold water. "Rachel went to Washington to live with Jessica. I stayed here to finish high school. The divorce was..." I swallowed hard, the ghost of the yelling still an unpleasant memory. "It was bad."

"But we survived it," Rachel added, her voice steady and proud. She reached out, lacing her fingers through mine. "Jessica bought an apartment in Federal Way. She retrofitted it. And when Michael turned eighteen, he flew out. We spent the last few years in Washington, building our own routines."

April listened intently, her eyes reflecting a profound, validating respect. We spent the next two hours filling in the massive gaps. We told her about Jessica’s success at Quantum, the tactical brilliance of our older sister’s planning, and how we eventually realized that the Pacific Northwest would never truly feel like home. We explained the decision to return to the coast, to build our permanent perimeter right here in Virginia Beach.

"And what about you, Sabomnim?" I asked. "You said the Academy is thriving?"

April smiled, her posture straightening with quiet pride. "It is. I finally broke completely away from Master Ray's shadow years ago. I built the Lynnhaven Academy of Seondu on the exact principles we talked about in the garage. No ego. No blunt-force conditioning. Just pure biomechanics, internal control, and protecting the spirit."

She looked at both of us, her gaze intense and focused.

"I meant what I said in the grocery store today," April told us. "You two are the reason I built it. You were my Founding Students. Friday night is the Alumni Class. It’s just going to be a handful of adults who grew up on my mats. I want you there."

"We'll be there," Rachel promised, her grip tightening on my hand.

We sat in the quiet safety of April’s townhouse, the late afternoon sun casting long shadows across the carpet. The fear of the Dojang had been permanently locked away. We had our pack, we had our home, and we had our teacher back. As I looked around the mirror-image of our future living room, I knew the universe had finally aligned the math perfectly in our favor.
