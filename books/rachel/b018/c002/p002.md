Breakfast was finished, and Mom drove us to one of the massive, warehouse-style furniture showrooms on Virginia Beach Boulevard. The sheer scale of the store was a bit overwhelming, but moving as a unified pack kept our sensory gauges level.

A sales associate wearing a branded polo shirt quickly approached us.

"My son and daughter are moving into a townhouse I bought for them while they attend college," Mom explained proudly. "They will need everything: living room, dining room, bedroom, office."

I stepped forward to handle the spatial logistics. "It is a three-bedroom: two smaller rooms and one big bedroom. We were thinking that we could each take the smaller room, and set up the big room as a guest room." I forced a polite, brotherly laugh to sell the script to the associate. "I love my sister and everything, but we still need our privacy. Rachel, did you remember the drawing we made?"

My sister reached into her pocket and unfolded a piece of graph paper where we had meticulously sketched out the floor plan.

"It is a two-story townhouse, and we are very excited to start this thing called being adults," Rachel told the associate. We migrated over to the living room section, immediately scanning the modular couches.

"Do you both want the old house sofa and dining room set?" Mom chimed in, walking up behind us.

Rachel stopped dead in her tracks. A sudden, heavy wave of emotion washed over her face, tears instantly shining in her dark eyes. "Sure," she whispered, her voice trembling with the profound weight of our history. "The one that Jessica slept on after the accident?"

"Yes, that one. We still have it in storage," Mom replied softly.

We didn't need to discuss it. That sofa wasn't just furniture; it was the original Base Camp. It was the exact spot where our pack had survived the darkest months of 1995.

Working with the sales associate, we color-matched a new entertainment center, a coffee table, and an end table to complement the old, floral-print sofa. We then transitioned into the home office section.

"I lived with our older sister for a short time, and her desk and computer setup allows her to not have to come into an office if she so chooses," I explained to the associate, envisioning the dual-desk command center we needed to build. "While we aren’t there yet, we will each want a desk to do homework. We plan to start at CCHR in the fall."

As we walked toward the bedroom galleries, Rachel pulled out the budget sheet, running the math on what we had spent so far. She bumped her shoulder against my arm, stopping in the aisle.

"Want to just share the one bedroom?" Rachel asked softly. "We could take turns in the bathroom for privacy."

"Sure," I replied, smiling at the brilliant simplicity of dropping the act when no one was listening. "This bathroom is attached to the bedroom, and I think we will manage privacy. Two dressers, and let’s look at beds."

We found a massive, sturdy King-sized bed that we both immediately loved.

"We should find a small bed for one of the other rooms," Rachel stated, maintaining her volume. Then, seeing a few other shoppers browsing in the next aisle, she stepped into my personal space, cupped her hand around my ear, and dropped her voice to a fierce, private whisper.

"I love you and everything, but you are my brother. Let’s find a small bed for you," Rachel recited the script aloud for anyone eavesdropping. Then, her breath warm against my ear, she delivered the absolute, unyielding truth of our sanctuary: "I promise we will share the King and sleep naked together. It is just that we are in public, and the rest of the world will not want us together."

I knew exactly what she meant. It wasn't about romance; it was about the absolute, unfiltered vulnerability that proved our environment was entirely threat-free. It was about stripping off the heavy, restrictive armor of the outside world and relying purely on skin-to-skin sensory grounding.

"Understood," I whispered back.

The associate returned, and I smoothly resumed the performance. "Yes, my sister and I are about to move in together. She picked out a bed and dresser for her room, and now I need a bed and dresser for my room."

Eventually, we had a flawless layout secured and reunited with Mom at the registers. We were well within budget.

"I start back up with Spot on Wednesday of next week, the start of the pay period," Rachel noted, her logistical brain pivoting to employment. "Maybe you should find a job too. How about the grocery store next to me? I don’t think siblings are supposed to work in the same establishment."

"How about I take you both grocery shopping next?" Mom offered as she signed the final receipt.

"Take us to the grocery store next to Spot, and I’ll fill out an application while there," I agreed. I thought about the HR policies for a moment. "If I do fill out an application for Spot as well, I will disclose that you are my sister."

"We probably should stop at Spot so that I can introduce myself to the manager," Rachel decided as we walked out to the car. "Let’s go there first, in case we get perishables at the grocery store. Yeah, there is a place you can say that you’re related to a current employee if you fill out an application. Also fill out one for the grocery store."

We pulled into the sprawling parking lot of Spot. The bright, familiar red-themed retail store was a comforting, standardized environment for Rachel. She found a worker near the front registers.

"Could I speak to the LOD, please?" Rachel asked, using the precise internal corporate terminology.

A few moments later, a middle-aged man in a red polo walked up.

"Good morning, I am Rachel Ragsdale, and I am the team member transferring in from Washington state," she introduced herself with crisp professionalism. "My start date is not this Wednesday but next Wednesday. I think my manager set it up so that I start here on the first day of the next pay period."

"Welcome to Virginia Beach, Rachel. My name is Tim, and I am the general manager of this Spot," he smiled, extending a hand. "Yes, I was the one who spoke to Harvey about your transfer. What brings you all the way to Virginia?"

Rachel didn't hesitate. She reached out, looping her arm securely through mine and pulling me flush against her side. "I came home to be with my brother, and our mother lives here in Virginia Beach also."

"Alright," Tim nodded. "I have not made the schedule yet, but please check back no later than Tuesday of next week for your times. Are there any availability concerns that I need to know about?"

"Probably not for the summer, however, I do intend to take classes at CCHR in the fall. We can talk about that closer to the start of the semester, since it is only May right now," Rachel replied sensibly.

Tim’s radio suddenly squawked with an urgent call, and he excused himself.

With employment secured, Rachel and I hit the aisles to pick up the supplies we needed for the townhouse. We started in the domestics department.

It was just the two of us in the quiet aisle. Rachel let out a long, happy breath. "We are sharing a King-sized bed. Yes, I want to share a bed with you." She turned and pulled me into a fierce, deep-pressure hug right there among the pillows, thrilled that the logistical lies were over.

We turned our attention to the bedding displays.

"I like this one," I said, pointing to a dark, expansive comforter.

"Astronomy!" Rachel squealed, her voice buzzing with excitement. "Remember when we had a telescope as kids?"

"I do, and I would love to get one again," I smiled as she eagerly pulled the heavy, star-patterned comforter off the shelf and dropped it into our red shopping cart.

"You picked the comforter, so I want to pick the sheets," she declared.

"Okay, that’s only fair," I conceded.

She scanned the shelves, her hands running over the different fabrics to verify their sensory safety, before pulling out a set of rich, deep forest green sheets.

We gathered up towels and organizers for the bathrooms before migrating to the home office section.

"I have a crazy idea," Rachel said, her eyes lighting up as she looked at the desk lamps. "One of the little bedrooms can be our desks for doing homework, and I was thinking that we could save up for a small bed for the other room if we have guests."

"The tactical decoy room," I grinned. "I love it."
