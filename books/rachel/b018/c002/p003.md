Mom was waiting for us with a cart full of cleaning supplies as we made our way to the checkout lanes.

"Groceries next?" Rachel asked, dropping our bedding into the trunk of the car.

"Yeah, we should. I am kind of getting hungry," I admitted, my stomach rumbling.

"We need bus passes," Rachel reminded me, her mental checklist never missing a beat.

"The customer service desk at the grocery store has them," I confirmed as we walked next door. "It’s thirty days from the day of first use."

"I remember, I think," my sister replied as we grabbed a shopping cart and headed into the produce section. "Now, this is our first time properly shopping for groceries together. Let’s think about what we both like to eat. I seem to remember that we both shared a lot of common interests in food."

We spent the next forty minutes meticulously filling the cart, agreeing on the exact brands of peanut butter, the right textures for snacks, and loading up on generic cola. Once we had everything we needed, including toiletries, we wheeled the cart up to the customer service desk.

"Two thirty-day bus passes, please," I told the clerk, handing over the cash.

As the clerk processed the transaction, a woman in line at the adjacent register turned around to grab a magazine from the rack.

She was wearing casual civilian clothes—faded denim jeans and a simple, comfortable sweater—but the way she carried her posture was instantly, undeniably familiar. It was the effortless, grounded stillness of perfect biomechanical control.

I froze, the change slipping from my fingers onto the counter.

"Sabomnim?" I gasped out, the old, revered title bypassing my adult brain completely.

April turned her head. For a fraction of a second, her eyes scanned right past us, looking down toward the height she expected ten-year-old Michael and eight-year-old Rachel to be. Then, she looked up.

Her jaw literally dropped.

"Michael? Rachel?" April breathed, abandoning her groceries entirely.

Rachel let out a sharp, joyful noise. We didn't care that we were standing in the middle of a crowded grocery store. The three of us collided in a massive, fierce, three-way hug.

The physical geometry of the embrace was profoundly shocking. When we last held her, we had to bury our faces into her waist. Now, at twenty-two and nineteen years old, we were looking our former protector directly in the eye. I was actually an inch taller than her.

"Look at you both," April whispered, pulling back just enough to grip our shoulders, her eyes shining with absolute, unfiltered pride and unshed tears. "You’re so tall. You grew up."

"We just moved back to Virginia Beach," Rachel rasped, her voice thick with emotion. "We bought a townhouse. We're home."

"I am so incredibly happy to hear that," April smiled, wiping a tear from her cheek. She looked us up and down, reading the solid, unbreakable bond still holding us together.

"Is the Academy still open?" I asked eagerly, my heart hammering against my ribs. "The Lynnhaven Academy of Seondu?"

"It is," April beamed, the pride in her Dojang evident. "I’m still running it. Still the *Sunhoe Sabomnim*, though I only teach the kids' classes now. But I do host a special, closed-door Alumni Class for my grown-up kids who have aged out of the youth ranks."

She looked at us, her expression shifting into that familiar, authoritative, deeply protective cadence.

"When you two are settled and ready," April offered, "you are welcome on my mats. And if you want, you can join the youth classes as *Jo-Kyo-Nim*—Assistant Instructors. My kids would be lucky to have you."

Rachel’s eyes widened. "But we can't spar. The doctors said—"

"I know exactly what the doctors said," April interrupted gently, her voice an absolute, unyielding shield. "I know you will never be medically cleared for contact sports. But *Seondu* isn't a sport. It's an art. I will continue to modify the curriculum for you, just like I did in the garage."

April tilted her head, her analytical brain already running the training schedule. "Where did you both leave off before the custody arrangement separated you in 2001?"

"Red belt," I answered immediately, the memory of our last rank burned into my brain. "Red belt with a solid black stripe. We were right on the edge."

April’s eyes lit up with a fierce, brilliant spark.

"Then we have an Il-Dan test to prepare for," April declared quietly, making a solemn, unbreakable vow right there in the grocery store aisle. "No sparring. No physical contact. Just Jeongsin and Tongje. You are going to earn your Nam-saek tti."

Rachel reached over and grabbed my hand, squeezing it with bruising force. The trauma of the Dojang was permanently dead. We were going back to the mats, and we were finally going to finish what we started.
