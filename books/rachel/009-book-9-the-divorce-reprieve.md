# Book 9: The Divorce Reprieve

## Chapter 1: The Mall (Friday, August 17<sup>th</sup>, 2001)

### Part 1: Vacation from Parents – 10:30 AM

The heavy trunk of the sedan slammed shut, the metallic thud echoing over the roar of jet engines at the Norfolk International Airport drop-off curb.

"Now remember, you two," Mom said, pulling the handle of her rolling suitcase up. "No visitors. Except maybe your father, if he wants to stop by."

"We know," Rachel huffed, her hands shoved deep into the pockets of her jacket. We had gone over this exact list of rules for the millionth time since Mom announced her two-week work trip.

"Don’t worry, Mom. No visitors," I said, leaning against the side of the car. "Rachel and I will be fine. And we probably won’t see Dad. He never seems to have time for us beyond demanding that you hold up your end of the bargain."

The "bargain" was the nightmare hanging over our heads. The legal separation paperwork had been filed back in August, and the negotiated terms were a death sentence to our ecosystem: Dad would take Rachel, and Mom would take me. They were legally going to split us up. The only thing keeping Rachel in the house right now was the logistical reprieve that Dad was still renting a single room and had nowhere to put her.

"I want to stay home where I have a bedroom," Rachel stammered, her voice suddenly spiking with panic. She stomped her foot against the concrete curb, the sudden shift in routine and the mention of Dad causing her anxiety to flare. "I don't want to go with him!"

I immediately stepped forward, wrapping my arms tightly around her shoulders, applying the deep, firm pressure I knew she needed.

"Don’t worry, you are staying home with me," I said, my voice low and steady to anchor her. "He doesn't have a place. You aren't going anywhere."

Mom offered a tight, exhausted smile, gave us both a quick hug, and disappeared through the sliding glass doors of the terminal.

It was just Rachel and me.

I walked around to the driver's side and slid behind the wheel. Back in August, the idea of driving had been a paralyzing sensory nightmare. But the threat of the divorce, the absolute necessity of not relying on our parents for survival, had forced my hand. I had spent the winter meticulously memorizing the traffic patterns, turning the terrifying variables of the road into rigid math until I got my license. I was the designated protector now, and protectors needed to drive.

"So, what did you want to do?" I asked, putting the car into gear. "Within reason, of course."

"Junk food," she giggled, the panic completely vanishing as we pulled away from the airport curb. "Two weeks of just you and me!"

"Yes. You and me," I replied.

My baby sister’s dark eyes completely lit up at the remark. The Delaney Street house was entirely ours for fourteen days. No screaming, no bitter arguments, no walking on eggshells.

"I wanna sleep on the sofa bed with you," she whimpered happily as we merged onto the interstate, the hum of the tires creating a soothing white noise in the cabin. "And I want to pet doggies all day long!"

When we finally unlocked the front door, Archie and Baby were practically vibrating with excitement. We dropped our coats on the floor and spent the next twenty minutes sitting on the linoleum, taking turns scratching Baby’s heavy gray ears and playing tug-of-war with Archie.

Rachel leaned heavily against my shoulder, her breathing perfectly regulated by the familiar weight of the dogs. "Will you take me to the mall later? I want to do teenager things and have lunch at the food court with you."

"We can go to the mall later," I agreed, though my brain immediately started running calculations. "But Mom didn’t leave us a lot of money. It has to last us the two weeks."

My sister pouted slightly, looking up at me. "How much did Mom give us?"

I could almost see the math forming in her eyes, knowing that whatever number I said, it wasn't going to be enough to buy the absolute, unlimited freedom she was craving.

### Part 2: Afternoon at the Mall – 1:00 PM

The Emerald Creek Mall was a massive, sprawling brick fortress. On a Friday afternoon, it was already vibrating with a low-frequency hum of activity, but I knew the layout well enough to avoid the worst of the friction. Instead of fighting the traffic at the main department store entrances, I navigated the ring road all the way to the back perimeter, deliberately aiming for the hidden glass elevator that bypassed the main concourse and delivered you straight to the second-floor food court.

"Suppose we should go straight upstairs and grab some lunch," I said, pulling the car into a parking spot near the elevator doors.

"Yes, please!" she squeaked, unbuckling her seatbelt, excited that her big brother was able to drive her to her favorite places.

I killed the engine, feeling a quiet, internal surge of pride. I had driven us here completely independently. My cerebral palsy made the physical and mental demands of driving exhausting, but I was keeping her safe.

"Where are we going to eat?" she asked as we stepped out into the cool spring air.

"We’ll pick something out, I guess," I said, not wanting to disappoint her. I was profoundly grateful that we got this reprieve in the divorce proceedings that were intended to separate us. The "bargain" still dictated that she belonged to Dad and I belonged to Mom, and I wanted my kid sister to know that staying with me was always the best option. "What are you in the mood for?"

It wasn’t a long walk to the glass elevator doors, but the moment we stepped inside the building, the sensory overload of the mall hit us. The echoing music, the smell of pretzels, the sheer volume of overlapping data to process—it was a lot.

Rachel immediately stepped close to my side, took my arm, and wrapped it firmly around her own shoulders. She leaned her weight into my side as we waited for the elevator to come downstairs, letting my physical presence ground her against the chaotic environment. I pulled her in tighter, happy that she actively sought out my protection in public without caring what anyone else thought.

The elevator bell chimed, dropping us off directly into the overwhelming smell of grease and sugar. We took care of business in the restroom first, establishing our baseline, and then started to wander the perimeter of the food court as we debated what to have.

"You know, I think this is the first time we’ve had quality brother-sister time together," I said. "It does feel nice to not have the pressures of parents breathing down our necks. Now, what are we hungry for?"

The choice seemed easy: one of the places had a smaller line, and the best part? Floats for our drinks.

"Mom would kill us if she found out we got the large floats," Rachel giggled, smiling as she looked into my eyes, so happy that the pressures of our parents divorcing were at least on pause.

We grabbed our tray, loading it up with a pair of hot dogs, the massive root beer floats, and a giant, salty soft pretzel to split down the middle. I handed the cashier the twenty-dollar bill I had brought, slipping the change—a five and three crumpled singles—into my pocket.

We found a quiet table near the edge of the food court balcony, facing away from the center of the room. Down below, the mall was a chaotic blur of echoing voices, bright neon signs, and rushing crowds. My brain started to buzz with the noise, the sheer volume of data threatening to overwhelm my system, but then Rachel leaned over, bumping her shoulder firmly against mine to grab her half of the pretzel. That simple, heavy pressure grounded me instantly.

After we finished our food, my sister really wanted to show me the stores. But thankfully, she had zero interest in the brightly lit, heavily perfumed clothing boutiques or the endless racks of shoes—the smells and the glaring lights were too abrasive for either of us. Instead, she steered me directly toward the large bookstore at the end of the concourse. The quiet, carpeted aisles were an absolute relief. We spent twenty minutes just standing shoulder-to-shoulder in the science fiction section, reading the backs of paperbacks and talking about the predictable, structured worlds inside them instead of the one falling apart at home.

When we finally wandered back out into the main mall, we passed the flashing lights of the arcade. An attract mode from a twin racing cabinet blared near the entrance, a synthesized voice belting out an incredibly cheesy, high-energy "Red-LLLIIINNNNEEEE!!!" melody.

"Want to?" I asked, pulling the five-dollar bill from my pocket.

Her eyes lit up. We fed the bill into the token machine and slid into the plastic bucket seats of the Redline racing cabinets. We spent the next half-hour aggressively bumping each other's pixelated stock cars off the track, mashing the manual shifters. She laughed louder than I had heard her laugh in months, the heavy, suffocating tension of Dad's anger completely vanishing from her posture. She was just a fourteen-year-old kid again.

As we headed toward the exit to go home, we passed a mechanical photo booth tucked under the escalators. I pulled the last three crumpled singles from my pocket.

"Come on," I said, nudging her toward the curtain.

We squeezed into the tiny, plastic-lined booth, sitting close together on the small stool. The enclosed space immediately acted as a sensory cocoon, blocking out the overwhelming lights and sounds of the mall. Before I could even feed the dollar bills into the slot, Rachel suddenly leaned her forehead against my shoulder. I felt a dampness soak through my shirt as she started to cry, but they weren't the heavy, anxious tears I had seen at the house. They were tears of pure, overwhelming relief.

"Thank you," she whispered, her voice trembling with joy. She pulled me into a fierce, tight hug, burying her face against my chest. "Thank you for just making things feel normal today."

I wrapped my arms around her, resting my chin on top of her head. "You don't have to thank me for that, Rachel."

She squeezed me tighter, her voice dropping to a vulnerable whisper. "If the divorce goes through the way they planned... if I have to go live with Dad and you stay with Mom..." She swallowed hard. "I don't want to do the usual silly faces for these pictures."

I understood exactly what she meant. For years, whenever we ended up in front of a camera, we had copied what other kids did—sticking out our tongues, making wide, exaggerated faces, following an invisible script because that was what neurotypical kids were expected to do to show they were having fun. But the threat of the divorce made all that masking feel impossible. It took too much energy to perform.

She looked up at me, her eyes red but shining with absolute sincerity. "I don't want to pretend. I just want to be close to you. If they separate us, this is exactly how I want to remember you. Safe, and just like this."

My chest ached at the very real threat of losing her to Dad's house, but I swallowed the lump in my throat and nodded. "Okay," I promised quietly. "No silly faces. Just us."

I reached over her shoulder and fed the last of our money into the machine. As the screen flashed its countdown, Rachel wrapped her arms firmly around my neck and leaned her head against my cheek. For all four flashes of the harsh camera light, we didn't perform or try to act funny. We just held on to each other, anchoring ourselves against the storm waiting for us at home.

We waited three minutes outside the curtain for the machine to spit out the strip of four glossy, slightly damp photos. I handed it to her. It was exactly what she had asked for: a tangible, physical piece of proof of our unwavering closeness, entirely unmasked and real.

She tucked the photo strip carefully into her pocket, linked her arm back through mine, and we walked out to the car.

### Part 3: Up All Night Party – 8:00 PM

By the time we got back to the quiet sanctuary of the Delaney Street house, the sun had set.

Rachel dropped her shopping bags on the kitchen counter and looked at me, her eyes heavy but excited. "Could we sleep together on the sofa? I just want to continue being at your side. Today was wonderful."

"Sure," I said, walking into the living room and looking at the folded couch. "But it will feel weird without Jessica here."

"Yeah, it will," Rachel agreed, her voice softening at the mention of our older sister. Then, a mischievous spark returned to her eyes. "I was thinking we could do all the things Mom and Dad would ground us for."

"Like staying up real late?" I asked, pulling the heavy cushions off the sofa and grabbing the metal handle to pull the mattress out.

"Are we going to stay up all night?" she asked, immediately rushing over to the windows and yanking the plastic blinds closed. "I don’t want anyone to see us up at night."

"Sure. Movies? Candy? Popcorn? Soda?" I offered, dropping the heavy blankets onto the mattress. "But we have to do it before trash day. If Mom sees all the junk food wrappers in the bin, we will be in so much trouble."

Rachel agreed, entirely committed to the logistics of our rebellion. She walked over to the wooden media cabinet and pulled out a worn, childhood-favorite VHS tape: Journey to the Sunlit Valley. She slid it into the VCR with a satisfying, mechanical clunk.

We changed into our pajamas, brought a massive bowl of popcorn and the remaining generic sodas into the living room, and climbed onto the mattress. We sat shoulder-to-shoulder, our legs tangled together under the quilt, leaning back against the couch frame.

The screen flickered, the white tracking lines clearing just in time for the previews. We both giggled as the classic, nostalgic Pizza Chalet commercial started playing. It was the one from the late eighties where the grumpy little brother is forced to endure his older sister’s birthday party, ultimately having to share his slice of pan pizza with a table full of giggling little girls. It felt incredibly grounding to watch a commercial we had memorized when we were still in elementary school.

"I like it when we snuggle up," Rachel whispered as the feature presentation started, shifting her weight so she was pressed flush against my side. The firm, continuous pressure was the perfect counter to the lingering sensory exhaustion of the mall. "So much better than fighting."

"Yeah," I agreed, resting my chin on the top of her head. "It is."

But as the movie progressed, our lighthearted, rebellious mood shifted.

We had completely forgotten just how incredibly dark and terrifying Donovan Vance’s animation could be. When the massive earthquake hit the prehistoric landscape, and Sprout’s mother bravely fought off the terrifying Razor-jaw to save her son, the atmosphere in the living room grew incredibly tense.

Then came the scene in the rain.

When the young, confused Sprout found his mother dying in the mud, and she told him that she would always be with him, even if he couldn't see her... the dam broke.

Rachel let out a sharp, ragged sob, burying her face directly into my chest. I felt my own throat lock up, hot tears immediately spilling over my eyelashes. As children, it was just a sad movie. But sitting there at sixteen and fourteen, with the very real threat of a divorce tearing our family apart and lawyers threatening to forcibly separate us, the theme of a shattered family hit entirely too close to home.

"How did they get away with this?" I whispered, my voice thick and shaking as I wrapped both arms tightly around Rachel's ribs, holding her together as she cried. "It's a kid's movie."

She didn't answer, just squeezed her hands into fists against my shirt. We held onto each other for dear life in the dark living room, letting the heavy, traumatizing weight of the Donovan Vance film wash over us. But because we were safely anchored to each other, the anxiety didn't spiral into a full meltdown.

We pressed on. We kept watching, drawing comfort from the predictable pressure of the blankets and our shared space, until Sprout and his friends finally emerged from the rocky tunnels and looked down upon the lush, green paradise of the Sunlit Valley. They had survived the destruction of their old world and found a new one, together.

By the time the end credits finally rolled, the grand plan of an all-night party had completely failed. The combination of the heavy, greasy food court lunch, the massive emotional release of the movie, and the sheer relief of the day caught up to us. We were both completely crashing.

"So much for late at night," Rachel said through a massive yawn, her eyes red and struggling to stay open. "I still want to share the sofa bed with you."

"Of course," I replied. I carefully extracted myself from the blankets, walked over to the TV, and hit the power button. The screen fizzled to black. I hit the rewind button on the VCR, listening to the loud, high-pitched whirring of the tape spinning backward.

The VCR clicked loudly, indicating the tape was done. I grumbled quietly, rubbing my tired eyes. "I suppose I should put it away before we forget. Then, we should get some sleep."

I slotted the tape back into its cardboard sleeve and turned off the living room lamp, plunging the room into darkness, illuminated only by the streetlights bleeding through the edges of the blinds. I crawled back under the heavy quilt.

Archie immediately jumped up onto the foot of the bed, circling twice before collapsing into a warm, heavy lump against our ankles. Baby let out a deep sigh from his spot on the rug nearby.

"Will you snuggle with me?" Rachel asked in the dark, her voice thick with sleep.

I slid over and put my arm firmly around my kid sister, pulling her securely against my chest. I made my choice right then and there. It felt infinitely better to hold onto her tightly than to argue with her. Lying there in the dark, surrounded by the rhythmic breathing of the dogs and the absolute quiet of the house, the terrifying math of the divorce didn't matter.

"I’ve enjoyed sharing with you these past few nights upstairs in my room," I whispered, the heavy pressure of her resting against me grounding my own racing thoughts. "But it is nicer having the larger bed this sofa offers. And the dogs are right here, too."

## Chapter 2: The Teenage Script (Saturday, August 18th, 2001)

### Part 1: The Elephant in the Room – 8:30 AM

I woke up to the heavy, rhythmic thumping of a dog’s tail hitting the side of the sofa bed.

I opened my eyes slowly. The living room was dim, illuminated only by the thin slivers of morning sunlight cutting through the closed plastic blinds. Archie was sitting on the rug right next to my head, staring at me with his one milky blue eye, his tail beating a steady tempo against the upholstery. At the foot of the bed, Baby let out a long, rumbling snore, still completely dead to the world.

I shifted my weight, my legs incredibly stiff from the awkward angle of the pull-out mattress. Rachel was still asleep next to me, her breathing deep and even, her forehead pressed against my shoulder.

For the first time in over a year, I didn't wake up bracing for the sound of our parents yelling in the kitchen. The Delaney Street house was entirely, beautifully silent.

"I'm awake," Rachel murmured, her voice muffled against my t-shirt. She didn't pull away; she just tightened her grip on the quilt.

"Morning," I whispered back, letting my head fall back onto the pillow.

We lay there in the quiet for a few more minutes, letting our brains slowly boot up and process the massive shift in reality. It was Saturday. Mom was in another state. Dad had moved into his rented room two days ago. For the next two weeks, we had absolute, unsupervised control over our physical environment.

"You know," I said quietly, staring up at the popcorn ceiling, "if this was a movie, or if we were normal teenagers, there’s a whole script for what we are supposed to be doing today."

Rachel shifted, propping herself up on her elbow to look down at me. Her hair was a messy, static-filled bird's nest. "What script?"

"The 'home alone for two weeks' script," I explained, tracing a pattern on the quilt with my thumb. "We're sixteen and fourteen. According to the rules of high school, the second Mom's plane took off, we were supposed to get on the rotary phone and invite fifty people over. We're supposed to be blasting loud music, breaking things, and sneaking our boyfriends and girlfriends in through the back door."

Rachel blinked, her face contorting in genuine horror at the idea. The thought of a house party—a massive influx of unpredictable strangers, loud, overlapping noise, and people touching our things—was a literal nightmare scenario.

"But I don't have a boyfriend," she stated, leaning heavily back against my side. "And you don't have a girlfriend."

"Exactly," I said. "We're failing the script."

"Good," she huffed. "The script sounds exhausting."

I smiled, resting my hand on her shoulder. "It is. But that's what people expect. Think about those kids looking at us at the mall yesterday. Society looks at two teenagers left completely alone in a house and assumes the worst. There's this massive elephant in the room about what teenagers are 'supposed' to do with zero supervision. They expect us to be reckless. They expect us to break all the big rules, or cross boundaries, or do something stupid just because nobody is here to stop us."

Rachel was quiet for a moment, processing the invisible neurotypical rules that always seemed to dictate how everyone else operated. She looked around our dark, quiet living room—the VCR still loaded with an animated dinosaur movie, the dogs sleeping peacefully, the heavy quilt pulled up to our chins.

"I don't want to cross boundaries or be reckless," Rachel said, her voice dropping to that serious, non-negotiable tone she used when establishing a permanent fact. "I just want to be right here. I want the blinds closed, and I want to know exactly where you are."

"I know," I promised, giving her shoulder a firm squeeze. "Me too."

The reality was that the "normal" teenage rebellion held zero appeal for us. We had spent the last twelve months surviving the chaotic, terrifying unpredictability of our parents' failing marriage. We didn't want the thrill of breaking the rules; we craved the absolute, iron-clad safety of a routine. Our rebellion wasn't throwing a wild party or sneaking people in; our rebellion was shutting the entire world out.

"So," Rachel said, her stomach letting out a loud, sudden growl. "Since we aren't throwing a massive party... what is our script for today?"

I ran the calculations for a perfect, low-demand Saturday. "We stay in our pajamas. We pour massive bowls of sugary cereal. We sit right back down on this sofa bed, and we watch whatever we want on TV without anyone telling us to go outside and be productive."

A wide, genuine smile broke across her face. "I like our script much better."

She pushed the heavy blankets back and climbed off the mattress. Archie immediately sprang up, grabbing his slobbery rope toy and trotting after her as she headed toward the kitchen. I sat up slowly, stretching my tight legs, the quiet morning air feeling lighter than it had in years.

The outside world could have their parties and their complicated social expectations. Inside the walls of the Delaney Street house, we were building our own rules.

### Part 2: The Morning Routine – 9:00 AM

As Rachel headed toward the kitchen, I stayed on the edge of the mattress for a moment, my bare feet resting on the cool living room carpet.

Watching her walk away, my brain snagged on the other, darker layer of that invisible societal script we had just talked about. I knew exactly how the outside world operated. High school was a vicious, rumor-obsessed environment, and society at large was no better. The world looked at a teenage boy and a teenage girl—even siblings—left entirely alone in a house, sharing a single bed, and immediately tried to force a messy, complicated, and entirely gross narrative onto the situation. Neurotypical people seemed obsessed with the idea that proximity automatically meant inappropriate tension.

The very concept was physically repulsive to both of us.

The outside world didn't understand the mechanics of our brains. They didn't understand that when Rachel sat in my lap or slept pressed against my ribs, it had absolutely zero to do with biology or romance. It was purely about gravity. She was a satellite spinning out of control in a terrifying, unpredictable world, and I was the heavy, solid mass that kept her from flying off into the dark. We shared a bed because the deep, physical pressure was the only thing that stopped our nervous systems from short-circuiting. To us, the neurotypical assumption wasn't just gross; it was a fundamental misunderstanding of how we survived. We completely rejected their script. We didn't need it.

I pushed those thoughts away, stood up, and followed my sister into the kitchen.

Rachel was standing by the sliding glass door. Archie was already sitting patiently on the linoleum, his tail giving a soft *thump-thump* against the cabinets. Baby, however, was still asleep on the rug in the living room.

I walked over and gave Baby’s massive gray hip a gentle nudge with my foot. The giant Lab grumbled, letting out a long, wheezing sigh before slowly forcing his heavy joints to cooperate. He lumbered into the kitchen just as Rachel slid the glass door open.

A wave of warm, humid Virginia morning air immediately rolled into the air-conditioned house.

A few years ago, the sound of that door opening would have sent both dogs sprinting into the backyard in a chaotic blur of barking and wrestling. But they were senior dogs now. The wild energy of their youth had faded into a slow, deliberate routine. Archie trotted out to his favorite patch of grass near the fence, took care of his business, and immediately turned around. Baby didn't even make it off the patio. He just stepped onto the concrete, answered the call of nature, and slowly turned his massive head back toward the kitchen, waiting to be let back inside.

"Good boys," Rachel said softly, sliding the glass door shut behind them and locking it with a satisfying, mechanical *click*.

We moved into the next phase of the routine without needing to speak. The kitchen was our assembly line.

Rachel went straight to the pantry. She grabbed the plastic scoop and filled the dogs' metal bowls. The sound of the dry kibble hitting the stainless steel was a sharp, rhythmic *shhhk-clatter* that echoed pleasantly in the quiet room. Archie immediately began to eat with aggressive enthusiasm, while Baby lay down on his stomach to slowly graze at his food.

While she handled the dogs, I opened the pantry and grabbed the brightest, most sugar-heavy boxes of cereal we owned. I pulled two heavy ceramic bowls from the cabinet and grabbed the gallon of milk from the fridge.

I poured the cereal, the satisfying crunch of the frosted pieces filling the bowls, followed by the exact, precise ratio of cold milk. I handed one bowl to Rachel.

We didn't sit at the dining room table. The table was where Mom had delivered the news about Dad moving out. It was a space contaminated by heavy, adult variables.

Instead, we carried our bowls back into the living room. We climbed right back onto the unfolded sofa bed, pulling our legs up and resting our backs against the couch cushions. We were both still in our oversized pajamas, our bare feet comfortably tangled in the heavy quilt.

I grabbed the remote and turned the TV on, keeping the volume low. The screen flickered to life, displaying the bright, mindless colors of a Saturday morning cartoon.

Rachel took a bite of her cereal, chewing slowly, her shoulder resting firmly against my arm. I took a bite of mine, the cold milk and sharp sugar perfectly hitting the spot. The house was locked. The dogs were fed. The air conditioning was humming a steady, predictable white noise.

Sitting there in the dim living room, completely insulated from the demands and expectations of the outside world, I realized I was genuinely happy. The divorce was still looming out there somewhere in the future, but right here, in this exact moment, everything was perfect.

### Part 3: The Cross-Country Bargain – 1:00 PM

The absolute peace of our Saturday lasted exactly until one o'clock in the afternoon.

We were still on the sofa bed, halfway through a marathon of old recorded VHS tapes, when the shrill, electronic ring of the kitchen landline shattered the quiet. The sudden, abrasive noise made Rachel flinch, her hands immediately flying up to cover her ears.

I paused the VCR and pushed the heavy quilt off my legs. "I'll get it. It's probably just Mom checking in."

I walked into the kitchen and grabbed the cordless phone off its charging cradle. I didn't check the caller ID before pressing the green button. "Hello?"

"Michael. It's Dad."

My stomach instantly plummeted. His voice didn't have the tight, defensive anger it usually carried when he was in the house. Instead, he sounded terrifyingly upbeat. He sounded like a man who had just solved a massive problem.

"I'm just calling to give you the update," Dad said, not bothering to ask how we were doing. "I'm not going to be renting this room for much longer. I met someone. Her name is Cathy. She lives out in Washington state. Vancouver, Washington, to be exact."

I stood frozen on the linoleum. *Washington state.* That was on the absolute opposite side of the country.

"I need to get as far away from your mother as humanly possible," Dad continued, his tone turning bitter for a fraction of a second before returning to that forced, cheerful cadence. "So, I'm moving out there with Cathy to get established. And as soon as I have a suitable house put together, I'm sending for Rachel. Have her start sorting through her room. I'll be in touch about the flights."

He didn't wait for a response. He just hung up.

I slowly lowered the phone, the dial tone buzzing in my ear. The kitchen suddenly felt freezing cold.

The "bargain" wasn't just a threat anymore; it was an active countdown. The legal rules of the separation were agonizingly clear: the moment Dad had a suitable place to live, he got Rachel. Mom got me. And Dad had just found a shortcut by moving in with a complete stranger three thousand miles away.

I walked back into the living room. Rachel was sitting up on the mattress, her knees pulled tightly to her chest. She had heard enough of my silence to know it wasn't Mom.

"What did he say?" she asked, her voice trembling.

I sat down heavily on the edge of the mattress. I didn't believe in lying to her to protect her feelings; our brains needed the raw, factual data to process reality, no matter how much it hurt. "Dad found someone named Cathy. She lives in Vancouver, Washington. He's moving out there to get away from Mom. And once he has a house... he's coming for you."

Rachel stopped breathing.

Her dark eyes dilated, completely overwhelmed by the sheer magnitude of the variables. A new state. A new time zone. A strange woman named Cathy. And worst of all—being completely severed from me. The absolute terror of the unknown hit her nervous system so hard she physically started to shake.

"No," Rachel whispered, shaking her head rapidly, her hands gripping her hair. "No, no, no. The rule is we stay together! Michael and me! I'm not going! I won't go!"

A hot, blinding wave of anger surged through my chest. The neurotypical adults who wrote these legal documents didn't care that they were physically tearing our survival mechanisms apart. They were treating us like furniture to be divided up.

"You aren't going," I said fiercely. I looked down at the cordless phone still clutched in my hand. I needed backup. I needed the person who had spent her entire life shielding us from this exact kind of chaos.

I dialed Jessica's cell phone number and hit the speaker button, setting the plastic handset on the coffee table between us.

It was 1:00 PM in Virginia Beach, which meant it was 10:00 AM in Seattle.

The phone rang three times before the line clicked open. *"Hello?"*

"Jess," I said, my voice cracking despite my anger. "It's us."

*"Michael? Rachel?"* Jessica’s voice immediately shifted from sleepy to hyper-alert, instantly detecting the panic in the room. *"What’s wrong? Are you guys okay? Are Mom and Dad fighting again?"*

"Dad called," I explained, the words rushing out of my mouth in a frantic, clinical stream. "He met a woman named Cathy in Vancouver, Washington. He's moving across the country. And the second he gets a house, he’s enforcing the separation agreement. He’s taking Rachel away."

Dead silence fell over the speakerphone, broken only by the faint static of the cellular connection.

*"He's what?"* Jessica finally breathed, her voice dripping with absolute, heartbroken fury. *"He can't do that. He can't just rip her out of the house!"*

"He's going to," Rachel sobbed, leaning forward and staring directly at the plastic phone on the table as if Jessica were inside it. "Jess, I'm so scared. I don't want to live with a stranger. I can't leave Michael."

Then, a desperate, logical spark lit up in Rachel's eyes. Washington state. Vancouver was in Washington.

"Jess!" Rachel cried out, grabbing the edge of the coffee table. "You're in Washington! You just moved there! Can I just come live with you? Please? Can Michael come too? We won't take up any space. We can sleep on the floor!"

On the other end of the line, I heard Jessica let out a sharp, devastated sob.

"Oh, baby," Jessica cried, the sound of our older sister breaking down hitting me like a physical blow. "I wish you could. I would take both of you in a heartbeat. I promise you I would. But I can't."

"Why not?" Rachel pleaded, the panic rising in her throat.

"Because I'm in a dorm," Jessica explained, her voice thick with helpless tears. "I just moved into my room at Northwest Pacific University. It's the size of a shoebox. I barely have enough room to turn my wheelchair around, Rachel. The university would never legally let two teenagers move into a freshman dorm. I don't have an apartment. I don't have a way to keep you here."

The reality of the math crushed the last bit of hope in the room. Jessica had spent her entire childhood fiercely protecting us, fighting our battles when we didn't have the words. But now, paralyzed by a wheelchair, trapped in a tiny campus dorm room three thousand miles away, and bound by university housing rules, she was entirely powerless to stop the legal system from destroying her family.

"I'm so sorry," Jessica wept through the speaker. "I'm so sorry I can't fix this for you guys."

"He hasn't moved yet," I whispered fiercely into her hair, my own tears hot and angry against my face. "He doesn't have a house yet. We still have time. And I promise you, Rachel, I will figure out a way to break their rules. You are not leaving this house."
