It took a few minutes for the arena to calm down after Jaxson Vance and Marcus Sterling cleared the ring. The lingering buzz of the hometown main event announcement was still thick in the air when the lights swept over the crowd again.

A fast-paced, electronic Lucha Libre track hit the PA system. El Relámpago sprinted out from behind the curtain, wearing a brilliant silver and blue mask that caught the arena spotlights. He didn't even use the steel stairs; he slid seamlessly under the bottom rope and scaled the turnbuckle in one fluid motion, playing to the crowd.

Seconds later, an upbeat punk rock anthem blasted through the speakers, and Johnny "Air" Strike burst onto the stage. He was absolutely buzzing with adrenaline, sprinting down the ramp and slapping the outstretched hands of the fans leaning over the barricade.

The bell rang, and the match instantly shifted into fifth gear.

Without the television commentary of Dallas Ward and Cal Stone filling our ears to explain the stakes, watching a cruiserweight match from the front row was an entirely different, incredibly raw experience. It wasn't a television show anymore; it was a high-speed athletic collision happening right in our laps.

We could hear everything. We heard the sharp squeak of their boots against the canvas. We heard the violent, echoing SMACK of knife-edge chops echoing through the building. Neither man stayed on the mat for more than two seconds, trading arm drags and lightning-fast reversals in a blinding flurry of chain wrestling.

About ten minutes into the bout, the fight spilled to the outside.

Johnny Strike took a vicious, spinning enzuigiri kick directly to the side of the head. He tumbled awkwardly through the middle ropes, his boots hitting the thin black floor mats with a heavy thud. He stumbled backward, completely dazed, until his spine slammed right into the steel barricade dead center of our section.

He was standing exactly a foot in front of us.

Because we were wearing our heavy isolation headphones around our necks to filter out the crowd noise when needed, the auditory experience of the athletes was incredibly intimate. I could hear Johnny violently gasping for air, his chest heaving. I could see the sweat dripping off his chin and the red, raw welts forming across his chest.

Inside the ring, El Relámpago saw his opening. He hit the far ropes, building up terrifying, uncontrolled speed.

I saw exactly what was about to happen.

Relámpago launched his body like a silver-and-blue missile, diving straight through the middle and top ropes.

It was a catastrophic collision. Relámpago's skull and shoulder crashed directly into Johnny's chest. The sheer, violent momentum of a two-hundred-pound man flying through the air sent Johnny violently backward.

The heavy steel barricade bowed inward toward us with a deafening, metallic CRASH.

I knew arena rules—you never reach over or touch the barricade—but I wasn't going to let that steel crush my sisters. Instantly, I shifted my weight forward. Planting my heavy work boots into the concrete, I threw my arms protectively across Jessica and Rachel like a heavy human seatbelt. I braced my entire body just inches from the bowing metal, ready to take the full blunt-force impact myself if the iron hinges snapped.

The barricade violently shuddered and stopped, holding steady a mere two inches from my knees.

Jessica didn't even flinch. She just sat back in her padded folding chair and watched the action, completely trusting my physical block. Beside me, Rachel grabbed the sleeve of my shirt to ground herself, her eyes wide as the two wrestlers collapsed in a heap of tangled limbs on the thin ringside mats right at our feet.

To the millions watching on TV, it looked like a perfectly executed, flawless stunt. But down here on the floor, the reality of the impact was brutal.

I could clearly hear the unamplified, desperate whuff of air leaving Johnny's lungs as the wind was completely knocked out of him. The referee slid out of the ring, crouching right in front of us.

"You good? You good?" the referee whispered urgently, his hand resting lightly on Johnny's back.

Johnny offered a microscopic nod. Relámpago, untangling his legs from the barricade, leaned in close to Johnny's ear and rapidly whispered the next sequence in breathless Spanish to set up the finish.

Relámpago scrambled to his feet, grabbed Johnny by the hair, and rolled him back under the bottom rope. He scaled the turnbuckle and launched himself into a breathtaking, picture-perfect 450 Splash.

The referee’s hand hit the mat three times.

The timekeeper rang the bell, and El Relámpago’s fast-paced Lucha theme hit the PA system again. The Portland crowd erupted into a massive, appreciative roar for the incredible opening bout.

As the noise level in the arena spiked violently, Rachel and I quickly slid our heavy isolation headphones fully over our ears. The thundering applause faded back into a manageable, muffled hum.

I pulled my arm back from my sisters, rolling my shoulders and exhaling a sharp breath. The show was only twenty minutes in, and the war zone had already reached our front door.
