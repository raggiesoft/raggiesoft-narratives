At exactly six o'clock, the ambient hum of the Rose City Center was instantly obliterated.

High above the entrance stage, the massive TitanTron flared to life. The metallic AWE logo slammed onto the screen, sparking with digital interference and a heavy bass drop. The sheer auditory force of the bass vibrated straight through the concrete and into the soles of my boots. Beside me, Rachel immediately slid her heavy isolation headphones over her ears, completely locked into her element, her hands gripping her screen-used foam finger. I mirrored her motion, securing my own headphones to filter out the deafening roar of the 14,000 fans as the arena lights went completely dark.

On the massive screen, a high-octane montage of heavy bumps, championship celebrations, and intense brawling played out to a driving, mid-2000s hard rock anthem. The crowd surrounding our front-row barricade went absolutely ballistic, screaming in anticipation as the sequence ended with the iconic MONDAY NIGHT WAR logo shattering the screen.

Normally, this would be the exact microsecond the stage would erupt in a terrifying, concussive volley of fireworks. But tonight, the arena remained dark and mercifully free of the sudden explosions that usually sent our nervous systems into a tailspin.

Instead, the broadcast transitioned to a gritty, slightly desaturated video package. Text appeared in the lower third of the giant screen: LAST WEEK.

The footage showed AWE General Manager Marcus Sterling standing in the center of the ring. He was wearing a sharply tailored, disgustingly expensive suit , looking down his nose at the camera with a smug, patronizing glare. Even through the heavy plastic shells of my headphones, I could hear the Portland crowd instantly erupt into a wall of venomous, localized heat at the sight of the corporate heel.

"Effective immediately, all arena pyrotechnics are banned from AWE programming," Sterling declared on the tape, his voice echoing cleanly through the massive PA system. "The fireworks are over. The 'Resistance' is over. Welcome to the bottom line".

The video package violently cut to black with the echoing sound of a slamming steel vault door.

The pyrotechnic ban was officially in effect, keeping our sensory perimeter perfectly safe, and Monday Night WAR was live on the air.
