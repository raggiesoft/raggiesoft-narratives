When the live feed from the loading dock cut out, the arena lights shifted instantly to an ominous, blood-red crimson.

Inside the ring, "The Anvil" Craig Bronson was already pacing like a rabid dog. He was furious, staring a hole through the entrance curtain, waiting to avenge his hospitalized tag team partner.

A heavy, slow drumbeat layered with distorted, grinding guitar riffs hit the PA system. Avalanche slowly walked through the curtain. He wasn't just a wrestler; he was a terrifying, 6-foot-9, 320-pound wall of muscle and malice. Behind him, Carter "The Fox" Finnley strutted down the ramp, grinning and casually adjusting his expensive sunglasses, fully expecting a massacre.

He got one.

The bell rang, and Bronson charged immediately. Operating purely on adrenaline, he threw a flurry of stiff, right-handed haymakers directly at Avalanche's jaw.

Avalanche didn't even bother to block them. He simply absorbed the punches, completely unfazed. He reached out with one massive hand, grabbed Bronson by the throat, and violently hurled the 240-pound brawler halfway across the ring into the turnbuckles like he was a child.

For the next two minutes, it was an absolute slaughter. Without the broadcast desk to contextualize the action, the visual storytelling was brutally clear. Avalanche methodically dismantled The Anvil. He hit a crushing corner splash, followed it up with a brutal military press drop, and slowly stalked around the ring.

Down at ringside, Finnley was laughing hysterically, leaning over the barricade just a few feet from us and yelling at the front row that this was exactly what happened when you messed with Team Sterling.

Inside the ring, Avalanche grabbed Bronson by the throat again. He lifted The Anvil high into the air and drove him straight down into the canvas with a catastrophic Chokeslam.

The impact was devastating.

Down in the front row, Rachel and I physically jolted in our padded folding chairs. With our heavy isolation headphones firmly clamped over our ears, we didn't hear the deafening crack of the ring canvas. Instead, we *felt* it. The massive kinetic shockwave traveled directly down through the steel ring posts, into the concrete floor, and shot straight up through the thick rubber soles of our boots.

Jessica's wheelchair rattled violently against the floor mats. Rachel gripped my forearm tightly, her eyes wide at the sheer, undeniable physical power of the giant.

Avalanche stood over Bronson's broken body. He casually placed a single, heavy boot squarely on Bronson's chest.

The referee dropped to the canvas to count. *ONE! TWO!*

Avalanche casually pulled his foot away, intentionally breaking his own pinfall. The crowd showered him with boos. He wasn't finished liquidating the asset; he wanted to punish Bronson even more.

Avalanche dragged a completely limp Bronson to his feet. He scooped him up onto his massive shoulders, preparing for a devastating running powerslam to finish the job for good.

But as Avalanche took his first heavy step forward, Bronson suddenly came alive.

Operating on absolute, sheer desperation, Bronson violently elbowed Avalanche in the side of the head. He slid down the giant's back and shoved Avalanche forcefully chest-first into the turnbuckle pad.

Avalanche staggered backward, dazed for just a fraction of a second.

Bronson didn't hesitate. He leaped into the air, wrapping his arms and legs completely around Avalanche's massive torso, dropping all of his dead weight backward into a desperate, textbook Crucifix Pin.

Avalanche's shoulders hit the mat. The referee dove into position.

ONE! TWO! THREE!

The Rose City Center didn't just cheer. The building completely came unglued. It was a deafening, sustained roar of pure shock and absolute ecstasy. Rachel and I quickly clamped our hands tightly over the plastic shells of our headphones, physically securing them against the insane, sudden spike in localized volume.

Bronson instantly broke the hold. He rolled backward, scrambled frantically under the bottom rope, and sprinted backward up the entrance ramp, clutching his ribs, his eyes wide in total disbelief.

Inside the ring, Avalanche sat up. He looked at his empty hands. The realization hit him. He snapped his head toward the referee, his expression shifting from momentary confusion to absolute, terrifying rage.

Avalanche charged the official, backing the terrified referee into the corner, screaming in a deep, guttural roar and aggressively tapping his own right shoulder, insisting he had kicked out in time. At ringside, Carter Finnley's jaw had literally dropped to the floor. He jumped onto the ring apron, waving his arms furiously at the official in protest.

Up on the massive TitanTron, the production truck immediately rolled the slow-motion replay for the live crowd.

Fourteen thousand people watched the screen as the referee's hand came down for the first count. *ONE.* Avalanche was struggling. *TWO.* Avalanche flexed his massive core to bridge up.

*THREE.* Literally a microsecond after the referee's hand made contact with the mat, Avalanche's right shoulder violently surged off the canvas. It was incredibly close, but the visual evidence was undeniable: the referee's hand hit the mat first. The decision was final.

The crowd erupted all over again, laughing at the giant's expense.

At the top of the stage, Craig Bronson pointed a single finger down at the ring, laughing breathlessly as the Portland crowd chanted his name.

Inside the ring, Avalanche let out another guttural roar of fury. He violently grabbed the top rope, shaking the entire 20-by-20 ring structure in a terrifying display of raw strength. Finnley was desperately trying to calm his giant down, yelling at him that they still had a job to do later tonight.

I looked up at the digital clock suspended above the stage. It was 7:40 PM.

The pyrotechnic ban was on the line. The AWE World Heavyweight Championship was on the line. Team Sterling was officially cracking.

The main event was next.
