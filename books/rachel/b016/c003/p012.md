The house lights dropped completely. Fourteen thousand people in the Rose City Center rose to their feet in absolute, buzzing unison.

The sound of shattering glass ripped through the PA system, immediately followed by a heavy, grinding grunge guitar riff.

The hometown pop for Jaxson "The Outlaw" Vance was so violently loud I thought the concrete floor was going to crack. Rachel and I instantly slid our heavy, over-ear isolation headphones firmly into place. The thick foam shells neutralized the concussive, deafening wall of sound from the screaming Oregonians, turning the overwhelming noise into a manageable, muffled roar so we could visually enjoy the adrenaline rush.

Kristin leaned over, smiling widely, and threw her arms up in the air alongside Jessica as Vance stepped through the curtain. He stomped down the ramp, his eyes burning with intensity, ignoring the outstretched hands of the fans. He slid into the ring, climbed the nearest turnbuckle, and threw a taped fist into the air.

The music violently shifted to a methodical, arrogant instrumental track. The arena instantly pivoted from a deafening cheer to a wall of localized, venomous heat.

"The Blueprint" Silas Thorne stepped onto the stage. He had shed his tailored suit for his pristine, silver-and-black in-ring gear, the massive, gleaming AWE World Heavyweight Championship strapped securely around his waist. He looked infuriated, flanked on either side by the grinning Carter "The Fox" Finnley and the terrifying Avalanche.

Thorne entered the ring and took his sweet time unbuckling the championship, taunting Vance. He finally shoved the heavy gold belt into the chest of Senior Official Jack Miller.

Jack Miller was an AWE institution—a twenty-five-year veteran referee with zero tolerance for locker room politics. He famously took no crap from the talent. Miller ripped the belt out of Thorne’s hands, silencing the champion's trash talk with a stern, aggressive point of his finger.

Honoring the time-tested tradition of World Championship matches, Miller stepped to the dead center of the ring. He raised the heavy gold belt high above his head, slowly rotating clockwise to present the championship to all four sides of the arena. The sheer prestige of the gold sent another shockwave of cheers through the building.

Miller handed the belt to a ringside technician, checked both competitors, and signaled the timekeeper.

The bell rang, and Vance absolutely exploded out of the corner.

He hit Thorne with a blistering running clothesline, backing him into the ropes, and unleashed a barrage of heavy, taped fists. Thorne quickly bailed out of the ring to catch his breath, but Vance was on him in a second, slamming the champion's head repeatedly into the hood of the ringside broadcast desk.

The match was a grueling, twelve-minute clash of styles. Thorne utilized his surgical technical prowess, grounding the brawler with brutal submission holds and violently targeting Vance's knees to take away his explosive power. But Vance was running on pure hometown adrenaline. Every single time Thorne thought he had him put away, Vance fought out of the hold, fueled by the localized thunder of the Portland crowd.

At exactly 7:52 PM, the climax hit.

Vance executed a massive back-body drop. Thorne scrambled to his feet, desperate to regain control. He attempted a wild, spinning heel kick, but Vance read it perfectly and ducked.

Thorne’s heavy boot connected cleanly with the side of Senior Official Jack Miller's head. The veteran referee collapsed to the canvas, completely unconscious.

The arena descended into absolute chaos.

With the official down, Avalanche and Carter Finnley immediately jumped onto the ring apron to attack Vance. But the Resistance had made a promise in the locker room.

Sprinting down the long entrance ramp came "Iron" Tommy Brooks and the Midnight Runners. They didn't even slow down; they violently yanked Finnley off the apron, initiating a massive, swinging brawl on the thin mats of the arena floor.

Inside the ring, Avalanche slid through the ropes, preparing to Chokeslam Vance.

Suddenly, a figure vaulted over the heavy steel barricade from the crowd area directly next to Kristin and Rachel. It was the masked wildcard, Fracture.

Fracture scrambled onto the ring apron, grabbed Avalanche from behind, and violently pulled the 320-pound giant completely out of the ring. The momentum carried both men crashing to the floor, sending Avalanche spine-first into the heavy steel ring steps with a sickening, unamplified *CRUNCH* that vibrated straight through our folding chairs.

Sitting inches away, I planted my heavy boots into the concrete and gripped the metal frame of Jessica's chair, acting as an unshakable physical anchor as the massive bodies flew around our perimeter. Rachel's eyes were wide, safely shielded from the deafening crowd noise but perfectly absorbing the raw, kinetic energy of the brawl happening just feet away.

With the ringside area turning into a literal warzone, General Manager Marcus Sterling sprinted down the ramp in a sheer panic. He grabbed the AWE World Championship belt from the timekeeper's table and slid it across the canvas to Silas Thorne.

Thorne gripped the heavy gold plates. Vance turned around, groggy. Thorne swung the championship like a baseball bat, aiming squarely for Vance's skull.

But Vance dropped to his knees.

The heavy gold plates of the championship belt sailed entirely over Vance's head and smashed violently into the taut top ring rope. The kinetic energy had nowhere to go but backward. The rope violently rebounded, snapping the heavy belt back like a rubber band and smacking Silas Thorne directly between the eyes with a sickening *THWACK*.

Thorne dropped the belt, stumbling backward, completely dazed.

Vance bounced off the opposite ropes, built terrifying speed, and hit his signature finishing maneuver—The Outlaw Lariat. It nearly decapitated the champion. Thorne hit the mat, lifeless.

Vance scrambled to hook the leg.

Slowly, groggily, Senior Official Jack Miller began to stir. The Portland crowd, recognizing the absolute magnitude of the moment, started counting along at the top of their lungs.

Miller crawled over, slamming his hand onto the canvas.

ONE!

TWO!

THREE!

The timekeeper hammered the bell frantically, and Vance's heavy grunge theme exploded through the PA system. The Rose City Center achieved maximum auditory capacity. It was an earth-shattering, concussive wave of pure joy.

Rachel and I pressed our hands firmly against the outside shells of our isolation headphones, grinning from ear to ear as the localized noise shook the building to its foundation. Inside the ring, Jack Miller retrieved the championship from the canvas and handed it to a bleeding, breathless Jaxson Vance. Vance held the gold high in the air, climbing the turnbuckle as his hometown worshipped him.

The Resistance had won. The corporate machine had been shattered on free television.

Jaxson “The Outlaw” Vance stood tall in the ring, the referee raising his hand as the crowd roared around us. No commentary in our ears—just the noise, the lights, the feeling that something had shifted.

I glanced up at the digital clock hanging above the stage.

7:56 PM.

That’s when it sank in.

This wasn’t about the title changing hands tonight—Vance hadn’t taken it.\
He’d taken *control*.

People around me started piecing it together at the same time—phones coming out, chatter spreading seat to seat, the implications hitting in waves.

By winning tonight, The Resistance had earned the right to *add a stipulation* to Sunday’s match.

And as the realization spread through the arena, it turned that victory into something far more dangerous:

If The Resistance wins this Sunday… the pyrotechnics ban is lifted.

You could feel the reaction ripple outward. Not just cheers—something sharper than that. Anticipation. Unease.

Because everyone in that building knew what it meant.

No restrictions.\
No corporate limits.

Just fire… waiting for a trigger.

And come Sunday in Seattle, that trigger might be The Resistance.
