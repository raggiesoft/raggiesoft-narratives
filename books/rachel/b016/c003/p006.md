After the backstage feed of Marcus Sterling's meltdown faded from the TitanTron, the arena lights shifted, bathing the 14,000 fans in a deep, regal purple hue.

A grandiose, highly pretentious remix of classical harpsichord music echoed through the PA system, accompanied by a heavy, driving bassline.

"The Empress" Victoria Hale stepped through the entrance curtain. She didn't walk down the ramp; she glided. She was draped in a lavish, faux-ermine robe, and perched perfectly atop her head was a glittering, gold-plated crown. She was closely trailed by a terrified-looking male attendant carrying a velvet pillow, upon which rested a heavy, jewel-encrusted scepter.

She stopped at the bottom of the ramp, looking out at the Portland crowd with an expression of pure, concentrated disgust, as if the oxygen in the arena was entirely beneath her standards.

Suddenly, the pretentious classical music was violently cut off by a blaring air raid siren, followed instantly by a frantic, heavily distorted punk rock track.

The crowd absolutely erupted. Next to me, Kristin shot out of her folding chair, enthusiastically pointing to the new, studded-graphic Roxy Dane t-shirt she had just picked out at the merchandise stand.

Roxy "The Riot" Dane exploded through the curtain like a cannonball. She was a ball of pure kinetic energy, wearing taped fists, kickboxing trunks, and a studded leather vest. She sprinted down the ramp, high-fiving the fans along the barricade, practically vibrating with adrenaline. She slid smoothly under the bottom rope, bounced off the far turnbuckle, and began aggressively shadowboxing in the center of the canvas.

Victoria Hale looked at her like she was a rodent.

The referee called for the bell, and Roxy didn't waste a single microsecond.

Because the ambient noise of the crowd was so overwhelming, Rachel and I had our heavy isolation headphones firmly locked over our ears. But sitting dead center in the front row, our ears were perfectly level with the ring apron. The headphones filtered out the 14,000 screaming fans, but they couldn't block out the immediate, acoustic violence of the ring itself.

When Roxy closed the distance and launched a blistering inside leg kick, it didn't look or sound like a choreographed television performance. It sounded like a baseball bat hitting a side of beef.

THWACK.

The heavy, dull echo of Roxy's shin bone colliding with Hale's thigh reverberated sharply off the taut canvas. It was the brutal reality of Japanese strong-style striking—they were genuinely hitting each other, just protecting the vital targets.

Jessica leaned forward in her wheelchair, her eyes wide, completely captivated by the sheer physical intensity unfolding five feet in front of us.

Roxy systematically dismantled the Empress's base, targeting the lead leg with a wince-inducing combination of stiff jabs and heavy kicks to prevent Hale from shooting for a technical takedown.

Desperate to stop the flurry, Hale retreated toward the ropes. In a brilliant piece of heel psychology, she used the referee's body to block his line of sight, quickly and viciously raking Roxy's eyes. Roxy stumbled backward, temporarily blinded. Hale instantly capitalized, sweeping Roxy's damaged leg and locking in a brutal, grounded surfboard stretch, pulling back on Roxy's arms with incredible leverage.

Even through the headphones, I could hear Roxy grunting in pain. The Portland crowd rallied behind her, starting a heavy, rhythmic clapping that shook the floorboards.

Roxy fed off the energy. Using her incredible core strength, she rolled violently forward, breaking Hale's grip and scrambling back to her feet.

Infuriated that her technical submission had been broken, Victoria Hale rolled under the bottom rope directly in front of us. She snatched the heavy, jewel-encrusted scepter right off her attendant's velvet pillow.

"She's got the scepter!" Kristin yelled over the crowd noise, though the referee's back was completely turned as he checked on Roxy.

Hale slid back into the ring, stalking up behind Roxy with the heavy metal prop raised high. She swung it like a baseball bat directly at the back of Roxy's head.

But Roxy's ring awareness was flawless. At the absolute last microsecond, she dropped her center of gravity, ducking cleanly under the swinging scepter. The momentum spun the Empress completely around, leaving her wide open.

Roxy unleashed her signature flurry. Right hook. Left hook. A spinning back elbow to the jaw that echoed like a gunshot.

As Hale dropped to her knees, completely dazed and dropping her scepter, Roxy bounced off the ropes, built up maximum speed, and delivered a devastating, running knee strike flush to the side of the Empress's head. Hale collapsed onto the mat, completely motionless.

Roxy hooked the leg. The referee dropped to the canvas.

ONE! TWO! THREE!

The timekeeper slammed the hammer into the bell, and Roxy's punk rock anthem blasted through the arena. The crowd exploded. Kristin and Jessica threw their arms in the air, cheering wildly, while Rachel clapped her hands in a rapid, joyous rhythm, her giant foam finger waving proudly in the air.

On the massive TitanTron, the graphic flashed: ROXY DANE WINS! ADVANCES TO NO QUARTER.

Roxy leaped onto the nearest turnbuckle, throwing her taped fists into the air to celebrate with the Portland crowd. She was going to the Pay-Per-View to fight for the Women's Championship, and our front-row seats had just given us an unforgettable, front-row masterclass in controlled chaos.
