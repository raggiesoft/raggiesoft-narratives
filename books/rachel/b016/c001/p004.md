We took the elevator down to the lobby and pushed through the heavy revolving glass doors of the Crown Regency.

The freezing, damp Pacific Northwest air hit our faces immediately. For my overloaded processor, the sharp bite of the November wind was the exact physical reset I needed. It shocked my system, clearing the last lingering traces of the heavy emotional fog that had kept me completely silent since my meltdown that morning.

The logistical brilliance of Jessica's hotel choice paid off the second our boots hit the pavement. We didn't have to navigate a crowded transit platform or squeeze onto a packed CTS train to get to the venue. We simply walked out the front doors and crossed the street.

The Rose City Center was massive, taking up an entire city block. As we approached the sprawling concrete plaza, the sheer scale of the operation became apparent. Massive AWE production trucks—painted with high-gloss graphics of Silas Thorne, Roxy Dane, and Jaxson Vance—were parked near the loading docks. The low, heavy rumble of diesel generators vibrated through the soles of our boots. Thick, black broadcast cables snaked across the concrete, and event staff in bright yellow jackets were already setting up the heavy steel barricades to corral the impending crowds.

Suddenly, Rachel's hand shot out. Her fingers gripped the thick sleeve of my winter coat like a vice.

I immediately stopped walking, allowing her to press her shoulder flush against my bicep. I looked down at her. Her eyes were wide, darting frantically between the massive production trucks, the arena signage, and the growing crowd of early-arrival fans. She was taking in the massive, overwhelming reality of her ultimate hyperfixation existing in physical space right in front of her.

Her threat-detection system wasn't spiking out of fear; it was simply paralyzed by the sheer, unadulterated magnitude of the Monday Night WAR.

My analytical brain saw the shutdown happening, and it triggered the absolute heaviest, most fundamental subroutine in my programming: *Protect the Bug.*

I swallowed hard, pushing against the heavy, invisible wall that had locked my vocal cords tight for the last four hours. I needed my voice. She needed her anchor.

"I've got you, Bug," I murmured.

The words came out as a quiet, raspy whisper, but the physical vibration of my chest against her shoulder made her blink. She looked up at me, her breathing shallow.

I wrapped my arm securely around her shoulders, pulling her flush against my side, and forced myself to speak again, pushing a little harder against the block.

"Look at the cables, Rachel," I said, my voice gaining a fraction more volume, stepping into the steady, mechanical cadence that always grounded her. "Look at the logistics. It's exactly what you told us at breakfast. It's not chaos. It's a highly controlled broadcast."

Rachel followed my pointing finger. She looked at the heavy black cables, neatly taped down to the concrete, routing perfectly from the production trucks into the massive loading bays. Her rapid breathing hitched, then slowly began to deepen as her brain latched onto the recognizable, underlying structure of the event.

"See?" I continued, the invisible wall in my throat finally shattering completely. The act of protecting her had acted as the ultimate system override. My voice dropped into its full, resonant, unwavering power. "It's all mapped out. Just like your production sheets. We are walking into a controlled environment, and our perimeter is completely solid."

Rachel let out a long, shuddering exhale. Her death grip on my winter coat relaxed into a steady, comfortable hold. She offered a small, grateful nod, her eyes shining with pure excitement rather than overload.

Jessica, who had paused her manual wheelchair to give us the space to regulate, offered a proud, subtle smirk. She wheeled forward smoothly over the wet concrete, retaking the point position to carve a path through the plaza. Kristin fell in line a half-step behind Rachel. Standing at a sturdy five-foot-nine, Kristin naturally eclipsed the rest of us, her broader frame effortlessly guarding her best friend's blind spot from the unpredictable civilian foot traffic.

We walked as a single, impenetrable unit past the towering, multi-story glass windows of the arena's main atrium, making our way directly toward the illuminated box office windows.

My vocal cords were fully online. My spine was straight. The Bear was fully awake. The trap Tom Ragsdale had inadvertently set for his daughter was about to be effortlessly dismantled, because Rachel wasn't walking up to that glass window alone.
