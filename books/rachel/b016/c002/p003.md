The main merchandise stand was a towering, brightly lit wall of black cotton, neon graphics, and glossy photographs. Even completely empty of the usual thousands of pushing, shoving fans, it was an overwhelming display of AWE spectacle.

An arena vendor was waiting for us behind the massive tables, looking slightly bored until Dave walked up and tapped the counter.

"Alright, folks," Dave said, gesturing to the wall. "As contest winners, you each get the full VIP spread. That means one hat, one t-shirt, one of those giant foam fingers, a mini replica championship belt, and an autographed eight-by-ten photo of any wrestler on the active roster."

"And don't forget the chairs," the vendor chimed in, pointing toward the arena floor. "Your tickets are Row 1. Those padded Ringsider folding chairs you're sitting in tonight? You get to fold those up and take them home after the main event."

Jessica let out a low whistle. "We're going to need an extra set of hands just to get all this onto the train tomorrow morning."

"We'll manage," I said, grinning.

Kristin immediately stepped up to the counter, her eyes scanning the wall of apparel. She confidently pointed out a studded-graphic Roxy "The Riot" Dane t-shirt and an autographed photo of the kickboxer. Jessica, ever the pragmatist, opted for a classic, understated black AWE logo hoodie and a glossy photo of "Sly" Diego Chavez.

I looked over at Rachel. She was standing perfectly still, her eyes darting frantically across the dozens of t-shirts pinned to the massive display grid. She looked down at the organized binders of autographed photos on the table, then back up to the wall. Her hands, which had been resting happily at her sides, slowly drifted up to her chest, her fingers curling inward.

I knew that posture. It was the physical manifestation of a rapidly crashing expectation.

I stepped closer to her, keeping my voice low. "Rachel? What's wrong? You have the pick of the entire litter."

She didn't speak. She just looked at me, her eyes welling up with tears, and gave her head a tiny, frustrated shake. She pointed a trembling finger toward the wall, and then made a subtle, sweeping gesture.

It took me a second to translate, but when I looked at the wall, it clicked. Rachel didn't want a Silas Thorne shirt. She didn't want a Jaxson Vance glossy.

She wanted Dave "The Arsenal" Cross gear. And because Dave had been retired from in-ring competition for years, his merchandise was no longer carried on the live touring trucks.

Rachel wasn't having a meltdown, but the profound sadness of standing right next to her absolute hero and not being able to represent him was threatening to push her into a sensory shutdown. She had built this exact moment up in her head, and the missing variable was causing a neurological misfire.

Before I could figure out how to pivot her attention, Dave stepped in. He had been watching the exchange silently.

"Hey," Dave said softly, his gravelly voice cutting through the quiet hum of the concourse. He stepped right up to the counter, putting himself at eye level with Rachel. "You looking for the old black-and-silver 'Arsenal' skull shirt?"

Rachel looked at him and gave a small, heartbreaking nod, a single tear escaping down her cheek.

Dave offered a warm, understanding smile. "I'm sorry, Rachel. I haven't had a shirt on that wall in three years. They only carry the active guys on the trucks now."

Rachel looked down at her shoes. I prepared to wrap my arm around her to start the grounding process, but Dave held up a hand, stopping me.

"But," Dave continued, leaning casually against the merch table. "I happen to know for a fact that the Stamford warehouse still has a few boxes of deadstock Arsenal gear sitting on a pallet in the back. And as a segment producer, I have the direct phone number to the warehouse manager."

Rachel's head snapped up.

"If you let Michael write down your shirt size and home address for me," Dave said, pulling a pen out of his pocket. "I will personally pay for an Arsenal t-shirt and hat, and I will have them overnight shipped directly to your house in Washington. Deal?"

The heavy, suffocating sadness instantly evaporated from Rachel's face, replaced by a look of absolute, starry-eyed wonder. The fact that Dave Cross—the Man of 1,000 Holds—was going to personally mail her his gear was infinitely better than buying it off a generic arena wall.

Rachel nodded enthusiastically, her hands flapping in a quick, happy burst of restored energy. A massive meltdown had just been flawlessly averted by the simple, observant kindness of a retired wrestler.

"Awesome," Dave said, handing me his notebook to jot down the Renton address. "In the meantime, you still have a free prize pack to claim right now. What's it going to be to hold you over?"

With her processor fully reset and the world making sense again, Rachel happily stepped up to the counter. She pointed to a classic Monday Night WAR logo shirt and selected an eight-by-ten glossy of Jaxson "The Outlaw" Vance to support the hometown Portland hero. I quickly followed suit, grabbing a Resistance faction shirt and a mini replica of the World Heavyweight Championship.

Within minutes, the vendor had our photos and mini belts safely bagged and set aside, leaving only the four t-shirts, four hats, and four giant foam fingers piled neatly on the counter.

"Alright, production crew is ready!" Dave called out, clipping his radio back onto his belt. "Let's make some television!"
