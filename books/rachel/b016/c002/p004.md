"Alright, everyone to your marks!" Dave Cross ordered, his voice echoing slightly in the vast, empty concourse. He pointed us toward the front of the merchandise table. "Stay loose. When Diego throws the money, just go crazy."

An AWE marketing rep crouched just out of the camera's frame, holding the specific hats, shirts, and the giant foam finger we had just picked out.

"Cameras speed. And... action!"

The heavy double doors down the concourse banged open. "Sly" Diego Chavez burst into the frame, jogging backward and flashing his signature, million-dollar smirk. He was waving a platinum money clip stuffed with thick, green hundred-dollar bills.

"Chavez!" a voice roared from the hallway.

Stomping into the light was "The Tycoon" Preston Sinclair. The billionaire heel was dressed in his pristine white Texan suit, his face contorted in absolute fury. But instead of a microphone, Sinclair was dragging a massive, thick-braided Texas Bull Rope behind him, a heavy iron cowbell clanking violently against the concrete floor.

"You think you can steal from me?!" Sinclair bellowed, winding the thick rope around his wrist. "I'm going to skin you alive this Sunday!"

"You gotta catch me first, Tycoon!" Diego yelled back, seamlessly hyping their upcoming Pay-Per-View bout.

Sinclair lunged forward, swinging the heavy bull rope with terrifying speed. The cowbell whipped through the air, but Diego nimbly dropped to a knee, ducking the strike perfectly. The rope smashed loudly against a concrete pillar instead, echoing through the concourse.

Diego just laughed, hopping effortlessly onto the top of the merchandise table. Sinclair tried to charge, but two AWE security extras rushed into the frame, holding the furious billionaire back.

"Oye, Portland!" Diego shouted, playing to the imaginary television audience and looking down at us. "Let me tell you a little secret about my good friend, Preston Sinclair! He thinks tying me up in a Texas Bull Rope Match this Sunday at No Quarter is gonna keep me down! But the man is incredibly generous! In fact, Señor Sinclair just told me he wants to buy every single person in this line some brand new gear! On him!"

We hit our cue perfectly. Kristin, Jessica, and I started cheering loudly, but Rachel was completely lost in the magic of the moment. She was jumping up and down, clapping her hands in a flurry of uncontainable excitement.

The AWE rep handed Diego our pre-selected merchandise out of frame.

"Give the kid in the front a hat!" Diego shouted, tossing the classic AWE cap directly into my hands.

"Give the lady in the back a foam finger, homes!" Diego yelled, looking right at Rachel. He tossed the giant, blue-and-silver foam prop. Rachel caught it, clutching it to her chest like it was made of solid gold.

Diego turned to the bewildered merchandise cashier, peeling a thick stack of bills off Sinclair's platinum clip and slapping them onto the table. "Keep the change, amigo! The Tycoon is paying for it!"

"Cut! Print that!" Dave called out from behind the monitors. "Great job, everyone. Reset for the backstage shot."

The second the word "Cut" hung in the air, the heavy, fictional tension evaporated. But for Rachel, the sheer weight of what was happening finally crashed down on her all at once.

She was going to be on national television. Dave Cross was personally mailing a shirt to her house. She was holding a prop used by one of her favorite wrestlers. It was entirely too much joy, too much electricity for her system to process at one time.

Rachel's knees buckled. She dropped to the polished concrete floor, pulling her knees to her chest and clutching the foam finger tight, burying her face in it as a wave of intense, frantic tears overtook her. Her free hand began to shake rapidly in a physical release of the overwhelming surge of emotion.

Instantly, the atmosphere shifted.

"Whoa, whoa, back up," Diego Chavez said. The charismatic, loudmouth persona vanished in a microsecond. The man behind the character—a father to a special-needs child in real life—recognized the signs immediately. He knew exactly what he was looking at.

He didn't rush her. He didn't bend down and demand to know if she was okay, which would have only made everything worse. Instead, Diego immediately turned his back to Rachel, holding his arms out wide to create a physical buffer zone between her and the camera crew.

"Give her space, guys," Diego instructed the crew in a calm, authoritative tone. "No cameras. Back it up."

Preston Sinclair, who had been panting and trying to fix his ruined suit, saw the commotion. He took one step toward Rachel out of genuine concern, but Diego caught his eye and gave a subtle, flat-handed gesture. Give her space. Sinclair, knowing about Diego's own family dynamics, nodded once in silent understanding and stepped back to help hold the perimeter.

With the outer perimeter secured by the two wrestlers, I immediately dropped to the floor right beside Rachel.

I didn't try to pull her to her feet or ask her what was wrong. I just wrapped my arms around her and pulled her into a very, very tight bear hug. I locked my arms and squeezed, applying deep, heavy, crushing pressure to her torso and shoulders. It was the only thing that ever grounded her when she got like this—a physical anchor to keep her from floating away into the panic.

Jessica wheeled her chair in close, speaking in a low, rhythmic, familiar cadence about the train ride tomorrow, giving Rachel a steady voice to focus on. Kristin stood tall right behind us, shielding Rachel's back from the rest of the arena.

I held her tightly against me, letting her squeeze the foam finger between us, until I felt her rapid breathing finally start to slow down. The frantic shaking in her hand gradually eased. After a couple of minutes, she wiped her eyes, looked up from the foam finger, and offered a massive, exhausted, but radiantly happy smile.

"You good?" I whispered, finally loosening my grip just a fraction.

She nodded firmly.

Diego looked over his shoulder. Seeing that the storm had passed and Rachel was smiling, he offered a warm, subtle nod of respect—first to Rachel, and then to me. He didn't make a scene or draw further attention to it. He just turned to Dave Cross and gave a thumbs-up.

"Alright, folks, you're clear," Dave said gently, walking over as I finally let go and helped Rachel to her feet. He reached into his pocket and pulled out the thick stack of bills Diego had slapped on the counter. He peeled off four of the realistic-looking \$100 bills and handed one to each of us.

Printed across the top in small letters were the words: For Motion Picture Use Only.

"Souvenirs from the Tycoon," Dave said with a smirk. "Keep 'em safe. And Rachel? Keep an eye on your mailbox next week."

Rachel clutched the prop money and her foam finger, her smile brighter than the arena lights. The main event hadn't even started yet, and it was already the greatest night of her life.
