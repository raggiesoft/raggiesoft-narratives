With Rachel securely grounded and our massive haul of VIP merchandise gathered up, it was time to actually find our seats.

"We need to get you guys down to the floor," Dave Cross told us, checking his watch. "The problem is, the only ADA-compliant elevator that goes from this concourse directly down to the arena floor level is located strictly behind the curtain, in the restricted locker room area. You can't get back there without an escort. Follow me."

For a wrestling fan, taking the restricted freight elevator was a golden ticket. Dave led us past a pair of massive security guards and through a set of heavy double doors. Suddenly, we were in the belly of the beast. We walked past towering stacks of black metal road cases stenciled with the AWE logo, dodging production assistants sprinting with headsets and lighting technicians carrying coils of heavy cabling.

Dave swiped his badge, and the heavy metal doors of the freight elevator slid open. We piled in, the hydraulic motor humming loudly as we descended to the ground level of the Rose City Center.

When the doors opened, Dave led us down a short concrete tunnel that spilled directly out onto the arena floor.

The sheer scale of the building from the floor level was breathtaking. Looking up into the cavernous rafters, surrounded by 14,000 empty seats, the magnitude of Monday Night WAR finally set in. But as we approached the ringside area, the logistical reality of our front-row tickets slammed into us like a brick wall.

"Okay," Jessica said, stopping her manual wheelchair at the edge of the floor mats. "That’s going to be a problem."

She was right. The front-row seats were directly against the heavy steel barricade, dead center with the ring. But to get to them, you had to navigate a minefield of thick, black television broadcast cables duct-taped to the concrete. Even worse, the premium padded folding chairs were zip-tied together to prevent them from moving.

They were jammed so incredibly close to one another that there was zero personal space. When you sat down, you were going to be completely shoulder-to-shoulder, physically touching the person sitting next to you. There was absolutely no room to navigate a wheelchair down the row, and the steel barricade blocked any access from the front.

"Leave the chair here by the tunnel entrance," I told Jessica, stepping up without missing a beat. "I'll carry you."

This was our standard operational protocol. I was the only one in the family with the physical strength and balance to carry my twenty-six-year-old sister. Jessica locked the brakes on her wheelchair, and I crouched down in front of her. She leaned forward, wrapping her arms securely around my neck. I hooked my arms under her legs and hoisted her up into a piggyback hold.

It was a massive display of absolute, unspoken sibling trust. She trusted me to carry her safely over the trip hazards, and I trusted her to hold on tight.

I carefully navigated the tight, awkward gap between the barricade and the first row of chairs, carrying Jessica all the way to the dead center of the section. I gently lowered her into the far-left seat of our block.

We filed in right behind her. Facing the ring, the seating order was locked in: Jessica on the far left, me sitting immediately to her right, Rachel next to me, and Kristin on the far right.

Because we were in the exact center of the long row, random fans would be sitting directly to Jessica's left and Kristin's right. Jessica was completely boxed in. With the chairs zip-tied together and people soon to be crammed into every available inch of space, there was absolutely no way Jessica was getting out of that seat to use the restroom or leave the arena unless I stood up and carried her back out the exact same way we came in. She was entirely dependent on my physical anchor, but she didn't look nervous. She just patted my shoulder and settled into her padded chair.

We were exhausted and running on adrenaline, which meant the inevitable hunger was starting to set in.

"I was hoping to grab a pretzel or something on the way down," Kristin said, looking around the floor. "But the concourse concession stands had their metal grates pulled down."

"They don't open the main food stands until the doors officially open to the public," I explained, pulling my heavy isolation headphones out of my bag and resting them around my neck. "We're going to have to rely on the roaming vendors selling peanuts and sodas once the crowd fills in. Thank God we ate that massive lunch before we got to the arena."

"I'm fine," Rachel said, speaking for the first time since we entered the building. Her voice was quiet, but she was smiling, her hands happily tracing the AWE logo on her giant foam finger.

At exactly 4:30 PM, the arena doors opened, and the Portland crowd began to flood into the building like a tidal wave. The ambient noise level rose from a quiet hum to a chaotic roar as thousands of fans found their seats.

At 5:30 PM, the massive overhead house lights suddenly dimmed, bathing the arena floor in a cool, shadowy blue. The bright, blinding spotlights over the ring snapped on.

The untelevised dark matches were about to begin. The Monday Night WAR had officially arrived.
