A few minutes after we handed the signed waivers back to the box office attendant, a heavy steel door next to the ticket windows clicked open.

Stepping out onto the concrete plaza was a man who looked like he was carved out of granite. He wasn't exceptionally tall, but he was built like a fire hydrant, carrying a clipboard and walking with a stiff, permanent limp—the undeniable toll of a career spent sacrificing his body in the ring. He wore a black AWE production polo and a headset resting around his neck, projecting an aura of absolute, ice-cold professionalism.

To the casual fan, he was just a backstage producer. But to anyone who actually understood the mechanics of professional wrestling, he was a living legend.

It was Dave "The Arsenal" Cross. The Man of 1,000 Holds.

Before a severe neck injury forced him into early retirement a few years ago, Dave was the most feared, surgically precise technical grappler on the planet. And more importantly, he was Rachel's absolute favorite wrestler of all time.

I felt Rachel’s entire body go rigid beside me. Her hands shot up to chest height, her fingers fluttering rapidly in a pure, uncontrolled stim of absolute disbelief. She was standing three feet away from her hero.

"Afternoon," Dave said, his voice quiet and gravelly. He didn't have a fake, booming television persona. He was just a serious man doing a serious job. "I'm Dave, one of the segment producers. You're our radio contest winners?"

"Yes, sir," I answered, instinctively tightening my arm around Rachel to keep her grounded before she completely floated away. "I'm Michael. This is Kristin, my older sister Jessica, and my younger sister Rachel."

Dave nodded, looking down at the signed waivers on his clipboard to verify the names. He traced a calloused finger down the paper, stopping abruptly. He looked back up, his stoic, intense eyes locking directly onto Rachel.

"Rachel Ragsdale?" Dave asked, his brow furrowing slightly. "From Washington State?"

Rachel, completely paralyzed by situational mutism and awe, could only manage a frantic, wide-eyed nod.

The ice-cold demeanor of "The Arsenal" suddenly melted into a rare, genuine smile. "I'll be damned," he said softly. "I know exactly who you are."

He stepped slightly closer, lowering his clipboard. "You send letters to the Stamford office. You're the one who completely broke down the biomechanics of my inverted Texas Cloverleaf. You actually corrected the broadcast team's commentary on how the fulcrum point works on the lower back."

Rachel's face flushed bright red, and she nodded violently, a massive, silent smile stretching across her face.

"Most people write to tell me I'm their favorite," Dave said, his tone filled with deep, unmistakable respect. "You write to tell me why the mechanics work. Nobody else does that. I kept those letters. Thank you, Rachel. It means a lot to know someone actually watches the footwork."

If Rachel could have spoken, she wouldn't have had the words anyway. The sheer, validating joy of being recognized not just as a fan, but as a student of the craft by her ultimate hero, was a monumental moment. Kristin gently squeezed Rachel's shoulder, beaming with pride for her best friend.

"Alright," Dave said, smoothly shifting back into producer mode, though his eyes were still warm. "Let's get you inside. We're taping a segment on the main concourse in about twenty minutes. We've got 'Sly' Diego Chavez out there."

Dave held the heavy door open for us, making sure Jessica had plenty of clearance for her wheelchair as we crossed the threshold into the quiet, cavernous arena.

"Here is the setup," Dave explained as we walked down the empty, polished concrete hallways. "In the storyline, Diego just picked the pocket of 'The Tycoon' Preston Sinclair backstage. He stole Sinclair's platinum money clip, which is loaded with hundred-dollar bills. Diego is going to come out to the main merchandise stand, cut a promo, and use Sinclair's stolen cash to buy out the merch stand for the fans."  

We turned a corner, and the massive, brightly lit AWE merchandise stand came into view. A small camera crew was busy setting up lighting rigs and taping cables to the floor.

"Since you four are our VIPs, you are playing our lucky fans," Dave said, stopping us just out of frame of the cameras. "Before we roll, I need you to look at the wall and tell the merch staff exactly which shirts, hats, and autographed photos you want for your prize packs. We'll bag the photos and belts for you to hold onto, but Diego is going to physically toss your hats and shirts to you on camera. When he throws the gear, I just need you to go crazy. Make it look like the best day of your life."  

He looked back at Rachel and offered a small wink. "Given the letters you write, I don't think you'll have to act too hard."
