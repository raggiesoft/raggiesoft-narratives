At exactly 5:30 PM, the massive overhead house lights of the Rose City Center completely cut out. A collective, electric roar rippled through the 14,000 fans as the blinding, brilliant white spotlights suspended above the ring snapped on, illuminating the canvas like a pristine stage.

The live television broadcast was still thirty minutes away, but the AWE always used untelevised "dark matches" to whip the live crowd into a frenzy before the cameras officially started rolling.

As a rookie tag team sprinted down the ramp to an upbeat rock track, high-fiving fans along the barricade, a booming, rhythmic voice cut through the ambient noise in our section.

"POPCORN! PEANUTS! ICE COLD BEER! GET YOUR ICE COLD BEER HERE!"

A roving arena vendor was squeezing his way down the narrow aisle behind the second row, carrying a massive, plastic-wrapped tray suspended by a neck strap.

"Hey! Over here!" I called out, twisting around in my zip-tied folding chair and waving an arm to flag him down.

The vendor leaned over the shoulders of the people in the second row, flashing a salesman's grin. "What can I get you, boss? Two tall boys to start the night right?"

"We'll skip the beer," I said with a laugh, gesturing down the row. "My younger sister and her best friend are only twenty anyway. But we are absolutely starving. We're going to need a lot of everything else."

I reached into my wallet and pulled out a stack of actual cash—careful not to mix it up with the Motion Picture Use Only hundreds Dave Cross had just given us.

Because we had skipped the concession stands on the concourse and were preparing to hunker down for a three-hour marathon of live wrestling, we didn't hold back. I handed the vendor a wad of bills to cover a massive haul: two sodas, two bottled waters, and two boxes of popcorn per person.

"You got it!" the vendor said, quickly counting the cash, handing me my change, and beginning the monumental task of unloading his tray.

Because we were sitting shoulder-to-shoulder in the dead center of the front row, the logistics of distributing twenty-four individual concession items down the line was a comical team effort. I took the first two boxes of popcorn and handed them left to Jessica. Then two sodas. Then two waters. She wedged the drinks securely under her seat and balanced the popcorn on her lap, effectively trapped but entirely happy about it.

Next came Rachel's haul, then Kristin's, and finally my own.

By the time the vendor moved on, shouting about his ice-cold beer to the next section, we had constructed a literal fortress of snacks. Rachel had her foam finger tucked securely under one arm, her isolation headphones resting around her neck, and a mountain of popcorn and drinks acting as a perfect, physical barrier in the incredibly tight seating arrangement. For an autistic nervous system that thrived on predictable boundaries, the snack fortress was actually an incredibly comforting perimeter.

Inside the ring, the dark matches delivered exactly what they promised.

There were no long, dramatic promos or complicated storylines—just pure, high-octane athleticism. The rookie tag team hit a dizzying sequence of dropkicks and arm drags against a pair of masked local wrestlers, sending the crowd into easy, rhythmic applause. Every time a wrestler hit the mat, the shockwave traveled down through the steel ring posts, vibrating the concrete floor beneath our boots.

It was a masterclass in crowd psychology. The AWE producers knew exactly how to warm up an audience, pacing the action so the cheers grew steadily louder with every near-fall and high-flying maneuver.

Rachel was mesmerized. She sat happily eating her popcorn, her eyes tracking every single footwork adjustment and wrist-lock in the ring. I leaned back in my padded chair, drinking a freezing cold soda and rubbing my tired legs, the physical exhaustion of carrying Jessica down the stairs temporarily masked by the sheer, buzzing energy of the arena.

The referee's hand hit the mat for the final three-count of the dark matches just as the digital clock above the entrance stage flipped to 5:55 PM.

The ring announcer climbed through the ropes, his voice echoing through the massive PA system.

"Ladies and gentlemen... we are five minutes away from going LIVE on national television! Portland, Oregon... ARE YOU READY FOR THE MONDAY NIGHT WAR?!"

The Rose City Center absolutely exploded. The dark matches had done their job perfectly. The crowd was hot, our snack perimeter was fully stocked, and the red light on the massive broadcast cameras began to blink.
