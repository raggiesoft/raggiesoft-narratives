The massive glass and steel facade of the Rose City Center loomed over us against the overcast, chilly Portland sky.

We made our way across the sprawling concrete plaza toward the box office. Jessica rolled smoothly beside us in her manual wheelchair, her breath visible in the damp November air. Kristin walked a half-step behind Rachel, keeping a protective, familiar proximity to her best friend, while I carried the collective anxiety of the group squarely on my shoulders.

When we reached the Will Call windows, the area was still relatively quiet. The doors wouldn't officially open to the 14,000 screaming fans for another two hours.

I stepped back, allowing Rachel to approach the glass.

When Tom had called the radio station to transfer the tickets out of his name, he had specifically designated Rachel as the primary pickup contact. It was a calculated, heavy move. I couldn't help but feel a dark twist in my gut thinking about it. Dad had been absolutely cruel to Rachel back in 2001, dismissing her and treating her neurodivergence like a burden. But now, five years later, he was forcing her to be the one to physically sign for his grand, expensive "olive branch." It felt like a trap wrapped in a gift box, but for tonight, we were playing along.

Rachel stepped up to the window. Due to her severe situational mutism, especially in unfamiliar public spaces, she didn't say a word. She just took a deep breath, pulled her Washington State ID from her wallet, and slid it quietly through the metal slot under the thick glass.

The box office attendant smiled, taking the ID and turning to a filing cabinet. A moment later, he returned, holding a thick, premium envelope stamped with the metallic AWE Monday Night WAR logo.

He slid the envelope, along with a clipboard, back through the slot. Rachel picked up the pen with a slightly trembling hand and signed her name on the release line.

"Alright, Rachel, you are all set," the attendant said through the speaker system. "Inside that envelope are your four front-row ringside tickets. You also have a VIP Merchandise Voucher. Because you won the premium radio package, I actually have two options for how you can redeem your gear today."

Rachel looked back at me, her eyes wide with sudden, overwhelming nervousness. The unexpected deviation from the standard script was throwing her processor off, and she was silently asking me to step in and process the logistics. I moved up to the glass, standing shoulder-to-shoulder with her, and firmly wrapped my arm around her. I applied a steady, deep pressure against her shoulder, giving her a physical anchor to help ground her racing nervous system.

"What are the options?" I asked, keeping my voice calm and level.

"Option A is the standard route," the attendant explained. "You can take that voucher to the main concourse right now, pick out your shirts and hats, and get it all out of the way before the crowds show up."

"And Option B?" Jessica asked, wheeling up slightly closer.

The attendant grinned. "Option B is a little more hands-on. AWE television production is actually looking for a small group of VIP fans to be extras in a pre-taped segment for tonight's broadcast. If you choose Option B, you have to sign a standard television release form right now. But in exchange, you get to be on national television, and your prize packs will be handed to you directly by one of the wrestlers on camera."

The entire group froze.

I turned my head. Kristin was already smiling, her eyes dancing with excitement. Jessica leaned back in her wheelchair, a knowing smirk crossing her face.

But I looked directly at Rachel. I could feel her trembling slightly beneath my arm, but it wasn't from anxiety anymore. AWE Monday Night WAR wasn't just a television show to her; it was her absolute favorite hyperfixation in the world. The sheer magnitude of what was just offered—the chance to not only meet the wrestlers but to actually exist inside the fictional universe she loved so much—was radiating off of her in waves of pure, unadulterated autistic joy.

She didn't have to speak. Her face said everything.

"We'll take Option B," I told the attendant without a single second of hesitation.

"Excellent choice," he said, sliding four thick, legal release waivers through the slot. "Sign these, and a segment producer will be right out to escort you onto the concourse."

Jessica, Kristin, and I quickly grabbed pens, but Rachel was the first one to finish signing her name. We were officially about to step onto the frontlines of the Monday Night WAR.
