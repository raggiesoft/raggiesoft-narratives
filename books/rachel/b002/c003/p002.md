Rachel and I had fallen into a steady, hypnotic rhythm at the far end of the mirrored room.

Dollyo Chagi. Block. Momtong Jireugi. Block.

It wasn't a fight; it was a metronome. The heavy, muffled thud of our foam pads connecting was the only sound we were making. We were entirely sealed off in our own quiet, regulated bubble, ignoring the absolute chaos happening in the center of the Dojang.

Out there, the neurotypical kids were actually sparring. The air was filled with aggressive kihaps, the sharp squeak of bare feet sliding across the linoleum, and Master Curtis aggressively barking corrections. They were trying to win; Rachel and I were just trying to survive until the clock ran out.

Then, a sickening, wet smack cut right through the noise, immediately followed by a sharp, violent gasp.

It wasn't a spirit shout. It was the sound of all the oxygen abruptly leaving a human body.

Rachel and I both froze, dropping our gloves.

In the center of the room, a boy named Daniel—a purple belt with a stripe who was about my age—was completely folded in half. He dropped heavily to his knees on the freezing tile, his foam helmet bouncing off the floor as he curled into the fetal position. Both of his padded gloves were securely clutched between his legs, and he was making a high-pitched, desperate wheezing sound as he tried to drag air back into his lungs.

Every guy in the room instinctively winced. It’s a universal, cross-cultural reaction. Getting kicked in the jewels is a paralyzing, nauseating shockwave of pain that completely short-circuits your brain.

But even with a direct hit, a hard plastic groin protector is designed to absorb the brunt of the trauma so you don't end up incapacitated on the floor.

"Kman!" Master Curtis bellowed, throwing his hand up to halt the rest of the matches.

The entire Dojang went dead silent, save for Daniel’s desperate gasping.

Master Curtis didn't rush over to check on him. He didn't drop to a knee to see if Daniel needed a medic or an ice pack. Instead, he walked slowly across the linoleum, his bare feet slapping the tile, and stood over the boy, his arms crossed tightly over his chest. He looked down at Daniel with pure, unadulterated disgust.

"Stand up, Daniel," Master Curtis ordered, his voice echoing off the massive mirror.

Daniel tried. He really did. He managed to push himself up onto one knee, his face pale and slick with sweat beneath the chin strap of his helmet, but his body betrayed him. He let out a choked sob and collapsed back onto his side.

"Where is your cup?" Master Curtis demanded coldly.

Daniel couldn't speak, but he weakly gestured toward the back of the room where our canvas gym bags were lined up against the wall.

A collective, sympathetic shudder rippled through the rest of the boys. He had forgotten his cup. He had stepped onto the sparring floor with absolutely zero armor protecting his most vulnerable target, and his sparring partner had just landed a flawless, unprotected front kick directly to the groin.

"So, you chose to step onto the mat unprepared," Master Curtis announced to the entire room, weaponizing Daniel’s agony into a public lecture. "You think the rules don't apply to you? You think gearing up is just a suggestion?"

Master Curtis began pacing around Daniel's curled-up form, gesturing sharply at him.

"In a real fight, there are no timeouts because you forgot your equipment. Your opponent does not care if you are hurting. This is what weakness looks like! This is what happens when you lack discipline!" Master Curtis yelled, completely ignoring the fact that Daniel was ten years old and currently fighting the urge to throw up on the linoleum. "If you cannot be responsible for your own armor, you deserve the pain!"

Rachel took a small half-step backward, pressing her shoulder against my arm. Her eyes were wide behind the cage of her foam helmet, terrified by the sheer volume of Master Curtis's voice.

I reached down through the heavy cotton fabric of my dobok pants and subtly tapped the hard plastic shell of my own genital cup, suddenly profoundly grateful for the uncomfortable, suffocating gear.

"Get off my floor," Master Curtis spat, looking down at Daniel one last time. "Crawl to your bag if you have to. The rest of you, Gyeorugi Junbi! Back to fighting!"

Daniel slowly, agonizingly dragged himself across the slick tile toward the perimeter of the room, utterly humiliated. Master Curtis didn't watch him go. He just clapped his hands, forcing the chaotic noise of the sparring matches to immediately resume.

I looked at Rachel. The bubble had popped. The reality of how dangerous this room was—and how little the adults cared about our safety—was suddenly blindingly obvious. I raised my heavy foam gloves back up, my muscles tense and shaking, and braced myself for her next kick.
