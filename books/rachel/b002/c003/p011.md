The triumphant, brass-heavy orchestral suite played over the scrolling blue credits of Return of the Guardians, filling the dark bedroom with a final wave of cinematic energy. As the music faded and the screen transitioned to the harsh, silent static of the tape's end, the exhaustion of the day finally crashed down on all of us.

Nobody made a move toward the door. The idea of untangling ourselves, walking across the cold hardwood hallway, and separating for the night felt entirely impossible.

Jessica let out a long, quiet yawn. She reached over my legs and pressed the heavy, plastic 'Rewind' button on the VCR. The machine responded with a loud, mechanical clunk, followed by the high-pitched, rhythmic whir of the tape spinning rapidly backward.

"Mom's going to be mad if we don't go to our own beds," Jessica whispered, her voice lacking any actual conviction.

"I'm not moving," Rachel murmured. She was already half-asleep, her cheek pressed against my shoulder, her eyes closed.

"Me neither," I agreed.

My bed was only a standard twin size—exactly thirty-eight inches wide. It was pushed flush against the side wall, positioned directly under the single window that looked out into the Virginia Beach night. Fitting a fourteen-year-old, a ten-year-old, and an eight-year-old onto a mattress designed for one was a logistical nightmare, but our family operated entirely on physical workarounds.

We didn't talk about it; we just shifted into formation.

I took the inside lane. I pressed my back flat against the cool plaster of the wall beneath the window sill, pulling my knees up slightly to accommodate the tight space. Rachel didn't even open her eyes. She just rolled with me, naturally slotting herself directly against my chest. I draped my arm over her, locking my hand against her side. Her breathing was already deep and even, the exhaustion of using her voice for the first time completely shutting her system down.

Jessica stood up from the floor, turned off the television to kill the static, and looked at the remaining sliver of mattress.

She climbed up onto the outside edge, carefully balancing on the precipice. She laid on her side facing us, acting as the physical barricade to keep Rachel and me from rolling off the bed. She pulled my heavy quilt up over all three of our shoulders, tucking the edges in tight.

Archie, realizing the floor was no longer the place to be, hopped up onto the very foot of the mattress. He curled into a tight black circle right on top of Jessica’s feet, letting his drool-soaked tennis ball roll softly onto the quilt. Down below, Baby let out a massive, rumbling sigh and collapsed onto the carpet, his enormous body taking up the entire floor space between the bed and the dresser.

The room was completely dark, save for the faint moonlight filtering through the window above my head.

It was the first time all three of us had crammed into a single bed together. In the past, if the world got too loud, I would retreat to the girls' room and curl up with Rachel on the bottom bunk while Jessica slept up top. But tonight was different. Tonight, the boundaries had dissolved.

The loud, rhythmic whir of the VCR rewinding the tape was a steady, mechanical lullaby. It drowned out the crickets outside and the memory of the Dojang entirely. Lying there squished against the wall, perfectly anchored by my sisters and the dogs, I closed my eyes. We were cramped, it was entirely improper, and Mom was definitely going to yell at us in the morning.

But as Rachel’s steady heartbeat thumped against my arm, I had never felt safer in my entire life.
