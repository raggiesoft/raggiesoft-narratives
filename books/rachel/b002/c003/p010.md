Rachel eventually guided her pixelated character past the final fortress of the current map, the screen fading to black. She let out a soft, satisfied sigh, dropping the grey SN-16 controller onto my carpet.

"Done," she announced quietly, her voice growing increasingly raspy as the evening wore on.

"Good job," Jessica said from her perch at the foot of my bed. She stretched her arms over her head, her flannel pajama pants rustling against the quilt. "Do you guys want to watch a movie before Mom yells at us to go to sleep?"

Rachel nodded emphatically.

I leaned forward and hit the power button on the Super Nova, the screen instantly turning to static. I reached down to the heavy, black VCR sitting on the bottom shelf of the TV stand. We had a massive, disorganized stack of VHS tapes piled next to it, mostly recorded off the television with handwritten labels, but at the very top were the pristine, official slipcovers of our favorite trilogy.

I grabbed the third tape in the stack: The Cosmic Rebellion: Return of the Guardians.

Watching The Cosmic Rebellion was a deeply regulating experience for us. We had watched the tapes so many times that I had the audio completely memorized. I knew exactly when the explosions were going to happen, I knew the precise pitch of the laser blasts, and I knew the exact rhythm of the orchestral score. There were no surprises, which meant my nervous system could completely relax.

I pushed the heavy plastic cassette into the VCR. It accepted the tape with a loud, mechanical clunk-whir, the screen briefly flashing tracking lines before the iconic, sweeping yellow text began crawling into the black void of space.

"You know," Jessica said, sliding off the bed and settling onto the carpet next to us, "Jimmy Miller at school told me there's a fourth movie."

I frowned, adjusting the volume dial on the television. "No there isn't. It's just The Cosmic Rebellion, The Sovereign's Wrath, and this one. The story is over."

"Jimmy said it was on TV a long time ago," Jessica insisted, pulling her knees to her chest as the spaceships flew across the screen. "He said his older brother saw it. It’s called The Winter Solstice Broadcast. He said it’s like two hours long, and the Scrap-Bears sing, and there’s a whole family of them, and it has cooking shows in it."

I stared at her, completely appalled by the concept. "That makes absolutely no sense. Why would the Scrap-Bears have a cooking show? They live in treehouses and fight the Sovereign's army with sticks."

"I don't know!" Jessica defended, laughing at my rigid logic. "That's just what Jimmy said. I think he's making it up. It sounds like a myth."

"It is a myth," I stated firmly, completely dismissing the idea. To my ten-year-old brain, the lore of the trilogy was sacred and contained entirely within the three VHS tapes sitting on my floor. The idea of a bizarre, musical holiday special floating around in the ether of 1970s television was a complete impossibility. "Jimmy is lying."

Rachel didn't care about Jimmy Miller or the mythical television broadcast.

As the movie started, she shimmied backward on the carpet, pressing her back against my chest again. I wrapped my arms around her instinctively, settling my chin lightly on top of her head. Archie the Lab readjusted his position, resting his heavy chin directly on Rachel's ankle, his one good eye lazily watching the bright flashes of light on the television.

For the next two hours, the room was completely at peace.

Rachel was completely mesmerized by the practical effects, pointing out the rubbery alien puppets in the desert palace and tracking the glowing energy swords of the Guardians. Whenever a loud, unexpected explosion rattled the cheap TV speakers, she would squeeze my arm, and I would hold her a little tighter to anchor her through the noise.

She didn't try to force full sentences anymore—she was too tired—but her new voice was still actively participating in the room.

When the tiny, furry Scrap-Bears finally appeared on the forest moon, coordinating their primitive traps against the shiny white armor of the Sovereign's troops, Rachel smiled.

"Scrap-Bears," she whispered, leaning her head back against me.

"Yeah," I whispered back.

When the movie finally ended, the triumphant, original orchestral music swelled over the forest celebration. There was no extra CGI, no polished scenes—just the raw, practical magic of a 1983 VHS tape playing exactly the way it always did.

Jessica leaned her head on my shoulder, Rachel drifted halfway to sleep against my chest, and Archie let out a soft, snoring breath through his tennis ball. The Dojang, Master Curtis, and the brackish water were a million miles away. In the quiet glow of the cathode-ray tube, the universe was perfectly in balance.
