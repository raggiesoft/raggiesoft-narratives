The house settled into its quiet, predictable evening rhythm.

I retreated to my bedroom and closed the door. Taking off my day clothes, I swapped the sweatpants for my favorite, softest pajamas. The fabric was worn paper-thin from hundreds of washes, completely devoid of any scratchy tags or stiff seams. I climbed onto my bed, pulling my knees to my chest, and let the quiet isolation of the room wash over me.

About ten minutes later, a soft, rhythmic knock-knock sounded at my door.

"Come in," I called out.

The door clicked open, and Jessica walked in, wearing an oversized t-shirt and flannel pajama pants. Rachel was right behind her, dressed in her own soft cotton nightgown, her hair completely freed from the tight Dojang bun and falling in a dark curtain over her shoulders. Archie, completely unbothered by the fact that he wasn't invited, squeezed through their legs and trotted into the room, his drool-soaked tennis ball still firmly wedged in his jaws.

Jessica sat at the foot of my bed, crossing her legs. Rachel didn't sit. She walked right up to the side of my mattress and stared at me.

"Play... Star... Brothers?" Rachel asked. Her voice was much quieter now, the sheer exhaustion of her new vocal cords making her raspy, but she forced the sentence out with deliberate focus.

"Yeah. We can play," I said, sliding off the mattress.

My television was sitting on a heavy wooden dresser, and directly underneath it was my prized possession: the Super Nova 16. It was a sleek, grey 16-bit console from Nova Gaming Corp that Mom and Dad had bought us for Christmas a few years ago.

I knelt on the carpet and grabbed the thick, black Code Wizard cartridge.

Rachel immediately sat down on the floor next to me, her eyes tracking my hands. She loved this part of the routine. The Code Wizard was a third-party cheat device. I plugged it directly into the SN-16’s cartridge slot, and then took the grey Super Star Brothers: World cartridge and snapped it firmly into the top of the Code Wizard.

I hit the power button. The screen flashed bright blue, bringing up the Code Wizard interface.

Playing video games the normal way was far too stressful for us. The timer running out caused sheer panic, losing a life felt like a massive failure, and the sudden, loud death music was a sensory nightmare. But with the Code Wizard, we could completely rewrite the rules of the digital world to make it safe.

I grabbed the grey, curved controller. "Read them out, Jess."

Jessica leaned over the edge of the bed, perfectly reciting the alphanumeric codes we had memorized. "C-2-2-2, dash, D-4-D-D."

I carefully used the D-pad to enter the letters. Infinite Lives. "D-F-C-E, dash, 6-4-A-0," Jessica continued.

I plugged it in. Always keep your power-up. "7-E-0-0, dash, 1-9-0-4," Jessica read. Then she smiled. "And D-D-A-6, dash, D-F-0-7."

I entered the final sequence and pressed start. Infinite Time. The blue screen faded to black, and the bright, cheerful title screen of Super Star Brothers: World popped up, the upbeat 16-bit music filling the bedroom.

I handed the controller to Rachel.

Normally, when we played games, she wouldn't say a word. She would just aggressively hand the controller back to me whenever a level got too hard or a jump was too wide. But tonight, she grabbed the controller, her thumb hovering over the bright purple buttons, and leaned back against my legs.

"I... am... player one," she declared softly, staring at the television.

"You're player one," I confirmed, sitting cross-legged on the floor right behind her. I didn't care that I wasn't playing.

Archie flopped down onto the carpet next to us, letting out a loud, dramatic sigh through his tennis ball. Up on the bed, Jessica rested her chin in her hands, watching the two of us with a soft, content smile.

Rachel pressed the start button, dropping her pixelated character onto the vibrant green map screen. She had infinite lives, infinite time, and absolute invincibility. There was no pressure, no timers, and no Master Curtis to yell at her if she missed a jump. She was completely safe.

"Jump," Rachel whispered to herself as she pressed the button, successfully navigating her character over a pixelated gap. She leaned her head back against my knee, fully settling into the environment.

We sat there for an hour, the soft glow of the television illuminating the dark bedroom. It had been the most exhausting, terrifying, and massive day of our entire lives. But as I listened to my little sister quietly narrate her way through the colorful levels, perfectly safe and armed with her brand-new voice, I knew we had won.
