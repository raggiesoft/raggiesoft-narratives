We didn't stay in the Dojang a second longer than we had to. We shoved our heavy, sweaty sparring gear into our canvas bags and climbed the metal stairs to the second-floor observation deck.

The observation deck was a long, narrow balcony overlooking the massive gymnastics floor. It was lined with three tiers of wooden bleacher seating, but it was a completely hostile design. There were no backrests. To sit on the first or second row meant sitting completely unsupported, forcing your core to constantly balance while kids kicked the wood behind you. I absolutely hated it.

I led Rachel straight to the top row, navigating all the way to the far right corner.

This was our spot. Up here, my back was pressed flat against the painted cinderblock wall, and my right shoulder was flush against the side wall. It was a perfect, 90-degree corner of solid, unmoving architecture.

Rachel immediately dropped her gym bag and climbed up beside me. She didn't hesitate. She wedged herself perfectly into my right side, turning slightly so she could press her back against my chest. I reached my right arm around her torso, locking my hand securely over her stomach to hold her in place.

It used to be just a mechanical workaround to survive loud places like Dad's truck or the mall, but sitting here overlooking the cavernous gym, I realized something had shifted. I loved it. I loved the solid, grounding weight of her against my chest. I loved that out of everyone in the world, I was the structure that made her feel safe. It was comfortable, and my racing heart finally began to slow down.

Down below, the gymnastics floor was a blur of motion. Fourteen-year-old Jessica and our cousin Janette were practicing on the vault, their bodies launching through the air with effortless coordination.

"Jessica is fast," a small, raspy voice vibrated against my chest.

I smiled, tightening my arm around her stomach. "Yeah. She is."

Rachel was staring down at the floor, her dark eyes tracking our older sister. For the first few minutes, she had just whispered single words, testing the friction of her vocal cords. But now, the dam had completely broken. She had eight years of locked-away observations, opinions, and thoughts, and she was frantically trying to catch up.

"She jumped really high," Rachel added, her voice cracking slightly as she experimented with the volume. "The floor is bouncy down there."

"It has springs under it," I explained quietly. I didn't want to talk too much. I just wanted to listen to her.

"Springs," she echoed, testing the shape of the word. She took a deep breath, the movement expanding her stomach against my hand. "It smells like dust up here. And salt."

"That's from the water fountain."

"Yucky," she stated firmly.

I let out a soft laugh. "Yeah. Super yucky."

We sat in our 90-degree corner for the next thirty minutes, completely insulated from the noise of the gym. I acted as her human backrest and her captive audience while she narrated everything she saw. She pointed out the colors of the mats, complained about the stiff fabric of her dobok, and firmly declared that she was never going back into Master Curtis's room ever again. I agreed with every single word.

Eventually, the buzzer blared down on the floor. Jessica and Janette grabbed their water bottles and waved up at us from the mats, signaling that their class was over.

We grabbed our bags and headed down to the lobby.

Aunt Janet was already waiting by the front glass doors. Mom’s sister had drawn the short straw for carpool duty today. She waved us over, her keys jingling in her hand. Jessica and Janette pushed through the double doors from the gym a few seconds later, their faces flushed and covered in a fine layer of white chalk dust.

"Good class, girls?" Aunt Janet asked brightly as we all piled out the front doors and into the humid August heat toward her sedan.

"It was fine," Jessica said, tossing her bag into the trunk. "I finally nailed the back handspring on the beam."

We scrambled into the back seat. I slid into the middle, and Rachel immediately pressed herself against my side by the window. Jessica took the front passenger seat, while Janette sat on the other side of me.

Aunt Janet started the engine and pulled out onto Professional Place, glancing at us in the rearview mirror. "How was karate, Michael? Did Master Ray teach you guys anything new?"

I opened my mouth to give my usual, flat one-word answer, but I didn't get the chance.

"Master Curtis was mean," Rachel announced from beside me.

Aunt Janet slammed on the brakes.

The car violently jerked to a halt at the red light. In the front passenger seat, Jessica whipped her head around so fast her ponytail smacked the headrest. She stared at Rachel, her eyes wide with absolute, unfiltered shock. Janette gasped quietly from my left side.

Rachel blinked, completely unfazed by the dramatic reaction she had just caused. She looked right at Jessica.

"He yelled," Rachel continued, her raspy voice filling the dead silence of the sedan. "He said Michael was weak. And I told him he was mean. Because he is."

Jessica’s jaw dropped. She looked from Rachel, to me, and back to Rachel. She had spent the last eight years translating Rachel's silent gestures, fighting Mom and Dad's rules, and protecting our little bubble. Hearing Rachel speak in full, defiant sentences was like watching the laws of physics suddenly reverse.

"You... you talked to him?" Jessica whispered, her voice trembling.

"Yes," Rachel nodded firmly. She leaned her head against my shoulder. "Michael held my hand. I talked."

Aunt Janet was still staring in the rearview mirror, completely paralyzed, the traffic light above us turning green without her noticing. "Oh my god," she breathed. "Ellen is going to lose her mind."

Jessica didn't care about the traffic light. A massive, brilliant smile broke across her face, and she reached her hand back between the front seats to gently squeeze Rachel's knee.

"Good for you, Rachel," Jessica said fiercely, her eyes shining with tears. "He is mean. You tell him."

The rest of the ride back to Delaney Street was the loudest car trip we had ever taken. Aunt Janet was still in shock, but Jessica completely took over, asking Rachel question after question just to hear her answer. And Rachel, leaning safely against my side, answered every single one.
