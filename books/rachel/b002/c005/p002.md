By half-past eleven, the Delaney Street living room had been completely reconfigured. We had pushed the heavy oak coffee table flush against the front of the sofa, creating a wide, open clearing of carpet right in the center of the room.

Jessica was camped out near Dad's oversized recliner, already deep into her pre-gymnastics routine. She was pressed flat into a flawless center split, casually reading a paperback novel as if her legs weren't currently stretched to impossible, agonizing angles. She had to be at *Tidewater Tumblers* by two o'clock, and she never went into a five-hour training block with cold muscles.

For Rachel and me, the living room floor was our makeshift dojang.

I was holding a heavy, rectangular foam kick shield against my chest. The blue vinyl was worn and scuffed from years of backyard practice. We had been at this for a long time. At ten and eight years old, Rachel and I were both Brown Belts. We had tested through White, Purple, and Green every three months, and survived the grueling six-month wait to earn our Brown *tti*. We weren't beginners; we had the muscle memory of seasoned students.

"Alright," I said, stepping back to the edge of the carpet and tapping the sides of the foam pad. I dropped into my best instructor cadence. *"Cha-ryeot!"*

Rachel instantly snapped to attention. She pulled her heels sharply together and pinned her hands flat against her sides. She stood entirely still, her posture rigid and focused.

*"Kyeong-rye,"* I commanded.

We both bowed at the waist, never breaking eye contact.

"Narani Junbi Sogi," I called out.

Rachel shifted her left foot, bringing her feet shoulder-width apart, and brought her fists up in front of her belt level in a perfect Parallel Ready Stance. Her dark eyes locked dead-center on the blue foam target.

"Let's warm up the kicks," I instructed, shifting the pad to the side. *"Dollyo Chagi*. Show me the Roundhouse. *Shi-jak!*"

Rachel pivoted on her planted foot, her hips snapping over with practiced, fluid precision. Her instep slammed into the center of the kick shield with a loud, sharp *smack*. The impact reverberated up my arms. She had serious power for an eight-year-old.

"Good," I praised, stepping back to give her space to reset. "Keep your guard up. *I-dan Ap Chagi!* Jumping Front Kick. *Shi-jak!*"

Rachel leaped off the carpet, driving her back knee up for momentum and snapping her front foot forward, kicking the shield dead in the center before landing perfectly balanced on two feet.

"Nice form, Rachel," Jessica called out from the floor, not even looking up from her paperback book.

"Okay, let's run a quick block drill before we do the shout," I said. "Drop into *Juchum Sogi*."

Rachel widened her stance, bending her knees deep into the Horse Riding Stance.

*"Arae Makgi,"* I commanded, swinging the bottom edge of the foam pad toward her waist.

Rachel's arm swept down in a flawless Low Block, deflecting the pad away.

*"Olgul Makgi,"* I called, bringing the pad up high toward her head.

Her forearm snapped upward, executing a perfect High Block to protect her face.

*"Kman,"* I instructed, stopping the drill. *"Ba-ro."*

Rachel immediately returned to her Parallel Ready Stance, breathing evenly. We knew the *hyung* patterns and the terminology backward and forward.

"Okay, now the real test," I said, lowering the pad for a second and grinning at her. "Master Curtis is going to want to hear you today. We need to practice the *kihap*. No holding back."

For years, the *kihap*—the mandatory spirit shout—had been Rachel's ultimate enemy. The situational mutism had locked her throat down every single time she stepped onto the mats. But yesterday, she had broken the lock. She had found her voice, and she was entirely ready to weaponize it.

I stepped forward and held the heavy pad flat against my stomach.

"Back into *Juchum Sogi*," I commanded.

Rachel dropped back into the wide squat. She pulled her right fist back to her hip, ready to strike.

"Give me a *Momtong Jireugi*," I challenged her, bracing myself behind the pad. "Middle punch. And I want to hear it. *Shi-jak!*"

Rachel stepped into the punch, driving her fist forward with her entire body weight behind it. She slammed her knuckles into the foam shield and opened her mouth.

"KIHAP!"

The shout tore through the living room. It was incredibly raspy, the vocal cords still raw from their debut the night before, but it was loud, fierce, and completely unapologetic. It wasn't a squeak, and it wasn't a whisper. It was the battle cry of a Brown Belt who finally had all of her weapons online.

Archie, who had been sleeping under the dining room table, lifted his head and let out an approving "woof" at the noise.

Jessica slammed her paperback book shut and started clapping from the floor. "Yes! That was awesome!"

Rachel pulled her fist back, a massive, glowing smile breaking across her face. She was practically vibrating with adrenaline and pride. She took a deep breath, letting the sound of her own voice echo in her ears.

"I... am... ready," Rachel rasped, tapping her knuckles against the foam shield.

"You're going to blow the roof off the Dojang tonight," I promised her, tossing the kick shield onto the sofa and wrapping my arm around her shoulders.

We were completely dialed in. Our stances were solid, and Rachel's voice was officially ready for deployment. In a few hours, we would put on our crisp white *doboks* and tie our Brown belts around our waists. The clock was slowly ticking toward our five o'clock class, and for the first time in my life, I couldn't wait to get there.
