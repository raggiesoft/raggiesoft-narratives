"Mom, you need to go inside and pull Michael and Rachel out of that Dojang right now," Jessica demanded before Mom could even put the car in drive.

Mom blinked, startled by the sudden hostility from the passenger seat. "Excuse me? Jessica, tone it down. What are you talking about?"

I sat rigid in the middle of the back seat. Rachel immediately pressed her face into my shoulder, curling her knees up onto the upholstery to protect her left side. Janette quickly slid into the seat right beside her, wrapping a fiercely protective arm across Rachel's back, boxing us into a safe corner.

"Master Curtis stepped on them," Jessica declared, pointing a shaking finger toward the back seat. "He walked down the line during leg lifts and stepped with his full body weight onto their stomachs. He intentionally lingered on Rachel to hurt her because he was mad about Tuesday. They couldn't even walk to the car, Mom! We had to carry them. They are both in agonizing pain."

Mom shifted the car into park and turned around to look at us.

I expected her to gasp. I expected the fierce, maternal outrage that usually accompanied any threat to her children. I expected her to unbuckle her seatbelt, march through those double glass doors, and demand Master Ray fire his subordinate on the spot.

Instead, Mom just sighed.

It was the heavy, exhausted sigh of a parent who had completely bought into the 1990s suburban martial arts culture, heavily reinforced by whatever conversation she'd had with Dad before picking us up.

"Jessica, calm down," Mom said, her voice dropping into a patronizing, soothing register that instantly made the nausea flare in my gut. "Dad already warned me they would complain tonight. It's a martial arts class. It's not supposed to be easy."

Jessica's jaw literally dropped. "Mom, he is a two-hundred-pound man! Rachel is eight years old! Look at her!"

"It's a standard conditioning drill, Jess," Mom reasoned, turning back to the steering wheel and shifting the car into drive. "The other parents talk about it in the lobby all the time. It teaches them how to brace for impact. It builds discipline. Master Curtis knows what he's doing."

"He did it to punish them!" Jessica yelled, entirely losing her battle to stay calm as the sedan pulled away from the curb.

"They need to toughen up," Mom countered sharply, her patience snapping. She shut down the argument with the ultimate, unquestionable adult logic. "If Michael and Rachel are going to earn their black belts, they can't cry every time a drill gets hard. It builds character."

In the front seat, Jessica fell completely silent. She stared out the passenger window, her hands gripping her gym bag so tightly her knuckles were stark white. She was the smartest person I knew, capable of out-arguing almost anyone, but she was structurally trapped. She was just a fourteen-year-old girl. She had no legal authority, no financial leverage, and no way to override the impenetrable wall of adult gaslighting.

In the back seat, the absolute dismissal of our trauma was the final, devastating blow.

Rachel realized that the adults who were supposed to protect us actively believed that being physically crushed was good for us. The safe perimeter of our family had just been completely invalidated.

Rachel didn't cry silently anymore.

She let out a loud, agonizing, open wail of pure despair. The sound filled the small cabin of the sedan, sharp and terrifying. But the wail instantly caught in her throat. The deep breath required to scream violently stretched the crushed, bruised muscles of her side, sending a blinding spike of agony straight through her chest. Her wail violently cut off into a choked, panicked shriek of pain.

"Rachel, please stop that," Mom commanded over her shoulder, clearly annoyed by the volume.

Rachel couldn't stop. She was entirely broken, trapped in a horrific loop where the emotional need to cry was triggering excruciating physical pain. She wrapped her arms tightly around my torso, sobbing in short, hyperventilating gasps, her hot tears soaking straight through my uniform.

Every single time her body heaved against me, the shockwave transferred directly into the deep, tearing fire in the center of my own gut. The pain was blinding, white-hot, and nauseating.

But I didn't say a word. I didn't push her away.

Beside us, Janette leaned over, pressing her forehead against my shoulder and stroking Rachel’s hair with a trembling hand, whispering soft, desperate reassurances that Mom completely ignored.

I just wrapped both of my arms around my little sister, pulling her as tightly against my chest as my ruined stomach would allow. I glared at the back of my mother's head in the rearview mirror, an intense, freezing realization settling over me. The adult world was completely blind, and the only people who would ever truly protect us were the two teenage girls sitting in this car.
