I picked up the heavy blue kick shield from the sofa and tossed it to Rachel.

She caught it against her chest with both hands. Even though she was only eight years old and significantly smaller than me, she knew exactly how to absorb an impact. She immediately stepped her feet wide and dropped her weight down into a flawless *Juchum Sogi*, bracing her center of gravity to act as my target.

I stepped back to the edge of the carpet. Earning my Brown *tti* had taken twice as long as the other kids in my age bracket, and every single stripe on it was a hard-fought negotiation with my own body.

*"Cha-ryeot,"* Rachel commanded, her raspy voice eagerly taking on the cadence of the instructor.

I pulled my heels together and pinned my hands to my sides, standing at attention.

"Kyeong-rye." We bowed.

*"Gyeorugi Junbi,"* Rachel called out.

I stepped back and brought my fists up to my face. My Fighting Ready Stance was technically correct, but it lacked the loose, bouncing fluidity Rachel had naturally displayed. Cerebral palsy didn't just mean my muscles were weak; it meant they were spastic. The signals from my brain were constantly caught in traffic, causing the muscles in my legs and arms to fight against each other instead of working together.

*"Momtong Jireugi,"* Rachel instructed, bracing the pad against her stomach. *"Shi-jak!"*

I stepped forward and drove my fist into the center of the foam shield.

*"KIHAP!"* I shouted.

The impact was heavy, but the motion was completely rigid. Where Rachel’s punches snapped like a whip, mine felt like pushing a heavy boulder. My shoulder tightened, my elbow locked a fraction of a second too late, and the kinetic chain stopped dead at my waist. I tried. I really tried. I understood the mechanics perfectly in my head, but translating them through my nervous system was like trying to run underwater.

"Good power," Rachel encouraged, her dark eyes entirely sincere over the top edge of the pad. *"Dollyo Chagi. Shi-jak!"*

I pivoted on my left foot to throw the Roundhouse kick. The moment my weight shifted to a single leg, the spasticity in my hamstrings flared up. I swung my right leg around, but my hip refused to turn over completely. The instep of my foot hit the pad with a blunt, clumsy thud, my balance wavering precariously as I fought to return my foot to the floor without stumbling.

"Keep your guard up by your chin," Jessica corrected from the floor, leaning forward over her stretched legs. "Your arms dropped when you kicked."

"Right," I muttered, shaking my arms out and resetting my stance.

Then came the absolute hardest part of the curriculum.

Rachel took a step back and held the kick shield up slightly higher. *"I-dan Ap Chagi,"* she commanded.

The Jumping Front Kick.

I took a deep, steadying breath. I ran two quick steps forward, drove my back knee upward to generate momentum, and launched myself off the carpet.

For a split second, I had the height. I was airborne. But the moment my brain sent the rapid-fire command for my right leg to extend and strike the pad, the neurological wall slammed down. The spasticity completely hijacked the signal. My thigh muscles seized, and my knee locked into a rigidly bent angle. No matter how hard I tried to force it, my leg simply refused to kick out.

I crashed back down onto the carpet, landing heavy and flat-footed, missing the blue foam shield entirely.

I let out a frustrated breath, dropping my hands to my knees. "My leg got stuck."

Rachel didn't offer any pity. She didn't lower the pad, and she didn't look at me with sympathy. She just tapped the center of the blue vinyl with her knuckles.

"Again," Rachel rasped quietly.

"You got the air, Michael," Jessica added from the recliner, turning a page in her paperback book. "Your takeoff was perfect. You just have to breathe through the transition in the air so your muscles don't panic."

I nodded, standing back up and returning to the edge of the carpet.

Master Curtis would have barked at me. He would have told me I wasn't focusing hard enough, or he would have made me drop to the floor and do pushups for failing to execute the technique. But here in the living room, surrounded by my pack, the failure wasn't a moral failing. It was just gravity and biology.

*"I-dan Ap Chagi,"* Rachel called out again, her voice steady and supportive. *"Shi-jak!"*

I ran forward and launched myself off the carpet again. My leg seized up exactly as it had before, locking tight in the air, and I landed heavily on the floor without striking the pad. But as I caught my balance, I didn't feel the suffocating weight of disappointment. I just reset my stance, locked eyes with Rachel, and prepared to try again.
