"Stand up! Fall in!" Master Curtis barked, clapping his hands together with a sharp, echoing crack.

The leg lifts were finally over. Around the room, the adult students and the older teenagers pushed themselves off the freezing linoleum, exhaling heavy breaths and returning to their ready stances.

I rolled onto my side and forced myself up to my knees. The pain in my stomach wasn't the dull, generalized burn of a tough workout; it was a sharp, tearing fire directly where the heel of his foot had dug deep into my gut. Something inside felt profoundly wrong, like a thick rubber band had been stretched too far and snapped. I clutched my midsection for a fraction of a second, but instantly dropped my hands and swallowed the groan building in my throat.

I couldn't let him see how badly it hurt. I knew if he saw I was broken, he would put me right back on the floor.

I managed to stand and immediately looked to my left.

Rachel was still on the floor. She was curled slightly on her side, her small hands clutching the heavy white cotton of her *dobok* tightly against her left side. Her dark eyes were wide, completely dilated with a pure, paralyzing terror, and locked desperately onto me. She was taking tiny, shallow gasps of air, terrified to let her chest expand fully. She couldn't get up. The sheer physical trauma of the lingering step, combined with the absolute psychological horror of being targeted, had completely short-circuited her system.

In a traditional Dojang, breaking formation to assist another student was a massive violation of protocol. You were supposed to maintain absolute, stoic discipline.

I didn't care. I broke formation.

Ignoring the sickening throb in my own core, I took two quick steps over to her, reached down, and firmly grasped her upper arm.

"I've got you," I whispered, pulling her up.

She leaned heavily against my leg the second her bare feet touched the tile, her breathing jagged and completely out of rhythm. I kept my hand securely on her shoulder, providing a physical anchor to keep her upright.

"How touching," a cold, grating voice sneered across the room.

Master Curtis was staring directly at us, his arms crossed over his chest, his eyes dripping with absolute contempt. The entire class froze.

"You are weak, Michael," Master Curtis announced loudly, ensuring every single student in the room heard the verdict. "If she cannot stand up on her own, she does not belong on my floor. You aren't helping her; you are enabling her weakness. You both lack the discipline required to wear those belts."

A sudden, blinding spike of hot, defensive rage surged into my throat. I wanted to scream at him. I wanted to point out that she was only eight years old, that she was tiny, and that what he had just done was cruel and unnatural. Every protective instinct I possessed demanded that I open my mouth and defend my kid sister.

But I bit my tongue so hard I tasted copper.

My analytical brain ran the terrifying, immediate calculation. If I talked back—if I broke the absolute rule of submitting to the instructor—the punishment wouldn't be a verbal lecture. He could order us back onto the floor. He could step on us again, or worse, he could isolate Rachel and force her to spar with one of the older, heavier teenagers as "conditioning." The safest, most logical path for our immediate physical survival was complete, agonizing silence.

I lowered my eyes to the linoleum, keeping my hand firmly on Rachel's shoulder, and swallowed the absolute humiliation.

"Return to your position. *Junbi Sogi*," Master Curtis commanded sharply.

I stepped back into my designated spot, and Rachel mirrored me, dropping into the ready stance.

I looked down at the heavy, solid brown belts tied around our waists. In the specific hierarchy of Tidewater Tumblers, the color progression was strict: White, Purple, Green, Brown, Red, and finally, the Midnight Blue belt that served as our equivalent of a black belt.

We were Brown Belts. We didn't even have the single stripe of black electrical tape that denoted the second degree of the rank. Yet, in the eyes of Master Curtis, the color of the dyed canvas around our waists meant we were supposed to be near-experts. We were expected to be hardened martial artists, fully capable of absorbing whatever physical and psychological abuse he decided to dish out.

He didn't see an eight-year-old girl and a ten-year-old boy. He only saw the brown fabric, and he used it as a license to break us.

"Basic punching drills! *Juchum Sogi*! Horse stance!" Master Curtis ordered.

For the next sixty minutes, the Dojang became a slow-motion, waking nightmare of hiding our agony.

Every single time I threw a punch, the sharp rotation of my hips felt like a hot knife twisting directly in the center of my stomach. My muscles violently locked up, threatening to pull me to the floor, and a cold sweat broke out down the back of my neck. But I forced my face to stay perfectly blank. I couldn't wince. I couldn't grab my gut. I had to pretend the fire inside me didn't exist. Every front kick pulled agonizingly against my torn middle, my vision swimming with the effort of keeping my balance.

Beside me, Rachel moved like a ghost. She threw her blocks and her strikes on absolute autopilot, but her movements were stiff and incredibly shallow. She was terrified to twist her torso, her left arm staying entirely too close to her side to protect her ribs. Her face was pale, and her jaw was clamped completely shut. Her situational mutism was locked in permanently for the evening. Every few minutes, her dark eyes would dart sideways to check my position, confirming that her anchor was still there.

We didn't look at the mirrors, and we didn't look at Master Curtis.

We just focused entirely on the ticking clock on the back wall, desperately trying to mask the heavy, persistent ache in our stomachs, silently wondering if the deep, bruising pain inside us was ever going to go away.
