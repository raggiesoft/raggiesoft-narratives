By half past eight, the adrenaline of the day had completely burned off, leaving the house heavy with the impending weight of Monday morning.

Aunt Janet stood near the front door, pulling her car keys from her purse. Maria and Katherine stood quietly beside her. They only lived about ten minutes away in another neighborhood of Virginia Beach, but after the revelations in the kitchen, the short drive felt like leaving a fortress.

"Alright, Janette," Aunt Janet said gently, looking over at her oldest daughter sitting on the floor mattress. "Last chance. Do you want to come home and sleep in your own bed tonight?"

Janette didn't even hesitate. She shook her head, her expression locked in absolute determination. "I'm staying on the perimeter. The pack stays together."

Aunt Janet offered a sad, understanding smile and nodded. "Okay. I'll be back tomorrow afternoon." She looked at Mom. "I'll bring the boys over with me, Ellen. They've been pacing the house looking for the kids."

Mom let out a tired sigh, rubbing her temples. "Okay. Just make sure they are leashed when they come through the door. We have to do this slowly."

The "boys" were our dogs, Baby and Archie. They were Black Labradors, brothers born exactly a year apart, but they couldn't have been more physically different. Archie was an average-sized Lab, sweet and endlessly goofy, easily identifiable by his left eye, which was blind and a cloudy, muddy blue. His entire personality revolved around bringing you a toy—usually whichever toy you had just handed to him seconds before.

Baby, on the other hand, was an absolute genetic anomaly. He was the size of a small Great Dane, a massive wall of black muscle. He was gentle, but he was clumsy. The thought of a hundred-and-thirty-pound dog accidentally stepping on Rachel's bruised spleen or my torn fascia was terrifying. Reintroducing them to the living room was going to be a highly delicate operation.

"I'll keep them calm," Aunt Janet promised. She gave Maria and Katherine a gentle push toward the door.

Grandma Loretta walked out of the kitchen, carrying a small overnight bag. "I'm heading upstairs, Ellen. I'll sleep in Michael's bed tonight so I'm out of the way, but I will be right at the table when you start making those phone calls tomorrow."

"Thank you, Mom," Mom whispered, pulling her mother into a tight hug.

With the extended family gone, it was time for the final, grueling logistics of the day.

Dad and Mom initiated the bathroom protocol one last time. Dad executed the rigid plank carry for me, protecting my dignity and my core, while Mom did the exact same for Rachel. By the time we were safely deposited back onto the foam wedges on the sofa bed, we were both completely exhausted.

Jessica and Janette took their turns in the downstairs bathroom, changing into baggy t-shirts and flannel pajama pants before returning to the living room to take up their posts on the floor mattresses.

"Alright, base camp, time to get flat," Dad announced.

Dad slid his arms under my back and knees, taking my full weight. "Take the wedge, Jess."

Jessica quickly pulled the dense medical foam out from behind me, and Dad slowly, carefully lowered my upper body until my spine was perfectly flat against the queen mattress. Mom and Janette executed the exact same synchronized maneuver for Rachel. The moment gravity stopped pulling down on our internal organs, a collective, silent sigh of relief washed over the bed.

"If you need anything, you yell," Dad reminded us, doing his final check of the deadbolts.

"We've got the watch, Uncle Tom," Janette promised from her spot on the floor.

"Goodnight, pack," Mom whispered softly.

We listened to the heavy, rhythmic creak of the wooden stairs. A moment later, the door to Mom and Dad's bedroom clicked shut. Across the upstairs hallway, the door to my bedroom clicked shut as Grandma Loretta turned in for the night.

The heavy, complicated burden of adult guilt and legal warfare vanished with the sound of the latches. The downstairs officially belonged to the pack once again.

Jessica reached up from the floor and carefully adjusted the three firm body pillows running down the center of our bed, making sure the barrier was perfectly straight. Janette reached over and clicked off the reading lamp, plunging the room into darkness, save for the faint orange glow of the streetlights filtering through the blinds.

I was lying flat on my back. I reached my left hand over the cotton mountain. A second later, Rachel’s fingers laced through mine. Beneath the heavy quilt, her heel hooked firmly against my left ankle.

The perimeter was locked.

For a long moment, the only sound was the soft hum of the central air conditioning.

Then, Rachel squeezed my hand.

"Bear?" she whispered.

Her raspy voice cut through the dark. She hadn't made a single sound while Mom, Dad, Aunt Janet, or Grandma Loretta were in the room. The selective mutism was an impenetrable armor against the adults who had failed her. But here, surrounded entirely by the pack, the vault door swung wide open.

"I'm right here, Bug," I whispered back, turning my head toward the pillow wall.

"I don't want Baby to jump on me tomorrow," Rachel admitted softly, the genuine fear of our massive dog slipping into her tone.

"I won't let him," Jess promised immediately from the left side of the room, her voice floating up from her floor mattress. "Janette and I will sit right on the edge of the bed. Baby and Archie won't even be able to get close to your pillows."

"We'll form a wall," Janette agreed fiercely from my right.

Rachel let out a slow, shuddering exhale, the tension bleeding out of her shoulders. She trusted Jessica and Janette implicitly. If they said the dogs wouldn't get past them, it was an absolute fact.

"My ribs are really tired," Rachel murmured, her voice growing heavy with sleep.

"Just breathe shallow, Bug," I told her softly, rubbing my thumb across her knuckles. "No more moving until tomorrow. We made it."

"We made it," Rachel echoed faintly.

We were broken, grounded, and facing four months of agonizing recovery. Tomorrow, the adults were going to go to war to make Curtis and Ray pay for what they did. But as I closed my eyes, anchored to my little sister and guarded by my oldest sister and cousin, I wasn't afraid of the morning.

The pack was holding the line.
