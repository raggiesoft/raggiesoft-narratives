As Sunday evening set in, the physical reality of lying on a mattress for three days straight began to catch up with us. The hospital smell—a sterile mix of iodine, rubbing alcohol, and medical tape—was clinging to our skin, mixing with the stale sweat of our own fear.

We couldn't stand in a shower. We couldn't sit up in a bathtub.

Just after eight o'clock, Mom walked into the living room carrying two plastic basins filled with warm, soapy water and a stack of clean, folded towels. Dad followed right behind her, carrying fresh medical binders and clean, oversized t-shirts.

"Alright, base camp," Dad announced quietly. "Hygiene protocol."

At ten years old, the immediate realization of what was about to happen made my chest tighten. My privacy had been heavily guarded during the bathroom routine, but this was different. We were in the middle of the living room. Jessica, Janette, Maria, and Katherine were all sitting on the floor mattresses.

Dad saw the panic flash in my eyes. He didn't hesitate. He took absolute command of the room's geometry.

"Girls, clear the room," Dad ordered, his tone brooking zero arguments. "Kitchen. Now."

Jessica immediately stood up, ushering Janette, Maria, and Katherine out of the living room. The heavy wooden door swung shut behind them, sealing the space.

"Rachel," Mom said gently, stepping to the left side of the pillow barrier. "You are going to roll your head to the left and look at the wall. Michael, you are going to look to the right. You do not look at each other until we say so."

I immediately turned my head, staring at the blank drywall next to the front door. I couldn't see Rachel at all over the three body pillows, but the enforced rule completely eliminated the terrifying anxiety of accidental exposure.

"We are doing this in sections," Dad explained, stepping up to my side of the bed. He was establishing the same clinical, gender-divided protocol we had used for the bathroom. He was my caregiver; Mom was Rachel's.

Dad draped a heavy, warm towel over my chest and stomach, completely covering me before he even unfastened the thick velcro of my medical binder.

"I am only washing what is exposed," Dad murmured, his voice low and incredibly respectful. "You stay completely covered the whole time."

He moved with total clinical efficiency. He washed my arms, then dried them. He carefully slid the towel down just enough to clean my chest, avoiding the massive, purple hematoma and the frightening bulge of the hernia altogether. Then he covered my chest again before moving to my legs. I never felt exposed. I never felt like a patient on display.

On the other side of the cotton wall, I could hear the soft wring of a washcloth as Mom executed the exact same "under-the-towel" method for Rachel.

"Does the warm water hurt your ribs, sweetie?" Mom asked softly.

Silence.

Facing her side of the room, Rachel kept her jaw locked shut tight. The invisible vault door was impenetrable. Mom was the one who had driven us to the Dojang on Thursday. Mom was the one who had told us to go back inside when we tried to escape. The betrayal was still too raw, and Rachel absolutely refused to give her parents the grace of her voice.

I heard Mom let out a shaky, heartbreaking exhale, the guilt weighing her down.

I kept my eyes locked on the drywall to my right. "Rachel," I called out firmly. "Tap the mattress twice if it hurts."

For a second, there was nothing. Then, I heard the soft *pat-pat* of Rachel’s right hand tapping against the edge of the mattress.

"It doesn't hurt her, Mom," I translated, acting as the absolute bridge between my selectively mute sister and the adults she currently resented. "She's okay."

"Thank you, Michael," Mom whispered, her voice thick with unshed tears.

The sheer relief of being clean was incredible. The warm water soothed the rigid tension in my muscles. When Dad finally pulled a clean, oversized t-shirt over my head and carefully re-fastened the fresh medical binder around my core, I felt human again.

"Alright," Dad announced, stepping back and picking up his plastic basin of soapy water. "You're good."

I turned my head back toward the center of the bed. Rachel was already looking at me, wearing a huge, baggy t-shirt that swallowed her small frame. Her dark eyes were perfectly calm.

I reached over the pillow barrier. Rachel met me halfway, our fingers lacing together. Our skin smelled like standard bar soap and clean cotton, the terrifying, sterile scent of the hospital finally washed away.

Dad walked over to the kitchen door and pushed it open. "All clear. Guards, you can resume your posts."

Jessica and Janette filed back into the living room, immediately retaking their positions on the floor mattresses as if nothing had happened. The indignity of the sponge bath had been completely neutralized by the adults' militant enforcement of our boundaries.

The base camp was clean, the perimeter was secure, and Sunday was officially over. We closed our eyes, holding tight to each other in the dark, bracing ourselves for the storm that Mom and Dad were going to unleash in the morning.
