The heavy wooden door connecting the kitchen to the living room slowly pushed open.

Maria and Katherine walked back into the living room, but their entire demeanor had drastically shifted. Before they went into the kitchen, they were just two young girls coming over to check on their sick cousins. Now, they knew the truth. They knew that monsters were real, that they wore bare feet instead of claws, and that the living room wasn't just a recovery space—it was a bunker.

Jessica hit the pause button on the VCR remote. The animated lions froze on the CRT screen.

Maria and Katherine didn't run over to the bed. They didn't ask a million rapid-fire questions. Instead, they moved with a quiet, reverent caution, treating the space around the queen-sized sofa bed like it was lined with tripwires.

Maria, who was usually loud and boisterous, tiptoed over to the right side of the bed. She sat down cross-legged on the carpet next to Janette's twin mattress, keeping a respectful distance from my side of the pillow wall.

"Michael?" Maria whispered, her ten-year-old voice thick with a new, heavy understanding of the world. "Do you need anything? We can get you water. Or more pillows."

I looked at my cousin. The sheer gravity in her eyes told me everything I needed to know about what the adults had explained in the kitchen.

"I'm okay, Maria," I answered softly, making sure not to flex my core as I spoke. "We just have to stay flat."

On the left side of the room, Katherine carefully walked up to Jessica's twin mattress. She sat down, looking over the edge of the sofa bed at Rachel. Katherine was eight, the exact same age as my sister. She understood immediately why Rachel had been completely paralyzed by Aunt Janet's request to speak.

Katherine didn't demand a greeting. She didn't try to coax a smile. She simply reached into the pocket of her denim shorts, pulled out a small, worn plastic figurine of a horse, and gently set it on the mattress near Rachel's uninjured right hand.

"You don't have to talk to us at all," Katherine whispered to Rachel, her voice incredibly gentle. "We're just going to sit here and help Jessica and Janette guard the doors."

Rachel stared at the small plastic horse. The deep, defensive rigidity in her shoulders—the lingering panic from Aunt Janet's trigger—finally began to melt away entirely. The extended pack had adapted. They understood the rules of the fortress.

Rachel reached her right hand out, her small fingers curling around the plastic toy. She didn't speak, but she gave Katherine a tiny, profound nod of gratitude.

Over the cotton barrier, I squeezed Rachel's left hand. *Bug and Bear.* We were anchored.

"Alright, guards," Jessica announced quietly, a fiercely proud smile touching the corners of her mouth as she looked at her younger cousins. She pointed the remote at the VCR. "Movie is resuming. Nobody gets past the living room archway without clearance."

For the rest of the afternoon, the living room operated in a state of perfectly synchronized peace. The kitchen remained the war room, with Dad, Mom, Grandma Loretta, and Aunt Janet compiling legal pads, medical discharge papers, and timelines for the lawyers. But the adults respected the boundary, occasionally peeking through the door to check on us without intruding.

Surrounded by my sisters and my cousins, completely shielded from the crushing weight of the outside world, I felt the heavy pull of the muscle relaxants dragging me toward sleep. I closed my eyes, listening to the animated music of the savanna, knowing the perimeter was absolutely impenetrable.
