The rigid structure of the Dojang vanished the instant we broke formation.

April didn't immediately start cleaning up or shooing us out the door. She walked to the center of the puzzle mats and dropped down, sitting cross-legged in her heavy blue *dobok*. Master April was gone; she was just April again, our pal, completely open and approachable.

It was exactly the signal the neurodivergent kids needed.

A six-year-old girl in a yellow belt immediately scrambled across the foam and threw her arms around April’s neck. April laughed, hugging her back tightly. Then, Rachel and I moved in. We dropped to our knees, exhausted but completely regulated, and pressed into the huddle. April wrapped one arm around Rachel’s shoulders and gave my padded shoulder a solid, affectionate squeeze.

"So," April asked, looking around the circle of sweaty kids, her voice warm and genuinely curious. "How did that feel? Better than Tuesday?"

A chorus of nods and relieved murmurs answered her.

"Good," April smiled, her eyes meeting mine. "You both did incredible work today. I want you to go home, drink a ton of water—real, clean Virginia Beach water, not that salty Chesapeake garbage at the Tumblers building—and get some sleep. Tomorrow is going to be loud, and it’s going to be crowded, but you are ready. You have the *Jeongsin*. Do not let anyone on that floor tell you otherwise."

I nodded, the anxiety of the impending test feeling manageable for the first time all week. Beside me, Rachel gave a tiny, determined nod.

Outside, the fading sunlight cast long shadows across the Lynnhaven neighborhood. The idling engines of minivans and station wagons hummed from the street as parents arrived for pickup.

"Alright, guys," April said, giving us one final, grounding squeeze before gently pushing us back. "Your rides are here. Get your gear and get out of here. I'll see you tomorrow."

Rachel and I scrambled up, grabbed our heavy gym bags, and walked out of the garage. The humid, heavy heat of the Virginia evening hit us instantly. We scanned the line of cars pulled to the curb and spotted Dad’s familiar truck idling near the end of the driveway.

We dragged our bags toward it, but before we even reached the tailgate, the back door of the cab swung open.

"Hey!"

A bright, familiar voice cut through the hum of the engines.

Rachel and I froze. Stepping out of the back seat, practically buzzing with energy, was Ashley Raybourn.

"Ash!" I yelled, dropping my gym bag right there on the grass.

I didn't care that my legs were exhausted. I practically tackled her. Ashley laughed, catching my weight and wrapping her arms around me in a massive, deep-pressure hug. She squeezed me tightly, providing exactly the kind of heavy, physical grounding my autistic brain craved after a long training session.

"Look at you guys!" Ashley beamed, pulling back just enough to grab Rachel, hauling my little sister into the embrace. Rachel buried her face into Ashley's shirt, her shoulders dropping in total relief.

Ashley was our absolute anchor. She was the honorary sister of the Ragsdale pack.

"What are you doing here?" I asked, grinning as we broke the hug.

"Your dad said you guys had your last practice before the big test tomorrow," Ashley said, helping us grab our gym bags. "There was no way I was missing the pickup. Plus, I need to make sure you're ready for my birthday next week."

"The 27th," Rachel whispered softly, the two syllables perfectly clear.

"Exactly!" Ashley smiled brightly, totally unfazed by Rachel's quiet voice.

We climbed into the back seat of the truck. The seating arrangement was an absolute, unspoken rule. I slid into the middle seat, wedged tightly between the two of them. It was my designated spot. I wrapped my left arm around Rachel’s shoulders, letting her lean her head against me, and I pressed my right shoulder firmly against Ashley's side.

With Ashley on one side and Rachel on the other, my physical perimeter was completely secure.

"Buckle up, kids," Dad called from the driver's seat, shifting the truck into gear. "How was practice?"

"It was perfect," I said, leaning my head back against the seat.

"Good," Ashley said, turning her head to look at me, her eyes completely serious and fiercely supportive. "Because I am going to be sitting right in the front row of the bleachers tomorrow morning. I want to see you both get those brown belts. You're going to crush it."

I squeezed Rachel's shoulder, and she squeezed my hand back. We were exhausted, and tomorrow on that massive spring floor was going to be terrifying. But as the truck pulled away from April's garage and headed deeper into Virginia Beach, surrounded by the only people in the world I truly trusted, I actually believed Ashley was right.
