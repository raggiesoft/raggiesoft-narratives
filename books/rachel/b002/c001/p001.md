The suffocating, humid heat of the 1995 Chesapeake water crisis hung heavy in the air, but inside Master April’s garage in the Lynnhaven district, the atmosphere was entirely different.

The massive aluminum garage door was rolled all the way up, letting the late afternoon sun spill across the colorful foam puzzle mats that covered the concrete floor. Two heavy-duty box fans hummed steadily in the corners, pushing the coastal Virginia breeze through the space.

This was the final Friday Open Gym session before the quarterly testing.

Tomorrow morning, Rachel and I would step onto the massive, rebounding spring floor of the main gymnastics arena at Tidewater Tumblers to test for our solid brown *tti*. But right now, we were still safely wrapped in our white *doboks*, proudly wearing our green belts with the single black stripe.

We didn't bow when we walked into the garage. We didn't snap to attention or scream a *kihap*.

As soon as we stepped onto the puzzle mats, the rigid rules of the Dojang evaporated. Rachel and I dropped our gym bags and launched ourselves at Master April.

April caught us effortlessly, dropping to her knees on the soft foam. She was wearing her solid midnight blue *dobok*, the intricate silver embroidery running vertically down her right lapel. To the adults in the martial arts world, those silver characters marked her as the *Sunhoe Sabomnim*—the absolute, geopolitical Voice of Korea. But to us, it was just April's *dobok*. She wrapped her arms around us, burying her face into Rachel’s shoulder and pulling me into a tight, grounding squeeze that instantly settled the high-tone spasticity in my legs.

She did this with all of her kids. To the Grandmasters in Seoul, she was the Sea of Truth. But sitting here on the puzzle mats, surrounded by neurodivergent kids who needed a safe space to exist, she was simply April, our pal.

"Alright, alright, let me look at you guys," April laughed, gently pushing us back to arm's length but keeping her hands warmly on our shoulders.

The digital clock on the wall read 4:55 PM. The last few minutes before the official Open Gym began were ticking down. The rest of the kids were scattered around the mats, stretching or quietly chatting.

April looked around her circle of students, her eyes bright and focused. She didn't ask us about our forms or our stances yet. She always checked our internal *Jeongsin* first.

"How is everyone doing?" April asked, her voice calm and projecting effortless safety. "How are things at home?"

A chorus of overlapping, comfortable murmurs filled the garage. Kids talking about video games, siblings, and the miserable taste of the city tap water.

April nodded, absorbing all of it. Then, a playful, knowing smile tugged at the corner of her mouth.

"So," April said, leaning forward slightly. "Are we ready for the summer to be over? Are we excited to go back to school?"

"NO!"

The shout came from the entire garage in near-unison. The sheer volume of the rejection actually made Rachel flinch, but she quickly settled when April let out a loud, genuine laugh.

She knew exactly what she was doing. She knew we still had a couple of weeks before the yellow buses started running again. But she also knew that despite the heavy pressure of the impending brown belt test, we were still just kids who desperately wanted to cling to the final, fading days of our summer break.

"Okay, okay, message received," April grinned, holding her hands up in surrender. "Summer break continues. But tomorrow is a big day for a lot of you. So today, we don't stress. We just refine."

She stood up, the playful energy seamlessly shifting into quiet, absolute focus.

"Line up," she instructed softly.

We scrambled to our spots on the colorful foam. It wasn't the terrifying, rigid scramble of Tidewater Tumblers. It was just a group of kids getting ready to learn from someone they entirely trusted.
