Dad pulled the truck into the driveway of our Delaney Street house and killed the engine.

The moment the doors opened and we stepped out into the humid evening air, the exhaustion in my legs was immediately replaced by a surge of pure, pack-driven anticipation. We didn't even make it fully through the front door before the welcome committee hit us.

The heavy thud of paws on hardwood floors echoed down the hallway, followed by an enthusiastic frenzy of black fur.

Baby reached us first. For a purebred black Lab, he was an absolute genetic anomaly—built like a small Great Dane with a massive, blocky head. He practically took up the entire entryway, but he had the gentlest, sweetest disposition imaginable. He buried his massive nose into my gym bag, his tail wagging so hard his entire back half shook.

Right behind him trotted his younger brother, Archie. Archie bumped affectionately into my spastic left leg, looking up at me with his one good brown eye and his cloudy, milky-blue blind eye. True to his absolute core personality, his jaws were clamped proudly around a slobber-soaked, stuffed squeaky squirrel. He didn't want us to throw it; he just desperately needed us to look at it and acknowledge what a good boy he was for bringing it to the door.

"I see it, Arch. Good boy," I laughed, scratching him behind the ears while Ashley leaned over to give Baby a heavy, two-handed scrub on his shoulders.

"They're home!" a voice called out from the kitchen.

We dumped our gear by the door and walked into the dining room. The house smelled incredibly warm—a rich, savory aroma of roasted chicken and garlic that instantly made my stomach growl.

The entire pack was here. The perimeter wasn't just secure; it was fortified.

Ashley’s parents, Jim and Joan, were standing near the kitchen counter. They were our honorary surrogate parents, the adults who seamlessly stepped in to run logistics and lift the heavy burdens when Mom and Dad were working.

But the real command center of the house was currently gathered around the dining room table.

My older sister Jessica, fourteen years old and the absolute pragmatic protector of the pack, was setting out the plates. Right beside her was our cousin Janette, and Ashley’s older sister, Melissa. Together, the three of them operated as an impenetrable shield wall for Rachel and me.

"Look who survived the puzzle mats!" Melissa cheered, walking over and immediately pulling Ashley into a hug before grabbing me by the shoulders and giving me a firm, grounding shake.

"How did it go?" Jessica asked, looking past the noise directly at Rachel and me. She wasn't just asking about the martial arts; she was checking our sensory gauges, looking for signs of overload or shutdown.

"It was perfect," I told her, my voice full of genuine conviction. "April helped us. We're ready for tomorrow."

Jessica’s eyes softened, a proud smile breaking across her face. She stepped forward and pulled me into a fierce, tight hug. It wasn't the deep-pressure squeeze Ashley gave, but it was anchored with absolute, protective certainty. She let me go and immediately scooped Rachel up into a hug, kissing the top of her dark hair.

Janette joined in, wrapping her arms around Rachel from the side, effectively sandwiching my little sister in a cocoon of safe, trusted family. Rachel leaned into them, her eyes closing as the last remaining ounces of Dojang tension bled out of her nervous system.

"Alright, enough hugging, let's get some food into these athletes," Jim laughed, clapping his hands together. "We've got a massive carb-load ready. You two have a huge day tomorrow, and you're going to need the energy to break some boards."

"Nobody is breaking boards, Dad," Ashley corrected, rolling her eyes playfully as we all scrambled to pull out our chairs. "They're doing forms. It's about *Tongje*."

"Whatever it is, they're going to dominate," Joan smiled warmly, setting a massive bowl of mashed potatoes in the center of the table.

I took my seat, automatically sliding into my designated spot with Rachel on my left and Ashley on my right. Baby immediately dropped his massive weight onto my bare feet under the table, serving as a living, breathing weighted blanket, while Archie happily chewed his squeaky squirrel near Rachel’s chair.

I looked around the table. The noise was chaotic—Melissa teasing Ashley, Dad talking to Jim about the miserable water crisis, Jessica and Janette coordinating the morning driving schedule. It was loud, but it wasn't the terrifying, demanding noise of Master Curtis. It was the safe, vibrant music of my family.

Tomorrow morning, Rachel and I had to walk onto that massive, rebounding spring floor and face the terrifying scrutiny of the 7th Dan Regional Director. We had to prove we were worthy of the brown *tti*. But sitting here in the dining room, surrounded by my sisters, my dogs, and the absolute safety of the Delaney Street base camp, I knew that no matter what happened on the mats, our pack was unbreakable.
