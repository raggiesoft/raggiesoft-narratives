The beauty of Friday Open Gym was the absolute lack of a metronome. At Tidewater Tumblers, Master Ray forced us to move to a frantic, collective count. But here on the puzzle mats, Master April ran the hour like an open study hall.

We scattered across the garage, each of us claiming our own six-foot perimeter. The air was filled with the soft *snap* of heavy cotton uniforms, the squeak of bare feet pivoting on foam, and the low hum of the box fans. Master April moved through the room like a quiet current, acting as an on-site tutor.

She spent ten minutes with a yellow belt, physically adjusting his hips so his *Arae Makgi* (low block) protected his knee. She worked with one of the 2nd Pooms, quietly debating the specific timing of the transitions in their advanced *poomsae*.

Then, she made her way to my corner of the mats.

I was exhausted. I was drenched in sweat, repeatedly attempting the *I-dan Ap Chagi*—the jumping front kick. No matter how hard my brain fired the command, my CP-spastic legs refused to translate it into vertical height. My back leg would drag, my front knee wouldn't elevate, and I kept landing heavy and off-balance. I was getting frustrated, my hands balling into fists.

"Freeze," Master April said softly, stepping into my perimeter.

I stopped, my chest heaving. I waited for her to tell me I wasn't trying hard enough, the way Master Curtis always did.

She didn't. She looked down at my legs, analyzing my biomechanics with absolute, clinical precision.

"You are fighting gravity, Michael," Master April explained, tapping the outside of my spastic left calf. "Your neurology makes your muscles tight. When you try to force a massive jump off this leg, your body panics and locks up to keep you from falling."

"I have to jump for the test tomorrow," I panted, wiping sweat from my eyes. "Master Scott will fail me if I don't."

"Master Scott evaluates *Tongje*. Control," April corrected gently. "I don't care about your vertical height. I care about your chamber and your landing. Tomorrow, you are testing on the main gymnastics floor. It’s a spring floor. It will give you the lift you need. Tonight, I just want you to focus on snapping the kick and landing perfectly balanced. No falling. Understand?"

"Ye, Sabomnim," I answered, the heavy, suffocating anxiety in my chest loosening. She wasn't lowering the bar; she was just giving me a ladder I could actually climb.

April gave me an approving nod and stepped over to where Rachel was drilling.

Rachel was standing in a perfect *Juchum Sogi* (horse stance). Her punches were sharp, cutting through the air with flawless *Gisul* (technique). But every time she reached the end of the sequence, where a *kihap* was absolutely mandatory, her entire body locked up. Her shoulders would hike up to her ears, her eyes would widen in panic, and she would throw the final punch in agonizing, suffocating silence.

She was terrifyingly aware that tomorrow was the test. A missing *kihap* was an automatic failure.

April knelt down on the puzzle mats directly in front of her, bringing herself down to an eight-year-old's eye level.

"Rachel," Master April said quietly, waiting for my sister to break her terrified stare at the floor and meet her eyes. "Why do we *kihap*?"

Rachel swallowed hard. She couldn't speak to answer, but she tapped two fingers over her own heart.

"Exactly," April smiled warmly. "We do it to show our *Jeongsin*. Our spirit. The sound is just the vehicle. It's the energy leaving your body so it doesn't get trapped inside."

April shifted her weight, sitting cross-legged on the foam.

"I know your voice gets stuck," Master April told her, completely validating Rachel's situational mutism without an ounce of pity. "But your spirit doesn't. If the sound won't work, we are going to use the rest of your body to show the Grandmasters exactly how much energy you have."

April stood back up and dropped into a horse stance next to Rachel.

"Watch me," April instructed. "Don't listen. Watch."

April chambered her fist at her hip. Instead of yelling, she actively exaggerated the physical mechanics of the shout. Her chest visibly heaved inward, gathering an immense amount of air. Her jaw dropped open. She threw the punch, exhaling the air in a sharp, silent, aggressive rush. The sheer physical exertion of the silent shout was undeniable; you could literally *see* the *kihap* happen, even without the decibels.

"Try it," Master April commanded softly.

Rachel squared her tiny shoulders. She dropped into her stance. She chambered her fist.

Instead of freezing in panic, Rachel followed the new blueprint. She sucked in a massive breath, her chest expanding. She opened her mouth wide, her face locked in an expression of fierce concentration, and she threw the punch, pushing the air out of her lungs in a silent, powerful wave.

It was a flawless execution of intent.

"That is it," Master April beamed, her voice filled with profound pride. "That is your *kihap*, Rachel. Tomorrow, when you are on that spring floor, if the sound comes out, let it. But if it gets stuck, you do exactly that. You show them your spirit."

Rachel’s posture completely transformed. The heavy, crushing dread of failing tomorrow vanished. She looked at her small fists, a tiny, triumphant smile breaking across her face.

For the next forty-five minutes, the garage hummed with quiet, relentless work. We drilled our forms, we corrected our stances, and we prepared our minds.

When the digital clock finally clicked to 6:00 PM, Master April stepped back to the center of the mats.

"*Geuman,*" she announced. Stop.

We scrambled back into our lines, our white *doboks* heavy with sweat but our minds entirely clear.

"*Cha-ryeot,*" April commanded. We snapped to attention. "*Kyeong-rye.*"

We bowed deeply to the *Sabomnim*, the class officially concluded.

When we stood back up, the rigid posture of the Dojang immediately evaporated. April clapped her hands together, the warm, approachable smile of our pal returning instantly to her face.

"Great work tonight, everyone!" April cheered, walking over to the mini-fridge in the corner to pull out a cardboard flat of juice boxes. "Grab a drink, get your gear, and get some sleep! I will see all of you bright and early tomorrow on the spring floor!"
