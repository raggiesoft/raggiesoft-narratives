The house finally quieted down as the living room completed its transformation into a full-scale bivouac.

Mom and Dad emerged from the kitchen, plates cleared, and began the nightly routine of herding the dogs down the hallway. This was much to the disappointment of the canine guard. Baby desperately wanted to drop his massive, Great Dane-sized weight right into the center of the room, and Archie was still trying to proudly display his slobber-soaked squirrel to anyone who would look. But their heavy paws and sharp claws were an absolute liability around the inflated plastic.

"Come on, boys. In here," Dad corralled them, ushering the two Labs into the master bedroom so they wouldn't accidentally pop the air mattresses in the middle of the night.

With the dogs safely secured, the pack settled into our designated stations. Over by the coffee table, Jessica and Janette claimed the first queen air mattress. As the older protectors of the group, they naturally took the position closest to the hallway, forming the first line of defense. Melissa stretched out on the second air mattress near the armchair, completing the outer ring of the room.

But the absolute center of gravity was the pull-out sofa.

I climbed in and slid right into the middle of the mattress, taking up my role as the designated anchor. Rachel climbed in on my left side, pulling her heavy quilt up, while Ashley settled in on my right.

The moment we laid down, the familiar, trauma-informed algorithm of our survival kicked in. I shifted my weight, providing that deep, grounding pressure for Rachel on one side and Ashley on the other. I acted as their physical tether, and in return, their proximity instantly quieted the chaotic noise in my own processor.

Mom stepped into the living room one last time, looking over the sea of blankets, pillows, and kids covering her floor. She smiled, reaching over to click off the main lamp.

"Get some sleep, guys," Mom whispered into the dark. "Big day tomorrow. We love you."

"Love you too," a few of us mumbled back, the exhaustion finally pulling us under.

The house went dark. The only sound was the low, steady rhythm of the central air conditioning fighting the muggy Virginia Beach heat, and the quiet breathing of my family.

Tomorrow morning, Rachel and I were going to step onto the massive spring floor of Tidewater Tumblers. We were going to face the terrifying scrutiny of Master Scott and the crushing expectations of the Dojang. But lying there on the sofa bed, anchored seamlessly between my best friend and my little sister, the fear simply couldn't reach me.

The perimeter was locked tight, and the pack was safe.
