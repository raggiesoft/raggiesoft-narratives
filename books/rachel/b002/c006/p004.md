The trauma attending physician returned to our bay, looking much more settled but no less serious. He pulled up a stool next to the two beds where Rachel and I were still locked in our silent, joined-hand perimeter.

"Ellen," the doctor began, addressing Mom. "I’ve reviewed the images with the radiologist. We are not comfortable sending them home today. Because of the location of Rachel’s splenic bruising, she is at a high risk for a delayed rupture. We need to monitor her hemoglobin every few hours to make sure she isn't losing blood internally. And for Michael, his core spasms are so severe that we want to keep him on an IV muscle relaxant for the next twenty-four hours to ensure that fascial tear doesn’t worsen when he moves."

Mom nodded, her hand trembling as she smoothed the hair back from Rachel’s forehead. "Whatever they need. We aren't going anywhere."

"Because of the nature of the case, a social worker will be coming by shortly," the doctor added softly. "It’s standard procedure for non-accidental trauma. They already spoke to the police, and they’ll just need to verify the home environment is safe and supportive. They’ll likely head out to your house this afternoon while you're here with the kids."

"I understand," Mom said. Her voice was hollow, but she didn't flinch. She knew the social worker would find a house full of guilt and a father who was currently pacing the foyer in a protective rage, but it was a safe house.

Before the doctor left, he leaned in closer. "I also thought you should know—the police called. Curtis was processed an hour ago. He is currently in the city jail pending a bond hearing. He won't be going anywhere for a while."

I felt a microscopic slackening in Rachel’s grip. For the first time since Tuesday, the predator was behind bars.
