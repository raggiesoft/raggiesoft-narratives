By nine o'clock, the reality of hospital protocol finally breached our sanctuary.

Nurse Sarah returned, her voice still incredibly gentle, but carrying the firm boundaries of the pediatric trauma ward. Visiting hours were officially over. Because we were in a specialized trauma bay and not a standard recovery room, fire codes and hospital policy dictated that only one parent could remain overnight. The rest of the pack had to leave.

Mom looked completely torn, her hand resting on the metal railing of Rachel's bed. She didn't want to leave us, but she also knew Jessica and Janette had been running on pure adrenaline since they walked off the mat Thursday night. They needed to go back to Delaney Street, eat a real meal, and sleep before the social worker arrived in the morning.

"I'll stay," Dad said. It wasn't a suggestion; it was an absolute, immovable statement.

Mom looked at him. He was still wearing his work boots and his heavy canvas jacket. He looked exhausted, completely hollowed out by the guilt of the afternoon.

"Tom, that chair doesn't even recline all the way," Mom whispered, gesturing to the miserable, vinyl hospital recliner wedged in the corner of the room. "You have to work on Monday."

"I don't care if it's made of broken glass," Dad replied, his voice a low, fierce rumble. He looked at Rachel, then at me. "I am not leaving this room. Take the girls home, Ellen. Lock the doors. I've got the perimeter here."

Mom nodded slowly. She leaned over, kissing Rachel gently on the forehead, being careful not to jostle her ribs. Then she came over to my bed, resting her hand lightly on my hair. "I love you both so much. I will be back first thing in the morning."

Saying goodbye to Jessica and Janette was harder. Janette gave Rachel's ankle one last, protective squeeze. Jessica stood at the foot of my bed, her eyes locking onto mine. The silent communication between us was clear: *We survived the worst of it. Hold the line until morning.*

When the heavy glass door slid shut behind them, the trauma bay felt incredibly large and terribly quiet.

Dad took off his heavy canvas jacket, folded it into a makeshift pillow, and dropped into the vinyl recliner. He pulled it right up to the gap between our beds, positioning himself directly beneath our joined hands.

"Try to sleep, guys," Dad whispered into the dark. "I'm right here."

But sleeping in a hospital is a completely fictional concept.

The reality of an overnight stay in a pediatric trauma ward is an agonizing, exhausting cycle of interruptions. Every single time my body finally succumbed to the heavy pull of the IV muscle relaxants, the machinery of medical observation dragged me violently back to consciousness.

At 11:00 PM, the automated blood pressure cuff permanently strapped to my arm violently inflated, squeezing my bicep like a vice. The sudden compression spiked my adrenaline, which caused my core to twitch, sending a flare of hot pain through my torn fascia.

At 1:00 AM, the door slid open. A phlebotomist came in with a cart, turning on a harsh, localized spotlight to draw a fresh vial of blood from Rachel's arm to check her hemoglobin levels. Rachel whimpered, her grip on my hand turning white-knuckled as the needle went in. Dad was out of his chair instantly, standing over her, his large hand resting comfortingly on her shoulder until the tech left.

At 3:00 AM, it was Nurse Sarah again, quietly shining a penlight to check our pupils, taking our temperatures, and adjusting the flow rate on my IV drip.

The beeping of the monitors was relentless. The hum of the air conditioning was freezing. The mattress felt like a slab of concrete against my spine. It was a miserable, maddening environment that offered absolutely zero rest. My eyes burned with exhaustion, and my stomach throbbed in a dull, synchronized rhythm with my heartbeat.

Around 4:30 AM, after another brutal vitals check, I looked over at Rachel. She was awake, staring blankly up at the ceiling tiles, completely exhausted but too wired by the constant interruptions to actually sleep.

I squeezed her hand. She squeezed back.

I looked down into the space between our beds. Dad was sitting in the vinyl chair, wide awake in the dark. He hadn't slept a single second. He was just watching the door, acting as the final, impenetrable barrier between his children and the rest of the world.

The hospital was a miserable place to spend the night. We were in pain, exhausted, and completely stripped of our normal comforts. But as the first faint traces of gray morning light began to bleed through the small window of the trauma bay, I realized that for the first time in months, I wasn't afraid.
