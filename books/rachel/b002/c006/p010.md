April stayed in the narrow gap between our hospital beds. The frantic, terrified energy that had consumed the afternoon was entirely gone, replaced by a quiet, fierce warmth.

"I know right now your bodies hurt in ways that aren't fair," April said softly, keeping her voice low and even. "And I know that looking at a martial arts uniform probably feels really scary right now. That’s okay. You have every right to be angry."

I swallowed hard. My torn fascia throbbed a dull rhythm beneath the heavy blanket of the IV muscle relaxants. "I don't ever want to go back there," I whispered, the thought of the harsh fluorescent lights and the massive mirrors of Tidewater Tumblers making my chest tight. "But I... I don't want to quit."

It was a terrifying admission. Master Curtis had taken the thing we loved and turned it into a weapon, and the idea of losing martial arts completely felt like letting him win.

April’s eyes softened with a profound, knowing empathy.

"You aren't quitting, Michael," she promised, her voice steady and absolute. "And you are never stepping foot on Master Ray’s mats ever again. I won't let you."

Rachel flinched slightly at the mention of the mats. She pulled her knees up a fraction of an inch, wincing as her cracked ribs protested. The adrenaline that had forced her vocal cords to open on Tuesday had been completely obliterated by the horror of Thursday. The situational mutism had locked her jaw completely tight again. The Dojang had proven that adults were dangerous, and even though April was wearing a t-shirt and jeans, she was still an adult. Rachel shrank back toward my side of the bed, her dark eyes wide and guarded, her grip on my hand turning white-knuckled.

April saw the fear. She didn't take it personally, and she didn't try to force eye contact. She simply kept her hands perfectly still and visible, resting lightly over ours, radiating absolute safety.

"But you both still have a spot in my garage," April continued, a gentle, hopeful smile breaking through the sorrow on her face. "My garage is safe. It will always be safe. When the doctors say you are healed up and medically cleared to train, I want you both to come back to Friday Open Gym. But not just for Fridays."

April looked back and forth between us, the 4th Dan *Sabomnim* making a sacred vow to her two youngest, bravest students.

"I will personally take over your training," April stated. "You are both Brown Belts. You have already survived the hardest parts of the curriculum. I am going to teach you everything I know. I am going to train you in my garage, away from all of that toxic noise, and I am going to be the one standing there when you test for your *Poom* rank."

A Junior Black Belt. The image of that half-red, half-black collar—the ultimate symbol of surviving the curriculum—suddenly didn't feel like a cursed object anymore.

"Just us?" I asked, needing the reassurance. "In the garage?"

"Just us," April swore. "We’ll use the colorful puzzle mats. We'll do forms. We'll hit the heavy bags." April paused, her throat tightening. She looked directly at Rachel, ensuring she heard this next part with absolute clarity. "And I promise you, the kind of 'conditioning' Curtis believes in does not exist in my academy. *Seondu* is an art, not a punishment. Nobody is ever putting a foot on you in my garage."

Rachel stared at her. Slowly, the rigid terror in her small shoulders began to thaw. She didn't speak—she couldn't—but she shifted her weight just a millimeter closer to April's hand, a silent acknowledgment of the vow.

"I will take you all the way to black belt, under my wing," April whispered. "If you want it."

I looked over at Rachel. Her face was pale and she was hooked up to a heart monitor, but the sheer, undeniable spark of resilience was burning bright in her eyes. The monster was in jail, the adults were protecting us, and the one teacher we truly loved had just surrendered her authority to make us feel safe.

Rachel gave my hand a sharp double-squeeze. It was our silent code. *Yes.*

"We'll be there," I told April, a deep, comforting warmth spreading through my chest, chasing away the freezing chill of the trauma bay. "The minute the doctors say it's okay, we're coming back."

April squeezed our hands one last time, sealing the pact.

In that quiet, amber-lit hospital room, wrapped in the safety of our family’s perimeter and the protective vow of a true Master, we locked our sights on the future. We had a plan. We were going to heal, we were going to get cleared, and we were going to go back to the safe, warm puzzle mats of April's garage to finish what we started.

We just had to wait for the doctors to give us the green light.
