The heavy glass door of the trauma bay slid open, and Detective Harris stepped back in. He looked tired, his tie loosened, but his eyes were kind.

"Alright, Rachel," the detective said, sitting back down at eye level. "I've talked to the doctors and I've talked to the other families. I just need to hear from you, in your own words, what happened during that second pass. Can you do that for me?"

Rachel didn't even look at him. She immediately went into a full, rigid shut-down. She pulled her knees to her chest—ignoring the sharp pain in her ribs—and tucked her chin into her hospital gown. Her eyes were wide and glazed, locked onto the far wall.

The situational mutism was absolute. In her mind, Master Curtis had explicitly linked her voice to her pain. Talking to an adult meant being stepped on. It was a neurological circuit-breaker that had been slammed into the 'off' position.

"Rachel, honey, please," Mom begged, leaning over the bed. "He’s a good man. He’s here to help us."

Rachel didn't move. She didn't blink. She was a statue of pure, unadulterated fear.

"Mom. Detective Harris. You have to leave," I said. My voice was surprisingly steady.

Detective Harris paused. "Michael, I need her statement for the warrant."

"She won't give it to you," I said, my protective instinct flaring. "Not with you here. Not even with Mom here. She thinks adults are the danger now. You have to go outside. Close the door. I’ll talk to her."

The detective looked at Mom, then back at me. He saw the way Rachel’s hand was still welded to mine. He stood up, motioning for Mom to follow him.

The heavy door slid shut. The trauma bay became a silent, soundproofed cube.

"Rachel," I whispered.

She didn't move.

"Rachel, look at me."

Slowly, her head turned. Her eyes were swimming with tears.

"They’re gone," I promised. "It’s just us. Like in the bedroom last night. Nobody is going to step on you. I won't let them. But I need to know... what did he say when he stayed on your stomach? I need to tell the detective so he can keep Curtis in jail forever."

Rachel took a long, jagged breath, her ribs hitching. She leaned closer to me, her voice a tiny, raspy thread that barely carried across the gap between the beds.

"He said... I was a weak girl," she whispered. "He said... silence is spirit. He said if I screamed, he’d press harder."

I felt a cold, jagged rage settle into my bones.

"He said... Michael is fat and soft," she continued, her voice gaining a microscopic bit of strength as she defended me. "He said we were both pathetic. He stayed so long, Michael. I couldn't breathe."

"I know," I said, squeezing her hand. "I know he did. But you're safe now. Do you trust me to tell them?"

She nodded once, a tiny, definitive movement. "Tell them."

I looked through the glass and gave the detective a thumbs-up.

When they stepped back in, Rachel immediately vanished back into her knees, the vault door locking once again. But it didn't matter. I had the truth.

I looked Detective Harris dead in the eye and delivered Rachel’s words with the weight of a judge. I told him about the lingering pressure. I told him about the threats. I told him how Curtis had weaponized her silence.

By the time I was finished, Mom was sobbing into her hands, and Detective Harris was writing so hard his pen tore through the paper of his notebook.

"We've got him, kids," the detective said, his voice thick with a quiet, lethal intent. "I promise you. We've got him."
