The twenty-minute drive from Dr. Young’s clinic near Lake James to the massive Children’s Hospital in Norfolk was a masterclass in agony.

Mom drove the sedan with a white-knuckled grip on the steering wheel, her eyes darting between the road and the rearview mirror. But no matter how carefully she tried to brake or accelerate, the physics of a moving vehicle were unavoidable. Every expansion joint on the highway, every minor pothole, and every gentle curve sent absolute shockwaves through our damaged bodies.

Beside me in the backseat, Rachel sat completely rigid, her eyes squeezed shut. Every time the car hit a bump, her lungs jostled against the bruised wall of her chest, forcing tiny, breathy whimpers through her teeth.

My reality wasn't much better. Because of my Cerebral Palsy, sitting up in a moving car required my core to constantly make micro-adjustments to keep my torso balanced. With a torn rectus abdominis fascia, those automatic adjustments triggered violent, involuntary muscle spasms that made me nauseous with pain.

"Almost there, guys," Mom promised tightly, her voice cracking as she navigated the downtown streets. "Just hold on."

When she finally pulled into the emergency drop-off circle, the towering brick and glass structure of the regional Children's Hospital loomed above us.

Getting out of the sedan was excruciating. I couldn't bend my torso at all. I had to slide my legs out sideways, plant my feet, and push entirely with my thighs, gritting my teeth against the searing fire in my gut. I reached back and helped carefully pull Rachel out, letting her anchor her uninjured right side against my hip.

The automatic sliding doors parted, hitting us with a blast of heavily air-conditioned, antiseptic air.

Mom didn’t hesitate. Dr. Young had called ahead, so she completely bypassed the crowded waiting room, marching us directly up to the thick plexiglass of the triage desk. She slid Dr. Young’s folded referral sheet under the gap, pushing it toward the intake nurse.

"Dr. Young sent us," Mom stated, her voice tight with a mixture of terror and maternal authority. "He said you were expecting them."

I watched the nurse unfold the paper. Her eyes scanned the top lines—*Severe blunt force trauma. Localized guarding. Suspected structural/fascial damage*—and her posture immediately straightened. She tapped rapidly on her keyboard, pulling up the file Dr. Young had flagged.

Then, her eyes dropped to the bottom of the page, landing on the heavily underlined sentence Dr. Young had written specifically for us.

The nurse looked up through the glass, her eyes shifting between me and Rachel. We were standing shoulder-to-shoulder, Rachel’s fingers locked in a death grip on the hem of my shirt. The imposing, chaotic environment of a regional trauma center was terrifying, and we were visually broadcasting exactly what Dr. Young had diagnosed: we were each other's only anchor.

"I see Dr. Young's clinical note about their grounding dependency," the triage nurse said, her voice dropping into a calm, accommodating register that immediately lowered the tension in my chest. "We have a double trauma bay open in the back. I'll flag their charts so radiology knows to bring the portable ultrasound and X-ray units directly to the room. We aren't going to separate them."

Rachel let out a tiny, shuddering exhale against my side. The first massive hurdle of the adult world had been cleared without a fight.

"Follow me," a pediatric orderly said, appearing from behind a set of double doors with a wheelchair.

We declined the chair—sitting down and standing back up was too painful—and instead slowly shuffled down the bright, sterile hallways until we were guided into a large, freezing room filled with humming monitors and coiled wires. Two parallel hospital beds sat in the center of the room, separated by only a few feet of linoleum.

"We need them in hospital gowns so the trauma attending and the radiologist can get clear scans," the orderly explained gently to Mom. "I'll give you a few minutes."

Changing was a humiliating, agonizing ordeal, but Mom helped us carefully navigate out of our sweatpants and t-shirts without putting any pressure on our midsections.

I climbed onto the bed on the right, keeping my back perfectly flat to prevent my torn fascia from seizing. Rachel climbed onto the bed on the left, favoring her injured ribs. Even though the beds were separate, the gap between them was small enough. I reached my left hand out over the metal railing, and Rachel reached her right hand out to meet it. Our fingers laced together across the empty space.

As we lay there under the harsh fluorescent lights, waiting for the imaging machines that would reveal the true extent of our injuries, and anticipating the police detective who was on his way to finally take Master Curtis down, the only thing keeping the panic at bay was the grip of my sister's hand in mine.
