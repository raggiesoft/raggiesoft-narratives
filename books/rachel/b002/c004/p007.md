By a quarter to nine, the chaotic energy inside the tent finally began to mellow into a heavy, comfortable exhaustion. The local FM pop station playing on the boombox transitioned into a block of slow, acoustic evening tracks.

Melissa, ever the vigilant commander, reached into her bag and checked the glowing numbers on her digital watch.

"Alright, troops. It’s eight-forty-five," Melissa announced, her voice cutting gently through the quiet chatter. "We need to initiate the shutdown sequence. Tomorrow is a full deployment day."

"Already?" Ashley groaned quietly from my right side, though she didn't actually move a muscle to get up.

"Already," Jessica agreed from her sentinel spot near the door, pulling her quilt up to her chin. "I have gymnastics tomorrow afternoon. If I’m yawning on the balance beam, my coach is going to make me run extra laps."

"And we have choir practice," Melissa reminded Ashley, reaching up to grab one of the heavy-duty plastic flashlights. "You have that solo to work on."

Thursday schedules during the summer were always packed, but for the first time in my life, the impending reality of a Thursday didn't fill my stomach with a heavy block of dread.

We had karate tomorrow.

Normally, the thought of stepping back onto the Dojang mats under Master Curtis's glare would be enough to send my CP spasticity flaring up all over again. But Rachel shifted against my left side, and the energy radiating off of her wasn't fear. It was pure, unfiltered anticipation.

She squeezed my arm, her dark eyes reflecting the dim beam of the flashlight.

"I... will... be... loud," Rachel rasped, a fierce, determined smile breaking across her face. She couldn't wait to get back into the heavy canvas uniform. She had found her voice, and she was entirely ready to use it again.

"You're going to be the loudest one there," I whispered back, resting my chin against the top of her head.

"Alright, base camp," Melissa said softly. "Turning off the main lights."

She clicked the heavy plastic switch, and the tent plunged into a comfortable darkness, illuminated only by the faint, ambient glow of the neighborhood streetlights filtering through the nylon walls. I reached out and pressed the power button on the portable stereo, cutting the radio and letting the rhythmic sound of the Virginia crickets completely take over.

"Good night, guys," Ashley whispered, nestling her head deeper into her pillow, her shoulder still pressed firmly against my arm.

"Night, Ash," Jessica replied softly from the door.

"Good night," Rachel forced out, her raspy voice incredibly quiet but perfectly clear in the dark.

"Good night," I echoed into the quiet tent.

I closed my eyes, surrounded by the steady breathing of my pack, the cool grass beneath the layers of sleeping bags, and the absolute certainty that tomorrow was going to be another great day.
