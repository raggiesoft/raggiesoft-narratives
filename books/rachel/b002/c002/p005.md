Curtis stood frozen. The decree—Bulhapgyeok—echoed in his ears, completely dismantling his reality.

But it wasn't just a fail. It was an absolute, institutional execution. The sickening realization of what he had just done crashed into his nervous system. He hadn't just questioned a grading criterion; he had argued with a Sunhoe Sabomnim. He had stood on a testing floor and openly challenged the Voice of Korea, effectively spitting in the faces of the Grandmasters in Seoul. It was the ultimate, unforgivable taboo of the art, and he had committed it simply because his fragile pride couldn't stomach bowing to a twenty-two-year-old woman.

He was thirty-four years old. He ran the Iron Shirt Underground. He was a hardened fighter. And he had just been publicly stripped of his promotion, humiliated, and metaphorically castrated in front of the entire franchise.

He turned and bowed stiffly, his vision swimming with a blinding, white-hot rage. As he walked off the center line, his bare feet slapping against the linoleum, his mind desperately scrambled to process the destruction of his ego.

He couldn't hate Scott; Scott was a 7th Dan god in this world. He couldn't attack Ray; Ray owned the building and was likely already plotting how to distance his LLC from Curtis's heresy. And he absolutely could not touch April. To even look at her with disrespect now would invite a global expulsion from the art. She was the Emissary. Her quiet, unbothered posture on the sidelines, the silver embroidery on her lapel catching the fluorescent lights as she remained completely unfazed by his fury, made him want to rip the mirrors off the walls.

As Curtis stepped back to the edge of the mat, his eyes found the new Brown Belts.

He looked at Michael, the heavy, disabled boy who couldn't even jump an inch off the ground. And then his eyes locked onto Rachel.

She was tiny. She was fragile. She hadn't even opened her mouth to make a sound. She was the absolute embodiment of everything Curtis believed was ruining the world—weakness, accommodation, softness.

And they were holding hands.

Right there, in the middle of the Green Belt line, the ten-year-old boy and the eight-year-old girl had their fingers firmly laced together. In a traditional Dojang, it was a glaring, punishable break in the *Cha-ryeot* stance.

Earlier in the test, Curtis had watched Master Scott’s eyes sweep over the ranks and pause on their linked hands. Curtis had practically vibrated with anticipation, waiting for the 7th Dan to bark a harsh reprimand and physically separate them for breaking tradition.

But Scott hadn't yelled. The Regional Director had simply looked from their hands to April.

April had met Scott's gaze and given a microscopic, almost imperceptible nod. *They are grounded. I allow it.*

And just like that, the 7th Dan had moved on, completely accepting the accommodation.

*She passed,* Curtis's mind whispered, a toxic, venomous thought taking root deep in his fractured ego as he stared at their gripped hands. *The mute little girl passed, and I failed.*

His narcissistic mind frantically rewrote the narrative to protect itself. He hadn't failed because he was sloppy. He hadn't failed because he displayed *Murye*—insolence. He failed because of *them*. He was being punished for pointing out the truth: that the Emissary was turning the Dojang into a daycare.

In Curtis's twisted psychology, Michael and Rachel weren't just students anymore. They were the physical avatars of his public emasculation. They were the reason he had broken the sacred taboo. They were the reason Master Scott and the *Sunhoe Sabomnim* had looked at him with utter disgust.

Curtis stared at Rachel’s dark, quiet eyes, watching her safely holding her brother’s hand in the ranks. He saw the unearned trust they had in the Dojang. He saw the safety the Emissary had promised them.

A cold, dark promise settled into Curtis's chest. He couldn't touch them today. He couldn't override Master Scott, and he certainly couldn't defy the Voice of Korea while she was standing in the room.

But Scott would eventually board a plane back to headquarters. April would leave and go back to her own academy. And eventually, Master Ray would retreat to his office, leaving Curtis completely alone on the floor to teach the advanced curriculum.

*Basic Form V requires core strength,* Curtis thought, his eyes dropping from their linked hands to the children's stomachs, remembering the exact word Scott had yelled at him. *It requires Tongje. They think they are safe because the Grandmasters' pet lets them hold hands and gave them a piece of colored tape.*

Curtis crossed his arms, his white uniform pulling tight across his chest. He was going to break them. He was going to find a drill, close the blinds, and crush the spirit right out of them, just to prove to the universe that he was the strong one.
