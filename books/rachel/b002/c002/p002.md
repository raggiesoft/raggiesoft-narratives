When the Green Belts with stripes were called forward, the tension in the room spiked. This was the test for the Brown tti—the gateway to the advanced ranks.

Master April stepped to the center of the mat, her clipboard resting against her forearm. She wasn't just standing off to the side; as the *Sunhoe Sabomnim*, she was actively commanding the floor. But even with the silver embroidery of her absolute authority catching the fluorescent light, her presence remained a quiet, steady anchor for the neurodivergent kids in the line.

April watched us perform Basic Form IV. She didn’t react to mistakes like they were sins. She watched the recovery. She watched the work.

*"Idan Ap Chagi,"* April called out.

The jumping front kick. It was a notoriously difficult technique that required explosive calf strength and perfect mid-air coordination. One by one, the kids stepped forward, jumped, snapped the kick, and landed.

Then it was my turn.

At ten years old, I carried the extra weight of my medication and the profound, neurological rigidness of Cerebral Palsy. I stepped into my fighting stance. My eyes flicked once to the parents in the bleachers, finding Mom and Dad—looking for a safe anchor.

I dipped my weight. I drove upward.

My body simply didn’t cooperate the way it did for the neurotypical kids. The jump wasn’t clean. The high-tone spasticity in my legs fought the upward momentum. The kick didn’t snap at chest height. It was a messy, heavy, grounded attempt.

But the effort was undeniable. It was written in the violent strain of my core, in the fierce concentration on my face, in the way I fought my own misfiring neurology just to get my foot off the floor. I landed hard, wobbled, and instantly locked my core to re-center myself.

I didn’t look at Curtis, who was standing on the sidelines with a visible sneer of disgust. I just waited—still and controlled.

April’s eyes softened a fraction. Not in pity, but in absolute attention and understanding. Because I was one of her regular students at her Friday Open Gym sessions, she knew my limitations intimately. She knew exactly what a jumping front kick cost my nervous system, and she knew I had just given her every single ounce of physical output I possessed.

She made a small mark on her sheet.

*"Hapgyeok,"* April stated. Pass.

The word hit me like sunlight. I didn’t grin. I just bowed—fast, relieved, shaking—and stepped back into line.

Curtis’s jaw locked. To him, martial arts was purely an aesthetic of violence. My kick was ugly. It was weak. Curtis shifted his weight, his disgust boiling over into silent rage. *She's lowering the standard,* Curtis thought, glaring fiercely at the twenty-two-year-old *Sunhoe Sabomnim*. *She's turning this place into a daycare.*
