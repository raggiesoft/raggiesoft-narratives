The trauma bay was crowded. Mom, Jessica, and Janette had arrived just as the trauma attending physician returned with the discharge coordinator to map out the logistics of our recovery.

Dad stood near the foot of our beds, his arms crossed over his heavy canvas jacket, listening intently as the doctor outlined the absolute necessity of keeping us on the first floor of the house.

"We pull the sofa out," Dad confirmed, his voice a low, steady rumble. "It’s a queen-size mattress. We put them both on it so they don't have to navigate stairs or be separated."

"That works," the doctor nodded, making a note on his clipboard. "But Tom, Ellen... I need to be absolutely clear about the rules of that shared mattress. We are sending them home with catastrophic internal damage. The physics of how they share that space are literally a matter of life and death."

The doctor stepped between my bed and Rachel's bed, looking directly at us.

"Michael. Rachel," the doctor began, his tone incredibly gentle but unyielding. "I know how you two rely on each other. I know Rachel likes to sit in your lap, and I know you like to hold her when you sleep. For the next four months, that is completely, absolutely forbidden."

The words hit my chest like an ice-cold bucket of water.

"What?" I gasped, the sudden spike of anxiety making my heavy, medicated limbs twitch.

"No cuddling. No sleeping on your sides facing each other. No draping arms or legs across each other’s torsos," the doctor listed, his eyes locking onto mine. "Michael, if you wrap your arm around Rachel and your CP triggers a high-tone spasm in your sleep, your arm will violently contract. That sudden compression would crush Rachel's cracked ribs and immediately rupture her swelling spleen. She would bleed out before the ambulance arrived."

Rachel’s eyes widened to the size of saucers. She looked terrified.

"And Rachel," the doctor continued softly, turning to her. "If you try to cuddle and you accidentally roll your body weight across Michael's stomach, your elbow or knee will press directly into his traumatic hernia. It could strangulate his intestines."

I felt the panic rising in my throat, a suffocating, terrifying wave. The deep, physical pressure of holding my sister was the only thing that grounded my misfiring neurological system. It was how we survived a chaotic world.

"I need to hold her," I cried out, my voice cracking, tears instantly spilling down my face. "I have to. It's the only way I can sleep without my muscles jumping. I need her."

Rachel frantically nodded her head, her small hands gripping the hospital blanket. She let out a distressed, breathy whimper, her chest hitching. She needed her anchor. The Dojang had stripped away our physical safety, and now the hospital was stripping away our only coping mechanism.

"I am so sorry, Michael," the doctor said firmly. "But the answer is no. You both have to sleep completely flat on your backs, separated by a physical barrier."

The neurological circuit-breaker in my brain violently snapped.

The terror of the Dojang, the blinding agony of the torn fascia, the exhaustion of the hospital, and now the complete severance of my anchor all collided at once. I spiraled into a full, catastrophic meltdown.

I started sobbing hysterically, my breathing turning into rapid, ragged gasps. Beside me, Rachel completely lost her silent composure. She let out a high-pitched, terrified wail, her body immediately trying to curl into a tight, protective fetal position.

Usually, when a meltdown hit, Mom and Dad would back off. They would dim the lights, clear the room, and let the storm burn itself out until we were exhausted enough to be reasoned with.

But riding out the storm was no longer a medical option.

"Stop her from curling!" the doctor shouted, the clinical calm completely vanishing from his voice. "If she curls her knees to her chest, she'll pop her spleen! Tom, hold her shoulders flat!"

Dad lunged forward. He didn't hesitate. He placed his massive, heavy hands gently but firmly on the front of Rachel's shoulders, pinning her flat against the mattress so she couldn't crush her own abdomen. "I've got you, Rachel! Stay flat! Breathe!"

But Rachel couldn't breathe. She was hyperventilating, her diaphragm violently expanding and contracting right against the swollen, bruised capsule of her spleen.

On my bed, the sheer emotional distress sent my Cerebral Palsy into overdrive. A massive, full-body spasm ripped through my core. The medical binder strained as my stomach muscles clamped down rigidly.

"Michael's seizing!" Jessica screamed from the corner, watching my face turn a terrifying shade of gray.

"His hernia is going to strangulate! Nurse!" the doctor yelled, abandoning his clipboard.

Nurse Sarah was already moving. She didn't try to talk us down. There was no time for psychological de-escalation; our bodies were actively destroying themselves from the inside out. She slammed two pre-filled syringes into the medication ports of our IV lines and pushed the plungers down hard.

"I'm pushing a heavy, fast-acting sedative and muscle relaxant," Nurse Sarah announced, her hands flying over the plastic tubing.

"Michael, look at me!" Mom cried, grabbing my left hand and pressing it hard against her own chest, giving me a peripheral point of deep pressure. "Squeeze my hand! Do not move your stomach!"

It took ten agonizing, terrifying seconds for the drugs to hit my bloodstream.

When they did, it felt like a heavy, lead blanket was dropped over my entire nervous system. The violent rigidity in my legs and core melted away, forcefully overridden by the chemical intervention. The hysterical sobbing caught in my throat, replaced by a thick, groggy exhaustion.

Across the gap, Rachel’s frantic wailing dissolved into soft, medicated hiccups. Her body went limp beneath Dad’s hands, her legs sliding back down until she was lying perfectly flat on the mattress again.

The only sound left in the room was the rapid, frantic beeping of our heart monitors slowly beginning to decelerate.

The doctor let out a long, shaky breath, wiping a bead of sweat from his forehead. He looked at Mom and Dad, both of whom were pale and trembling from the near-miss.

"Listen to me," the doctor said, his voice dropping to a harsh, absolute whisper. "You cannot let them get to that point again. They cannot cry like that. They cannot panic. A meltdown will literally kill them. You have to keep their environment perfectly calm, and you have to find a way to anchor them without letting them cross the center of that mattress."

Dad slowly took his hands off Rachel's shoulders. He looked down at us, watching our heavily medicated, half-open eyes staring blankly at the ceiling.

The illusion of a simple recovery at home was gone. The living room wasn't just going to be a recovery space; it had to be a perfectly engineered, zero-stress sanctuary. The pack wasn't just protecting us from the outside world anymore. They had to protect us from our own panic.
