The heavy, suffocating weight of Rachel's four-month prognosis settled over the trauma bay. Dad was still leaning over her bed, holding her hand, murmuring soft, fierce promises into the quiet hum of the machinery.

I watched my little sister cry, and a profound, overwhelming wave of helplessness crashed over me. My neurological system, already frayed and misfiring from the trauma of the past thirty-six hours, couldn't process the emotional surge.

With Cerebral Palsy, high-tone spasticity doesn't just react to physical movement; it reacts to stress.

As I stared at Rachel's bruised, heaving chest, my breathing hitched. My heart rate spiked. And before I could stop it, a massive, involuntary, whole-body muscle spasm violently ripped through my core.

My stomach clamped down with terrifying, uncontrollable force. The partially torn abdominal fascia—the thick connective tissue that had snapped under the weight of Master Curtis's foot on Thursday—was suddenly subjected to the maximum, rigid tension of my own seizing muscles.

I felt a sickening, profound *pop* deep inside my gut, followed instantly by a blinding flash of agony that eclipsed anything I had felt on the Dojang floor.

I gasped, a wet, choking sound, my free hand violently flying to my stomach. I couldn't breathe. My entire body locked into a rigid board of pure pain.

Dad’s head snapped up.

He looked at me, his eyes wide, seeing the sweat instantly break out on my forehead. He watched me gasping for air, clutching the bruised center of my stomach.

For a fraction of a second, the old instincts flared in his eyes. The rationalizing, tough-love patriarch tried to assert control over the panic. *It's sympathetic pain,* he thought. *He's watching his sister cry, and his anxiety is manifesting physically. He just needs to calm down.*

But then, Dad remembered the vow he had made on his knees the night before. *I will listen to you.*

Dad didn't dismiss it. He didn't tell me to take a deep breath. Instead, he stood up, turned his massive frame around, and physically blocked the trauma attending physician from walking out the glass door.

"Stop," Dad commanded, his voice a low, terrifying rumble of absolute authority. He pointed at me. "Look at my son. Right now."

The doctor turned, saw my rigid posture and gray face, and immediately rushed to the side of my bed. "Michael? Talk to me. What happened?"

"It popped," I gasped out, tears of pure agony leaking from the corners of my eyes. "My stomach... the spasm... it popped again."

The doctor didn't hesitate. He pulled the hospital gown back, exposing the massive, purple footprint bruise.

Dad let out a sharp hiss of breath.

Right in the center of the dark hematoma, just beneath my belly button, a small, distinct bulge had appeared under the skin.

The doctor gently pressed his gloved fingers around the perimeter of the bulge, and I let out a jagged scream, my vision swimming with black spots.

"Push the IV muscle relaxant to the maximum safe dose," the doctor ordered Nurse Sarah immediately, his clinical mask locking back into place. He turned back to Dad. "Tom, the fascial tear just gave way completely. When his CP triggered that spasm, the muscle tension pulled the tear entirely apart. He has a traumatic ventral hernia. A portion of his underlying tissue is now pushing through the abdominal wall."

Dad looked sick. "Fix it. Do the surgery. Put it back."

"I can't," the doctor replied grimly. "Not right now. If we take him into the OR and surgically suture that muscle wall together, the very first time he wakes up and has a CP-related muscle spasm, the tension will violently rip the stitches right out of the tissue. It would cause catastrophic internal damage."

"Then what do we do?" Dad demanded, his voice cracking with desperation.

"We brace it," the doctor explained. "We are going to fit him with a heavy, medical-grade abdominal binder. It will act as an artificial muscle wall, keeping the tissue pushed inside while the fascia naturally scars over and thickens. But Tom... the fascia is the slowest healing tissue in the human body."

The doctor looked directly at me, his eyes full of deep, sympathetic regret.

"To heal a full tear without surgical intervention, you cannot engage your core at all," the doctor stated clearly. "No bending, no twisting, no sitting up on your own. You are looking at the exact same timeline as your sister. Four months of strict, absolute couch rest."

I stared at the ceiling, the heavy, narcotic warmth of the increased IV drip finally beginning to dull the blinding pain. Four months. Rachel and I were officially, completely grounded. Master Curtis had stolen the entire fall from us.

Dad didn't say a word. He looked at the bulge on my stomach, then looked across the gap at Rachel, who was watching me with wide, terrified eyes.

He slowly walked over to my bed, leaned down, and kissed the top of my forehead.

"I've got you," Dad whispered. "Both of you. Don't you worry about a thing."

Dad turned and walked out of the trauma bay, the heavy glass door sliding shut behind him.

Out in the hallway, Dad walked directly to the bank of payphones near the nurses' station. He dropped a quarter into the slot, his hands shaking with a terrifying, cold rage, and dialed the house on Delaney Street.

Mom answered on the first ring. "Tom? What's wrong? Are they okay?"

"Get Jessica and Janette," Dad ordered, his voice completely devoid of its usual booming volume. It was quiet, lethal, and absolute. "Bring them back to the hospital right now. We are having a family meeting."

"Tom, what happened?" Mom asked, panic rising in her throat.

"They're both grounded for four months, Ellen," Dad stated, his voice trembling as the sheer magnitude of the destruction finally settled over him. "Rachel's spleen is swelling, and Michael's abdominal wall just tore completely open. They are going to be living on the living room couch until Christmas."

Mom let out a devastated sob through the receiver.

"I need the pack here," Dad continued, gripping the plastic receiver so hard it creaked. "We are setting up the schedules. We are figuring out how we carry them, how we bathe them, and how we protect them."

Dad paused, looking back through the glass window of the trauma bay, watching Rachel and me desperately reaching across the gap to lace our fingers together once again.

"And Ellen?" Dad added, the fierce, protective patriarch fully resurrecting, but this time, aimed entirely at the outside world.

"What?" Mom whispered.

"First thing Monday morning, I want you to find the most ruthless, vicious personal injury and civil rights lawyer in the state of Virginia," Dad commanded. "I don't care what it costs. We are going to sue Tidewater Tumblers until the physical building is nothing but dust, and then we are going to sue the ground it was built on."
