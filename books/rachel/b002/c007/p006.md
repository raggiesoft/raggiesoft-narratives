The transition from day to night in our new living room fortress required a delicate, deliberate shifting of gears.

By ten-thirty, the sheer physical and emotional exhaustion of the hospital discharge had caught up with everyone. Mom administered our final dose of medication for the night—muscle relaxants for me, heavy Tylenol for Rachel—and kissed our foreheads. Dad did a final, meticulous sweep of the house, checking the deadbolts on the front and back doors twice.

"We are right at the top of the stairs," Dad promised, standing at the edge of the living room. "If you need the bathroom, if you need water, if you feel even a twinge of pain, you yell for me, and I will be down here in three seconds. Understand?"

"We've got it, Dad," Jessica said softly, sitting cross-legged on her twin mattress on the floor to the left of our sofa bed.

"Goodnight, pack," Dad murmured.

We listened to the heavy, rhythmic creak of the wooden stairs as Mom and Dad climbed to the second floor. We heard the soft *click* of their bedroom door shutting.

The moment the latch caught, the entire atmospheric pressure of the downstairs shifted.

The heavy, complicated burden of adult guilt, parental authority, and the unspoken tension of the Dojang vanished. The downstairs officially belonged to the pack.

Janette walked over to the wall switch, flicking off the bright overhead lights and leaving only the soft, warm glow of the corner reading lamp. She walked back to her own twin mattress, positioned on the floor to the right of the sofa bed, effectively flanking us.

Jessica reached up from her mattress and gently adjusted the three firm body pillows running down the exact center of our queen bed, making sure the structural barrier was perfectly straight.

"Water checks," Jess announced quietly. She placed a plastic cup with a long, bendy straw on the floor right next to Rachel’s head, and Janette did the same on my side. We didn't have to engage our cores to drink; we just had to turn our heads.

I was lying flat on my back, the heavy medical binder tight around my stomach. I reached my left arm up over the cotton mountain of the pillow wall. A second later, Rachel’s small hand met mine. Our fingers laced together, anchoring us in the dark. Beneath the blankets, I felt her right heel slide over and hook firmly against my left ankle.

The perimeter was locked.

For a few minutes, the only sound in the room was the hum of the air conditioning and the soft rustle of blankets as Jess and Janette settled into their beds on the floor.

Then, a tiny, raspy whisper broke the silence.

"Are they gone?"

Jess shifted on her mattress, her head popping up to look over the edge of the sofa bed. Janette immediately turned her head toward the center of the room.

It was Rachel.

With the adults completely removed from the environment, the invisible vault door in Rachel’s mind had finally unlocked. She wasn't just selectively mute; she was profoundly traumatized by the failure of the adults in her life. Master Curtis had weaponized her voice, and Mom and Dad had delivered her to him. The betrayal was still too raw. But here, surrounded strictly by the four of us, she was safe.

"They're upstairs, Rachel," Jess whispered back, her voice incredibly gentle. "Their door is shut. It's just us."

Rachel squeezed my hand tightly over the pillow wall. I could hear her breathing hitch slightly, testing the waters of her own voice, proving to herself that the monster wasn't going to materialize from the shadows to crush her for speaking.

"I'm mad at them," Rachel stated. Her voice was barely more than a breath, trembling with the weight of an eight-year-old's unfiltered grief. "They didn't listen on Thursday. They made us go back inside."

"I know," I whispered, turning my head toward the pillow barrier so my voice would carry directly to her. "I'm mad at them, too."

"They're trying to fix it," Janette offered softly from the floor on my side, not defending the parents, but simply stating the new reality. "Uncle Tom was ready to tear the hospital down today."

"I don't care," Rachel whispered, a single, hot tear slipping down the side of her face and dropping onto the mattress. "I only want to talk to you guys. I don't want to talk to them."

"Then you don't have to," Jess said immediately, validating her boundary without a single ounce of hesitation. "You don't owe them your voice right now, Rachel. If you only want to talk when it's just the pack, then that is exactly what we'll do. We'll be your voice when they are in the room."

"We protect the perimeter," Janette agreed fiercely from the dark. "Whatever you need."

Rachel let out a long, shuddering exhale. The tension that had been locking her jaw and her shoulders all day finally, truly dissolved. The boundaries were set, and her pack had unconditionally accepted them.

"My ribs hurt," Rachel murmured, the adrenaline fading to leave only the heavy, aching reality of her injuries.

"I know, bug," I whispered, rubbing my thumb gently across her knuckles. "My stomach hurts, too. But we don't have to go anywhere tomorrow. We just have to sleep."

"Okay," Rachel whispered.

I closed my eyes, letting the heavy, narcotic pull of the muscle relaxers drag me down. The road ahead of us was going to be four months of agonizing physical restriction and intense legal warfare. But as I lay there, flanked by my oldest sister and my fiercely loyal cousin, holding my little sister's hand over the barricade, I knew we were going to survive it.

The adults were upstairs. But the pack was holding the line.
