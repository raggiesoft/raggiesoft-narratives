The adrenaline that had fueled the adults through the Monday War finally began to burn off as the sun set over Delaney Street. The living room darkened, illuminated only by the warm, flickering glow of the television screen and the small lamp on the end table.

Dinner was a quiet, exhausted affair. Mom brought out the vanilla milkshakes Dr. Evans had delivered, along with small bowls of lukewarm chicken broth. We drank our meals through plastic straws, still propped up on our rigid, forty-five-degree medical wedges.

Nobody talked about the civil suit. Nobody talked about the Dojang. The absolute, permanent loss of our martial arts journey was a heavy, invisible weight pressing down on the room, but the pack handled it the only way we could: by tightening the perimeter and focusing on the immediate steps in front of us.

"Alright, base camp," Mom announced gently, walking into the living room carrying a plastic washbasin, two cups of water, and a hand towel. "Time for hygiene protocol."

Jessica grabbed the plastic grocery bag from the coffee table and pulled out the two ultra-soft bristle toothbrushes Dr. Evans had dropped off. She handed the blue one to me and the red one to Rachel.

Brushing our teeth, a task that used to be a mindless two-minute routine upstairs, was now a major logistical operation. Because we couldn't lean forward over a sink, Mom had to hold the plastic basin directly under our chins while we lay back against the wedges.

I squeezed a tiny dot of toothpaste onto the blue bristles. As I started to brush, I instantly understood why Dr. Evans had brought them. The standard, aggressive motion of brushing naturally engages the core muscles. Doing it with a regular, firm toothbrush would have sent painful tremors straight down into my torn fascia. But the surgical-soft bristles required almost no pressure.

Beside me, Rachel was discovering the same thing. She kept her eyes focused on the popcorn ceiling, moving the red brush in slow, careful circles to keep her diaphragm perfectly still. When it was time to spit, Mom lifted the basin, and we let the water dribble out of our mouths, entirely stripped of our dignity but grateful for the pain-free execution.

"Good job," Mom whispered, wiping Rachel’s chin with the towel. "Almost done for the day."

The final hurdle was the last bathroom break of the night.

Dad stepped up to the edge of the mattress. The exhaustion in his eyes was profound, but his physical strength never wavered.

"Ready, Michael?" Dad asked quietly.

I nodded. He slid his massive arms under my shoulders and knees, locking my spine and core into the rigid plank carry. He lifted me from the mattress. The shift in gravity pulled painfully against my stomach, and I squeezed my eyes shut, letting out a sharp breath. Dad moved with swift, calculated steps down the short hallway to the half-bath, giving me my privacy before carrying me back.

He repeated the exact same flawless, gentle maneuver for Rachel.

Once we were both back on the sofa bed, the nightly teardown commenced.

"On three," Dad instructed, taking his position on my side while Mom stood by Rachel. "One, two, three."

They lifted our upper bodies just enough for Jessica and Janette to pull the heavy, foam medical wedges out from behind our backs. Mom and Dad slowly lowered us until we were lying perfectly flat against the mattress.

The relief of taking the weight off our spines was immediate, but it was quickly overshadowed by the isolation of the sleeping arrangement. Mom fluffed the massive, three-pillow barrier that ran directly down the center of the bed, creating the cotton wall that kept us from accidentally rolling into each other during the night.

I turned my head to the left. Over the top of the pillows, I could just barely see the top of Rachel’s dark hair and her eyes looking back at me.

I reached my hand up, resting it on top of the middle pillow. A second later, Rachel’s small, pale hand reached up and laced her fingers tightly through mine.

"I'm right here, Bug," I whispered into the quiet room.

Rachel didn't speak. The day had taken every ounce of energy she had, but she squeezed my fingers twice—a silent, desperate anchor.

Around us, the pack settled into their positions. Jessica clicked off the small lamp, plunging the room into shadows. She and Janette lay down on their floor mattresses, pulling their blankets up to their chins.

Baby, having patrolled the house one last time, let out a massive, rumbling sigh and collapsed onto the carpet right next to Rachel’s side of the bed. He rested his heavy chin on his front paws, facing the hallway. Archie curled into a tight, fuzzy ball underneath the coffee table, his good eye already shut.

In the hallway, Grandma Loretta stepped out of the kitchen carrying a small overnight bag.

"Alright, Ellen. I'm heading up," Grandma Loretta said softly.

Tomorrow was Tuesday. The adrenaline of the long weekend was over, and Mom and Dad had to return to their jobs in the morning. Tomorrow marked the first official day of the true "new normal," and Grandma Loretta was staying the night to act as the daytime warden. Because she couldn't safely lift our dead weight for the wedges or the bathroom, the grueling "Lunch Lift" protocol would officially begin at noon tomorrow.

"Thank you, Mom," Mom whispered, hugging her. "Michael's room is all made up for you."

Grandma Loretta walked to the edge of the living room, looking over the base camp. "Sleep well, my angels. I'll be right upstairs."

As Grandma Loretta's footsteps faded up the wooden stairs toward my empty 10x10 bedroom, Mom and Dad stood at the archway for a long moment, looking at the triage center they had built in the center of their home. The monsters were locked in concrete cells. The civil suit was filed. The district was on notice.

The Monday War was officially over.

"Goodnight, base camp," Dad rumbled softly.

"We love you guys," Mom added, her voice thick with emotion. "Sleep safe."

They turned and walked quietly up the stairs, leaving the heavy door to the kitchen propped open.

I lay flat on my back, staring up at the dark ceiling, feeling the rhythmic, comforting pressure of Rachel's pulse against my palm. The first day of our new reality was done. We had survived it. But as the silence of the house settled over us, I knew this wasn't the end of the battle. It was just the beginning of a long, agonizingly slow endurance test.

We had to survive tomorrow. And the day after that. And the day after that.

But as Baby let out a soft snore from the floor, and Rachel's breathing finally deepened into the steady rhythm of sleep, I knew we wouldn't be doing it alone. The perimeter was locked. The pack was together.

Let the four months begin.
