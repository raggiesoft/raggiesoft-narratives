The hum of a familiar engine pulled into the driveway, followed by the unmistakable sound of heavy paws thumping against the floor of a van.

"The boys are here," Jessica announced, standing up from her floor mattress.

Rachel’s hand tightened in mine instantly. Her anxiety about the dogs wasn't about a lack of love; it was about the physics of a hundred-and-thirty-pound Black Lab in a room full of broken ribs.

"Janette, Jess, take the wall," Dad ordered, stepping out of the kitchen.

My sister and cousin moved with practiced, military precision. Jessica sat on the very edge of the sofa bed on Rachel's side, and Janette did the same on mine. They dangled their legs off the side, creating a "knee-wall" that blocked any direct path for a dog to jump onto the mattress. They were the last line of defense for our cores.

Aunt Janet opened the front door, and the air was immediately filled with the rhythmic *clack-clack-clack* of nails on the hardwood and the jingle of heavy metal collars.

"Easy, boys! Easy!" Aunt Janet's voice was strained as she held two heavy nylon leashes.

Archie came around the corner first. He was the smaller of the two, but he was a kinetic explosion of energy. His tail was a blur, thwacking against the doorframe like a drumstick. In his mouth, he held a tattered, neon-green tennis ball—his favorite toy of the minute because it was the last one Janet had touched.

Archie’s left eye, cloudy and muddy blue from his blindness, caught the light as he scanned the room. The second his good eye landed on us, he let out a high-pitched, desperate whine and lunged forward, his front paws sliding on the linoleum as he fought the leash to get to the bed.

"Archie, sit!" Dad commanded, his voice a low, stern anchor.

Archie’s butt hit the floor instantly, though his entire body continued to vibrate. He dropped the tennis ball and nudged it toward the bed with his nose, offering the pack his most prized possession as a peace offering.

Then, Baby stepped into the room.

Baby was a massive wall of black muscle, so tall he could rest his chin on the dining table without trying. Usually, he was a clumsy, lovable disaster—a "bull in a china shop" who didn't know his own strength.

But as Baby crossed the threshold of the living room, something shifted. He didn't lung. He didn't bark. He stopped dead, his large head tilting as he caught the scent of the room: the sharp, clinical smell of the medical binders, the scent of the painkillers, and the underlying pheromones of our exhaustion.

Dogs know when the pack is broken.

Baby let out a low, mournful huff and walked forward with a caution I had never seen from him. He moved slowly, his heavy paws landing silently on the carpet. He ignored the tennis ball. He ignored Janette and Jessica.

He walked straight to the gap between the two girls and stopped inches from the mattress. He was so tall that even standing on the floor, his head was level with mine.

Baby looked at me, his deep brown eyes filled with an eerie, ancient understanding. He sniffed the air near my medical binder, his nose twitching, then he looked over the three-pillow wall at Rachel.

Rachel was frozen, her eyes wide as she watched the massive dog loom over the bed.

Baby reached out and very, very gently rested his heavy chin on the edge of the quilt, right next to Rachel’s hip. He didn't move. He didn't try to climb up. He just applied the slightest bit of pressure—a warm, grounding weight that told her he was on guard.

Archie, seeing Baby’s lead, finally calmed down. He crawled forward on his belly, dragging his leash behind him, and tucked himself under the coffee table right in front of us. He rested his chin on his paws, his blue eye fixed on the front door.

"See, Bug?" I whispered, my voice thick with relief. "They know."

Rachel let out a long, shuddering breath. She reached her left hand—the one not holding mine—and let her fingers sink into the thick, soft fur on top of Baby's head.

"Good boys," she rasped, her voice tiny but steady.

The perimeter was finally complete. We had Mom and Grandma in the kitchen, Dad in the hallway, Jessica and Janette on the edge of the bed, and the boys on the floor.

The house was no longer just a building in Norfolk; it was a fortress. The Ragsdale pack was entirely under one roof, and for the first time since Thursday night, the crushing weight of the world felt just a little bit easier to carry.
