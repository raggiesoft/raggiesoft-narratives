By mid-morning, Janette had swapped *The Desert Prince* for another Majestic Studios classic: *The Lost Valley*.

It was an animated epic about a young, orphaned dinosaur trying to survive a harsh, prehistoric world and find a mythical sanctuary. It was a movie we had seen a dozen times before. We knew the story. We knew the beats.

But trauma changes the way you process the world.

Twenty minutes into the movie, the sky on the television screen turned dark with animated rain. The young dinosaur’s mother had just fought off a massive, terrifying predator to protect her baby. She had won, but the fight had cost her everything. As she collapsed onto the muddy ground, her breathing slow and heavy, she spoke her final, heartbreaking words of comfort to her child before closing her eyes forever.

The baby dinosaur was left completely alone, crying in the rain, entirely vulnerable to the monsters of the world.

On the sofa bed, the emotional dam completely shattered.

It wasn't just a sad movie scene anymore. It was a mirror reflecting our own profound helplessness. It hit the deepest, rawest nerve of Thursday night—the terrifying realization that the adults who were supposed to protect us couldn't always stop the monsters.

Rachel’s breathing hitched sharply. A violent, ragged sob tore its way out of her throat.

Instantly, the physical consequences of that sob sent a shockwave of agony through her body. The sudden, violent contraction of her diaphragm pushed her cracked ribs inward, grinding them against the swollen, blood-filled capsule of her spleen.

She gasped in pain, her hands flying to her left side. The pain triggered pure panic, and the panic fueled the sobbing. Her knees started to curl upward in a desperate, instinctive attempt to pull into a fetal position to protect her organs.

Beside her, my own reaction was just as catastrophic.

Seeing Rachel in pain—seeing her cry and knowing I couldn't roll over to hold her—broke me. I started to cry, heavy, heaving sobs that violently engaged my shredded core. Every time my chest heaved, it felt like a hot knife was dragging across the torn fascia in my stomach. The medical binder squeezed like a vice.

The pain was blinding. The panic was absolute.

Within seconds, we were both trapped in a severe, escalating meltdown, completely unable to self-soothe, and entirely locked in situational mutism. Neither of us could speak. We were just gasping, sobbing, and suffocating under the weight of our own bodies.

"Mom! Grandma!" Jessica screamed, her voice cracking with sheer terror as she leaped up from her floor mattress. "Help! They're crying! Help!"

Janette lunged toward the VCR, desperately reaching for the stop button, but the damage was already done.

Mom and Grandma Loretta burst into the living room, their faces pale with horror. Mom took one look at Rachel’s heaving chest and my rigid, agonizing contractions, and her tactical war-room demeanor evaporated, replaced by pure, adrenaline-fueled medical triage.

"Oh my god, their cores!" Mom shouted, rushing to the left side of the bed. She grabbed Rachel’s shoulders, gently but firmly pressing her back flat against the mattress to stop her from curling into a ball. "Rachel, look at me! Look at me! You have to stop! You are going to rupture!"

Grandma Loretta rushed to my side, her trembling hands resting on my shoulders, trying to anchor me. "Breathe, Michael! Deep breaths, baby, you have to relax your stomach!"

But we couldn't. The panic was a runaway train. We were drowning in the trauma, desperately reaching for each other over the pillow wall, our fingers slipping as we sobbed.

Just as the chaos reached a fever pitch, the front door lock clicked, and the heavy oak door swung open.

Dad walked in, still wearing his sharp court suit, his tie loosened. He had just returned from watching the judge lock Curtis and Ray in their cages.

He dropped his keys on the entryway table. He took one look at the living room—the crying, the panic, Mom physically holding Rachel flat to protect her spleen—and he didn't ask a single question.

Dad moved with terrifying, protective speed.

He bypassed Grandma Loretta, dropping to his knees right beside my side of the sofa bed. He didn't yell. He didn't panic. He utilized the one thing that no monster and no nightmare could overcome: his absolute, immovable presence.

"Base camp," Dad commanded. His voice wasn't loud, but it was a deep, resonant rumble that vibrated through the floorboards, completely cutting through the noise of the television and the panic.

He placed one massive, warm hand flat against the center of my chest, completely bypassing the medical binder, and reached his other hand directly over the pillow barrier to place it flat against Rachel’s right shoulder.

He physically anchored both of us to the mattress.

"Look at me," Dad ordered gently, his dark eyes locking onto mine, then shifting to Rachel. "Right here. Look at my eyes."

We stared up at him, our chests heaving, tears streaming down our faces.

"I have you," Dad rumbled, his voice the ultimate, unshakable foundation. "The monsters are locked in a cage. They are never getting out. You are safe in this room. But you have to breathe for me. Shallow and slow. Do not use your stomachs. Use your throats."

Mom knelt on the opposite side of the bed, her hands covering Rachel's. "Listen to Dad. Shallow breaths. Like you're sipping through a straw. Sip the air."

"With me," Dad instructed. He inhaled a slow, controlled, shallow breath.

I stared at the familiar lines of my father's face, anchoring my entire existence to his calm. I forced my jaw open and mimicked him. A short, shallow sip of air.

Beside me, Rachel’s dark eyes were locked onto Dad’s hand resting on her shoulder. She let out a shaky, stuttering exhale, and then she took a shallow sip of air, too.

"Good. Again," Dad praised quietly, never breaking eye contact, never lifting his hands.

For five agonizing minutes, the adults functioned as our external nervous systems. Dad and Mom breathed for us, forcing our frantic, traumatized bodies to sync with their calm, steady rhythms. Slowly, the violent heaving in our chests began to subside. The agonizing, hot flares of pain in my fascia dulled back into a steady ache. Rachel’s knees slowly relaxed, lying flat against the quilts once more.

The situational mutism held us in a silent, exhausted grip. We couldn't speak, but the lethal physical danger had passed.

The living room was completely silent, save for the animated rain still falling on the television screen.

Janette, who had been frozen near the entertainment center, let out a shaky breath. She reached out toward the VCR panel.

"I'm turning it off," Janette whispered, her hand hovering over the eject button. "I'm sorry. I shouldn't have picked this one."

*No.* The protest flared up inside me instantly. It was terrifying, and it hurt, but shutting it off now meant leaving the baby dinosaur alone in the rain. It meant the trauma won.

I couldn't speak, but I aggressively shook my head, my eyes wide as I looked at Janette.

On the other side of the pillow wall, Rachel reacted exactly the same way. She squeezed my hand fiercely and shook her head at Janette, a desperate, pleading look in her eyes.

"Guys, it's too upsetting," Jessica said gently from her spot on the floor. "You can't risk crying again."

I swallowed hard, fighting through the heavy, paralyzing wall of the mutism. I looked at Dad, then at Mom.

"No," I rasped, my voice barely a whisper, completely wrecked from the sobbing. "Don't turn it off."

"Michael, your stomach—" Mom started to argue, her protective instincts still on high alert.

"We have to see him make it," I pleaded, my voice cracking. I turned my head to look at Rachel. "Right, Bug?"

Rachel gave a tiny, resolute nod. She didn't want to be left in the dark with the memory of the mother dying. She needed to see the little dinosaur find his friends. She needed to see him find the sanctuary. She needed the emotional closure to prove that survival was possible after the monsters took everything.

Dad looked at me, then looked at Rachel. He understood the psychological necessity of it perfectly.

"Leave it on, Janette," Dad ordered softly, slowly lifting his hands from our chests now that the danger had passed. He looked down at us, his eyes filled with profound pride. "They're going to finish it."

Janette pulled her hand back from the VCR. She and Jessica returned to their floor mattresses, retaking their guard posts. Mom and Grandma Loretta slowly backed away, retreating to the kitchen, but leaving the heavy wooden door cracked open an inch so they could listen in.

Dad didn't leave.

He didn't go upstairs to change out of his suit. He simply unbuttoned his suit jacket, sat down on the edge of the coffee table directly in front of the sofa bed, and crossed his arms.

We locked our fingers together over the pillow wall, our breathing shallow and completely controlled, and watched the rest of the movie with our father standing guard.
