The house was quiet, wrapped in the exhausted, mid-day lull of the Homebound schedule. Rachel and I were lying perfectly flat against the mattress, our fingers locked together over the three-pillow barrier, when a soft knock sounded at the front door.

Uncle Jim stepped out of the kitchen, wiping his hands on a dish towel.

On their floor mattresses, Jessica and Janette instantly sat up. Their protective postures locked into place, their eyes fixed on the entryway. Archie let out a soft huff from under the coffee table, while Baby lifted his massive head, his ears swiveling forward.

Jim pulled the front door open.

Standing on the porch was April.

Seeing her outside the Dojang was jarring, but seeing her out of her uniform was almost unrecognizable. She wasn't wearing the midnight blue *dobok* of a 4th Dan. She was wearing faded denim jeans, a plain gray t-shirt, and white canvas sneakers. She just looked like an average twenty-two-year-old girl. Tucked under her arm was a thick, hardcover book.

"Hi, I'm April," she said softly, her voice carrying none of the booming authority of a martial arts instructor. "I know it’s the middle of the day. But Tom and Ellen said I was on the approved list to visit."

"I'm Jim Raybourn, Tom's neighbor," Jim replied, his voice a steady, protective rumble as he extended a hand. "I'm helping with the midday lifts. They're just resting. Come on in."

"Thank you, Mr. Raybourn," April said, shaking his hand before stepping into the foyer.

As she rounded the corner into the living room, the absolute devastation of the Base Camp hit her. She saw the heavy, white canvas medical binders wrapped around our waists. She saw the massive pillow wall dividing the bed. She saw Baby, the hundred-and-thirty-pound Black Lab, acting as a literal shield for Rachel. And she saw Jessica and Janette, sitting on the floor with their arms crossed, watching her every single move with the fierce, unwavering intensity of prison guards.

April didn't take a single step toward the mattress. She stopped at the edge of the carpet.

She looked at me, and then at Rachel. Her eyes filled with a profound, quiet sorrow. She didn't offer a hollow greeting. Instead, she brought her heels together, placed her hands flat against her thighs, and bowed.

It was a deep, formal, ninety-degree bow at the waist.

In the rigid hierarchy of Korean martial arts, a 4th Dan never bows to a Brown *tti*. It was a massive, deliberate break from tradition. But April wasn't bowing to our rank. She was bowing to our survival, our perseverance, and the sheer, agonizing reality of our injuries.

She held the bow for three long seconds before rising.

"Hi, guys," April whispered.

"Hi, Master April," I rasped.

April offered a small, gentle smile, stepping carefully toward the vinyl recliner stationed near my side of the bed. "Can I sit down?" she asked, actively requesting consent to breach the perimeter.

I looked at Jessica and Janette. They didn't move, but their postures softened just a fraction. I looked over the pillows at Rachel, who gave a firm nod.

"You can sit," I answered.

April walked slowly to the recliner and sat down, setting her thick book on her lap. She looked closely at us, reading the heavy toll the morning's tutoring had taken. "How are you guys healing?"

I swallowed hard. The memory of Nurse Brenda’s clinical assessment from the day before was a heavy weight in my chest.

"We're doing okay," I started, my voice trembling slightly. "But... Nurse Brenda was here yesterday. She told us the rules for when we heal."

April leaned forward slightly, her eyes locking onto mine. "What are the rules, Michael?"

"She said my stomach muscles will just be scar tissue, and Rachel's spleen will always be fragile," I explained, tears pricking the corners of my eyes. "She said we are permanently banned from contact sports. We can never, ever do martial arts again."

April went completely still.

She had dedicated her entire life to the art, and she had promised to be there when we earned our Junior Black Belts. Hearing that Curtis hadn't just injured us, but had permanently, biologically stripped the mat away from us, was a devastating blow.

April closed her eyes for a brief second. When she opened them, the grief was masked by an unbreakable, quiet strength. She reached her hand out, gently resting her fingers over mine where they gripped the edge of the quilt. Then, she reached across the small gap and gave Rachel's hand a soft, reassuring squeeze.

"Curtis took the mat away from you," April said, her voice a fierce, steady whisper. "But he did not take the art away from you. The mat is just a floor. What he cared about was fighting—the martial part. But this discipline is an *Art*. And the art is in your mind."

April picked up the heavy book from her lap. The cover was clean and academic, featuring a traditional Korean geometric knot.

"This is called *Seondu: Tradition and Form in Korean Karate*," April explained, tracing the gold lettering. "It was written by Grandmaster Lee Dong-won. He is the man who oversees our entire governing body in Seoul. This book is the absolute bible of our artform. I have been studying it since I was six years old, and now, I want to teach it to you."

Rachel’s eyes widened slightly.

"I signed a commercial lease this morning," April announced, a profound, quiet pride in her voice. "It isn't going to be just a garage anymore. I am opening a proper, fully sanctioned Dojang. It will be called the Lynnhaven Academy of Seondu. And I want you and Rachel to be my very first, official founding students."

My chest tightened with a massive surge of emotion. "But we can't leave the sofa."

"I know," April smiled gently. "So until you are completely healed and ready, I am bringing the Dojang to you."

Rachel tapped my knuckles twice, her dark eyes shining.

"We want to learn," I told her. "Thank you, Master April."

April held up her hand, her expression softening even further. "If we are going to do this, we are going to do it the right way. We are going to slowly learn the true language and tradition of the art. I know Curtis used to bark at you and demand that you call him 'Sir.' I don't ever want you to feel like you have to say that word again. It belongs to a place that hurt you."

She tapped her chest. "In my Dojang, we use the traditional Korean titles to remove the ego. You don't have to call me Master April. You can call me *Sabomnim*. It simply means 'Teacher.' And when I ask you a question, you don't have to shout 'Yes, Sir.' You just say, '*Ye, Sabomnim*.'"

*Ye, Sabomnim.* I rolled the words over in my mind. They felt incredibly safe. They didn't carry the harsh, militaristic edge of the strip mall; they felt rooted in ancient, academic respect.

I looked at her and nodded. "*Ye, Sabomnim*."

April opened the heavy cover. She didn't read with the booming, aggressive voice of an instructor. She read with the calm, methodical cadence of a scholar. For the next thirty minutes, she read the introduction and the entire first chapter. She taught us the history of the lineage, the true meaning of the word *Seondu* (to lead by example), and the absolute necessity of internal discipline.

As she read, the exhaustion of the morning's tutoring block finally began to catch up with us. My eyelids grew impossibly heavy, and beside me, Rachel’s breathing deepened into a slow, tired rhythm.

April noticed immediately. She didn't push us to finish a section. She simply closed the book, the soft thud of the pages echoing in the quiet room.

"That's enough for today," April whispered.

"Uncle Jim?" I called out, my voice groggy. "Bathroom break, please."

Jim stepped out of the kitchen instantly. "You got it, buddy."

April slid her chair back, giving Jim plenty of room. She watched in absolute silence as Jim dropped to one knee, spoke gently to me, and slid his arms under my dead weight. She watched the agonizingly slow, calculated rigid plank carry. When Jim returned and executed the exact same flawless, terrifying maneuver for Rachel, April’s expression tightened.

This whole time, Jessica and Janette stayed right at their posts as sentinels, watching her every move, making sure April didn't do anything to hurt us. But April didn't. She had even stopped reading the moment she saw we were getting overwhelmed. Seeing the sheer physical dependency—the absolute fragility of our bodies—solidified her resolve.

Once we were both flat on the mattress again, Jim retreated to the kitchen.

"Sabomnim?" I asked quietly, testing the new, safe title. "What is going to happen to Ray and Curtis?"

"Your parents are handling the police and the American lawsuits," April said, leaning forward. "But as a *Sunhoe Sabomnim*, I am the Voice of Korea. I am handling the art. I have compiled a massive dossier of everything Curtis did. I am submitting it to Grandmaster Lee Dong-won to have them completely excommunicated."

Rachel frowned behind the pillows, her brow furrowing in confusion.

"What does excommunicated mean?" I asked for her. "We've never heard that word."

"It means they are erased," April stated, her voice forged from cold steel. "The Grandmasters will strike their names from the international registry. They will be stripped of their Dan ranks and their titles. They will never be allowed to wear a black belt, and they will be banned from every sanctioned Dojang in the world. They won't just go to jail; they will lose the art forever."

The finality of the word hung in the air. Erased. It was exactly what they deserved.

Rachel looked at April. She looked at the heavy bible of *Seondu* in the 22-year-old's lap, and then she looked at me. She gave me a firm nod, her eyes reflecting a deep, newly restored trust. April had respected the perimeter. She had stopped reading when we were tired. She was protecting our legacy.

"Sabomnim?" I rasped. "Next time you come... you can wear your uniform. So it feels like a formal lesson."

April’s eyes shined with unshed tears. For us to permit the *dobok*—the very symbol of our trauma—back into our sanctuary meant we implicitly trusted her with our lives.

"It would be my honor," April whispered.

Suddenly, the yellow wall phone in the kitchen rang. Grandma Loretta answered it. A moment later, she stepped into the living room, holding the long cord.

"April, it's Ellen," Grandma Loretta said. "She's calling on her lunch break from the mall."

April stood up, took the receiver, and stretched the cord so she could speak quietly. "Hi, Ellen... Yes, I'm here. They're doing great."

April listened for a moment, her eyes widening slightly in surprise.

"Ellen, you don't have to do that," April said softly. "I'm doing this because they are my students... Are you sure? Okay. Thank you. I accept."

April handed the receiver back to Grandma Loretta and walked back to the edge of the bed.

"Your mom just told me that since I am still teaching you, they are going to continue paying your tuition directly to my new academy," April explained, her voice thick with gratitude. "You are officially students of the Lynnhaven Academy of Seondu."

We couldn't physically bow, but the profound shift in our reality was absolute. We had a Dojang again.

"I'm going to head out and let you two sleep," April said, stepping back from the bed. She reached out, giving my hand one last, gentle squeeze, and then did the same for Rachel.

April brought her heels together and bowed deeply to us on her way out.

As she walked out the front door, the deadbolt clicking shut behind her, the heavy exhaustion of the day finally pulled me under. I closed my eyes, my hand locked tightly in Rachel's over the pillows. The first day of fifth grade was over, and as we drifted off into a desperately needed nap, we knew our training as true martial artists had just been reborn.
