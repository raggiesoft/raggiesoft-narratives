The final forty-five minutes of the tutoring block required a completely different kind of focus.

Mrs. Higgins collected our reading comprehension packets, seamlessly trading them for two thick, heavy-duty plastic folders. One was bright red, and the other was bright green.

"Alright, let's talk about the schedule," Mrs. Higgins said, pulling her chair slightly closer to the coffee table. "I am only authorized to be in this house for nine hours a week. Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday. But the state of Virginia requires five days of academic instruction."

I shifted slightly against my foam wedge, my neck burning from looking down at the lap desk for so long. "So what happens on Mondays and Fridays?"

"You teach yourselves," Mrs. Higgins answered flatly.

Before I could even process the weight of that, Jessica shifted on her floor mattress. She had been sitting in perfect, absolute silence for over two hours, but her protective instincts immediately seized on the logistical gap.

"Janette and I get home from Morrison Middle at three-thirty on Mondays and Fridays," Jessica spoke up, keeping her voice incredibly polite but firm. "We can supervise their independent blocks. If they need someone to proctor the time, we will make sure they stay on task."

Mrs. Higgins looked over at the fourteen-year-old, offering a small, genuinely appreciative smile. She didn't dismiss the teenager; she recognized the absolute dedication of the pack. "Thank you, Jessica. Having a designated proctor is incredibly helpful."

She turned her attention back to the sofa bed. "As I was saying, I will leave independent reading assignments, math worksheets, and science modules in the Green folder on Thursday afternoon. You are responsible for completing them on Friday and Monday, and moving the finished work to the Red folder."

Rachel reached out, her small fingers tracing the edge of the Green folder resting on her lap desk.

"I know it sounds like a lot of responsibility," Mrs. Higgins continued. Her eyes swept over the massive, white medical binders wrapped around our waists, the heavy, three-pillow barrier running down the center of our bed, and finally settled on the two fiercely vigilant teenage sentinels guarding our flanks. "But looking at the two of you, and how carefully you follow the physical rules of this living room, I don't think discipline is going to be an issue."

It was a profound, validating observation. She didn't see us as helpless victims; she saw us as a highly functional, disciplined unit operating under extreme constraints.

"Rachel," Mrs. Higgins said, shifting her attention across the pillows. "For your independent reading days, I am going to leave a set of multiple-choice quizzes. Just like today, if you don't want to use a pencil, or if it hurts to press down, you don't have to write. You can just fold the corner of the paper next to the correct answer. I will know what it means when I pick it up on Tuesday."

Rachel’s eyes widened. She looked at the teacher, completely stunned by the sheer, practical grace of the accommodation. She didn't have to speak, and she didn't have to hurt her ribs by bearing down on the desk. Mrs. Higgins was actively engineering ways for her to succeed without pain.

Rachel gave a firm, deeply appreciative nod.

"Excellent," Mrs. Higgins smiled, checking the watch on her wrist. "We have about twenty minutes left. Let's do a quick review of the math diagnostics you finished earlier, and then we will wrap up for the day."

For the final stretch, we didn't have to write. We just listened. Mrs. Higgins went over the common mistakes she had seen in our math packets, explaining the concepts clearly and concisely. From their posts on the floor, Jessica and Janette remained perfectly, fiercely silent, honoring their agreement with the teacher to the letter. There was no pressure to perform, no chaotic classroom distractions, and no fear of being called on randomly. It was pure, unfiltered academic data, delivered in the absolute safety of our fortress.
