The heavy, resonant chime of the grandfather clock in the hallway began to strike noon.

Mrs. Higgins immediately stopped talking mid-sentence. She didn't try to squeeze in one last lesson, and she didn't ask us to finish a final page.

"And that is our time," Mrs. Higgins announced, standing up from the heavy oak dining chair.

She moved with practiced efficiency, gathering the diagnostic packets, her red pens, and her clipboard, and sliding them back into her massive canvas tote bag. She pushed the oak chair back into the dining room where she had found it, restoring the living room exactly to its original, carefully curated layout.

Right as she was hoisting the heavy bag over her shoulder, the front door lock clicked open.

Uncle Jim walked into the foyer, right on schedule, letting in a brief gust of crisp September air. He was already rolling the sleeves of his flannel shirt up to his elbows.

"Good afternoon, Jim," Mrs. Higgins greeted him warmly, stepping into the hallway.

"Afternoon, Mrs. Higgins," Jim nodded, stepping aside to give her room to pass. "How did the first day go?"

"Flawlessly," she replied, looking back into the living room. She met my eyes, and then looked at Rachel. "They are incredibly hard workers. I will see you both tomorrow at nine o'clock sharp."

"Thank you, Mrs. Higgins," I rasped, my voice tired but genuine.

Rachel lifted her hand off her lap desk and gave the teacher a small, polite wave.

Mrs. Higgins offered one final nod and stepped out the front door. The deadbolt clicked shut behind her, immediately sealing the outside world away. The academic perimeter was officially down, and the physical perimeter was back online.

"Alright, base camp," Uncle Jim said, stepping up to the edge of the mattress. He looked down at the wooden lap desks resting over our legs. "Class is dismissed. Let's get these desks off you and get the noon lifts out of the way so you can finally rest."

I let out a long, heavy exhale, letting my head fall completely back against the foam wedge. My neck ached, my torn stomach throbbed, and my brain felt entirely depleted. But as Jim carefully lifted the lap desk away and prepared to carry me down the hall, a deep, quiet sense of victory settled into my bones.

The first day of fifth grade was in the books. The tutor was safe. The system worked.
