The heavy wooden door swung open, and Dad stepped into the foyer. He was still wearing his Baja Taco manager uniform, carrying the faint scent of the kitchen fryers, but his posture was entirely different today. He wasn't slumped with the usual exhaustion of a daytime shift. His shoulders were pulled back, his jaw was set, and his eyes were burning with a quiet, intense victory.

In his right hand, he held a standard, white letter-sized envelope.

Archie scrambled out from under the coffee table, his tail thumping against the carpet, but Dad didn't reach down to pet him. He bypassed the kitchen entirely and walked straight to the edge of the living room carpet.

"Ellen," Dad called out, his voice a deep, resonant rumble that carried perfectly through the open archway to the dining room. "Come in here. Get Loretta, too."

Mom stepped out of the kitchen, wiping her hands on a dish towel. Grandma Loretta followed right behind her, pausing her knitting. At the dining table, Jessica and Janette immediately dropped their pencils and turned their chairs around, recognizing the absolute gravity in Dad's voice.

Dad stepped up to the foot of the sofa bed. He looked down at Rachel and me, trapped in our heavy white medical binders.

"Base camp," Dad said gently, waiting until he had our full, undivided attention. "Mr. Vance called me at the restaurant today. The civil injunction he filed against Ray completely froze his bank accounts. He couldn't pay his commercial lease for September, and he couldn't pay it for October."

Rachel’s hand tightened around mine. The adult financial logistics were complex, but she was tracking his tone perfectly.

"The property management company down in Chesapeake didn't wait," Dad continued, his voice hardening into steel. "They executed a complete commercial eviction this morning. And because of the lawsuit Mom started, the bank seized everything inside to pay for your medical bills."

Mom let out a sharp, breathless gasp. Her hands flew to her mouth, tears of pure, vindicated rage instantly springing to her eyes. The Monday War she had waged from the kitchen telephone—the relentless coalition she had built with Drew's mother and the other families—had finally delivered the killing blow.

Dad reached into the white envelope. He didn't pull out a dense legal document. He knew that for an eight-year-old and a ten-year-old, legal jargon meant nothing. They needed visual, undeniable proof.

He pulled out a glossy Polaroid photograph and leaned forward, holding it out over the foot of the bed so we didn't have to engage our cores to see it.

"Look," Dad commanded softly.
