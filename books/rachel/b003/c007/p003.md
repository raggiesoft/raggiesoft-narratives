On the mattress, Rachel shifted slightly.

The sensory input of the crowded room was high. Under normal circumstances, prior to the Monday War, this level of acoustic and visual static would have triggered an immediate, silent retreat upstairs. Rachel would have grabbed my wrist, pulled me into the pitch-black void of the bedroom closet, and climbed into my lap to deploy the crushing, regulating weight of deep pressure.

But that architectural hack was entirely offline. The torn fascia in our abdomens made any kind of deep pressure not just impossible, but medically catastrophic. The physical touch that our neurobiology desperately craved to regulate the noise was the exact thing that would send us to the emergency room. We missed the crushing hugs with a visceral, biological ache.

Rachel couldn't sit up. She couldn't climb into my lap. So, she used the only physical connection the medical parameters allowed.

She reached her small, pale hand across the massive pillow barrier dividing the sofa bed. I met her halfway, interlocking my fingers with hers. I squeezed as hard as my grip would allow, trying to project as much proprioceptive force as I could through just our hands. It wasn't the closet, and it wasn't the Lap Anchor, but it was a closed circuit.

Jessica saw the hand-hold. She leaned her arm against the edge of the sofa mattress, resting her head near our joined hands. Janette shifted closer on the other side. Ashley leaned in from her spot on the floor mattress.

They didn't try to hug us. They didn't accidentally bump the bed. The youth guard understood the physics of our survival perfectly. They simply collapsed the physical space around the sofa bed, creating a tight, impenetrable ring of bodies.

The adults stayed in the dining room. The acoustic bleed continued to drift over the threshold. But inside the living room, anchored by the Lead Guard on her fifteenth birthday, the pack remained flawlessly, perfectly unified.
