By the time Sunday morning arrived, marking the end of our first full week trapped in the living room, the Base Camp had settled into a fragile, highly calculated rhythm.

Sunday mornings in a retail and fast-food household, however, meant the schedules were completely upended.

The morning light filtering through the front windows was brighter and quieter than a weekday. On the sofa bed, Rachel and I were already propped up on our forty-five-degree medical wedges, finishing our paper cups of morning medication and small plates of scrambled eggs that Mom had carefully cut into tiny, easily manageable pieces.

At the foot of the bed, Dad was nursing a mug of black coffee, looking over the master schedule Mom had taped to the refrigerator door. Because it was the weekend, the adults were walking into the most grueling shifts of their week.

"Alright, base camp, let's go over the timeline for today," Dad said, his deep voice keeping the room grounded. "My schedule is flipped today. I'm working the closing shift at *Baja Taco*. I clock in at six-thirty tonight, and I won't be home until after three in the morning."

I felt a tight knot form in my chest. Dad was the ultimate anchor. He was the one who pulled the wedges and executed the final night-time lifts. The idea of him not being in the house when the lights went out was unsettling.

"The dining room closes at nine, and the drive-thru closes at two," Dad explained, reading the anxiety on my face perfectly. "Then I have an hour of store cleanup. So I will not be here for the bedtime teardown tonight. You are going to have to trust the rest of the pack."

"I've got the teardown, Tom," Mom assured him, walking out of the kitchen with a fresh cup of coffee. She wasn't wearing her *Wellington’s* uniform yet. "I work twelve-forty-five to nine-fifteen tonight. I'll be home by nine-forty, well before they need to go to sleep."

"Good," Dad nodded, taking a sip of his coffee.

"But we have a gap," Mom pointed out, looking at the clock. "I have to leave for the mall by twelve-fifteen."

Grandma Loretta stepped into the living room, dressed impeccably in her Sunday best, her purse over her shoulder. Like many families in our neighborhood, Grandma had Sunday morning obligations she attended every week.

"I'm heading out now, Ellen," Grandma Loretta said, adjusting her coat. "Our service ends at a quarter to one. I will be back right around one o'clock."

"Take your time, Mom. Tom has the midday gap," Mom smiled, walking her to the front door.

As the door clicked shut behind Grandma, Rachel reached her hand over the pillow wall and laced her fingers through mine. She gave my hand two quick squeezes, her eyes wide with anticipation. Today wasn't just Sunday. It was August 27th.

It was Ashley’s tenth birthday.

"I know, Bug," I smiled gently, keeping my voice low. "She's coming."

"The Raybourn cavalry is arriving right after one o'clock," Dad confirmed, looking down at us. "Jim, Joan, Ashley, and Melissa are all coming straight over after their own Sunday morning obligations. They are going to stay the entire afternoon and evening to cover the shifts while Mom and I are at work."

Knowing that the entire Raybourn family was coming completely shifted the energy in the room. Melissa, Ashley’s seventeen-year-old sister, was practically another older sibling to us. She was old enough to help Jessica and Janette run logistics. But more importantly, Joan was coming.

"Now, listen to me," Dad said, his tone shifting into his serious, protective register as he looked specifically at Rachel. "Uncle Jim and I will handle Michael's bathroom lifts today. But when Mom goes to work, Joan is going to handle yours."

Rachel’s breath hitched slightly, but she didn't panic. Joan Raybourn was a profoundly kind, gentle woman who had patched our scraped knees and baked us cookies for years. Rachel trusted her. But trusting someone emotionally and trusting them to physically lift a broken body were two different things.

"I am going to train Joan exactly the same way I trained Jim," Dad promised Rachel gently, leaning down so he was closer to her eye level. "I will teach her the rigid plank carry. I will not let her lift you until she proves to me that she can keep your core perfectly flat. Do you understand?"

Rachel stared at Dad for a moment, absorbing the promise. The iron-clad rule of the Base Camp was that we didn't do anything unless the adults proved it was safe first. Rachel gave Dad a tiny, resolute nod.

"Good," Dad rumbled, standing back up. He looked over at the floor mattresses, where Jessica and Janette were already awake, folding their blankets. "Jess, Janette, you're on guard duty for the next few hours. Let the dogs out, keep the TV low. I'm going to try to catch a few hours of sleep before my shift."

"We've got the perimeter, Dad," Jessica confirmed instantly.

Dad headed upstairs to get some necessary rest, and Mom retreated to the kitchen to start prepping the ingredients for Ashley's modified birthday lunch.

The house settled into a quiet, peaceful lull. The dogs, sensing the relaxed morning energy, stretched out completely flat on the carpet, taking up as much space as possible. Archie gnawed lazily on his neon-green tennis ball, while Baby rested his heavy chin directly on the edge of Jessica's mattress, keeping one dark eye fixed on the front door.

It was just the pack, holding the line, waiting for the birthday girl to arrive.
