Before the Raybourn family could fully gather their things to head out the front door, the physical toll of the long afternoon finally caught up with me.

We had been propped up at the rigid, forty-five-degree angle for hours. The medical wedges were designed to keep our cores from engaging, but gravity was relentless. The continuous pressure on my lower spine was radiating upward into a dull, burning ache, and the heavy medical binder felt like it was actively squeezing the breath out of my lungs.

I looked across the pillow wall. Rachel was shifting uncomfortably, her brow furrowed as she made tiny, calculated adjustments to her shoulders, desperately trying to find a position that didn't aggravate her cracked ribs.

"Uncle Jim?" I called out quietly, my voice raspy from exhaustion.

Jim stopped near the entryway, instantly turning back toward the living room. "What is it, buddy? Do you need the bathroom again?"

"No," I swallowed hard. "Can we take the wedges out before you leave? My back is really starting to burn."

Rachel immediately stopped shifting. She looked at Joan, who was standing next to Jim, and gave a firm, emphatic nod. She was entirely ready to lie flat.

"Of course we can," Joan answered warmly, setting her purse down on the entryway table and walking straight back to the left side of the sofa bed.

Because Dad was already at work, and Grandma Loretta couldn't safely bear our dead weight, this was the absolute safest window to execute the bedtime teardown. Uncle Jim stepped up to my side of the mattress, rolling his flannel sleeves back up to his elbows.

The teenagers instantly fell into formation. Jessica moved to the head of the bed on my side, and Melissa dropped her canvas bag to take the corresponding spot on Rachel's side.

"Alright, same protocol as always," Jim instructed, his deep voice keeping the room completely grounded. "We lift, the girls pull the foam, and we lower you down flat. Keep your stomachs completely limp."

I closed my eyes and took a shallow breath, letting my body go entirely slack.

"On three," Jim counted. "One, two, three."

Jim’s hands slid under my shoulders, and Joan mirrored the movement for Rachel. They lifted our upper bodies in a perfectly synchronized, gentle motion. The second our weight was off the foam, Jessica and Melissa swiftly slid the heavy, awkward medical wedges out from behind our backs, tossing them onto the carpet out of the way.

Jim and Joan slowly, carefully lowered us down until our spines rested entirely flat against the mattress.

The relief was instantaneous and profound. Without gravity forcing my weight down into my hips, the suffocating pressure of the binder eased. Beside me, Rachel let out a long, quiet sigh, her shoulders finally dropping their rigid tension as her spine aligned perfectly with the mattress.

"Better?" Jim asked softly, looking down at me.

"Much better," I breathed. "Thank you."

Joan leaned over and gently smoothed the quilt over Rachel's chest. "You rest now, sweetheart. You did so beautifully today."

Rachel didn't speak, but she reached out and gently squeezed Joan's wrist.

With the teardown safely completed, it was finally time for the Raybourns to retreat. They lived just one block over, so there was no need to pack into a car. They were just going to walk home through the muggy, quiet August night. Ashley walked over to the bed one last time, gently tapping the top of Rachel's hand, and then tapped mine.

"Happy birthday, Ash," I whispered.

"See you guys tomorrow," Ashley promised.

The heavy front door opened, letting in the sound of the Virginia crickets for a brief second before it clicked shut behind them. The deadbolt turned.

The living room was suddenly much emptier, but the perimeter remained secure. Grandma Loretta walked over to the armchair near the window, adjusting the cushion and sitting down with a soft sigh. She picked up her knitting needles, the rhythmic, metallic clicking returning as the house settled.

At the foot of the bed, the canine guard was officially clocking out. Archie was already curled in a tight, fuzzy circle under the coffee table, dead to the world. Baby let out a massive, rumbling groan, circling twice on the carpet next to Rachel’s side of the bed before collapsing heavily onto his side, his deep snores vibrating through the floorboards a moment later.

Jessica and Janette retreated to their floor mattresses, pulling their blankets up, exhausted from a full day of hyper-vigilant guard duty.

It was time for bed, but the house couldn't fully power down.

The small lamp on the end table remained brightly lit, casting a warm yellow glow across the popcorn ceiling. Mom wasn't scheduled to get off her shift at Wellington’s until nine-fifteen, which meant she wouldn't walk through the front door until at least nine-forty. The lights had to stay on to guide her home, and until the mother of the pack was safely inside the fortress, true sleep was impossible.

I turned my head to the left, looking over the three-pillow barrier. Rachel’s eyes were heavy, fighting a losing battle against the exhaustion, but she was still awake. She reached her hand up, resting it on the cotton wall. I slid my fingers through hers, anchoring us together.

We lay completely flat against the mattress, listening to the clicking of Grandma's knitting needles and the heavy snores of the dogs, waiting in the quiet light for Mom to finally come home.
