The afternoon stretched out before us, vast and empty.

Grandma Loretta brought out lunch—simple turkey sandwiches cut into tiny, bite-sized squares so we wouldn't have to engage our cores to tear off bites, and small cups of ginger ale. We ate quietly, the exhaustion of the noon lift still hanging over us.

Once the trays were cleared, the reality of the four-month marathon truly set in. We couldn't play video games because we couldn't sit up to hold the controllers properly. We couldn't read books because holding our arms up for that long tired out our chest muscles.

Ashley, realizing the physical limitations of the room, dug into her canvas backpack.

"I brought something," Ashley announced, pulling out a brightly colored, oversized notepad. "*Silly Fill-Ins*."

It was the absolute perfect solution. It required zero physical movement from Rachel or me, but it allowed the entire pack to participate.

Ashley crawled onto Jessica's floor mattress and clicked her pen. "Okay, the first story is called 'A Day at the Zoo.' I need an adjective."

"Smelly," Janette offered immediately.

"Noun," Ashley demanded, scribbling it down.

I looked at the ceiling, thinking. "Refrigerator," I croaked.

Ashley giggled. "Okay, Rachel. I need a verb ending in 'ing'."

Ashley looked over at Rachel, waiting. Rachel looked at Grandma Loretta, who was sitting in the armchair near the window, quietly knitting. The situational mutism held firm. Rachel wouldn't speak with an adult in the room.

But Ashley didn't pressure her. She just crawled closer to the edge of the mattress and leaned her ear right next to the bed.

Rachel reached her left hand out, tugged on Ashley's sleeve, and brought her mouth right next to her best friend's ear. She whispered the word so quietly that even I couldn't hear it.

Ashley sat up, a huge grin spreading across her face. She wrote the word down with a flourish. "Exploding. Good one."

For the next two hours, the living room was filled with quiet, careful laughter. We built ridiculous stories about exploding refrigerators, giant purple dogs, and running away from angry toaster ovens. Every time we laughed, I had to hold my breath to keep my stomach from shaking, and Rachel would press her hand against her ribs to brace herself, but it was worth it.

It was a brilliant, necessary distraction. Ashley acted as our hands, Jessica and Janette acted as our guards, and Uncle Jim and Grandma Loretta held the physical perimeter.

When three o'clock rolled around, Jim came back in for the second bathroom lift. This time, Rachel didn't flinch quite as hard. The terror was still there, but the trust had been cemented. Jim executed the lifts with the exact same flawless precision as before.

As the late afternoon sun began to dip behind the houses on Delaney Street, turning the living room golden, the heavy dread of the morning had completely dissipated. The adults had gone to work, the routine had been established, and we hadn't been broken.

The marathon was going to be agonizingly long, but as I listened to Ashley read another ridiculous *Silly Fill-Ins* story while Rachel squeezed my hand over the pillows, I knew we were going to survive it.
