At exactly seven o'clock, the heavy front door swung open. Dad walked in, still wearing his *Baja Taco* manager uniform, carrying the faint, unmistakable scent of corn shells and fryer oil. His shoulders were slumped with the exhaustion of an eight-and-a-half-hour shift, but the moment his eyes scanned the living room and found the perimeter intact, a wave of profound relief washed over his face.

Twenty minutes later, Mom walked through the door. She looked equally drained, the chaotic retail energy of *Wellington’s* shoe department still lingering in her posture.

The adults had survived their first day back in the outside world, and the base camp had held the line at home.

Uncle Jim stood up from the armchair, quietly giving Dad the rundown of the midday and afternoon bathroom lifts. "They did great, Tom. Kept their cores perfectly flat. No scares, no drops."

"Thank you, Jim," Dad rumbled, shaking his friend's hand firmly. "I owe you."

"You don't owe me a thing," Jim insisted softly.

Ashley grabbed her canvas backpack and slung it over her shoulder. It was time for the Raybourns to go home. She walked over to the gap between the floor mattresses, kneeling down so she was at eye level with the edge of the sofa bed.

"I have to go," Ashley whispered. She looked back and forth between Rachel and me. "But my tenth birthday is on Sunday. And since you guys can't come to my house for the party, my dad said I can bring my birthday over here. I'm going to spend the whole day in base camp with you."

Rachel’s eyes lit up. A birthday meant cake, and it meant a full day of safe, predictable distraction with an honorary pack member. Rachel reached her hand out from under the quilt and gave Ashley a firm, double-squeeze.

"We'll be right here," I promised, a genuine smile breaking through my exhaustion.

Ashley stood up, hugged Jessica and Janette fiercely, and followed her dad out the front door. The lock clicked shut behind them.

"Alright, crew," Mom sighed, dropping her purse on the entryway table and rolling up her sleeves. "Time for the evening wipedown."

The reality of being trapped in rigid medical binders meant taking a real shower was completely out of the question for the next four months. We had to rely on warm washcloths and basins of soapy water.

"Jess, Janette, dining room," Dad ordered gently.

The older girls didn't complain. They immediately stood up, abandoned their guard posts on the floor, and retreated down the hallway to the dining area, sealing themselves out of sight. Maintaining our dignity was just as important as maintaining our safety.

Mom brought out two plastic basins of warm water and fresh washcloths. Dad took his place on my side of the bed, and Mom took hers on Rachel's side.

"Okay, guys, privacy protocol," Mom instructed softly.

I immediately turned my head as far to the left as I could, locking my eyes onto the brickwork of the front-wall fireplace. On the other side of the pillow barrier, Rachel turned her head sharply to the right, staring toward the hallway. By completely averting our eyes, we created an invisible wall of privacy in the middle of a shared bed.

Dad worked quickly and carefully, washing my arms, chest, and legs, meticulously avoiding the thick medical binder wrapped around my torn fascia. Mom did the same for Rachel, using incredibly gentle strokes around her cracked ribs and swollen spleen. The warm water felt amazing against our skin, washing away the stagnant, medicinal feeling of lying in bed all day.

"All done," Mom announced, pulling the fresh quilts back up to our chests. "You can look back."

We turned our heads back toward the ceiling.

"Clear!" Dad called out toward the hallway.

Jessica and Janette immediately marched back into the living room, seamlessly transitioning the base camp from the bath to the dental hygiene protocol. Jessica was carrying the two plastic basins, while Janette held the ultra-soft blue and red toothbrushes Dr. Evans had brought us, already prepped with toothpaste.

We executed the routine exactly as we had on Monday night. Mom held the basins under our chins while we remained propped against the foam wedges, using the surgical-soft bristles to gently brush our teeth without sending any vibrations down into our healing cores. We dribbled the water out into the basins, totally exhausted but completely clean.

Dad stepped up to the edge of the bed for the final, crucial maneuver of the night. It was time to pull the wedges.

"On three," Dad instructed, positioning his massive hands under my shoulders. Mom matched his position on Rachel’s side. "Jess, Janette, get the foam."

"One, two, three."

They lifted our upper bodies just a few inches. Jessica and Janette smoothly slid the heavy wedges out from behind us. Mom and Dad slowly lowered us until our spines were lying perfectly flat against the mattress.

The immediate relief on my lower back was immense. Mom moved in, fluffing the massive, three-pillow barrier that ran directly down the center of the bed, re-establishing the cotton wall that kept us separated.

I turned my head to the left, looking over the top of the middle pillow. Rachel’s dark eyes were already looking back at me.

I reached my hand up, resting it on the top of the pillow barrier. A second later, Rachel’s fingers slid tightly through mine, locking our grip together.

"I'm right here, Bug," I whispered, the exhaustion heavy in my voice.

Rachel gave my fingers two tight, rhythmic squeezes. *I know, Bear.* Jessica clicked off the small living room lamp, plunging the room into shadows. She and Janette lay down on their floor mattresses. Baby let out a deep, rumbling sigh and collapsed onto the carpet next to Rachel’s side of the bed, while Archie crawled under the coffee table and shut his good eye.

Grandma Loretta stepped into the hallway, giving Mom and Dad a quiet goodnight before heading upstairs to sleep in my tiny, 10x10 bedroom.

Mom and Dad stood in the archway for a long moment, looking over the darkened triage center. The adults had gone to work, the Raybourns had held the line, and the perimeter was completely intact. The first full day of the marathon was officially in the books.

"Goodnight, base camp," Dad rumbled softly, his voice a protective anchor in the dark.

"Sleep safe," Mom whispered.

They turned and walked up the wooden stairs. I lay flat on my back, listening to the quiet, overlapping breathing of the pack. The dull ache in my core was still there, and the terrifying reality of the next four months still loomed ahead, but as Rachel's pulse beat steadily against my palm, I closed my eyes. We had survived Tuesday.
