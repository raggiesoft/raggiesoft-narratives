As the late afternoon shadows stretched longer across the living room carpet, the quiet, mundane rhythms of a normal household began to assert themselves against the trauma.

"Alright, base camp needs clean uniforms," Jessica announced, standing up and stretching her arms over her head. "Janette, help me grab the laundry baskets."

The architecture of the first floor meant that the girls didn't have to go far. Directly behind the living room sofa was a central hallway that ran in a straight, functional line from the front entryway all the way back to the dining area. It was the main artery of the house. Along that corridor sat the downstairs half-bath, the heavy fire door leading out to the garage, and a set of slatted bifold doors that hid the washer and dryer.

"I'll get the darks, you get the lights," Janette said, following Jessica into the hall.

A moment later, the rhythmic, sloshing hum of the washing machine vibrating through the drywall became the new background soundtrack of the afternoon.

With the older teenagers occupied, the perimeter was technically down two guards. Ashley noticed the shift immediately. She closed her *Silly Fill-Ins* notepad, set it on the coffee table, and adjusted her posture. She climbed off Jessica's mattress and moved directly to the center of the gap, sitting cross-legged on the floor right in front of the television.

Ashley puffed her chest out slightly, her blue eyes scanning the room with absolute, ten-year-old solemnity. She wasn't just hanging out anymore; she had been temporarily promoted to Lead Guard, and she took the assignment incredibly seriously.

Rachel watched Ashley's dramatic, vigilant posture and a tiny, genuine smile tugged at the corner of her mouth. She reached her left hand out from under the quilts and gave Ashley a quick thumbs-up.

Ashley beamed, returning the thumbs-up with militant precision.

In the kitchen, Grandma Loretta and Uncle Jim were already moving on to the next phase of the daily schedule. At exactly five o'clock, Jim walked into the living room, wiping his hands on a dish towel.

"Dinner time, crew," Jim announced gently.

Jessica and Janette emerged from the laundry hallway, the dryer now humming steadily alongside the washer, and quickly helped Jim adjust our upper bodies. They slid the firm medical wedges directly behind our backs, propping Rachel and me up at the mandatory forty-five-degree angle.

Grandma Loretta followed right behind with the food. Because chewing heavily or leaning forward was still completely impossible, dinner was a rotation of soft, easily swallowed comfort foods. Tonight was a massive, steaming pot of macaroni and cheese, cooked until the noodles were practically melting, served in shallow bowls so we could scoop it up without bending our necks.

We ate quietly on our trays while the rest of the pack ate sitting cross-legged on the floor mattresses. The presence of the adults kept Rachel completely mute, but the atmosphere wasn't tense. It just felt like a family dinner that had been temporarily relocated to the floor.

When the bowls were scraped clean, Janette and Jessica collected the trays and carried them into the kitchen.

A few minutes later, the heavy, mechanical whir and splashing of the dishwasher kicked on. It was a deliberate, calculated move by Grandma Loretta. Running the heavy appliance now meant it would finish its loud, rumbling cycle well before eight o'clock. When it was finally time for the house to go to sleep, there wouldn't be any sudden, unexpected clanking or water rushing through the pipes to trigger our hyper-vigilance.

The house was clean. The dishes were washing. The perimeter was secure.

I leaned back against my wedge, the dull ache in my core settling into a familiar throb, and listened to the overlapping hums of the dryer and the dishwasher. They were just normal, boring household sounds, but right now, they were the greatest sounds in the world. They meant that the outside world was locked away, and our fortress was holding strong.
