Tuesday morning felt entirely different than Monday.

Monday had been fueled by the pure, adrenaline-laced vengeance of the courtroom and the war room. But as the sun rose on Tuesday, casting long shadows across the living room carpet, the adrenaline was gone. In its place was the cold, heavy reality of the long haul.

The adults had to go back to work.

Mom walked into the living room dressed in her professional slacks and a sensible blouse, her name tag for *Wellington’s* Department Store already clipped to her lapel. She worked the morning shift in the shoe department over at Mariner’s Circle Mall in Norfolk—back when Mariner’s Circle was still the absolute pinnacle of Hampton Roads retail.

A moment later, Dad came down the stairs, adjusting the collar of his uniform. He was the assistant manager at *Baja Taco* over on Independence Boulevard.

They were both scheduled to clock in at 10:00 and 10:30 AM, respectively. But the harsh realities of retail and fast-food management meant they only got thirty-minute unpaid lunch breaks.

"Alright, base camp, listen up," Dad said, standing at the foot of the sofa bed. We were already propped up on our forty-five-degree wedges. "We have a logistical change for today."

"Grandma is staying with you," Mom explained, setting our morning paper cups of medication on our breakfast trays. "But Grandma cannot lift your cores. And because of the midday traffic on Independence and Virginia Beach Boulevard, your father and I cannot make it from our stores to Delaney Street, do the bathroom protocol, and get back to work in thirty minutes."

I felt a sudden spike of panic. If Mom and Dad couldn't come home, and Grandma couldn't lift us, we were trapped.

"Don't worry," Dad rumbled, catching the fear in my eyes instantly. "We've got backup. The Raybourns are stepping in."

The tension in my chest immediately evaporated.

Jim Raybourn wasn't just a neighbor; he was family. When Mom and Dad had first moved our family down from Newport, Rhode Island to Virginia Beach in 1987, Jim had been the very first person to welcome us to the neighborhood. He had helped Dad carry the couch into this exact living room. And his daughter, Ashley, who was turning ten in just five days, was our absolute best friend. She was an honorary sibling, a permanent fixture at our birthday parties, and the only person outside of the house who truly understood how Rachel and I operated.

Right on cue, the doorbell rang.

Dad opened the front door, and Archie’s tail immediately started thumping against the floorboards. Archie and Baby didn't bark; they knew the Raybourns belonged in the house.

Jim walked in first, holding a white paper bag that smelled distinctly of warm powdered sugar and glazed donuts. Ashley was right behind him.

Normally, when Ashley walked into our house, it was a kinetic event. She was a tornado of energy, and her standard greeting was to sprint across the carpet, throw herself onto the sofa, and tackle Rachel and me into a fierce, breathless double-hug.

But as Ashley stepped into the living room and saw the medical base camp for the first time, she froze.

She saw the rigid foam wedges holding us up. She saw the massive, white medical binders wrapped tightly around our waists. She saw the heavy, three-pillow wall running right down the middle of the bed, completely dividing my half from Rachel's.

Ashley’s eyes welled with tears. She understood the physics of the room immediately. A flying hug would send us to the emergency room.

She didn't run. She walked slowly toward the edge of the mattress, bypassing the dogs. Jessica and Janette stood up from their floor mattresses, and Ashley immediately threw her arms around Jessica's waist, burying her face in my older sister's shirt. Jessica hugged her back fiercely, rubbing her shoulders.

Ashley pulled away, wiping her eyes, and stepped right up to the left side of the bed. She looked at Rachel. She reached out with both hands and gently wrapped them around Rachel’s pale fingers, squeezing hard. It was a silent, desperate transfer of love.

Rachel squeezed back, her dark eyes shining, but she kept her jaw locked tight.

Because of the massive pillow wall, Ashley couldn't reach me from where she stood. She let go of Rachel, carefully stepped over the corner of Jessica's floor mattress, and walked all the way around the foot of the bed to my side. She reached out and squeezed my hand next, her chin trembling.

"Hey, guys," Jim said softly, setting the donuts on the coffee table. He looked at Dad. "I'm off work today and tomorrow. I'm taking the noon lift and the three o'clock lift. You guys go make your money. I've got the perimeter."

"I need to show you the mechanics, Jim," Dad said, his voice dropping into a dead-serious register. He gestured for Jim to step up to my side of the bed.

Dad wasn't just handing off a chore; he was transferring the physical safety of his critically injured children.

"They have zero core stability," Dad explained, pointing to the thick medical binder wrapped around my ribs. "If they bend at the waist, their fascia tears. If Rachel twists, her spleen ruptures. You cannot pick them up under the armpits."

Jim nodded, his expression completely somber, absorbing every word.

"You have to execute a rigid plank carry," Dad instructed. He reached down and demonstrated the exact hand placements, sliding one massive arm under my knees and the other directly under my shoulder blades. "You lock their spine against your chest. You do all the bending. They stay perfectly flat."

Dad looked Jim dead in the eyes, the protective weight of a father pushing through his exhaustion. "I wouldn't trust another man on this earth to carry my daughter to the bathroom right now, Jim. But you're family."

"I've got them, Tom," Jim promised quietly, clapping a hand on Dad's shoulder. "They stay flat. I won't let them fall."

"Thank you," Mom whispered, pulling Jim into a quick hug.

Breakfast was fast. Dad and Mom ate their donuts standing up in the kitchen, double-checking the emergency contact lists pinned to the refrigerator. At nine-fifteen, it was time for them to go.

"Be good for Grandma," Mom said, kissing our foreheads.

"Trust Uncle Jim with your weight," Dad added, resting his hand on my shoulder. "He knows what to do."

With a final wave, the front door clicked shut. The adults were officially gone to the outside world.

In the kitchen, Grandma Loretta pulled out a chair at the table. "Alright, Jim. Let me show you the medication log and the schedule."

"Lead the way, Loretta. I'll get the coffee going," Jim replied.

The heavy wooden door to the kitchen swung shut, clicking into its frame.

The living room was suddenly completely devoid of adults. It was just the dogs sleeping on the floor, Jessica and Janette sitting cross-legged on their mattresses, and Ashley standing by the edge of the bed.

The silence stretched for a few seconds. Then, on the left side of the pillow wall, Rachel took a slow, shuddering breath.

"Ashley," Rachel whispered.

Ashley gasped softly. It was the first time Rachel had used her voice since Master Curtis had crushed her ribs. The sound was tiny, raspy, and incredibly fragile.

"You're talking," Ashley breathed, her eyes going wide.

"Only to the pack," Rachel rasped, her grip on the edge of her blanket tightening. "And you're pack."

I felt a massive wave of relief wash over me. Rachel’s situational mutism wasn't broken, but she had managed to draw a boundary. Adults were terrifying. Adults had the power to hurt us. But Ashley was safe.

"I'm so sorry I can't hug you guys," Ashley whispered, her voice trembling as she looked back and forth between us across the pillow wall. "Does it hurt really bad?"

"Yes," I answered for her, keeping my voice just as low. "The doctors say we can't twist or bend for four months."

Rachel swallowed hard, a fresh tear escaping the corner of her eye. She looked at her oldest friend, dropping the heavy, iron-clad emotional shield she wore whenever the adults were in the room.

"I'm scared, Ash," Rachel confessed, her voice breaking on the words. "I'm so scared. Every time a heavy door closes, or someone walks too loud on the floor... I think he's coming back to step on me again."

Ashley didn't offer a hollow platitude. She didn't tell Rachel that everything was going to be fine. She was almost ten years old; she was old enough to know that monsters were real, because one of them had nearly killed her best friends.

Instead, Ashley stepped right up to the edge of Jessica's floor mattress, next to where Baby the black lab was resting his massive head. She carefully leaned forward and rested her own head gently on the edge of the quilts, just inches from Rachel’s shoulder, mimicking the dog's protective posture.

"He's not coming back," Ashley promised fiercely. "Jessica and Janette are here. The dogs are here. My dad is right in the kitchen. And I'm not going home. I'm staying right here on the floor with you."

"Promise?" Rachel whispered.

"I promise," Ashley said.

I looked over at Jessica and Janette. They both gave small, approving nods. The Base Camp had just drafted its newest recruit. The adults were gone, the house was quiet, and the marathon had officially begun. But as long as we kept adding safe people to the perimeter, I knew we could hold the line.
