"Alright, class," April announced, transitioning seamlessly into her instructional cadence. "We are going to study the Shadow Arts today. It is a specialized form of training designed entirely around biomechanical isolation."

She set her heavy book down on the coffee table and stepped to the foot of the sofa bed.

"Your cores are healing. Your legs are healing," April observed, gesturing to the medical wedges propping us up at forty-five-degree angles. "So we completely remove them from the equation. *Seondu* does not require you to stand. It requires your mind to connect perfectly with your body. We are going to execute Basic Form IV and Basic Form V using only your arms."

I looked at her, my heart racing with a mixture of excitement and hesitation. I hadn't thrown a punch since the day my fascia tore. The fear of triggering a spasm was a constant, heavy weight in my chest.

April saw the panic flashing in my eyes. She didn't bark at me to push through the pain, and she didn't demand perfection.

"I am not asking you to break yourselves," April said, her voice dropping into a quiet, profoundly reassuring register. "In this Dojang, effort is the only metric that matters. I just want you to try. If it hurts, you stop. But you have to trust your minds enough to try."

I turned my head to the left, looking over the three-pillow barrier. Rachel was staring back at me. Her dark eyes were wide, calculating the terrifying variables of moving her injured body. But she looked at the silver embroidery on April's belt, and then she looked at Jessica and Janette standing perfectly still on their floor mattresses, holding the perimeter.

The room was mathematically secure. Rachel gave me a single, determined nod. We were going to try.

"Do not engage your stomach," April instructed, reading our silent agreement instantly. "Let the medical wedges hold your weight. I want you to focus entirely on the kinetic snap of your wrists, the rotation of your forearms, and the mental visualization of your stances."

She demonstrated the first sequence of Basic Form IV from a rigid, stationary stance.

"Hana," April counted softly in Korean.

I took a shallow breath, isolating my upper body, and threw my left arm across my chest, executing an *Olgul Makgi*—a high block. The movement was slow and cautious, but as my forearm snapped into place, my torn stomach muscles remained completely quiet. The wedge absorbed the torque.

Beside me, Rachel mirrored the movement perfectly. Her dark eyes were locked onto April, completely mesmerized by the reality that she was actually doing martial arts again.

"Dul," April counted.

We both executed a *Momtong Jireugi*—a middle punch. My fist cut through the air above my lap. It didn't have the explosive, full-body power of a standing strike, but the mechanical alignment of my wrist and knuckles was flawless.

For the next twenty minutes, April guided us through the precise, upper-body choreography of the forms. We blocked, we struck, and we parried invisible opponents from the safety of our sofa bed. The heavy, suffocating identity of being "hospital patients" began to burn away with every strike. We were practitioners again.

As we transitioned into the more complex, motor-chaining movements of Basic Form V, April paused. She lowered her own arms, observing our seated postures.

"Visualization is the key here," April explained, stepping slightly to the left. "Since you cannot pivot your hips, you must picture the invisible center line. When we transition from the *Arae Makgi* into the outward strike, where does your secondary hand chamber?"

She wasn't demonstrating it. She was testing our mental map of the curriculum.

I opened my mouth to answer, but before I could engage my vocal cords, a tiny, raspy friction broke the silence of the living room.

"On the hip," a voice whispered.

I froze. Jessica and Janette went entirely still on their floor mattresses.

It was Rachel.

For the first time since the devastating Thursday in August when Master Curtis had crushed her ribs and weaponized her voice against her, Rachel had spoken. The words were incredibly soft, her vocal cords raw and unused, but the answer was clear. Surrounded only by her brother, her two teenage sentinels, and the safest teacher she had ever known, the situational mutism had finally thawed.

My heart hammered in my chest. I wanted to cheer. I wanted to turn and hug her.

But April was an absolute master of the zero-reaction protocol.

She didn't gasp. She didn't praise Rachel for speaking. She didn't drop to her knees and make a massive, overwhelming emotional spectacle out of the milestone. She knew that drawing attention to the voice would immediately terrify Rachel and slam the vault door shut again.

"Exactly," April replied smoothly, her tone perfectly neutral and focused entirely on the *Art*. "The secondary hand chambers tightly on the hip to create the kinetic tension for the next strike. Show me."

Rachel let out a tiny, invisible exhale of profound relief. She wasn't being punished for speaking, and she wasn't being suffocated with praise. She just chambered her right fist perfectly against her hip.

"Set," April counted.

We snapped our arms forward. The flow of the lesson continued, the heavy, impossible barrier of Rachel's silence quietly broken in the safety of the Shadow Arts.

Then, the heavy wooden door to the kitchen squeaked open.

Uncle Jim stepped into the archway, holding two small plastic cups of water. "Just checking on the Base Camp. Everyone doing okay in here?"

The shift in the room's atmospheric pressure was instantaneous and catastrophic.

Rachel’s entire body locked rigid against her medical wedge. Her jaw clamped completely shut. Her hands fell limply to her lap, and her dark eyes dilated with a sudden, overwhelming spike of fear. An adult male had just breached the perimeter. The phantom memory of Master Curtis looming over her hijacked her processor, and the situational mutism slammed back into place like a one-ton steel door.

April sensed the immediate, violent drop in Rachel's baseline. She didn't miss a single beat.

She turned on her heel, placing her body squarely between the sofa bed and the kitchen archway, effectively shielding Rachel from Uncle Jim's line of sight.

"We are perfectly fine, Mr. Raybourn," April said, her voice polite but carrying the unmistakable, iron-clad authority of a *Sabomnim* controlling her floor. "We are in the middle of a highly focused sequence. We will take a hydration break in exactly ten minutes."

Uncle Jim blinked, slightly surprised by the formal dismissal, but he was a man who deeply respected boundaries. He looked at April's solid midnight-blue uniform, gave a respectful nod, and immediately stepped backward.

"Understood," Jim said. "Ten minutes."

The kitchen door clicked firmly shut.

April waited two full seconds before turning back to face the sofa bed. She didn't ask Rachel if she was okay, and she didn't apologize for the interruption. She simply looked at our raised fists and re-engaged the curriculum as if the outside world didn't exist.

"Let's reset the chamber," April instructed calmly, her eyes locking onto Rachel's. "Mind over body. We control the perimeter."

Rachel took a slow, jagged sip of air. Her terrified posture slowly melted back into the mattress. The adult was gone, the threat was neutralized, and April had effortlessly defended the Dojang. Rachel raised her fists back into position, her dark eyes shining with an absolute, unwavering trust in the 22-year-old standing at the foot of the bed.
