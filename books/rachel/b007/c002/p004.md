The elevator bank at the end of the hall looked like it hadn't been updated since 1968. The heavy, faux-wood paneled doors slid open with a rattling metal screech, revealing a cramped cab that smelled faintly of old machine oil and stale cigarette smoke masked by heavy floral perfume.

It was a tight fit. Mom backed Jessica’s manual chair in first, then Rachel and I squeezed in around the brass luggage cart. The elevator groaned in protest as it hauled our cross-country payload up to the fourth floor.

We found Room 412 halfway down the long, quiet corridor. Mom swiped the plastic keycard, the lock clicking green, and pushed the heavy fire door open.

The room was standard, clean, and dominated by two large queen beds.

The sleeping arrangements didn't even require a discussion; it was an automatic, wordless sorting. Rachel immediately dropped her backpack, walked past the first bed, and climbed onto the mattress closest to the large window overlooking the U-District. I followed her, sitting on the edge of the mattress and taking my shoes off.

We had spent the last four months crammed together on the Delaney Street sofa bed. Sharing a mattress wasn't just comfortable; it was a fundamental requirement for our operational security. Jessica wheeled over, locking her brakes and smoothly transferring onto the edge of our bed. The kids had claimed the window. The pack was anchored together.

Mom took the bed closest to the door, placing herself directly between her children and the hallway. She was the vanguard of the perimeter.

"Alright," Mom sighed, kicking her shoes off and falling back against her pillows. "It's only three-thirty in the afternoon here, but our bodies think it's pushing seven o'clock at night. Everyone is running on fumes. Get some sleep. We'll figure out food when we wake up."

The heavy blackout curtains were drawn. Within minutes, the crushing, adrenaline-depleted exhaustion of the trip pulled me under. Rachel was already fast asleep, her hand loosely gripping the hem of my shirt.

The room was silent for hours.

When my eyes finally fluttered open, the thin sliver of light bleeding through the gap in the curtains had shifted from bright afternoon gold to the bruised, hazy purple of early evening. My internal clock was completely scrambled. My head pounded with the heavy, disorienting fog of time zone lag.

I sat up slowly, careful not to wake Rachel.

Across the room, the small brass lamp on the desk was turned on, casting a warm pool of light over the laminated wood. Jessica was sitting in her wheelchair, leaning over the desk. She hadn't slept.

Spread out in front of her was the thick, glossy welcome packet from Northwest Pacific University, sitting right next to the massive, heavy-stock orientation folder from the Quantum Corporation.

I watched her quietly. She was tracing her finger down a printed ledger sheet.

She wasn't just going to school. Quantum wasn't just paying for her entire NPU tuition, her dorm, and her premium meal plan; they were actively putting her on the corporate payroll. She was a nineteen-year-old girl who couldn't put a single ounce of weight on her feet without collapsing, but she was about to start earning a highly competitive tech salary while simultaneously earning her degree.

It was a staggering, monumental level of independence. I thought about Dad, hiding behind his Baja Taco inventory sheet at four-thirty in the morning, complaining about the cost of a long-distance phone call. And then I looked at my sister, sitting in the glow of the desk lamp, holding the keys to a multi-billion dollar tech empire in her hands.

Jessica looked up, sensing I was awake. The stoic armor was down. Her eyes were bright, wide, and entirely alive.

"I move in tomorrow," she whispered across the quiet room.

"I know," I whispered back.

Jessica set the packet down and rubbed her stomach. "My body thinks it's almost nine o'clock at night. I'm starving."

She spun her wheels around and rolled over to the beds. She reached out, gently shaking Rachel’s shoulder. "Hey, Rachel. Wake up."

Rachel stirred, letting out a small, confused hum before blinking her eyes open against the dim light. Jessica then wheeled over to Mom’s bed, tapping the mattress.

Mom woke up instantly, her eyes snapping to the door before locking onto Jessica. "What time is it?"

"A little past six here," Jessica said. "But we're all starving. We need dinner."

Mom sat up, rubbing her temples to clear the time-zone fog. "Okay. Okay, let's go see what's around."

Twenty minutes later, we were back on the cool, darkening Seattle sidewalks. We didn't have the energy to navigate the transit grid again, so we just walked down the block.

Nestled between a row of old brick storefronts and a university bookstore was a brightly lit, standalone fast-food restaurant. We had never seen or heard of the place before. A massive, glowing plastic sign shaped like a grinning clown head spun slowly on a tall pole above the parking lot.

The sign read: **JESTER'S**.

We pushed through the glass doors. The interior was a sea of bright purple and teal geometric booths. But it was the glowing menu board above the registers that caused all four of us to stop and stare in complete confusion.

Back on the East Coast, fast food was strictly segmented. Burger places sold burgers. Baja Taco sold tacos. But the glowing board at Jester's offered an absurd, chaotic mix of everything. There were double bacon cheeseburgers pictured right next to deep-fried tacos, teriyaki chicken bowls, and egg rolls.

"What kind of restaurant is this?" Mom murmured, entirely bewildered by the West Coast culinary anarchy.

"I don't know," Jessica said, a small, genuine smile breaking across her face as she looked up at the menu. "But I want a cheeseburger and two tacos."
