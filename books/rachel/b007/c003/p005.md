By eight o'clock, the golden hour had faded into a cool, dusky Pacific Northwest twilight. We walked the few blocks back to the engineering quad, our shadows stretching long across the concrete, and pushed into the quiet, brightly lit corridor of the upperclassman dorm.

Jessica unlocked the heavy wooden door of Room 114.

The apartment was exactly as we had engineered it. We walked past the kitchen alcove, where the green digital clock on the new microwave glowed in the dim light, and stepped into the main sleeping area.

The heavy beige CRT monitor sat dormant on the custom, accessible desk, completely hardwired into the university’s high-speed network. Just a few feet away, perfectly flush with the turning radius of her manual chair, was her lowered, full-sized bed.

We stepped inside, but Mom didn't take her light jacket off. She stood in the center of the hardwood floor, her hands buried deep in her pockets.

The reality of the logistics suddenly hit the room like a physical shockwave. Mom, Rachel, and I still had Room 412 secured at the hotel. We weren't sleeping here. The extraction was over.

Rachel realized it first. She froze near the edge of Jessica's full-sized mattress, her dark eyes darting between Mom and Jessica. Her breathing hitched, coming in short, shallow gasps.

Ever since Jessica came home from the hospital in late November, the three of us hadn't spent a single night apart. We had pulled the heavy floral mattress out of the sofa and built a permanent Base Camp in the middle of the Delaney Street living room. We slept in a pile—a tangled, hyper-vigilant knot of siblings relying on each other's physical proximity to keep the dark, chaotic energy of our father out.

Tonight, that perimeter was officially being dismantled.

The sudden, terrifying reality of the separation slammed into Rachel's system. Her vocal cords locked entirely, the words trapping themselves behind a heavy, impenetrable wall of panic in her throat. She couldn't speak. She reached out blindly, grabbing the hem of my shirt in a white-knuckled fist, pulling herself into my shadow to hide from the massive shift in gravity.

Mom walked over to the ADA-compliant kitchen counter.

"We will meet you in the hotel lobby at exactly nine o'clock tomorrow morning," Mom stated, establishing the hard, non-negotiable operational timeline. Because the dorm's secure exterior and hallway doors required resident access, we couldn't simply come back and knock on her door. "We will be waiting right by the front sliding doors."

Mom reached into her purse, pulled out one of the plastic magnetic keycards for Room 412, and set it down firmly on the laminate surface.

"Listen to me, Jess," Mom continued, her voice perfectly steady, though her eyes betrayed the terrifying, visceral fear of a mother leaving her permanently mobility-impaired nineteen-year-old daughter alone on the other side of the continent. "I am leaving this key right here. If the silence in this room gets too heavy tonight... if you miss the noise, or if you just need us, you do not hesitate. You take the local bus straight to the hotel, and you swipe into our room. Do you understand?"

Jessica looked at the plastic keycard on the counter. She knew exactly what Mom was offering. It was a safety net. It was permission to retreat to the pack if the isolation was too much to bear.

Jessica looked back up at Mom. The stoic, unyielding armor of the vanguard locked perfectly into place.

"I won't need it," Jessica stated, her voice ringing with absolute, undeniable certainty. "But thank you."

Mom nodded slowly. She stepped forward, wrapping her arms around Jessica’s shoulders in a fierce, breathless hug. "You are the strongest person I know. I am so proud of you."

Mom stepped back, clearing her throat to hide the emotion, and moved toward the door.

Rachel scrambled forward next. She didn't offer a gentle hug. She threw her arms around Jessica’s neck, burying her face into her older sister’s shoulder, holding on with a desperate, trembling grip as if the floor were actively falling away beneath them. Jessica dropped her hands from her push-rims, wrapping both arms around Rachel's back and holding her tight.

"I've got you, Rachel," Jessica whispered fiercely into her hair, providing the heavy physical anchor her sister desperately needed. "I'm not going anywhere. I will be in the hotel lobby at nine o'clock sharp."

It took a long time for the trembling to stop. When Rachel finally let go, she wiped her eyes with the back of her sleeve and stepped back into my shadow.

I was the last one.

I didn't say a word. I stepped forward, wrapped my arms around my older sister, and applied a massive, crushing wave of deep-pressure grounding. I squeezed tightly, letting her feel the absolute, heavy physical weight of her bear one last time before the isolation set in.

"Nine o'clock," I mumbled quietly into her shoulder, forcing the words through the tight knot in my own chest. "We're going to be tourists."

"Go get some sleep, Michael," Jessica whispered back, squeezing my arms. "I'll see you at the hotel. I've got the perimeter."

I pulled away, grabbed Rachel’s hand, and followed Mom out into the brightly lit hallway.

We didn't look back. Mom knew that if she turned around, her resolve would fracture. We walked down the corridor, through the locked interior doors, and out the heavy sliding glass doors into the cool Seattle night to catch the bus back to the hotel.

Inside Room 114, Jessica wheeled forward and grabbed the heavy brass handle of the wooden door. She pulled it shut.

*Click.* She threw the deadbolt, locking the door from the inside.

The apartment was completely silent. She was three thousand miles away from the father whose slipped grip had shattered her foundation. She was sitting in a fortress built exclusively to accommodate her brilliant mind, with the keys to a corporate tech empire waiting for her on Monday morning.

Jessica Marie Ragsdale rested her hands on her push-rims, alone in the quiet dark, and smiled.
