By six-thirty in the evening, the high-altitude, crisp Pacific Northwest sun was finally beginning to cast long, golden shadows across the concrete of the engineering quad. The vanguard had successfully secured the apartment, mapped the local transit grid, and armed the kitchen, but the adrenaline that had been powering our central nervous systems since the 4:30 AM East Coast extraction was violently bottoming out.

"We need food," Mom announced, looking at the three of us huddled under the transit shelter. Her voice was entirely devoid of energy. "Real food. Not protein bars from the grocery store."

Jessica unzipped her messenger bag, pulling out the massive NPU campus map and laying it flat across her lap. She ran her finger along the western edge of the university boundary.

"There's a high-density commercial zone one block over," Jessica reported, tracing the grid. "The street is listed as University Way Northeast. It runs entirely parallel to the campus."

Mom nodded, adjusting the heavy strap of her canvas purse. "Let's go find a restaurant."

We didn't wait for a bus. We walked the short distance, leaving the manicured, quiet brick plazas of the university behind. We reached the busy intersection and looked up at the green metal street sign hanging from the traffic wire: *University Way NE*.

Mom scanned the chaotic, bustling street. It was packed with college students, roaring diesel transit buses, and an overwhelming sea of glowing storefronts. Finding a specific place to eat in the neon chaos was daunting.

An older student, hauling a heavy canvas laundry bag over his shoulder, noticed Mom looking back and forth between the street sign and the map in Jessica's lap.

"Looking for dinner?" the student asked, pausing on the corner before the crosswalk signal changed. "You guys look new. Just head down The Ave. There are a dozen cheap places in the next three blocks."

Mom looked at him, slightly confused, and pointed up at the metal street sign. "The map says this is University Way."

The student laughed, shaking his head. "Nobody calls it that. It's The Ave. Welcome to Seattle." He gave a quick wave and jogged across the crosswalk.

Jessica looked up at the street sign, processing the new, localized data, and then looked down the bustling corridor. "The Ave," she repeated, officially committing the nomenclature to her mental database. "Alright. Let's go."

We merged into the heavy pedestrian flow. The air on The Ave was thick, carrying a chaotic sensory mix of roasting coffee beans, old bookstore paper, and heavy fryers. I reached into my backpack and pulled my headphones over my ears, keeping the volume low enough to hear Mom's commands but high enough to build an auditory shield against the roaring traffic. Rachel immediately grabbed the back hem of my shirt, locking her eyes onto the pavement and stepping perfectly into my shadow.

We bypassed the standard, brightly lit chain restaurants, looking for something authentic. Halfway down the block, nestled tightly between an independent record shop and a twenty-four-hour laundromat, we found it.

It was a tiny, slightly gritty hole-in-the-wall with faded, sun-bleached pictures of food taped to the front window. The glowing neon sign in the glass simply read: *Teriyaki*.

Mom pulled the heavy glass door open. It was cramped, loud, and smelled incredibly rich. The tables were packed tightly together, but Jessica navigated the tight geometry flawlessly, carving a path straight to a corner booth in the back.

She parked her manual chair at the head of the table, locking her brakes and facing the door. I slid into the booth, taking the inside seat, and Rachel immediately scrambled in next to me, pinning herself between my side and the wall for maximum deep-pressure grounding.

In the year 2000, Seattle-style teriyaki wasn't just a meal; it was an absolute cultural institution. It was the blue-collar, high-calorie fuel of the Pacific Northwest. Mom went to the front counter and ordered four massive orders of chicken teriyaki, paying with a crumpled twenty-dollar bill from Grandma Loretta’s emergency fund.

When the food arrived, it wasn't served on plates. It was handed to us in heavy, white styrofoam clamshells. We popped them open, the steam rising into the cool air of the restaurant.

We didn't talk. We just ate.

The chicken was heavily charred from an open grill, chopped into strips, and dripping in a thin, intensely sweet-and-savory soy glaze that tasted absolutely nothing like the thick, artificial fast-food mall teriyaki we were used to back in Virginia. It was served over a massive mound of sticky white calrose rice, accompanied by a simple iceberg lettuce salad drenched in a sweet, creamy dressing.

Sitting in that cramped booth, eating with cheap white plastic forks, the sheer, crushing weight of the last forty-eight hours finally began to lift.

I looked around the table. Mom was actually resting against the back of the booth. Rachel had let go of my shirt and was methodically mixing her rice into the teriyaki sauce. Jessica’s hands were completely off her push-rims, resting loosely on the table, the heavy lactic acid burn in her triceps finally beginning to fade.

There was no ticking grandfather clock in the hallway. There was no booming, resentful voice complaining about the cost of a long-distance phone call or the price of an airline ticket. There was no oppressive, suffocating guilt over a slipped grip on a frosted roof.

There was just the pack. We were sitting three thousand miles away from the shadow of Baja Taco and the fractured reality of 1852 Delaney Street. We were eating local food, completely autonomous, and entirely safe.

Jessica took a bite of her chicken, looking out the plate-glass window at the neon lights reflecting off the Seattle pavement. A quiet, profound peace settled into her shoulders, softening the rigid lines of her stoic armor.

"I could get used to this," Jessica murmured.
