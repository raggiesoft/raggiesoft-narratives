The ride back to the hotel on the TAPS bus was entirely silent. The cool, dark Seattle streets blurred past the window, but none of us were really looking at the city. The extraction was complete, the perimeter had been handed over, and the adrenaline was completely gone. All that was left was the heavy, hollow reality of the separation.

We walked through the automatic sliding doors of the hotel lobby. The space was quiet, smelling faintly of floor wax and the indoor pool. Mom walked straight up to the mahogany front desk.

"Ragsdale, Room 412," Mom said to the night clerk, her voice exhausted. "Are there any messages for us?"

It was approaching one o'clock in the morning back in Norfolk. Dad had been off his shift at Baja Taco for hours. He knew we were flying across the country today. He knew his disabled daughter was moving into a dorm three thousand miles away.

The clerk checked the computer, then glanced at the physical message slots on the wall behind him. "Nothing, Mrs. Ragsdale. Your line is clear."

Mom’s jaw locked. She gave a single, tight nod. "Thank you."

We took the rattling, wood-paneled elevator up to the fourth floor and swiped into Room 412. The mechanical routine of bedtime took over, a cold, wordless efficiency masking the emotional crater in the room. We took turns in the cramped bathroom. Rachel went first, changing into her pajamas and brushing her teeth. I went second. Mom went last.

When I stepped out of the bathroom, Rachel was already sitting on the edge of the queen mattress closest to the large window overlooking the U-District. Mom took the bed closest to the door, maintaining her vanguard position. Mom reached over and clicked off the brass bedside lamp, plunging the room into darkness, save for the ambient amber glow of the streetlights bleeding through the edges of the blackout curtains.

I climbed into the bed next to Rachel, pulling the heavy, starched hotel comforter up to my chest.

For the first five minutes, the room was dead silent. But as my eyes adjusted to the dark, a massive, terrifying spike of panic began to flood my nervous system.

For the last nine months, ever since Jessica came home from the hospital in a wheelchair, the physical geometry of my safety had been perfectly, flawlessly balanced. I slept in the dead center of the Delaney Street sofa bed. I was the deep-pressure buffer. Rachel was always anchored to my left side, and Jessica was always anchored to my right.

Tonight, my right side was completely empty.

The physical void was agonizing. Without the bilateral pressure of a sister on either side of me, my system couldn't regulate. The mattress felt entirely too big. The air in the room felt too cold. The sheer distance between me and Jessica—the knowledge that she was locked behind a wooden door blocks away—hit me like a physical strike to the chest.

My breathing hitched. I clamped my eyes shut, desperately trying to force my system to calm down, but the lock had already been triggered. Hot, heavy tears spilled over my eyelashes, soaking into the hotel pillowcase. I curled inward, pulling my knees toward my chest, quietly sobbing in the dark. I wasn't just missing my sister; the physical architecture of my survival had been ripped away.

Next to me, Rachel shifted.

She was hurting just as much as I was. The separation anxiety was vibrating off of her in tense, rigid waves. But when she heard the quiet, jagged hitch of my breathing, the Bug realized her Bear was actively falling apart.

Rachel didn't freeze. She closed the distance.

She slid entirely across the mattress, pressing the full length of her body firmly against my back. She threw her arm heavily over my ribs, gripping the front of my shirt in a tight, unyielding fist, and pressed her forehead directly between my shoulder blades.

She wasn't a tiny child anymore; she applied a massive, desperate wave of deep-pressure grounding, forcing her own physical weight to compensate for the empty flank on my right side.

"I've got you, Bear," Rachel whispered into the dark, her voice trembling but fiercely determined.

I reached up, resting my hand over hers where it gripped my shirt. The heavy, physical anchor didn't instantly stop the tears, but it kept me from completely shattering. She held me together, returning the exact same protection I had given her on the flight over the Midwest.

From the bed by the door, Mom didn't say a word. She didn't tell us to be quiet, and she didn't get up to comfort us. I could hear the slow, unsteady rhythm of her breathing. She was lying awake, staring at the ceiling, feeling the exact same devastating fracture in the pack, silently masking her own grief so we could process ours.

Falling asleep was a brutal, agonizingly slow battle against the silence. Every time I closed my eyes, my brain screamed that the perimeter was broken. But as Rachel’s breathing eventually slowed into a heavy, rhythmic cadence against my back, I held onto the only logistical fact that mattered.

The sun would come up. We would walk back to the engineering quad. We would see her tomorrow.
