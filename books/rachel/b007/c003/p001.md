Friday morning broke with the bright, sharp clarity unique to the Pacific Northwest. Our internal clocks were still violently protesting, but the adrenaline of Move-In Day overrode the fatigue.

We didn't check out of Room 412. Mom had the hotel secured until next Monday, the day Jessica's classes officially started. Instead, we executed a highly targeted extraction. We separated Jessica’s massive canvas bags and the heavy computer box from the rest of the pack's luggage, loading only her gear onto a brass luggage cart.

Mom marched us out of the hotel lobby and directly toward the sprawling, gothic architecture of the Northwest Pacific University campus.

The U-District was already buzzing. Thousands of students were dragging futons, mini-fridges, and plastic crates down the sidewalks. But Mom didn't lead us toward the massive, chaotic high-rise dormitories dominating the skyline. Quantum Corporation wasn't paying for standard student housing. As the sole national winner of the Catalyst Initiative, Jessica’s full-ride scholarship commanded priority logistics.

We followed the campus map to a newer, low-rise brick building nestled near the engineering quad. The architecture was deliberately modern, featuring wide, flush concrete pathways and oversized automatic sliding glass doors.

We checked in at the front desk. The resident advisor handed Jessica a single brass key and a welcome packet.

"Room 114," the RA smiled. "First floor, right down the hall. Fully accessible layout."

We hauled the gear down the wide, brightly lit corridor. Jessica slid the brass key into the lock and pushed the heavy wooden door open.

It wasn't a standard cramped dorm room. Because the standard ADA dorms were backlogged, the university had bypassed the freshman tiers entirely and placed her in a premium, single-occupancy upperclassman apartment.

The floors were smooth, polished hardwood—no thick carpets to bog down her casters. The bed was intentionally lowered. The attached private bathroom featured a roll-in shower and heavy steel grab bars.

But it was the kitchen alcove that brought the entire reality of the West Coast extraction into sharp, devastating focus.

Mom stood in the center of the hardwood floor, staring at the architecture. The stainless-steel sink had no cabinetry underneath it; it was completely open, allowing Jessica’s wheelchair to roll perfectly flush beneath the basin. The oven and stove unit featured controls mounted on the front panel, completely eliminating the need for her to reach across hot burners. There was a full-size refrigerator and a dishwasher installed directly at her level.

Four months ago, standing in the Delaney Street dining room, Dad had used the kitchen to highlight her physical liability. *“You can't even reach the top shelves in our own kitchen yet. How are you going to manage an entire life by yourself?”* He was wrong. She wasn't going to have to reach the top shelves. The university had simply built a world where top shelves were irrelevant.

"Alright," Mom said, clapping her hands together, a fierce, vindicated pride in her eyes. "Michael, unpack the computer. That's the priority. Rachel, start unpacking the canvas bags into the dresser."

The pack went to work. I hauled the heavy cardboard box over to the custom desk. The desk was built high, with absolutely no drawers underneath, giving her maximum turning radius.

I lifted the massive, beige tower and the heavy CRT monitor out of the styrofoam packing. As I routed the thick grey power cables, I noticed a plastic wall plate directly behind the desk. It wasn't a phone jack. It was a slightly wider, squared-off RJ-45 Ethernet port. The university's ResNet.

I didn't have to tie up a phone line. I didn't have to listen to the frantic, high-pitched screech of a 56k dial-up modem. I ran a Cat5 cable from the back of the tower straight into the wall, instantly connecting her to the university’s high-speed, always-on broadband network.

I hit the heavy plastic power button. The CRT monitor hummed, warming up until it displayed the familiar, slightly dated teal background of the Quantum OS 98 desktop.

Jessica wheeled over next to me, looking at the glowing screen. Because Dad had choked off the family finances, upgrading her software back in Virginia had been impossible.

"Only for the weekend," Jessica muttered, tapping her fingers on the edge of the desk. "Monday morning, I get my Blue Badge at the Overlake campus orientation. Full-time Quantineer. As soon as they hand it to me, I’m going straight to the corporate company store. They let employees buy software for the absolute Cost of Goods Sold. Fifteen dollars a box."

I looked at her, stunned. "Fifteen dollars? That software costs hundreds at Broil's Electronics."

"Corporate perks," she smiled. "By Monday afternoon, this machine is getting completely wiped. I'm installing Quantum OS 2000 Professional, the full Quantum Suite 2000 Premium, and Developer Suite 6.0."

While the computer finished booting, Jessica unfolded the massive, color-coded TAPS regional transit map she had pulled from her Quantum welcome folder.

"And the Blue Badge doesn't just get me into the company store," Jessica added, her analytical brain locking onto the transit lines. "It's a subsidized transit pass. I don't even have to carry quarters anymore. I just flash the badge to the bus driver, and I ride for free."

Mom paused, holding a stack of folded t-shirts. "You're taking a city bus all the way across the lake?"

"The TAPS 545," Jessica nodded, tracing the blue line with her finger. "I take a local down to the Montlake Freeway Station. The 545 picks up right on the highway, crosses the floating bridge, and drops me off at the Northeast 40th Freeway Station. It's a three-block roll from there to my desk."

"Is it safe?" Mom asked, her protective instincts immediately flaring.

"It's not just safe, Mom. It's the pipeline," Jessica smiled. "The packet says half the engineering department rides that exact route every morning. The orientation guide literally warns new hires to bring headphones if they don't want to get pulled into a rolling software bug-bash. Plus, I've heard the stories about the gridlock on the 520 bridge. When traffic backs up over the water, there’s plenty of time to write and compile code before the bus even hits Redmond."

By noon, the room was completely unpacked. The clothes were in the drawers. The bed was made. The heavy CRT monitor was quietly humming on the desk.

Mom walked over, leaning down to wrap her arms tightly around Jessica’s shoulders. She held the embrace for a long time, burying her face into her daughter’s hair.

"You don't need us anymore," Mom whispered quietly, her voice thick with emotion. "You're exactly where you're supposed to be."
