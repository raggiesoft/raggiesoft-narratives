We left the ticketing counter behind, leaving our massive canvas bags and the heavy computer tower in the hands of the Meridian Airlines baggage handlers. Stripped down to just our carry-on backpacks, Mom marched us directly toward the B Concourse security checkpoint.

The security line was nothing more than a short maze of black velvet ropes leading up to a single metal detector and a grey x-ray conveyor belt. It was manned by a few private security contractors in white button-down shirts who looked just as tired as the early-morning business travelers.

Mom orchestrated the crossing with military precision. She tossed our backpacks onto the conveyor belt and pointed at the metal archway.

"Michael, go through. Rachel, right behind him," Mom instructed.

I kept my headphones clamped firmly over my ears, letting the steady beat from my portable CD player wash out the drone of the terminal. I walked through the archway. No beeps. I turned around just in time to see Rachel step through, her eyes locked onto the floor, her hand immediately shooting out to grab the hem of my shirt the second she cleared the detector.

"Got you, bug," I muttered, letting her step perfectly into my shadow.

Mom walked through next, grabbing our bags off the rollers.

Jessica’s crossing required a slight detour. Because of her manual wheelchair, she couldn't go through the standard magnetometer. A female security contractor simply unhooked a velvet rope and waved Jessica around the side of the metal detector.

"Just need to do a quick check, hon," the guard said, her tone polite and entirely routine.

Jessica locked her brakes. The guard passed a black plastic wand over Jessica’s arms and legs, lightly patted down the sides of the wheelchair cushion, and visually checked the metal spokes. The entire process took less than two minutes.

"You're all set. Have a good flight," the guard smiled, stepping back.

Jessica unlocked her brakes and pushed herself forward, seamlessly rejoining our formation on the other side of the checkpoint.

We walked deeper into the concourse. The sun was just beginning to turn the sky outside the massive terminal windows a bruised, hazy purple. The heavy scent of industrial floor wax mixed with the sharp, bitter smell of roasting coffee beans from the terminal kiosks.

"Alright," Mom said, checking her wristwatch as we approached our gate. "We have about half an hour before they call for pre-boarding. I want everyone to eat something now so we aren't boarding on empty stomachs."

"Are we buying breakfast?" Jessica asked, looking at the glowing menu board of a nearby bakery counter.

"Just a quick bite," Mom corrected, her mind clearly calculating the cash Grandma Loretta and the aunts had pooled together. "Meridian will serve a hot breakfast once we hit cruising altitude on the way to Chicago. We just need something to hold us over until then."

Mom stepped up to the bakery counter. She bought two massive, cellophane-wrapped blueberry muffins to split between the three of us, a bottle of orange juice for Rachel, and a large black coffee for herself.

We found an empty cluster of blue vinyl seats right against the floor-to-ceiling windows near Gate B4. The massive, twin-engine Meridian Airlines jet was parked right outside, tethered to the jet bridge, its red and blue tail illuminated by the towering tarmac floodlights.

Rachel sat in the seat right next to me, silently picking off pieces of the blueberry muffin and eating them while watching the baggage handlers load the cargo hold below the plane. I kept my music going, the rhythmic, heavy bass acting as a shield against the echoing announcements overhead.

Jessica parked her wheelchair at the end of the row, leaning slightly forward to rest her elbows on her armrests. She stared out the window at the heavy commercial jet, her eyes tracing the line of the jet bridge. I knew exactly what she was looking at. She was mapping the distance. She was calculating the exact moment she would have to surrender her chair and be strapped into the narrow, rigid transfer cart.

Mom sat on the other side of Rachel, sipping her black coffee. She watched the three of us with a quiet, fierce intensity. She had navigated the packing, the extraction from Delaney Street, the ticketing counter, and the security perimeter entirely on her own. She hadn't needed Tom for a single logistical step.

"Finish your muffins," Mom said softly, her eyes flickering to the gate podium where the flight attendants were beginning to gather their clipboards. "They're going to call us soon."
