# Book 18: The Eastern Anchor

## Chapter 1: The Foundation (Friday, March 30th, 2007)

### Part 1: The Tarmac and the Tear – 10:15 PM (EST)

The heavy, mechanical thud of the landing gear deploying echoed through the cabin of the aircraft, followed minutes later by the jarring, high-speed screech of the tires hitting the tarmac at Norfolk International Airport.

As the massive jet engines roared into reverse thrust, pressing us firmly against our seatbelts, a complicated, heavy mixture of emotions crashed into my exhausted processor. I stared out the thick plastic window at the dark, familiar runway lights of Virginia.

"We’re home," I whispered, the words feeling dry in my throat as the plane began its slow taxi toward the gate.

Now that the cross-country flight was officially over, the geographic reality was finally starting to sink in. We were back on the East Coast. The suffocating, toxic nightmare of Kent was permanently behind me, and I was free. But as the adrenaline of the escape faded, a hollow, pulling ache settled deep in my chest. Part of me desperately wanted to turn the plane around. I wanted to be standing back in the damp Seattle air, pulling Jessica and Kristin into a massive, three-way pack hug.

"You’re torn," Rachel commented softly.

I didn't even have to explain. My little sister was twenty years old now, and after everything we had survived, her ability to read my nervous system was flawless. She unbuckled her seatbelt the second the captain turned off the overhead chime, leaned over the armrest, and wrapped her arm securely around my shoulders.

"You wish all of us were gathered in Renton right now, don’t you?" Rachel asked, her dark eyes completely empathetic.

I let out a slow, shaky breath, leaning my weight into her grounding pressure. "Yeah. I do."

On one hand, the relief of being back in Virginia—where the air smelled like salt and the geography made sense to my brain—was immense. But the fracture of our pack was agonizing. The memory of standing at the SeaTac security checkpoint twelve hours earlier was still burning in my mind. I had completely broken down, crying into Kristin’s shoulder while Jessica held my hand, terrified to sever the physical tether that had saved my life. They had both promised to keep in touch every single day, but the distance felt insurmountable.

Kristin and Rachel's lease down in Lakewood was up. Kristin was preparing for her new life at a public university in Washington, and I desperately needed to reset my life on familiar ground. I had never truly belonged in the Pacific Northwest; it had always been a temporary bunker.

My mind kept reminding me of the mathematical truth: Virginia Beach was my home. That’s where I was. I was home.

By the time we navigated the crowded terminal and claimed our heavy suitcases from the baggage carousel, the exhaustion was absolute. We pushed through the sliding glass doors into the chilly, damp March night air.

Mom was waiting for us at the curb in her sedan.

We loaded the bags into the trunk, and the second we climbed into the back seat, Mom's nervous, maternal energy completely filled the cabin. As she steered the car onto the interstate, she launched into a rapid-fire, highly administrative monologue.

"So, the closing paperwork is officially finalized," Mom explained, glancing at us in the rearview mirror. "We get the keys to the townhouse on Monday morning. I had my accountant look over everything, and doing it this way makes the most sense for tax purposes, Michael. We structured the deed so that..."

My brain instantly threw up a static wall. I couldn't process the financial variables. Between the time zone jump, the emotional devastation of leaving Jessica, and the sheer physical exhaustion of navigating two major airports, my executive function was completely offline. I just stared blankly at the back of her headrest.

Rachel sensed my system crashing. She didn't hesitate. She slid across the back seat, pressing her shoulder firmly against mine to anchor me, and stepped right into the line of fire to act as my shield.

"We are going home, Mom," Rachel interrupted, her voice dropping into a firm, pragmatic register that sounded exactly like Jessica. "It’s late, and we are both tired. The tax stuff can wait."

Mom blinked, slightly taken aback by the authoritative tone, but she nodded and quieted down.

Rachel turned her attention back to Mom, initiating a highly calculated logistical maneuver. "I think Michael and I can share a bed for one night if we’re going to be in the townhouse tomorrow anyway."

"Of course," Mom agreed softly, catching the exhaustion in our postures. "The spare room is set up."

### Part 2: The Decoy Protocol – 11:30 PM (EST)

It was half-past eleven by the time we finally dragged our heavy suitcases through the front door of Mom’s house.

The quiet of the hallway was a massive relief. I dropped my bag on the carpet of the spare bedroom and looked at Rachel, profoundly glad to have her standing at my side.

"Let’s just leave the unpacking for tomorrow," I said, rubbing my burning eyes. "Let's just crash for the night."

"I’d like that too," my sister agreed, wrapping her arms around me in a tight, heavy hug. She reached behind my back and twisted the lock on the bedroom door, the mechanical *click* officially sealing our perimeter from the rest of the house. "But we need to call Jessica and Kristin and let them know we landed safely."

"Could we sleep together?" I asked, my face flushing slightly as a wave of anxiety hit me.

I wasn't blushing out of romance; I was blushing out of the deeply ingrained, humiliating fear of neurotypical judgment. We were adults now. The outside world—and our mother—expected twenty-two and twenty-year-old siblings to demand strict, separate privacy. But my nervous system was still completely frayed from the Delores nightmare. I was terrified of being alone in the dark, and I desperately needed the heavy, continuous deep-pressure of my sister's proximity to keep my brain from spiraling into a panic attack.

Rachel understood the anxiety instantly. She knew exactly what I was asking, and why I felt guilty for needing it.

"Sure," Rachel replied, her voice soft and entirely devoid of judgment. "We can sleep in the same bed together. But we should probably take turns in the bathroom to wash up so that Mom doesn't get suspicious or ask questions."

It was a brilliant, tactical compromise. We would perform the expected neurotypical "privacy routine" for the hallway to keep Mom's radar quiet, ensuring our true sanctuary behind the locked door remained unquestioned.

We took our turns slipping down the hall to brush our teeth and change. When we were finally both back inside the spare room, Rachel double-checked the lock on the door and pulled her brand-new cell phone from her pocket.

She dialed Jessica’s number and immediately hit the speakerphone button, setting the device on the nightstand between us.

"We’re safely at Mom’s house for the night," I announced the second the line clicked open. "We get the keys to the townhouse on Monday. We are so tired, mostly because we’re exhausted from the flights, but we wanted to say goodnight."

"We’re in the spare bedroom," Rachel added, leaning her head against my shoulder. "It’s late here on the East Coast, and we need to get used to the time difference sooner rather than later."

"I'm so glad you made it back in one piece," Jessica’s voice crackled through the tiny speaker, carrying that warm, fiercely protective affection we both relied on. "Thank you for letting me know that you’re back in Virginia. Get some sleep, Base Camp. I'll call you tomorrow."

"Goodnight, Jess," we echoed together.

Rachel ended the call. We pulled back the heavy comforter of the small, full-sized guest bed and climbed in. The mattress was incredibly cramped for two adults, but the lack of space was exactly what my system required.

I wrapped my arm entirely around my sister, allowing her to snuggle close against my ribs. Her weight pressed heavily against me, providing an immediate, grounding physical anchor that manually slowed my racing heart rate.

"We will need a much bigger bed than this if we are going to share it every night," Rachel whispered into the dark room, already running the architectural calculations for our new, permanent sanctuary. "Three bedrooms. Maybe we should get a twin bed for each of the small bedrooms, and a big bed for the master?"

I ran her logic through my own processor. Setting up two twin beds would look perfectly "normal" to any outside observers, but it didn't maximize our safety.

"How about a King bed for the master bedroom," I replied, pulling the quilt up over our shoulders, "and a Full bed for the spare bedroom?"

Rachel paused, processing the geometry. A King bed would give us the massive, comfortable space we needed to sleep together every single night as our primary anchor. And the Full bed in the spare room would serve a dual purpose: it would act as a neurotypical decoy for guests, but more importantly, it would give us a tactical "compression" bed. If one of us was having a severe meltdown and needed the inescapable, tight physical boundaries of a small mattress to force us together, the Full bed would be waiting.

"That works," Rachel yawned, completely satisfied with the mathematical perfection of the plan.

We pulled each other into a heavy, final hug, the agonizing stress of the last two years finally bleeding out into the mattress. The perimeter was locked, our future was plotted, and as we drifted off to sleep, the Virginia night felt entirely safe.

## Chapter 2: The Blueprint and the Sabomnim (Saturday, March 31st, 2007)

### Part 1: The Transition Wake-Up – 9:00 AM

Morning rolled around, the bright Virginia sun fighting its way through the guest room blinds. I blinked my eyes open, staring at the ceiling, my internal clock completely scrambled. Rachel and I were still hardwired to West Coast time.

I shifted my weight on the cramped mattress and gently prodded her. "Morning, sis," I whispered, hugging her tightly. "Did you sleep well?"

Rachel groaned, burying her face into my chest. "Morning. This bed is too small for us." She shifted again, letting out a frustrated sigh as her knee bumped the edge of the mattress. "What time is it?"

"It’s already nine, but our brains think that it’s only six," I replied, carefully releasing my sister from the hug and slowly sitting up.

"We should probably get dressed, have breakfast, and get situated for the day. We have moving to do," Rachel replied, sounding much more awake at the prospect of finalizing our sanctuary. She sat up, reached for her canvas gym bag on the floor, and started pulling out comfortable clothes for the day. "We can take turns in the bathroom again, then walk downstairs together for food."

"When Mom talks to us about how we want the townhouse laid out, we will say that we will take each of the smaller bedrooms," I whispered, formulating our defensive strategy against neurotypical expectations. "But when it is just you and me, we will, of course, really make the big bedroom our bedroom."

"We can do that, or at least say we’ll do that with the little bedrooms," my sister replied, her dark eyes entirely serious. "The only reason I was uncomfortable in this bed with you is because it is so small. When you slept with me in Washington, the bed was a queen. This, I think, is one size smaller at full."

We were both fully dressed within minutes. After taking turns in the bathroom to maintain the illusion of a standard routine, we made our way downstairs together.

The moment our feet hit the first floor, a chaotic, joyful flurry of golden fur intercepted us. Jimbo, a bright golden retriever, and Teddy, whose coat was a duller, softer golden hue, bounded over to us, their tails wagging so hard their entire back halves shook.

Rachel immediately dropped to her knees on the linoleum, letting out a soft, happy hum as Teddy shoved his heavy head directly into her chest, desperately seeking affection. It was Rachel’s first time back in this house since Baby and Archie had passed away. The grief of losing our childhood canine guards still echoed in the back of my mind, but the pure, unadulterated love these two retrievers offered was an incredible balm.

Mom was waiting for us at the kitchen island, a folder of closing documents resting near her coffee mug. She started explaining the layout of the new townhouse.

"We looked at the pictures you sent us, and we think we know what we will do," Rachel suggested, keeping her voice casual as she scratched Jimbo behind the ears. "We can each take the smaller bedrooms, and that big bedroom will be a guest bedroom."

Mom smiled, accepting the neurotypical geometry without question. Breakfast consisted of quick bowls of cereal so that we could head out to the furniture store to start picking out pieces for the townhouse sooner rather than later.

"How about we start on Furniture Row on the Boulevard?" Rachel suggested, her logistical brain already mapping the route.

"It’s Saturday, and the sale of the century is in progress," I giggled, reaching down to pet Teddy as he nudged my knee, completely ready to start building our adult lives.

### Part 2: The Adult Perimeter – 10:30 AM

Breakfast was finished, and Mom drove us to one of the massive, warehouse-style furniture showrooms on Virginia Beach Boulevard. The sheer scale of the store was a bit overwhelming, but moving as a unified pack kept our sensory gauges level.

A sales associate wearing a branded polo shirt quickly approached us.

"My son and daughter are moving into a townhouse I bought for them while they attend college," Mom explained proudly. "They will need everything: living room, dining room, bedroom, office."

I stepped forward to handle the spatial logistics. "It is a three-bedroom: two smaller rooms and one big bedroom. We were thinking that we could each take the smaller room, and set up the big room as a guest room." I forced a polite, brotherly laugh to sell the script to the associate. "I love my sister and everything, but we still need our privacy. Rachel, did you remember the drawing we made?"

My sister reached into her pocket and unfolded a piece of graph paper where we had meticulously sketched out the floor plan.

"It is a two-story townhouse, and we are very excited to start this thing called being adults," Rachel told the associate. We migrated over to the living room section, immediately scanning the modular couches.

"Do you both want the old house sofa and dining room set?" Mom chimed in, walking up behind us.

Rachel stopped dead in her tracks. A sudden, heavy wave of emotion washed over her face, tears instantly shining in her dark eyes. "Sure," she whispered, her voice trembling with the profound weight of our history. "The one that Jessica slept on after the accident?"

"Yes, that one. We still have it in storage," Mom replied softly.

We didn't need to discuss it. That sofa wasn't just furniture; it was the original Base Camp. It was the exact spot where our pack had survived the darkest months of 1995.

Working with the sales associate, we color-matched a new entertainment center, a coffee table, and an end table to complement the old, floral-print sofa. We then transitioned into the home office section.

"I lived with our older sister for a short time, and her desk and computer setup allows her to not have to come into an office if she so chooses," I explained to the associate, envisioning the dual-desk command center we needed to build. "While we aren’t there yet, we will each want a desk to do homework. We plan to start at CCHR in the fall."

As we walked toward the bedroom galleries, Rachel pulled out the budget sheet, running the math on what we had spent so far. She bumped her shoulder against my arm, stopping in the aisle.

"Want to just share the one bedroom?" Rachel asked softly. "We could take turns in the bathroom for privacy."

"Sure," I replied, smiling at the brilliant simplicity of dropping the act when no one was listening. "This bathroom is attached to the bedroom, and I think we will manage privacy. Two dressers, and let’s look at beds."

We found a massive, sturdy King-sized bed that we both immediately loved.

"We should find a small bed for one of the other rooms," Rachel stated, maintaining her volume. Then, seeing a few other shoppers browsing in the next aisle, she stepped into my personal space, cupped her hand around my ear, and dropped her voice to a fierce, private whisper.

"I love you and everything, but you are my brother. Let’s find a small bed for you," Rachel recited the script aloud for anyone eavesdropping. Then, her breath warm against my ear, she delivered the absolute, unyielding truth of our sanctuary: "I promise we will share the King and sleep naked together. It is just that we are in public, and the rest of the world will not want us together."

I knew exactly what she meant. It wasn't about romance; it was about the absolute, unfiltered vulnerability that proved our environment was entirely threat-free. It was about stripping off the heavy, restrictive armor of the outside world and relying purely on skin-to-skin sensory grounding.

"Understood," I whispered back.

The associate returned, and I smoothly resumed the performance. "Yes, my sister and I are about to move in together. She picked out a bed and dresser for her room, and now I need a bed and dresser for my room."

Eventually, we had a flawless layout secured and reunited with Mom at the registers. We were well within budget.

"I start back up with Spot on Wednesday of next week, the start of the pay period," Rachel noted, her logistical brain pivoting to employment. "Maybe you should find a job too. How about the grocery store next to me? I don’t think siblings are supposed to work in the same establishment."

"How about I take you both grocery shopping next?" Mom offered as she signed the final receipt.

"Take us to the grocery store next to Spot, and I’ll fill out an application while there," I agreed. I thought about the HR policies for a moment. "If I do fill out an application for Spot as well, I will disclose that you are my sister."

"We probably should stop at Spot so that I can introduce myself to the manager," Rachel decided as we walked out to the car. "Let’s go there first, in case we get perishables at the grocery store. Yeah, there is a place you can say that you’re related to a current employee if you fill out an application. Also fill out one for the grocery store."

We pulled into the sprawling parking lot of Spot. The bright, familiar red-themed retail store was a comforting, standardized environment for Rachel. She found a worker near the front registers.

"Could I speak to the LOD, please?" Rachel asked, using the precise internal corporate terminology.

A few moments later, a middle-aged man in a red polo walked up.

"Good morning, I am Rachel Ragsdale, and I am the team member transferring in from Washington state," she introduced herself with crisp professionalism. "My start date is not this Wednesday but next Wednesday. I think my manager set it up so that I start here on the first day of the next pay period."

"Welcome to Virginia Beach, Rachel. My name is Tim, and I am the general manager of this Spot," he smiled, extending a hand. "Yes, I was the one who spoke to Harvey about your transfer. What brings you all the way to Virginia?"

Rachel didn't hesitate. She reached out, looping her arm securely through mine and pulling me flush against her side. "I came home to be with my brother, and our mother lives here in Virginia Beach also."

"Alright," Tim nodded. "I have not made the schedule yet, but please check back no later than Tuesday of next week for your times. Are there any availability concerns that I need to know about?"

"Probably not for the summer, however, I do intend to take classes at CCHR in the fall. We can talk about that closer to the start of the semester, since it is only May right now," Rachel replied sensibly.

Tim’s radio suddenly squawked with an urgent call, and he excused himself.

With employment secured, Rachel and I hit the aisles to pick up the supplies we needed for the townhouse. We started in the domestics department.

It was just the two of us in the quiet aisle. Rachel let out a long, happy breath. "We are sharing a King-sized bed. Yes, I want to share a bed with you." She turned and pulled me into a fierce, deep-pressure hug right there among the pillows, thrilled that the logistical lies were over.

We turned our attention to the bedding displays.

"I like this one," I said, pointing to a dark, expansive comforter.

"Astronomy!" Rachel squealed, her voice buzzing with excitement. "Remember when we had a telescope as kids?"

"I do, and I would love to get one again," I smiled as she eagerly pulled the heavy, star-patterned comforter off the shelf and dropped it into our red shopping cart.

"You picked the comforter, so I want to pick the sheets," she declared.

"Okay, that’s only fair," I conceded.

She scanned the shelves, her hands running over the different fabrics to verify their sensory safety, before pulling out a set of rich, deep forest green sheets.

We gathered up towels and organizers for the bathrooms before migrating to the home office section.

"I have a crazy idea," Rachel said, her eyes lighting up as she looked at the desk lamps. "One of the little bedrooms can be our desks for doing homework, and I was thinking that we could save up for a small bed for the other room if we have guests."

"The tactical decoy room," I grinned. "I love it."

### Part 3: The Reunion at the Register – 1:00 PM

Mom was waiting for us with a cart full of cleaning supplies as we made our way to the checkout lanes.

"Groceries next?" Rachel asked, dropping our bedding into the trunk of the car.

"Yeah, we should. I am kind of getting hungry," I admitted, my stomach rumbling.

"We need bus passes," Rachel reminded me, her mental checklist never missing a beat.

"The customer service desk at the grocery store has them," I confirmed as we walked next door. "It’s thirty days from the day of first use."

"I remember, I think," my sister replied as we grabbed a shopping cart and headed into the produce section. "Now, this is our first time properly shopping for groceries together. Let’s think about what we both like to eat. I seem to remember that we both shared a lot of common interests in food."

We spent the next forty minutes meticulously filling the cart, agreeing on the exact brands of peanut butter, the right textures for snacks, and loading up on generic cola. Once we had everything we needed, including toiletries, we wheeled the cart up to the customer service desk.

"Two thirty-day bus passes, please," I told the clerk, handing over the cash.

As the clerk processed the transaction, a woman in line at the adjacent register turned around to grab a magazine from the rack.

She was wearing casual civilian clothes—faded denim jeans and a simple, comfortable sweater—but the way she carried her posture was instantly, undeniably familiar. It was the effortless, grounded stillness of perfect biomechanical control.

I froze, the change slipping from my fingers onto the counter.

"Sabomnim?" I gasped out, the old, revered title bypassing my adult brain completely.

April turned her head. For a fraction of a second, her eyes scanned right past us, looking down toward the height she expected ten-year-old Michael and eight-year-old Rachel to be. Then, she looked up.

Her jaw literally dropped.

"Michael? Rachel?" April breathed, abandoning her groceries entirely.

Rachel let out a sharp, joyful noise. We didn't care that we were standing in the middle of a crowded grocery store. The three of us collided in a massive, fierce, three-way hug.

The physical geometry of the embrace was profoundly shocking. When we last held her, we had to bury our faces into her waist. Now, at twenty-two and nineteen years old, we were looking our former protector directly in the eye. I was actually an inch taller than her.

"Look at you both," April whispered, pulling back just enough to grip our shoulders, her eyes shining with absolute, unfiltered pride and unshed tears. "You’re so tall. You grew up."

"We just moved back to Virginia Beach," Rachel rasped, her voice thick with emotion. "We bought a townhouse. We're home."

"I am so incredibly happy to hear that," April smiled, wiping a tear from her cheek. She looked us up and down, reading the solid, unbreakable bond still holding us together.

"Is the Academy still open?" I asked eagerly, my heart hammering against my ribs. "The Lynnhaven Academy of Seondu?"

"It is," April beamed, the pride in her Dojang evident. "I’m still running it. Still the *Sunhoe Sabomnim*, though I only teach the kids' classes now. But I do host a special, closed-door Alumni Class for my grown-up kids who have aged out of the youth ranks."

She looked at us, her expression shifting into that familiar, authoritative, deeply protective cadence.

"When you two are settled and ready," April offered, "you are welcome on my mats. And if you want, you can join the youth classes as *Jo-Kyo-Nim*—Assistant Instructors. My kids would be lucky to have you."

Rachel’s eyes widened. "But we can't spar. The doctors said—"

"I know exactly what the doctors said," April interrupted gently, her voice an absolute, unyielding shield. "I know you will never be medically cleared for contact sports. But *Seondu* isn't a sport. It's an art. I will continue to modify the curriculum for you, just like I did in the garage."

April tilted her head, her analytical brain already running the training schedule. "Where did you both leave off before the custody arrangement separated you in 2001?"

"Red belt," I answered immediately, the memory of our last rank burned into my brain. "Red belt with a solid black stripe. We were right on the edge."

April’s eyes lit up with a fierce, brilliant spark.

"Then we have an Il-Dan test to prepare for," April declared quietly, making a solemn, unbreakable vow right there in the grocery store aisle. "No sparring. No physical contact. Just Jeongsin and Tongje. You are going to earn your Nam-saek tti."

Rachel reached over and grabbed my hand, squeezing it with bruising force. The trauma of the Dojang was permanently dead. We were going back to the mats, and we were finally going to finish what we started.

### Part 4: The Alumni Roster – 1:15 PM

I stared at April, completely overwhelmed by the reality that our journey wasn't over. Beside me, Rachel was practically vibrating, her dark bob bouncing slightly as she looked up at our former protector.

We weren't the terrified, broken kids from 1995 anymore. I was twenty-two years old, standing tall in my blue v-neck shirt and glasses, and Rachel was twenty, looking confident and grounded in her jeans and red-trimmed t-shirt. We were adults who had survived the absolute worst the world had thrown at us, but the profound, unwavering respect we held for the woman standing in the grocery store aisle hadn't changed a single bit.

"I need both of your phone numbers," April announced, her voice shifting back into that brisk, logistical rhythm we knew so well. She reached into her purse and pulled out her cell phone. "And I need to get you the address for the academy so you can come tour the mats."

Rachel immediately pulled her sleek phone from her pocket, her thumbs flying across the keypad. "I'm sending you a text right now," Rachel rasped, her voice clear and steady.

A second later, April's phone buzzed. She smiled, saving the contact, and then looked at me. I rattled off my own number, and within moments, my phone buzzed in my pocket with a text from her containing the address for the Lynnhaven Academy of Seondu.

"The Alumni Class meets late on Friday nights," April explained, tucking her phone away. "The building is completely locked down and empty of all the youth students. It's just the grown-ups who aged out of my program, so the environment is incredibly controlled. You two can come observe this Friday, get a feel for the floor, and we will talk about getting your doboks ordered."

"Friday night," Rachel repeated, her dark eyes locking onto the schedule. "We'll be there."

"And like I said," April added, her eyes crinkling warmly behind her glasses, "think about the Assistant Instructor roles. My neurodivergent kids could really use Jo-Kyo-Nims who actually understand how their brains work. You two have lived it. You are the perfect translators."

My chest swelled with a massive, validating sense of purpose. We weren't just going back as students; April saw us as peers. She saw our trauma and our survival as a specialized toolkit that could actively protect the next generation of kids on her mats.

"We'll definitely think about it, Sabomnim," I promised, offering a polite, respectful bow of my head. Rachel perfectly mirrored the motion beside me.

"Go enjoy your weekend, you two," April smiled, waving us off as she returned to her shopping cart. "I'll see you on Friday."

Rachel and I turned back toward the checkout lanes, our hands naturally finding each other. We laced our fingers together, walking back to where Mom was waiting near the registers. The ambient noise of the grocery store, the beeping of the scanners, and the chatter of the other shoppers didn't bother us at all. The perimeter was secured, and the future was completely mapped out.

"Did you get the bus passes?" Mom asked as we walked up to the cart.

"We got them," I said, slipping the cards into my wallet. "And we ran into Master April."

Mom’s eyes widened in genuine surprise. "April? How is she?"

"She's great," Rachel answered, a huge, uncontainable smile spreading across her face. "She still runs her own academy. We're going to start training again. We're going to get our Midnight Blue belts."

Mom let out a soft, emotional gasp, covering her mouth with her hand. She knew exactly what that meant to us. It was the ultimate closure to a decade-old wound. "I am so incredibly happy for you both," she whispered.

We loaded our weekend groceries, the new bathroom supplies, and our brand-new bedding into the trunk of the car. Since we didn't get the keys to the townhouse until Monday morning, we were heading back to Mom's house to ride out the rest of the weekend.

As Mom steered the sedan out of the Spot parking lot, she glanced at us in the rearview mirror, shifting into her administrative mode.

"So, I spoke with the sales associate while you two were picking out the bedding," Mom explained. "Because we ordered the massive King bed, the new dressers, and requested the old Delaney Street sofa out of storage, delivery is going to take a little bit of time. They said it will be at least a week and a half before the truck can bring everything to the townhouse."

Rachel’s brow furrowed. She leaned forward against her seatbelt. "A week and a half?"

"Yes," Mom nodded apologetically. "Which leaves us with a logistical choice. You two can stay in the spare bedroom at my house until the furniture arrives, or, on Monday after we get the keys, we can go buy some temporary gear to get you by. We could get a couple of premium air mattresses, a folding card table for the dining room, and some cheap folding chairs so you can set up your computer desks."

Rachel and I looked at each other in the back seat.

Staying at Mom's house meant sleeping in a cramped Full-sized bed and sharing space with a neurotypical adult who, despite her best intentions, still represented a layer of childhood trauma and lost autonomy. Moving into the townhouse on Monday with temporary air mattresses meant establishing our absolute, independent Base Camp immediately.

The math wasn't even close.

"Temporary gear," Rachel stated, her voice carrying absolute, non-negotiable certainty. "We want to move in on Monday. We can sleep on air mattresses. We just want our own perimeter."

"I agree," I nodded, squeezing Rachel's hand. "A card table and air mattresses are perfectly fine to get us by until the real furniture gets delivered."

"I figured you'd say that," Mom smiled, pulling into her driveway. "We will hit the store first thing Monday morning after the lawyer hands over the keys."

We carried our weekend groceries into the kitchen, greeted enthusiastically by Jimbo and Teddy. Once the perishables were put away, Rachel grabbed the massive shopping bag containing our new bedding.

"Come on," she instructed, heading straight for the stairs.

We walked into the small spare bedroom and locked the door behind us. Even though we were stuck on the cramped, Full-sized guest bed for two more nights, Rachel absolutely refused to wait to use our new gear. We stripped the generic guest blankets off the mattress and stretched the deep forest green sheets over the smaller bed, tucking the massive excess fabric under the mattress. Then, we threw the heavy, astronomy-themed King comforter over the top. It draped heavily onto the floor on all sides, completely swallowing the small bed.

Rachel kicked her shoes off and scrambled under the heavy, star-patterned quilt, situating herself perfectly against the wall.

I took my shoes off and climbed in right beside her. Because the bed was so small and the comforter was so massive, we were cocooned entirely in the dark, heavy fabric.

We pulled the blankets up, closed the distance, and wrapped our arms around each other. The deep, heavy, rib-crushing pressure of her weight against my side was exactly the grounding anchor my nervous system needed after the overwhelming sensory data of the shopping trip.

"We get the keys in two days, Bug," I murmured, resting my chin against the top of her dark hair.

"Two days, Bear," Rachel echoed softly, her breathing slowing into the comfortable rhythm of absolute safety. "Air mattresses on Monday. And the Dojang on Friday."

### Part 5: The Canadian Arch Perimeter – 4:00 PM

The heavy, suffocating exhaustion of the morning’s shopping marathon finally burned off after a deep, two-hour nap in the spare bedroom.

When Rachel and I trudged back downstairs, our neurodivergent batteries were successfully recharged. Mom was waiting in the kitchen, her car keys already in hand.

"Since we have a few hours before dinner, I thought we could take a quick drive," Mom suggested, smiling as she grabbed her purse. "I know the lawyer doesn't hand over the official keys until Monday morning, but I figured you two might want to actually see the outside of your new townhouse in person."
