# Book 13: Michael’s Senior Year

## Chapter 1: The Empty Shell (Monday, July 14th, 2003)

### Part 1: The Last Morning – 7:30 AM

I opened my eyes, the morning light filtering harshly through the plastic blinds.

I wasn't in my own room. I was lying fully dressed on the bottom mattress of the bunk bed in what used to be my sisters’ room. Beside me, Ashley Raybourn shifted, her arm draped heavily and protectively across my chest. The computer monitor on the desk in the corner was still glowing, a faint, humming rectangle in the quiet space, left on from whatever mindless task we had been using to distract our brains last night.

I sat up slowly, the shift in weight waking the Sentinel. My chest felt impossibly tight.

Hot, unprompted tears immediately started leaking from my eyes, tracking silently down my cheeks as the reality of the calendar processed in my mind. The time difference was a brutal, invisible wall. It was 7:30 AM here in Virginia, which meant it was 4:30 AM on the West Coast. I couldn't call Jessica in Seattle, and I couldn't call Rachel in Vancouver.

Ashley didn't say a word. She just sat up, wrapped both arms around my ribs, and applied a firm, grounding squeeze. I leaned into her, desperate for the deep pressure. She was the absolute last member of the pack left. After we walked out of this house today, she was riding with me to Norfolk. She had promised to spend the rest of the summer guarding my perimeter, but we both knew the clock was ticking. By the last week of August, she would be packing her car to drive west to Shenandoah Valley University.

"Let's do the final sweep," Ashley whispered, her voice rough with sleep but steady with purpose.

We pushed ourselves off the mattress and walked downstairs to get ready for the day. Breakfast was a dull, mechanical affair. The kitchen, usually the loud, chaotic center of the house, was completely dead. Even the rhythmic, comforting sounds of Archie and Baby were gone. The dogs had already been moved to Norfolk, waiting in the unfamiliar condo Mom’s boyfriend owned at 849 Baldwin Avenue.

I loaded my cereal bowl into the dishwasher and pushed the heavy door shut, locking the latch. I hit the 'Start' button, listening to the water cycle kick on for the absolute last time in this house.

Another tear rolled hot down my cheek. I wiped it away roughly with the back of my hand. Today was the final day. Mom had sold the house. The closing was finished, the boxes were moved, and this physical structure—the only real sanctuary I had ever known—would no longer belong to us.

We walked back upstairs, our footsteps echoing far too loudly on the bare wooden treads.

I pushed open the door to the girls' room again. Without the furniture Mom had already packed, the room looked massive and completely wrong. The absence of their things allowed all the phantom memories of our childhood to come flooding back, hitting my system like a physical wave. This house wasn't just wood and drywall. It was the only place we could run to where the world made sense. It had been our literal playground—the place where we mapped out our survival and built a fortress that no one was supposed to be able to destroy.

But the pack had been systematically dismantled. Jessica's departure for NPU in August of 2000 had been a planned, logical extraction. We had time to prepare for it.

Rachel's departure was a violent, catastrophic fracture. My chest heaved with a sudden, sharp sob as my processor flashed back to September 20th, 2001. As soon as the airspace had cleared after the terror attacks, Dad had forced her onto a flight out of ORF, dragging her all the way to a second-floor walk-up in Vancouver, Washington. The structural integrity of our family hadn't just broken; it had been forcibly torn apart by court papers and bitterness.

I walked over to the closet. Tucked all the way in the back, collecting a thick layer of dust, was the heavy plastic toybox from our childhood.

I knelt on the carpet and pulled the lid off. Inside was a chaotic, tangled collection of the past. There were the rigid, structured toys meant for little boys, mixed completely with the brightly colored dolls and ponies meant for my sisters.

I let out a sudden, jagged howl. The sound ripped out of my throat, bouncing off the empty walls.

I didn't care about the societal rules of what an eighteen-year-old high school graduate was supposed to be doing. The crushing heartbreak broke my executive function entirely. I suddenly, desperately wanted to sit on this carpet and play with my sisters. I wanted to map out the imaginary neighborhoods, set up the rigid rules of the game, and include my action figures in their dollhouse. I wanted to regress to a time when the three of us were an unbroken, impenetrable unit, hiding out in a world that no one else could touch.

My hands were shaking as I reached into the box. My internal telemetry was redlining. I needed a physical, tactile representation of the pattern I had lost.

I pulled out the plastic folding dollhouse we used to keep in Jessica's corner of the room. I opened it up on the carpet. I dug through the box and found one of the small, articulated boy dolls and two of the girl dolls.

Slowly, methodically, I set them up in the tiny plastic living room. I arranged them close together, shoulder-to-shoulder, exactly how we used to sit on the downstairs sofa bed when the world outside was too loud. I set them up as a perfect, unbroken little family. Me, Jess, and Rachel. Together.

The standard hardware world outside would never understand why an eighteen-year-old guy was experiencing a catastrophic system crash over a dollhouse in an empty room. But Ashley wasn't the outside world.

She didn't tell me to grow up. She didn't tell me to stop crying. She dropped to the carpet right beside me, wrapping her arms completely around my back and locking her hands over my chest. She pulled me flush against her, letting her physical weight act as a cargo strap to hold my fracturing nervous system together.

She reached her hand out, picked up a fourth plastic doll from the box, and gently set it right next to the other three in the plastic living room.

I looked at the four figures. My breath hitched.

"The perimeter is still intact, Michael," Ashley promised, resting her chin heavily on my shoulder. "It just covers a larger geographic grid now. Seattle, Vancouver, and Norfolk. We're still the pack."

I sat back on my heels, leaning my full weight into her. I reached into my backpack and pulled out the cheap disposable camera I had bought to document the move. I wound the plastic gear until it clicked, raised the viewfinder to my eye, and pressed the button. The bright flash illuminated the dark closet for a fraction of a second. I wanted one last, physical piece of proof that in this playground, we had belonged together.

We sat there on the floor for a long time, letting the deep compression bleed the panic out of my muscles. Finally, Ashley helped haul me to my feet.

I went into the bathroom to splash cold water on my face and clear the redness from my eyes. When I came back out, Ashley had my canvas bag slung over her shoulder.

We walked downstairs together and locked the front door of 1852 Delaney Street for the final time.

We walked out to the driveway where Mom’s sedan was idling. Ashley tossed her bag into the back and climbed into the passenger seat, keeping her promise to stand watch until SVU called her away.

I got into the driver’s seat and put the car in gear. The drive toward Norfolk was usually a mathematically predictable, uneventful route, but today, every mile marker felt impossibly heavy. I was heading into completely uncharted territory. The dogs were waiting for me, and they would be a comfort, but as I pulled onto the interstate and looked in the rearview mirror, watching 1852 Delaney Street disappear forever, I knew the hard truth.

The physical Base Camp was gone, and the long, grueling era of operating without my pack had officially begun.

### Part 2: The Baldwin Briefing – 8:45 AM

Traffic on Interstate 264 was remarkably light for a Monday morning. Exactly forty-five minutes after locking the door to my childhood, I steered Mom’s sedan into the ground-floor parking garage of the Ghent Villas, pulling perfectly into the reserved stall exactly second from the right.

The physical transition into the new perimeter was immediately jarring. There was no familiar driveway, no sprawling front yard, and no quiet cul-de-sac. We grabbed our canvas bags from the trunk and walked up the wooden exterior decking, the hollow thud of our footsteps echoing under the awning until we reached Unit 2E.

I unlocked the door and pushed it open.

The architectural friction of the condo hit my processor instantly. The front door opened directly into the base of a carpeted staircase. There was absolutely no open foyer to decompress in.

But before the claustrophobia could trigger an operational freeze, a frantic, rhythmic clicking of claws on hardwood rushed down the narrow hallway.

"Archie!" Ashley dropped her heavy bag, a massive grin breaking across her face.

The one-eyed dog practically vibrated with excitement, his tail whipping so hard his entire back half shook. Right behind him, moving with the stiff, lumbering gait of an older gentleman, was Baby.

I didn't care about the bags or the cramped entryway. I dropped straight to my knees on the floor. Baby immediately pushed his massive, graying head directly into my chest, letting out a loud, contented grumble. I wrapped my arms completely around the senior Lab’s thick neck and buried my face in his fur. Archie squeezed in right beside him, aggressively licking Ashley’s hands as she knelt next to me.

They hadn't seen us in weeks during the chaotic transition of the move. The deep, heavy, unyielding pressure of Baby leaning his entire body weight against my chest was exactly the physical compression my fried nervous system was begging for. For three full minutes, we just sat in the tiny entryway, letting the undeniable loyalty of the dogs bleed the panic out of my muscles.

"Okay, boys, let them breathe," Mom’s voice echoed gently from down the hall.

I gave Baby one last squeeze and let Ashley haul me to my feet. The spasticity in my legs was flaring fiercely from the emotional toll of the morning, so I kept one hand firmly anchored on Ashley's shoulder for balance as we walked past the stairs.

To my right, a cramped galley kitchen felt more like a hallway than a room, complete with a tiny, apartment-sized stacked washer and dryer and a modern fridge with a bottom freezer drawer. Mom and Greg Lawson were standing near the island.

Greg was a practical, neurotypical guy. He wasn't overtly hostile like my father had been, but he was an adult who liked his space, and his space was currently being invaded by two teenagers and their heavy baggage.

"Morning, kids," Greg said, offering a tight but polite smile. "Leave the bags by the stairs for now. Come take a seat in the living room. We need to go over the logistics of how this is going to work."

Ashley and I moved to the end of the short hallway and stepped into the living room. It was painfully small, dominated by a television entertainment center and a standard sofa.

I stared at the sofa for a long moment. It didn't pull out into a bed. The physical, impenetrable barrier of the Monday Wars—the exact piece of furniture we had used to build the Base Camp—was permanently gone.

Ashley sensed the spike in my internal telemetry. She sat down on the sofa first, pulling me down right next to her. She immediately shifted her weight, pressing her shoulder and hip completely flush against mine, locking in the physical perimeter.

Mom and Greg pulled up two folding chairs across the small coffee table.

"I know this is a massive adjustment," Mom started, her voice laced with an exhausted, apologetic tone. "The house is officially closed. This is our home base now. But this condo is a fraction of the size of Delaney Street, and it belongs to Greg. So we have to operate on a different set of rules."

"It's just about traffic flow," Greg explained, leaning forward and resting his elbows on his knees. He spoke in clear, literal parameters, which my processor deeply appreciated, even if I hated the rules themselves. "There are four adults living in a two-bedroom unit. If we aren't careful, we are going to constantly be bumping into each other."

He laid out the operational realities of 849 Baldwin Ave.

The galley kitchen was a strictly one-person zone. If someone was cooking, no one else could be in there. The washing machine was too small for massive loads, meaning laundry had to be strictly scheduled.

"And upstairs," Greg continued, looking specifically at me and Ashley. "You two are sharing the room at the top of the stairs, directly across from the linen closet. It has an en-suite bathroom. But the walls are thin, and the floor plan is tight."

"We understand, Mr. Lawson," Ashley replied smoothly, her voice projecting absolute, mature competence. She kept her shoulder firmly pressed against mine, acting as my translator for the neurotypical adults. "Michael and I are used to operating in close quarters. We'll keep our footprint small. You won't even know we're up there."

"I appreciate that, Ashley," Greg nodded, visibly relieved by her neurotypical reassurance. "And Michael, I know this isn't what you're used to. But we're going to make it work. Just keep your routines tight, and we won't have any friction."

My chest hitched slightly, but I forced a stiff nod.

"Good," Mom smiled, though her eyes looked tired. "Why don't you two take your bags upstairs and get settled? We'll figure out dinner later."

We stood up. Ashley grabbed her heavy canvas bag with one hand and offered me her other. I took it, relying on her balance as my CP-tightened legs navigated the narrow, carpeted stairs.

As we hit the top landing and executed the tight U-turn into our new room, the reality of the briefing settled over me. Greg’s rules made perfect logistical sense for standard hardware adults. But to my autistic processor, the condo felt like a minefield of invisible tripwires.

I sat down heavily on the edge of the queen-sized bed, the room so small I could practically touch both walls at once.

"It's too tight," I whispered, the panic starting to hum under my skin again. "If I make a mistake in the kitchen, or if I break a rule... there's nowhere to hide here."

Ashley dropped her bag. She didn't offer empty platitudes. She just sat down directly beside me, wrapping both arms around my ribs and applying a massive, crushing squeeze to short-circuit the rising static in my brain.

"You don't need to hide," the Sentinel promised, her voice a low, unyielding anchor in the cramped room. "Because for the next five weeks, I am your perimeter. We'll navigate the rules together."

### Part 3: The Frictionless Trap – 9:15 AM

The heavy, crushing pressure of Ashley’s arms around my ribs slowly bled the buzzing panic out of my muscles. We sat on the edge of the queen-sized bed for a long time, the stifling heat of the Virginia summer pressing against the small bedroom window.

Eventually, the physical reality of the morning caught up with us. We were both covered in the heavy, suffocating dust of the Delaney Street attic, our clothes sticking to our skin from the sweat of loading the cars.

"We need to wash this off," I muttered, carefully shifting my weight to stand up. My legs were burning, the familiar, tight spasticity of my Cerebral Palsy flaring up after hours of heavy lifting and emotional redlining.

"Agreed," Ashley sighed, pushing herself off the mattress. She reached into her canvas bag and pulled out her toiletries and a clean t-shirt. "You go first. I'll take my turn after you're done."

It was the standard script. Two young adults sharing a space meant establishing a rotating schedule to maintain standard hardware privacy.

I nodded, grabbing my own towel, and took the three short steps into the en-suite bathroom.

I reached out to turn on the light switch, but as the small room illuminated, I stopped dead in the doorway. I didn't step inside. My eyes immediately locked onto the architecture of the shower, and my processor began running a rapid, terrifying sequence of spatial geometry and physical physics.

"What's wrong?" Ashley asked, stepping up right behind my left shoulder.

"It's a hazard," I whispered, my chest tightening as my internal telemetry began to spike all over again.

I pointed to the shower enclosure. It wasn't a standard setup. There was no fiberglass tub to step into, and absolutely no raised lip to contain the water. Instead, the floor was 100% smooth, glazed tile that sloped slightly inward toward a center drain. Worse, the walls were the exact same slick tile, completely devoid of any textured surfaces.

There were no grab bars. There was absolutely nothing to hold onto.

For Greg or Mom—people running on standard hardware with flawless equilibrium—it was just a modern, space-saving bathroom design. But for me, it was a frictionless trap.

"I can't do it, Ash," I said, my voice dropping to a harsh, panicked whisper. "My legs are already shaking from the CP. The second the soap and hot water hit that tile, it's going to turn into a sheet of ice. If my knee buckles or a muscle spasms, there's nothing to catch me. I'll hit the floor."

The operational freeze locked my feet to the carpet. The condo had already stripped away the physical perimeter of Delaney Street and the safety of the pull-out sofa. Now, it was actively threatening my physical safety over the simple act of trying to get clean.

Ashley didn't offer empty reassurances. She didn't tell me I was overthinking it or that I would be fine if I was just careful. She stepped past me into the bathroom, looking down at the sloping tile and running her hand flat against the smooth, unyielding wall.

She did the mechanical math, and she reached the exact same conclusion I did.

"You're right," Ashley said, turning back to face me. Her voice shifted instantly into the low, absolute register of the Sentinel. "It's a massive fall risk. So, we scratch the schedule. We don't take turns."

I blinked, my processor struggling to catch up. "What?"

"We shower together," she stated, her tone completely factual and entirely devoid of hesitation. "My balance is perfect. I don't have the spasticity. I can plant my feet on that tile and not move an inch. You use me as the grab bar."

In the outside world, a neurotypical young adult girl offering to step into a tiny, enclosed shower with a young adult guy would carry a massive, complicated web of romantic or sexual context.

But inside our perimeter, those standard societal rules simply did not exist. We had been best friends since we were toddlers. She had spent the last two years acting as a literal, physical cargo strap to hold my fracturing nervous system together whenever the world got too loud. To my brain, her physical body wasn't a complication; it was the ultimate, infallible safety mechanism.

"Ashley, you don't have to—"

"Michael, look at me," she interrupted gently, stepping back out of the bathroom and placing both hands firmly on my shoulders. She locked eyes with me, delivering the parameters with crystal-clear, non-negotiable logic to bypass my Gray Area Freeze. "We are operating in hostile architecture. I am not going to stand on the other side of that door and listen for you to crack your head open on the tile just because society says teenagers need privacy. Safety overrides the rules. Always."

I let out a long, shaky breath, the tension bleeding out of my jaw. The sheer, undeniable logic of her plan instantly stabilized my nervous system.

"I have to hold onto you the whole time," I warned her quietly, needing to explicitly define the physical boundaries of the workaround. "If I let go, I'll slip."

"Then you don't let go," she promised, her grip tightening on my shoulders. "I will anchor you. We wash the dirt off, we keep you upright, and we survive the condo. Understood?"

"Understood," I whispered, the crushing weight of the morning finally lifting just a fraction of an inch.

We didn't hesitate, and we didn't negotiate. We simply executed the workaround.

Ten minutes later, the tiny en-suite bathroom was completely filled with thick, suffocating steam. The exhaust fan hummed loudly overhead, but it couldn't compete with the deafening, chaotic roar of the scalding hot water hitting the slick tile floor.

The environment was exactly the frictionless hazard we had predicted. The soapy water turned the inward-sloping tile into a sheet of ice. The spasticity in my legs flared violently, my knees trembling as my CP-affected muscles fought a losing battle against gravity.

But I didn't fall.

Ashley stood directly in front of me, her feet planted wide and immovably on the tile. Because her neurobiology and physical balance were completely intact, she was incredibly stable.

I had my arms wrapped heavily over her shoulders, my hands locked tightly behind her neck. I surrendered my physical weight entirely to her, letting my center of gravity sag forward until I was resting flush against her collarbone. She had her arms wrapped firmly around my waist, acting as a literal, unyielding cargo strap to keep my hips locked in place.

It wasn't a hug. It was architectural scaffolding.

I squeezed my eyes shut, burying my face into the crook of her neck to block out the visual static of the small room. The scalding hot water beat down on my back, a massive, overwhelming sensory input that effectively drowned out the lingering hum of the morning's trauma. The intense heat washed away the sweat, the dust of the plastic toybox, and the terrifying chill of the empty Delaney Street house.

We stood there in the cramped, steamy shower, locked together in absolute silence. The outside world would have fundamentally misunderstood the mechanics of this perimeter. But as I leaned all of my broken, exhausted weight into my best friend, feeling her hold me flawlessly steady against the slick tile, I realized the transition to the condo hadn't defeated us.

The walls of Base Camp had changed, but my Anchor was still standing right beside me.

### Part 4: The Transit Grid – 2:00 PM

The entry-level job hunt of 2003 was not designed for someone running on my hardware, and the logistics of the Ghent condo made it infinitely harder.

Greg’s parameter had been clear: I needed a job to cover my own gas and expenses now that high school was over. Mom had tried to help by handing Ashley and me two freshly printed CVTA 30-Day Passes. They were small, magnetic-stripe cards, valid for unlimited rides for exactly thirty days from the first swipe. I respected the logic of the hunt, and I made it my absolute, hyper-focused mission.

But Mom needed the sedan for work during the day, which meant the protective, air-conditioned bubble of a personal vehicle was gone. I was entirely reliant on the sprawling Norfolk bus grid.

By 2:00 PM on Thursday, the coastal Virginia heat and the constant, jarring vibrations of the transit system had pushed my internal battery dangerously close to zero.

The walking-distance options around the condo had been eliminated on day one. Colley Avenue and the Redgate district were packed with restaurants, but my processor had hit a massive, insurmountable wall on the very first application.

*“Applicants are required to possess a valid City of Norfolk Food Handler's Permit.”*

To my literal, rule-abiding brain, a printed prerequisite was absolute law. I didn't know that getting the permit simply required sitting through a ninety-minute class hosted by the Department of Environmental Health. I just saw the parameter, recognized I didn't meet it, and locked up. The Gray Area Freeze deployed instantly. I crossed every restaurant off the list.

"Okay, scratch the food industry," Ashley pivoted smoothly. We were sitting side-by-side on the sticky vinyl seats of the CVTA Route 2 bus as it rumbled up Hampton Boulevard. "What about the university?"

We drove past the sprawling brick campus of Virginia Dominion University.

I looked out the smeared bus window at the massive VDU Bookstore and the Pro-Dine dining halls, my brain immediately running the literal parameters of the environment.

"I can't," I told her, my voice flat, resting my head against the vibrating glass. "It's the VDU Bookstore. I'm not a VDU student. You have to be enrolled to get those jobs."

It was a completely incorrect assumption. I didn't understand that the bookstore and the dining services were run by third-party corporate contractors. As far as my autistic operating system was concerned, the building said VDU, therefore the jobs were strictly reserved for VDU students. I lacked the neurotypical audacity to walk in and question the sign.

With food and the university out, we were forced to execute a brutal transfer.

We rode Route 2 all the way to Little Creek Road, stepping off the bus into the punishing afternoon sun right in front of Chapel's Chicken. My legs were already burning from the physical effort of stabilizing myself on the moving bus. Ashley stayed right at my side as we walked a block up Baylor Place to catch our connection.

When the Route 15 bus finally arrived, it initiated a grueling, agonizing hour-long ride across the city to Glenrock Mall.

I had done the logistical math earlier in the week: if I actually managed to get a job at the mall, I wouldn't take this route. I would take the Route 2 to Downtown Norfolk and transfer to the Route 20—a forty-five-minute ride that was far more efficient. But for the initial application sweep, we were stuck on the 15.

The physical toll of the system was devastating. The constant bumping of the bus floorboards and the heavy, uneven walking had caused the Cerebral Palsy spasticity in my legs to flare fiercely. By the time we walked into Pixel Stop near the mall's food court, my rigid posture and limping gait were glaringly obvious.

It made perfect, logical sense to apply there. I had the specs of the Nova 64, the MagnaByte ecosystem, and every current RPG completely memorized. I knew the inventory better than the people working there. But when I handed my paper application to the manager, her eyes immediately performed a prejudiced visual assessment.

"We're really looking for someone with prior cashiering experience," she said, looking sympathetically at my stiff legs. "And honestly, it's an eight-hour shift of standing perfectly still behind the register. We don't have stools."

My brain held all the correct data, but my physical chassis couldn't pass the inspection.

When we finally took the buses back to Ghent and walked up the wooden exterior stairs to Unit 2E, the crushing weight of the week finally caught up to me. The rejections, the physical pain in my legs, and the impossible neurotypical riddles on the application forms had completely drained me. I felt like a broken, defective machine.

As soon as we reached the safety of the tiny upstairs bedroom, I collapsed onto the edge of the queen-sized bed, burying my face in my hands. The system crash was imminent.

Ashley didn't say a word. She didn't try to give me a pep talk about how I would get the next one. She just dropped her heavy canvas bag, sat down right beside me, and wrapped her arms completely around my torso. She pulled me flush against her side, applying a massive, deep-pressure hug.

It was the ultimate physical countermeasure. The heavy, unyielding squeeze grounded my frantic nervous system, acting as a manual override for the panic.

While she held me together with her right arm, she reached into her bag with her left hand and pulled out a thick spiral notebook.

She opened it to a meticulously maintained, hand-drawn spreadsheet. It listed every single place we had walked to, every CVTA route we had taken, the transfer times, the name of the manager I had spoken to, and the exact reason for the rejection.

It was her Sentinel logbook.

Ashley knew exactly how Greg’s standard hardware brain worked. She knew that when he got home from work, he was going to look at me—sitting in his condo without a job—and assume I just wasn't trying. Because the autistic effort is invisible to the outside world, she was translating my physical and mental agony into hard, undeniable data.

"When he asks," Ashley promised, resting her chin on my shoulder and tapping the notebook, "I am going to show him the math. I am going to prove exactly how hard you are fighting."

I leaned my heavy head against hers, closing my eyes. I didn't have a job, and the physical Base Camp was gone, but as long as the Sentinel was holding the logbook, I wasn't completely defenseless.

### Part 5: The Logistical Paradox – 5:30 PM

The suffocating Virginia heat was still radiating off the asphalt when the front door of Unit 2E clicked open at five-thirty.

I was leaning heavily against the tiny kitchen island, desperately drinking a glass of ice water, when Mom and Greg walked in. My physical chassis was completely failing. Hours of riding heavy CVTA transit buses, combined with the uneven walking and the constant, jarring vibrations of the road, had sent the Cerebral Palsy spasticity in my legs into overdrive. I wasn't just standing stiffly; my right knee was locked, and I was gripping the edge of the granite counter just to keep myself upright.

Greg set his briefcase down, his practical eyes immediately zeroing in on my rigid posture and the sweat soaking my shirt.

"Did you get any traction today?" Greg asked, loosening his tie as he stepped into the galley kitchen. "Or did you spend the whole afternoon riding the bus again?"

My jaw tightened. I desperately wanted to explain the sheer volume of parameters I had tried to navigate. I wanted to explain the physical agony of the transit grid. But my processor was redlining from the pain and the executive exhaustion. The Gray Area Freeze deployed, completely locking my vocal cords.

"He is hitting the targets, Mr. Lawson," Ashley stated smoothly, stepping out from the narrow hallway.

She walked over to the island and slapped her thick spiral notebook down onto the granite counter. She flipped it open to her meticulously hand-drawn spreadsheet, displaying the dates, the transit routes, and the store names.

"He hit the Admiral Center, the Little Creek corridor, and Glenrock Mall today," Ashley explained, operating in full Sentinel mode to intercept the neurotypical interrogation. "But the transit routes are incredibly taxing on his hardware, and when he finally gets in front of a manager, they visually disqualify him because of his legs. They aren't going to hire a cashier who can't stand for an eight-hour shift."

Greg leaned over the island, looking down at the columns of ink. He didn't look sympathetic; he looked mildly exasperated by the inefficiency.

"This is exactly why you shouldn't be taking the bus," Greg sighed, shaking his head at the spreadsheet. "It's a waste of the whole day. If you want to get hired, you need to be efficient. You need to drive to these interviews so you're fresh when you walk in the door."

I stared at him, my chest heaving, my brain completely short-circuiting at the blatant flaw in his math.

"He can't drive, Greg," Ashley pointed out, her tone tight but fiercely controlled. "Ellen has the sedan at work all day. There is no vehicle for him to use. The bus is the only option."

"Which is exactly why securing a job is the priority," Greg countered smoothly, completely missing the impossible paradox he had just constructed. He looked at me, delivering the parameters with absolute, neurotypical authority. "You get a job. You save up your paychecks. And the very first thing you do is buy a used car. Then you don't have to worry about the bus system ruining your legs."

He had just built a flawless, unassailable circle of flawed logic. I needed a car to get a job, but I needed a job to buy a car. I was trapped in an impossible loop, and the adult in the room refused to acknowledge the missing variable.

Greg shifted his attention, looking directly at Ashley.

"And speaking of cars," Greg continued, "why aren't your name and routes in this notebook, Ashley? If you two are going out every day, you should be dropping off applications just as fast as he is. You need to be saving for a car, too."

Ashley blinked, her own processor stalling for a fraction of a second at the sheer absurdity of the question.

"Mr. Lawson, it is the middle of July," Ashley said, stating the timeline as a rigid, undeniable fact. "I leave for Shenandoah Valley University at the end of August. No retail manager is going to hire someone who has to quit in exactly five weeks. It makes me entirely unhirable."

Greg waved his hand dismissively, brushing past the logistical reality as if it were just an excuse.

"That's a quitter's mindset," Greg lectured, leaning back against the fridge. "You don't tell them you're leaving in five weeks. You just get the job, put the money in the bank, and put your two weeks' notice in when it's time to pack for college. It's called playing the game."

The cramped condo went dead silent.

Mom looked down at the floor, offering absolutely no backup, quietly complicit in the standard hardware script. Greg looked at both of us, completely satisfied that he had solved our logistical problems with a motivational speech.

"Get some rest tonight," Greg instructed, picking up his briefcase. "Tomorrow is a new day. Hit the pavement again, and let's see some actual results."

He walked up the stairs, leaving the heavy, suffocating weight of his expectations hanging in the galley kitchen.

I stood there gripping the granite counter, the spasticity in my leg vibrating painfully against the cabinets. The rules of the adult world weren't just confusing; they were rigged. They demanded results based on tools we didn't possess and timelines that didn't make sense.

I looked at Ashley. She slowly pulled the spiral notebook off the counter and hugged it to her chest, her jaw tight with silent fury.

The physical Base Camp was gone. The Sentinel could log the data, and she could shield me from the worst of the sensory noise, but as I limped away from the kitchen and dragged myself toward the stairs, the terrifying truth of the Baldwin Ave condo settled over me.

There was no sanctuary here. And in five weeks, I would be facing this completely alone.

### Part 6: The Extraction Plan – 7:30 PM

The suffocating tension from the kitchen had followed us upstairs.

Ashley and I were sitting cross-legged on the queen-sized bed, the door firmly shut against the rest of the condo. We hadn't spoken since we came upstairs. I was staring blankly at the wall, my processor locked in a dark, spinning loop of Greg’s impossible logistical paradox, while my right leg continued to twitch with residual CP spasticity.
