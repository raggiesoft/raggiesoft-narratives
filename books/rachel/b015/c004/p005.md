I drove the motorized cart to the front of Franklin's Market, navigating the bustling weekend crowds with Rachel pressed firmly against the left side of the chassis. Her hand was locked onto the armrest, her shoulder brushing against mine with every slight turn of the tiller. She was absolutely refusing to yield her perimeter.

As I parked the cart near the customer service desk to return the key, my eyes caught a vibrant splash of color on a tall, spinning acrylic display rack near the sliding exit doors. It was a local tourism kiosk packed with pamphlets for Seattle boat tours and Mt. Rainier hiking trails.

But one specific, glossy tri-fold brochure stood out from the rest.

I reached out, pulling it from the acrylic slot. The cover featured a breathtaking, high-definition photograph of a massive, 1890s timber-framed lodge sitting against a jagged, snow-capped granite peak.

**The Prospector’s Keep** *A Sovereign Legacy on Ruby Mountain*

I opened the pamphlet, my analytical processor instantly engaged by the architecture. The text described a sprawling, commercial alpine resort hidden deep in the North Cascades. It boasted massive river-stone fireplaces, old-growth timber beams, and a rich history tied directly to the Klondike Gold Rush of 1897. According to the glossy print, the 7,000-foot alpine fortress was proudly owned and operated by the exact same family bloodline.

It looked like an absolute, impenetrable sanctuary.

"What did you find?" Jessica asked, walking up behind us as Kristin loaded the heavy shopping bags into her backpack.

"Just looking," I murmured, handing the brochure to my older sister. "It looks incredible. But the back panel says the resort officially closes to the public on November 1st."

"It has to," Jessica explained, her logistical brain instantly recognizing the geography. "It sits at the top of a five-mile private road just off State Route 20. They usually throw a massive Halloween Gala for their final guests, and then WSDOT drops the heavy steel barricades across the highway to block off the avalanche zones. Whoever stays up on that mountain is completely cut off from the planet for seven months."

She looked at the photograph of the massive timber lodge, then looked down at me, her dark eyes softening with a fierce, protective warmth.

"But the highway reopens in May," Jessica noted, tapping the glossy paper against her hand. She slipped the brochure into her messenger bag. "I think a summer trip is exactly what this pack needs. Once you are fully rested, and this entire nightmare is just a speck in the rearview mirror, we are going to book a suite up there. A real getaway. Just the four of us, thousands of feet above the rest of the world."

Rachel tightened her grip on my shoulder, resting her chin near my collarbone. "I love that idea," she rasped.

"It's a promise," Jessica declared, checking her watch. "Now, let's get you back to your new command center so you can load those games."

We stepped through the sliding doors, the damp, freezing Renton air biting at our cheeks as the first light flurries of snow began to stick to the grass along the sidewalks. Because the pavement was still clear, Jessica’s wheelchair rolled smoothly as we marched back toward Rainier Avenue.

The transit math was just as flawless on the return trip.

We boarded the massive, articulated TAPS Route 106, riding it straight back into the Renton Transit Center. The transfer was immediate. We crossed the concrete bay, boarded the local Route 105, and rode it the short distance back down Edmonds Avenue.

Through the entire transit loop, Rachel never let go. She sat pressed against my side on the bus seats, her knee locked against mine, her hand gripping my bicep. She was my physical shield against the civilian world, and the deep, continuous pressure kept my nervous system entirely grounded.

When we finally crossed the street and the deadbolt clicked shut on Jessica’s ground-floor apartment, the cold, unpredictable outside world was officially severed.

We shed our heavy winter coats. Kristin immediately carried the bags of my new clothes to the laundry room to wash the Franklin's Market sizing starch out of them, officially preparing my new armor for wear.

I didn't wait. I walked straight into Jessica's bedroom and sat down at the small desk in the corner.

The piano-black chassis of the 17-inch rig was waiting. I nudged the weighted optical mouse, and the massive, high-definition widescreen flared to life, the translucent glass borders of *Quantum OS Clarity* glowing brilliantly.

I reached into the shopping bag and pulled out the heavy plastic cases for *The Denizens 2* franchise.

For the last two years, my digital life had been violently policed. Delores had dictated when I was allowed to use the computer, what I was allowed to look at, and how much time I was permitted to spend playing games. If I ever bought software for myself, it was treated as an unforgivable financial crime.

But as I sat there, there was no screaming from the hallway. There was no looming threat.

Rachel dragged a chair over, planting it right next to mine. She wrapped her arm around my waist, resting her head heavily on my shoulder to watch the screen.

"Which expansion pack are you loading first?" Rachel asked softly.

"There is a highly specific order," I explained, clicking open my web browser. My analytical processor demanded absolute structural perfection. If you installed heavy simulation expansions out of order, the registry files could tangle and cause the digital architecture to crash.

I quickly searched the internet for the official software release dates. Once the page loaded, I methodically stacked the heavy plastic cases on the desk in exact chronological order: *The Denizens 2* base game on the bottom, followed by *College Days*, then *City Nights*, *Corporate Boss*, and finally the brand-new *Furry Friends* sitting on top.

"All of them," I smiled, looking at the perfectly organized tower of software. "In exact release order."

I pressed the tactile eject button on the Blu-Ray drive and snapped the base installation disc onto the spindle. The quiet, rhythmic *clack-whir* of the optical drive spun up, and the blue progress bar appeared on the screen.

I was going to install every single one of them. I was going to build massive, intricate digital houses, engineer complex neighborhoods, and play until my eyes burned. And nobody was going to tell me to turn it off.

The world belonged entirely to me again.
