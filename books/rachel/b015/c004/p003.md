The TAPS Route 106 dropped us off right on the exact same side of the street as the sprawling, massive Franklin’s Market. The massive commercial sector of Rainier Avenue South was bustling with weekend traffic, the damp pavement reflecting the gray afternoon sky.

But as the heavy articulated bus pulled away, Jessica didn't steer her wheelchair toward the grocery superstore.

Instead, she banked left, navigating down the sidewalk toward a very familiar, bright red-and-white fast-food sign glowing near the edge of the massive parking lot. Sitting atop the tall metal pole was the slightly absurd, School Bus Yellow plastic head of the *Jumping Jester*.

"Change of plans," Jessica smiled, pushing the heavy glass door of the restaurant open. "We are doing lunch before we tackle the clothing aisles. You need a hot meal that you didn't have to cook, and you definitely deserve some junk food after the last forty-eight hours."

We walked inside. The warm air smelled heavily of salt, frying oil, and grilled meat—a stark, comforting contrast to the cold November air outside.

I stopped a few feet away from the counter, my eyes scanning up to the glowing, backlit menu board above the registers. Suddenly, my brain completely glitched on the paradox of choice.

Under Delores's regime, my autonomy didn't exist. I was never allowed to pick my own food. Meals were strictly, coercively dictated by whatever was cheapest, whatever Aunt Lynne threw together, or whatever Delores explicitly demanded. Fast food was considered an absolute waste of money—a punishable offense—unless it was specifically purchased to satisfy her cravings. If I had ever dared to ask for something I actually wanted, it would have triggered a screaming, humiliating meltdown.

I stood there, frozen, staring at the brightly colored pictures of double cheeseburgers, tacos, and seasoned curly fries. My shoulders tensed instinctively, my trauma-wired brain waiting for the inevitable reprimand.

"Jess... what am I allowed to get?" I asked, my voice dropping into a quiet, conditioned whisper.

Jessica parked her wheelchair right next to me. She looked up, her expression softening with profound, heartbreaking understanding. She didn't pity me; she just recognized the invisible chains I was still fighting.

"Michael," Jessica said, her voice steady, pragmatic, and absolute. "I am paying for everyone, and I am handing you a blank check. I want you to look at that board, and I want you to pick *anything* you want. Get the biggest burger they have. Get two. Get a mountain of curly fries. Get a milkshake. You get exactly what you want, and nobody is going to say a single word about it."

Rachel squeezed my left arm, pulling herself a fraction of an inch closer. "Pick whatever you want, Bear," she rasped gently, her dark eyes fiercely protective. "Nobody is timing you. Nobody is judging you."

I swallowed hard, the magnitude of the moment hitting my chest like a physical weight. The permission wasn't just about the food; it was about reclaiming the space I was allowed to occupy in the world.

I took a deep breath, stepping up to the register. Rachel moved with me in perfect lockstep, her arm still threaded tightly through mine.

For the first time in two agonizing years, I looked a cashier in the eye, and I used my own voice to dictate my own existence. I ordered a massive, double-patty burger with bacon and cheese, a large order of seasoned curly fries, two tacos, and an extra-large cold soda.

We took a booth in the quiet corner of the restaurant. Rachel slid into the vinyl seat first, pulling me in right next to her. She pressed her knee and shoulder firmly against mine so the physical contact was never broken. Kristin and Jessica took the opposite side of the table.

When they called my order number, Kristin kindly hopped up to grab the red plastic trays, saving Rachel and me from having to unspool our perimeter.

Kristin set the tray down in front of me. The food was piping hot. I unwrapped the foil from the massive burger, picked up a perfectly seasoned, glistening curly fry, and took a bite.

The rush of salt, grease, and warmth was absolutely euphoric. It grounded my scrambled processor instantly.

I looked around the table. Jessica was calmly eating her own lunch, sipping her drink and reviewing the transit schedule on her phone to plan our trip back. Kristin was laughing at a joke, happily eating a taco. And Rachel was sitting right by my side, eating her fries with her left hand while her right hand rested securely on my knee. Her dark eyes were completely relaxed, entirely free of the terror that had haunted her in Kent.

It was just a Jumping Jester. The lighting was fluorescent, and the tables were cheap plastic. But as I took a massive bite of my burger, savoring the absolute freedom of a hot meal that came with zero strings attached, zero coercive threats, and zero guilt, it tasted like the greatest victory of my entire life.

The hell was over. My choices belonged to me again.

When the last curly fry was gone and the wrappers were crumpled onto the tray, I leaned back against the vinyl booth, my system fortified and my chest feeling lighter than it had in years.

"Ready?" Jessica asked, tossing her napkin onto the tray and unlocking the brakes on her wheelchair.

I looked down at the old, ill-fitting Quantum t-shirt and the oversized sweatpants I was still wearing. They were clean, but they were borrowed armor. It was time to forge my own.

"Yeah," I nodded, looking out the restaurant window toward the massive retail superstore across the parking lot. "Let's go to Franklin's. Let's burn the last of Kent to the ground."
