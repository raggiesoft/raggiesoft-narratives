The digital clock in the corner of the 17-inch widescreen shifted to 6:00 PM.

Outside the apartment window, the Renton sky had already surrendered to the pitch-black, freezing night. Inside the warm bedroom, my physical processor was beginning to heavily lag. My internal clock was still violently tethered to the East Coast. To my exhausted body, it was already nine o'clock at night. Combine the three-hour time zone displacement with the agonizing, sleep-deprived torture I had endured Thursday night in Kent, and my eyes were practically burning.

But I didn't want to sleep yet. I was too completely engrossed in the absolute safety of my new digital sanctuary.

Rachel hadn't shifted her weight. She was still sitting on the dining chair right beside me, her left arm locked like a vice around my waist, her right hand resting heavily on my knee. Her chin was resting on my shoulder, her breathing slow and rhythmic as she watched the screen.

With Bug successfully saved to the neighborhood bin, I clicked the 'Create New' button again.

"Who's next?" Rachel asked softly, turning her head just enough to press a kiss against my shoulder.

"The architect," I replied, grabbing the mouse.

I selected the female framework again, adjusting the sliders to build an older sister. I gave her dark hair, sharp, intelligent eyes, and wire-rimmed prescription glasses. I navigated to the clothing tabs, outfitting her in a sleek, professional blazer and dark slacks—the exact kind of corporate armor Jessica wore when she commanded the halls of Quantum's Building 35.

But as I clicked through the final accessory and body modification tabs, my hand slowed, and a heavy, sinking feeling hit my chest. I clicked through the menus a second time, searching desperately, but my analytical brain already knew the software's limitations.

It was 2006. The game didn't have wheelchairs.

I dropped my hand from the mouse, a sudden, frustrating wave of sadness washing over me. Jessica had literally orchestrated my salvation. She had spent thousands of dollars out of pocket to build this command center and buy my armor. She was the absolute foundation of my physical safety, and the game couldn't even render her reality.

"I can't build her right," I whispered, my voice thick with exhaustion and defeat. "There's no chair. It feels wrong."

Rachel immediately picked up on the sudden drop in my emotional baseline. She squeezed my waist fiercely, pulling herself flush against my side to provide maximum grounding pressure.

"It's okay, Bear," Rachel promised, her raspy voice steady and reassuring. "She's standing up in the game, but it's still her. Look at the glasses. Look at the blazer. She's commanding the digital world just like she commands the real one. Jess will love it."

I took a slow breath, absorbing the heavy pressure of my Bug's grip. She was right. I typed *Jessica* into the naming box and clicked save, immortalizing our big sister in the neighborhood bin right next to Rachel.

I clicked 'Create New' one more time.

I needed to finish the perimeter. I adjusted the facial sliders, giving this new avatar a fierce, unyielding posture. I selected a long, blonde hairstyle and outfitted her in practical, comfortable clothes. As I spun the digital avatar around to check the parameters, I felt Rachel suddenly stiffen against my side.

"I know her," Rachel gasped, her eyes going wide behind her glasses. A massive, nostalgic smile broke across her face. "Is that... is that The Sentinel?"

"Yeah," I nodded, a bittersweet ache blooming in my chest. "It's Ashley."

Rachel squeezed my arm, a rush of fond memories flooding her voice. "Oh my god, Ashley. I remember all the sleepovers we used to have. She was always there. She was exactly like you—just this massive, protective wall. If you weren't standing right next to me, she was." Rachel paused, her smile faltering slightly as the reality of the present timeline caught up with her. "Whatever happened to her, Michael?"

I stared at the glowing monitor, the cursor blinking steadily in the name box. The absolute, toxic reality of the last two years reared its ugly head in the quiet room.

"Delores happened," I whispered, the shame burning hot in my throat.

Delores's primary weapon had been isolation. She couldn't assert total dominance over me if I had an external support system. Piece by piece, month by month, she had methodically forced me to burn my bridges. She had screamed, threatened, and manipulated me until I stopped calling my friends. She had forced me to delete numbers, throw away addresses, and cut ties with anyone who might recognize the abuse.

"I don't have her contact information anymore," I confessed, my voice breaking. "I don't have her email. I don't have her phone number. Delores made sure I lost all of it. My childhood best friend... and she's just gone."

Rachel didn't offer empty platitudes. She didn't tell me it was going to be okay, because the loss of The Sentinel was a genuine tragedy. Instead, she just tightened her unyielding grip around my torso, burying her face into the side of my neck.

"She's not gone," Rachel rasped fiercely against my collarbone. "She's right there on the screen. You built her. She's in the pack."

I typed *Ashley* into the box and hit save. The Sentinel joined the digital sanctuary. The perimeter was whole.

From down the hallway, the quiet, comforting sounds of domestic normalcy drifted into the bedroom. Kristin and Jessica were working together in the accessible kitchen. I could hear the clatter of plates, the hum of the microwave, and the quiet, easy banter between the Senior TPM and the retail worker.

"Dinner!" Kristin called out cheerfully from the living room.

Rachel finally unhooked her arm from my waist, but only so she could stand up. The second she was on her feet, she grabbed my hand, pulling me up from the desk. She instantly linked her arm back through mine, locking our shoulders together as we walked out to the living room.

Jessica had wheeled herself up to the coffee table, and Kristin was setting down a massive, steaming plate of comfort food.

It wasn't a complex, gourmet meal. It was simple, low-pressure perfection: hot dogs loaded into soft buns, flanked by massive piles of salty, crispy potato chips.

Rachel and I sat down together on the couch. She pressed her leg firmly against mine, refusing to leave any gaps in our physical armor, and handed me a plate.

I took a bite of the hot dog, the simple, salty warmth grounding my exhausted processor. I looked across the table at Jessica and Kristin, and then down at my Bug, who was happily munching on a potato chip while still leaning her weight entirely against my shoulder.

I had lost a lot to Delores over the last two years. The phantom ache of losing Ashley's contact information still stung. But sitting in that warm apartment, eating a hot dog while perfectly shielded by the women who had risked everything to pull me out of hell, the math was absolute.

I was safe. And tomorrow, we could start figuring out how to rebuild the rest.
