The blue progress bar for the *Furry Friends* expansion finally hit one hundred percent, closing out nearly two hours of quiet, meditative disc-swapping. The final installation wizard closed, leaving the *Quantum OS Clarity* desktop perfectly clean.

I double-clicked the brand-new shortcut icon.

The heavy, dual-core processor chewed through the initialization sequence effortlessly. *The Denizens 2* launched, the high-definition graphics and crisp, cheerful soundtrack filling the quiet bedroom.

Rachel was still sitting on the dining chair she had dragged right next to mine. She hadn't moved an inch. Her separation anxiety was still completely redlining. The sheer terror of the previous forty-eight hours hadn't fully dissipated from her system, and she simply refused to unhook herself from my side. Her left arm was wrapped fiercely around my waist, her right hand rested on my knee, and her head was heavy against my shoulder.

I bypassed the pre-built neighborhoods entirely, clicking straight into the game's 'Create-A-Character' mode.

"What are you building first?" Rachel asked softly, her raspy voice vibrating against my collarbone as a blank, default adult avatar loaded onto the digital pedestal.

I didn't answer right away. I grabbed the weighted optical mouse, my hand moving with steady, deliberate precision. I wasn't going to build myself yet, and I certainly wasn't going to build anything that even remotely resembled Kent.

I selected the female framework. I navigated to the facial sliders, tweaking the jawline and the slope of the nose to match the fierce, determined profile resting against my shoulder. I selected a practical, shoulder-length brown hairstyle, adjusting the color until it was perfect. I scrolled through the accessories tab, adding a pair of sleek, wire-rimmed glasses.

Then, I moved to the clothing. I bypassed the generic dresses and formal wear, clicking through the casual tabs until I found exactly what I was looking for. I dressed the digital avatar in a bright, solid red t-shirt, mirroring the exact color of the employee uniform she wore every day at The Spot.

I spun the avatar around on the screen, verifying the parameters. She was perfectly proportioned, standing tall and fiercely independent.

I clicked the final confirmation box. The text cursor blinked, asking for the character's first name.

I placed my hands on the keyboard and typed: *Bug*.

Rachel gasped softly. The grip she had around my waist tightened significantly, pulling me completely flush against her side, mirroring the exact, heavy physical pressure of the hugs we always shared.

I had been stripped of everything over the last two years. My dignity, my finances, my autonomy, and my perimeter. But right here, on a brand-new \$4,000 command center, the very first thing I chose to create—the very first digital life I brought into my newly secured, impenetrable universe—was her.

"You're my first citizen," I told her quietly, saving the file to the neighborhood bin.

Rachel didn't say a word. She didn't need to. She just buried her face deeper into the crook of my neck, letting out a long, shaky exhale, completely overwhelmed by the quiet, absolute proof of my loyalty. I felt the rigid, terrified tension in her shoulders finally begin to melt.

She understood exactly what the gesture meant. Her Bear was officially back online, and she was the absolute center of his world.
