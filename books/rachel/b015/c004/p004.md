We pushed through the heavy glass doors of the *Jumping Jester*, stepping back out into the bitter, damp chill of the Renton afternoon. The sky overhead was a flat, unbroken sheet of gray.

We had to cross the massive, sprawling parking lot to get to the entrance of Franklin's Market. As we walked, my eyes instinctively tracked southward down the busy arterial stretch of Rainier Avenue.

Suddenly, a cold, jagged spike of adrenaline pierced right through the warmth of the victory meal. My chest tightened, my breath catching in my throat.

My highly analytical brain had just connected the geographic dots.

I knew exactly where we were. While this specific Franklin's Market wasn't the absolute closest grocery store to Aunt Lynne's house, it was by far the easiest one to reach from her neighborhood. State Route 167 ran directly out of the heart of Kent and transitioned seamlessly into Rainier Avenue South. It was a straight, frictionless shot. Delores or her aunt could hop in their car, drive straight up the highway, and pull into this exact parking lot without making a single turn.

My eyes frantically scanned the rows of parked cars, my processor redlining as I searched for Aunt Lynne’s familiar vehicle. Every sudden noise, every screech of a shopping cart wheel, made my muscles coil with the absolute certainty that Delores was about to step out from behind a concrete pillar and start screaming.

Rachel felt the sudden, rigid shift in my posture. She didn't need me to explain what was happening. Her grip on my left arm tightened instantly, her hands locking like a vice around my bicep.

"Look at me, Bear," Rachel commanded softly, her raspy voice cutting through the ambient noise of the parking lot.

I forced my eyes away from the southbound traffic and looked down at her.

"She is not here," Rachel promised, her dark eyes burning with absolute, terrifying ferocity. "And even if she somehow was, she would have to go through me, Kristin, and Jessica to get to you. That is a war she will not win. You are heavily guarded. She cannot touch you."

"Okay," I swallowed hard, forcing my lungs to expand. "Okay. I'm sorry, I just—"

"Do not apologize for surviving," Jessica stated firmly, rolling up alongside us. "Your threat-detection system is just hyperactive right now. It kept you alive, but we are your perimeter today. Let us do the scanning. You just focus on moving."

But as the adrenaline from the parking lot panic began to recede, the physical toll of the last forty-eight hours finally caught up with me. The severe lack of sleep, the violent stress, and the sheer exertion of the escape caused a sudden, uncontrollable flare-up in my muscles. My legs felt like lead, and painful, spastic tremors began to shoot through my calves and thighs, making it incredibly difficult to stand upright.

We crossed the threshold into the bright, cavernous expanse of Franklin's Market, and Kristin immediately noticed my agonizing limp. She didn't say a word; she just power-walked over to the customer service desk and grabbed the key for one of the store's motorized shopping carts, driving it smoothly over to our perimeter.

I sank into the padded vinyl seat with a heavy sigh of relief, resting my hands on the steering tiller as my exhausted legs finally caught a break.

Rachel adjusted her logistics without missing a single beat. Because I was seated, she couldn't link arms with me anymore, so she simply stepped flush against the left side of the motorized cart. As I slowly drove us down the wide aisles, she kept one hand clamped firmly on the cart's armrest and her other hand resting heavily on my shoulder. She refused to let the machinery create even an inch of separation between us.

"Let's hit the apparel section first," Jessica instructed, rolling her own manual chair alongside my motorized one. "I want heavy denim. I want soft, warm flannels. I want a heavy winter coat that actually fits you. Kristin has her discount card ready, and my budget is open."

With Rachel physically anchored to my shoulder, I steered the cart through the clothing racks. For two years, I had worn whatever cheap, threadbare clothing Aunt Lynne or Delores permitted me to have. My clothes always smelled faintly of the squalor, the ammonia, and the suffocating control of that house.

Today, I was forging brand-new armor.

I reached out and pulled thick, dark jeans off the shelves. I found a stack of soft, heavy flannel button-downs that I knew would provide the deep, comforting sensory weight my processor craved. I grabbed a pack of fresh socks and clean t-shirts. Finally, I pulled a heavy, insulated winter jacket off the rack and dropped it all into the wire basket of the cart.

"Perfect," Jessica nodded, watching the basket fill up. "Now, let's swing by the electronics department. I promised you a game yesterday at Broil's, and we didn't have time to grab it before the bus."

I steered us to the back of the store. Protected by the pack, the ghost of Delores was finally fading from the fluorescent aisles.

I parked the cart in front of the PC software display, scanning the rows of plastic cases. I had been eyeing a specific simulation franchise for a long time. I reached out and grabbed the base game for *The Denizens 2*. Then, utilizing Jessica's completely open budget, I methodically grabbed every single expansion pack that had been released for it up to that point—*College Days*, *City Nights*, *Corporate Boss*, and the newly released *Furry Friends*—stacking the massive pile of life-simulation software into my basket.

Kristin wandered over to the DVD display stand nearby. "Movie night?" she suggested, pulling a brightly colored case off the shelf.

It was a combo pack of *House Guard* and *House Guard 2: Lost in Manhattan*. It was the absolute perfect choice for a cozy, secure evening—and a universally accepted fact among our family that they had only ever made two of those movies, and absolutely anyone claiming a third one existed was lying.

We made our way to the checkout lanes. Kristin expertly swiped her employee badge, bringing the massive total down significantly before Jessica covered the rest without blinking an eye.

The cashier bagged the clothes and the media, and Kristin loaded the heavy plastic bags onto the back of Jessica's wheelchair to keep my cart clear.

As I drove the motorized cart back to the front of the store, eventually standing up to return the key and step back out through the sliding doors toward the bus stop, I didn't look south toward SR-167 again. The psychological ghost of Kent had tried to ambush me in the parking lot, but the perimeter had held flawlessly.

I had a full stomach, a secure command center waiting back at base, my Bug holding fiercely onto my arm, and a bag full of clean armor. The monster wasn't coming for me today.
