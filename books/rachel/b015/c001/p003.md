We rolled out into the freezing dusk of the Kent Station drop-off zone. Aunt Lynne was waiting in a battered, incredibly cramped compact sedan at the curb, the exhaust pluming in the damp, heavy air.

She greeted us with a wide, tight-lipped smile that didn’t quite reach her eyes. "Oh, Jessica! So lovely to finally meet you," Lynne cooed, her voice dripping with a saccharine, performative sweetness. "Delores has told us just... *so* much about you and your sister."

The logistical nightmare began immediately.

The trunk space of the compact sedan was practically non-existent. The absolute only reason Jessica’s manual wheelchair fit inside at all was because it was a collapsible model, and even folded down, it was a brutal squeeze. Cramming into the back seat alongside Delores was a claustrophobic, tense affair. The drive to the house was suffocatingly quiet, the windows fogging up from our wet coats.

When Lynne finally pulled into the driveway, the hostile architecture of the environment asserted itself before we even reached the front door.

Leading up to the small porch was a steep, uneven set of three concrete steps. Delores and Lynne walked up them, completely ignoring the obstacle, leaving me and Jessica at the bottom.

"I've got it," I murmured, grabbing the rubber push-handles of her chair.

This was a baseline routine for us. I stepped firmly onto the small metal tilt-bar near the rear right wheel, leveraging my body weight to tip the wheelchair backward onto its rear tires. I smoothly popped the front casters up the first step, rolled the massive rear wheels up, and repeated the fluid, rocking motion twice more until we successfully cleared the porch.

I pushed her over the threshold into the house. The air inside was stale, laced with the sharp, acidic smell of cat urine and unwashed laundry. But Jessica’s eyes didn't linger on the squalor. They immediately locked onto the architecture directly in front of us.

Looming at the end of the short entryway was a narrow, incredibly steep, carpeted staircase leading up to the second floor.

Delores stopped at the bottom step, pressing a hand to her chest in a flawless, theatrical display of feigned innocence. "Oh, goodness!" she gasped softly, her eyes wide. "I completely forgot our room was upstairs. I am *so* sorry, Jess. My memory is just terrible lately."

Jessica looked at the stairs, and then looked at Delores.

The lie was entirely exposed. It was a massive, deliberate architectural trap. Delores, Lynne, and Brandi all wore the exact same mask of overly sweet, helpless concern, waiting to see how the brilliant tech executive would handle the humiliation of being physically barred from her own brother's sleeping quarters.

Jessica didn't yell. She didn't call them out on the pathetic theater. She just let out a slow, deeply exhausted sigh, her stoic armor locking firmly into place.

My chest seized with a nauseating panic.

Jessica wasn't paralyzed. She had full feeling and complete range of motion in her legs. But the catastrophic axial load injury from her fall in 1999 had completely pulverized the calcaneus and talus bones in her heels and ankles. Her feet were hollow, pinned-together remnants that structurally could not support the weight of gravity. If she attempted to stand or put pressure on the soles of her feet, the collapse would be absolute, resulting in blinding, excruciating pain.

Because the staircase was far too steep and narrow for me to safely carry her dead weight without risking a fall, the only way she was getting up there was by dragging herself up backward, tread by agonizing tread, using purely her triceps and shoulders.

Jessica stared at the steps, running the physical calculus.

"It's only six-thirty," Jessica stated flatly, her pragmatic brain instantly rejecting the trap. She locked eyes with Delores. "We haven't eaten dinner yet. I am not hauling my body weight up those treads just to crawl back down in an hour to eat. I will stay on the first floor until it is time to sleep."

Without waiting for permission or an invitation, Jessica grabbed her push-rims, spun her casters away from the staircase, and rolled herself directly into their living room.

She wasn't going to be hidden away upstairs. She was planting herself directly in the center of their territory.
