We gathered in the living room with Aunt Lynne and Brandi. The atmosphere was thick and intensely radioactive.

Jessica parked her wheelchair at the edge of the seating arrangement. She reached into her heavy messenger bag, deliberately pulling out her wallet to seize control of the room's tense dynamic.

"I know it's been a long day of travel," Jessica announced, her voice projecting a smooth, absolute authority. "So tonight, we need to discuss dinner. I would like to offer to treat. Yes, for all five of us."

Brandi, sitting on the sofa, offered a helpless, overly apologetic pout. "I'd just love to take you out, but that little car out there is all we have, and we all won’t fit."

"We could take the TAPS bus somewhere," Jessica suggested reasonably.

"Oh, I just couldn't bear making you ride the bus in this cold," Brandi deflected, layering her petulance under a thick veneer of hospitality. "Besides, the nearest stop is way out on the main road. It's much too far to walk in the dark, especially for... well, you know."

Brandi wasn't entirely wrong. The Kent suburbs were a sprawling, pedestrian-hostile nightmare of cul-de-sacs without sidewalks. Pushing a manual chair to the main arterial road in the freezing cold was an objective logistical hurdle. But Brandi was clearly using the lack of infrastructure as a convenient excuse to shut down the idea entirely and keep us isolated in the squalor of the house.

I felt a familiar, suffocating weight press down on my chest. We were stuck here.

I looked at Jessica, hoping she might pull some brilliant logistical solution out of thin air, but I could see the heavy toll of the day wearing on her. Between the grueling deployment in Canada, the long train ride, and the massive adrenaline spike of intercepting me at King Street, her usually flawless processor was lagging. She was running on fumes.

Jessica looked around the room, taking in the hostile geometry of Delores, Lynne, and Brandi. Whatever calculations she was running behind those tinted glasses, the conclusion was clear: forcing a confrontation tonight wasn't worth the energy she didn't have. She had to play diplomacy. She had to bide her time.

Jessica’s eyes narrowed slightly, accepting the tactical compromise. "How about I order a delivery pizza, then?"

Aunt Lynne clasped her hands together. "Oh, we couldn't possibly ask you to do that, sweetheart. But... if you insist!"

The pizza arrived forty minutes later, and the evening passed in a tense, incredibly awkward haze. I felt completely trapped between the chaotic, passive-aggressive energy of Delores's family and the sharp, observant silence of my older sister.

As we ate, Delores and Brandi took their positions on either side of me on the small, battered sofa, completely boxing me in. It wasn't a casual seating arrangement; it was a coordinated, tactical blockade.

Delores reached over, wrapping both of her arms tightly around my neck and pressing her face heavily against my cheek. Brandi immediately mirrored the movement on my other side, leaning her entire body weight against my opposite shoulder and hooking her arm firmly through mine.

"We just love having him here," Delores cooed, looking directly at Jessica over my shoulder with a sickly sweet, victorious smile.

My chest instantly seized. My breathing hitched, trapped under the weight of the two women.

To a neurotypical observer, it looked like a smothering, overly affectionate embrace. But my analytical processor recognized exactly what was happening, and the realization made my blood run cold. It was a stolen algorithm.

During the infamous Thanksgiving of 2004—the same holiday where Delores had paraded around naked to assert her dominance—she had watched my sisters interact with me. She had witnessed the deep-pressure grounding hugs Rachel and Jessica routinely deployed to manually reset my nervous system. Delores had realized that in my pack, that specific, heavy physical compression was the ultimate, sacred currency of absolute safety and trust.

So, she stole the geometry of it.

But Delores completely lacked the empathy to understand the physics. The hug she and Brandi were executing right now didn't provide calming, regulatory pressure; it was a suffocating, coercive cage. They were actively weaponizing my own coping mechanism to assert physical ownership over me, staging a grotesque, mocking pantomime of my family's affection specifically for Jessica’s benefit.

I felt my muscles lock into rigid, defensive granite. Every instinct in my body was screaming at me to shove them away and break the perimeter.

But my processor was heavily compromised by months of psychological conditioning. My mind knew the trap. If I rejected the embrace, the performative smiles would instantly vanish, replaced by venomous, screaming accusations that I was ungrateful or embarrassed of them. I would be brutally punished for it the absolute second my sister left the room.

So, I forced my heavy, exhausted arms to lift. I wrapped one arm around Delores and the other around Brandi, awkwardly returning the suffocating, corrupted hug. It felt completely sickening—a physical toll I had to pay just to keep the peace.

I looked over at Jessica.

My sister was watching from her wheelchair at the edge of the living room. She didn't take the bait. She didn't snap, and she didn't argue. But even behind her tinted glasses, I could see the rigid, calculating set of her jaw. I didn't know exactly what she was thinking, but I knew my sister. She was a systems engineer. She wasn't just eating her pizza; she was silently observing the room, watching the sheer terror bleed through my forced compliance, and cataloging every single toxic data point.
