The damp, biting November wind off the Puget Sound cut right through my jacket as Delores and I walked through the heavy doors of King Street Station. The terminal was bustling with the late-afternoon Friday commuter rush, a chaotic swirl of rolling luggage and damp umbrellas.

I kept my hands shoved deep into my pockets, my muscles tight with an anxious, vibrating energy. We had spent the entire day wandering around downtown Seattle, and the exhaustion was setting in.

I desperately wanted to call Rachel. Just hearing her voice would have grounded my redlining nervous system. But my analytical processor engaged, blocking the impulse with cold logistics. Rachel lived all the way down in Pierce County, and it was Friday afternoon. She was probably trapped in the middle of a retail shift at The Spot. Even if she could somehow leave early, the southbound lanes of Interstate 5 were guaranteed to be a parking lot right now. Calling her just to say I was stressed and stuck at a train station would only make her worry when she was stuck in gridlock and geographically powerless to do anything about it.

I needed someone who could actually fix the transit math.

I pulled my silver flip phone from my pocket, my thumb hovering over the keypad. I hadn't told my older sister we were coming to Washington. For all I knew, Jessica was sitting at her desk on the Quantum campus in Redmond or at her apartment somewhere in the Puget Sound area.

But Delores was freezing, miserable, and absolutely furious about the missed train. My own processor was too exhausted from the three-day bus ride to calculate a backup route to Kent that wouldn't just make her angrier. I needed a project manager. If Jessica stepped in, she could take over the logistics, override the friction, and get us moving efficiently. Once we were sitting on a warm train, Delores would finally be able to relax and be happy again.

I hit dial and held the phone to my ear.

"Michael?" Jessica's voice answered. She sounded completely caught off guard. "Is everything okay?"

"Everything is fine," I said, trying to keep my voice steady. "I know this is totally unexpected, but Delores and I are actually in Seattle right now. We're at King Street Station, waiting for the commuter train down to Kent."

There was a sharp, heavy pause on the line. Even through the terrible cellular connection, I could practically hear the gears turning in her head—her internal threat matrix instantly lighting up.

"You are at King Street Station?" Jessica repeated. Her tone dropped entirely, shifting instantly into that familiar, unyielding, pragmatic authority. "Right now?"

"Yeah. Waiting on the TAPS Southbound."

"Michael, listen to me," she said, the rhythmic, heavy background noise of steel wheels rolling over tracks suddenly audible through the speaker. "I am on the ARTS train coming back from a deployment in Vancouver. We are passing through Edmonds right now. I will be rolling into King Street Station in less than thirty minutes. Stay exactly where you are. Do not get on a train or a bus without me."

The line clicked dead. A massive wave of relief hit my chest, immediately followed by a sickening spike of dread.

"Who was that?" Delores demanded, stepping into my line of sight. Her eyes were already narrowed, scanning my face for any sign of defiance.

"That... that was Jessica," I stammered, putting the phone back in my pocket. "She’s actually on a train from Canada. She's arriving here in twenty-five minutes, and she’s going to ride down to Kent with us."

Delores’s jaw locked so hard I could see the muscles pulse beneath her skin. "Absolutely not. We are not waiting around in this freezing station for her. We are getting on the next train."

"We can't," I said, my voice shaking as I pointed up at the massive digital departure board. "The TAPS Southbound trains only run on a strict commuter schedule. We literally missed the last one by five minutes. The next one doesn't depart for another thirty-five minutes."

Delores glared up at the glowing amber letters on the board, realizing I was telling the truth. The regional transit timetable had effectively trapped her. She let out a sharp, furious huff of air, crossing her arms tightly over her chest. "Fine. But I am not dealing with her bossing me around all night."

We stood in suffocating silence for twenty long minutes. Finally, the heavy silver and green ARTS train hissed to a halt on the main platform. The mechanical lift lowered, and Jessica rolled out into the freezing Seattle air.

I stepped forward, and she immediately reached up, wrapping her arms around my neck and hugging me tightly. The moment she squeezed my shoulders, a sudden, heavy lump formed in my throat. I hadn't seen her since Christmas of 2004, and the grounding pressure of her hold was something my nervous system had been starved of.

When she finally pulled back, her sharp, observant eyes shifted to Delores standing a few feet away. Delores didn't say a word, her posture radiating pure hostility. I saw the muscles in Jessica's jaw physically tighten. She offered a polite, incredibly strained smile, but she let out a quiet, internal groan. She hated that I was still with Delores, and she didn't try very hard to hide it.

Jessica looked down at her watch, instantly pivoting to logistics. "We’ll have time to talk on the train. We’re going to Kent, right?"

"That’s right," Delores snapped, her tone dripping with ice. "My aunt lives in Kent. She'll pick us up from the station."

"Follow me, then," Jess said, grabbing her push-rims and showing us the way toward the local commuter platforms. "The reason for the rush is because I want to get good seats. The TAPS Southbound train is incredibly popular on a Friday afternoon. It’s kind of confusing getting to the platform: you’ll need to take me up the elevator and then down the other side, since they really don't want people crossing the active tracks."

I took the rubber handles of her wheelchair, navigating her into the glass elevator while Delores followed closely behind.

When we reached the row of heavy metal Ticket Vending Machines on the TAPS platform, Delores aggressively stepped up to the kiosk, stabbing the physical buttons to purchase her own paper ticket for the Southbound line.

Before I could reach for my wallet to buy mine, Jessica waved me off. She reached into her heavy messenger bag, pulled out her debit card, and fed it into the machine, buying my paper ticket and handing it to me. One less thing for Delores to hold over my head.

"What about yours?" I asked.

Jessica reached back into her bag and pulled out her corporate ID lanyard. She tapped the glossy blue plastic card. "My Quantum Blue Badge counts as valid fare on any transit agency in the region," she explained smoothly. "I just have to flash it to the conductor."

The heavy, bi-level TAPS Commuter train rolled into the station. I pushed Jessica up the ramp and into the designated accessible seating area on the lower level.

As she locked her brakes into the floor restraints, she looked up at the passing conductor, holding up her Blue Badge. "Wheelchair getting off at Kent," she stated clearly.

Once we were settled and the train lurched forward, the rhythmic rattling of the tracks provided a steady background noise.

"So, what brought you to Canada?" I asked, leaning forward in my seat. "Seems odd to be traveling right before the holidays."

"I was up there on business, actually," Jessica explained, adjusting her coat. "Quantum is working on a major enterprise deployment with a partner up there, and my team was managing the security architecture. Now, it is time to focus on the holidays."

Jessica shifted her gaze to Delores. Her expression was entirely unreadable—a perfect, tactical mask. "Delores, you and I have had our bumps, but you are my brother’s girlfriend, and your family is inviting me into their home. You are on the first floor, right?"

Delores blinked, shifting slightly in her seat. "Yes, first floor," she answered.

I stared out the window as the gray Washington landscape blurred past. I knew Delores was lying. Aunt Lynne's house was two stories, and the room Delores and I were sharing for the trip was at the top of a steep, carpeted staircase. I didn't correct her, my stomach twisting into an anxious knot. I hated the friction, and I hated the confrontation that was inevitably coming.

“The train is approaching Kent Station… Kent Station… Look around for personal belongings and watch your head on the luggage rack,” the automated voice echoed through the carriage as the heavy air brakes engaged.

"This is us," Jessica said, unlocking her wheels. She caught the attention of the conductor as he walked past. "Wheelchair getting off here in Kent."
