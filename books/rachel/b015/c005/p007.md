With the massive room service feast completely cleared and the rolling cart pushed safely out into the hallway, a heavy, deeply satisfied calm settled over our third-floor fortress.

Jessica wheeled herself over to the small sitting area, flipping open her own sleek corporate laptop to review emails. Kristin stretched out on the far queen bed, flipping through the channels on the hotel television with the volume kept low.

I stood up from the mattress, my muscles delightfully heavy from the hydrotherapy and the massive influx of calories. I walked over to the large, polished mahogany desk sitting opposite the beds.

It was time to fully deploy the command center.

I reached into my padded bag and grabbed the 17-inch piano-black rig. After a long day of transit and the physical toll my cerebral palsy-affected muscles had taken from carrying Jessica, lifting the massive "desktop replacement" laptop felt like hoisting a cinder block. It weighed nearly ten pounds, a sprawling slab of premium 2006 hardware. I carefully set it down on the desk, flipping the heavy screen open.

Unlike the cramped, fold-out tray on the ARTS *Cascades* train, this hotel desk was sprawling. I had massive amounts of clearance. I plugged in the weighted optical mouse, the red laser glowing sharply against the wood grain.

I booted the system, the *Quantum OS Clarity* desktop flaring to life. The *Denizens 2* disc was already sitting in the optical drive. I double-clicked the icon, passed the SecuROM check, and let the high-definition simulation load.

The second the cheerful, quirky loading music drifted from the laptop's premium speakers, I heard the scrape of wooden legs against the carpet.

Rachel had dragged one of the heavy, upholstered hotel chairs right next to mine. She sat down, instantly hooking her left arm securely around my waist. She leaned her entire body weight flush against my right side and rested her chin heavily on my shoulder. Her separation anxiety was no longer driving her into a frantic panic, but the physical tether was her absolute safe zone. She needed to be locked in, watching her Bear construct his world.

"Room to breathe?" Rachel rasped softly, noting the wide expanse of the hotel desk.

"Plenty of room," I smiled, wrapping my right arm around her back and giving her a tight, one-armed squeeze before returning my hand to the mouse.

The digital neighborhood loaded. I clicked on the family bin, pulling up the household I had started building back in Renton. The avatars of Bug, Jessica, and Ashley were waiting in the queue.

But my analytical processor wasn't satisfied with my first pass.

"What are you doing?" Rachel asked as I bypassed the 'Move In' button and instead pressed a highly specific combination of keys on the keyboard.

A small, translucent gray command console dropped down from the top edge of the screen.

"I need to refine the architecture," I explained, my fingers flying across the keys. I typed in the developer-level cheat string: *bool_prop edit_citizen true*. I hit enter, and the console vanished.

Holding down the shift key, I clicked directly on the digital version of Bug. A hidden pie-menu popped up, filled with raw data strings and debug commands. I selected *Force Create-A-Character*.

The game paused, the background dissolving back into the familiar, blue-lit pedestal room. The digital Bug avatar appeared, ready to be edited.

"You're tweaking her?" Rachel asked, her arm tightening around my ribs in a heavy, affectionate hug.

"I have to get it right," I said, my eyes completely locked onto the screen.

With the spatial freedom of the mouse, I was able to navigate the complex sliders with total precision. I zoomed in on the avatar's face. I adjusted the bridge of the wire-rimmed glasses, ensuring they sat perfectly. I tweaked the jawline just a fraction of a millimeter, making it slightly sharper, perfectly capturing the fierce, unyielding determination my sister had shown sitting in the diner that morning.

Then, I navigated to the clothing tabs. I found the exact, signature bright red t-shirt that mirrored her uniform from The Spot. I adjusted the texture, ensuring the digital fabric looked crisp and authentic.

"She looks exactly like me," Rachel whispered, a profound warmth radiating from her voice as she watched the digital clone spin on the pedestal.

I didn't just hit save. I moved my left hand to the keyboard and rested my finger over the 'C' key.

I rotated the avatar into a strong, confident pose and pressed the key.

*Click-whir.* The game's simulated camera shutter sound echoed through the speakers, capturing a high-resolution screenshot of the Bug and saving it directly to the hard drive's directory. I zoomed in on her face and took another. *Click.* I zoomed out for a full-body shot. *Click.* "What are the pictures for?" Rachel asked, resting her cheek against my collarbone.

"The website," I explained, minimizing the game for a second to bring up the *Vertex Creative Studio* HTML file I had started coding on the train. I pointed to a blank image tag sitting in the code. "I can't just write the lore. I need visual documentation. I'm going to pull the screenshots from the game's directory, crop them in the photo editor, and embed them directly into the story."

Rachel looked at the raw HTML code, and then back at the game. She understood the depth of the project instantly. I wasn't just playing a video game; I was actively, meticulously documenting our survival and rebuilding our legacy in a digital format where Delores could never touch it.

Rachel let out a long, happy sigh, burying her face into my neck and giving me another heavy, crushing hug.

"Build the Bear next," she demanded quietly, refusing to let go.

"You got it," I promised, maximizing the game window and preparing to drop myself into the sanctuary.
