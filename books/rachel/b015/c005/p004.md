The heavy, rhythmic hum of the steel wheels began to slow. The ARTS *Cascades* train eased off its cruising speed, banking smoothly through the industrial outskirts of the city until the sprawling rail yard of Portland Union Station came into view.

I looked out the reinforced window of the Business Class car. Towering above the historic brick clocktower, cutting through the overcast, damp Pacific Northwest sky, was the massive, glowing neon sign: *GO BY TRAIN*.

We had made it.

"Alright, pack," Jessica announced, snapping her laptop shut and sliding it into her messenger bag. "Transit math is shifting. Let's mobilize."

The train hissed to a complete stop against the platform. Because Union Station was a fully operational, heavy-duty transit hub, the boarding process was much smoother than the temporary wooden planks in Tukwila. The conductor deployed the mobile mechanical lift, and Jessica navigated her wheelchair onto the metal plating with practiced precision.

Kristin stepped off next, followed immediately by Rachel and me.

We stood together on the concrete platform beneath the iconic neon sign. The air smelled of diesel exhaust, wet asphalt, and the faint, salty breeze of the nearby river. Rachel pressed her shoulder flush against my ribs, her hand gripping the thick denim of my jacket. Her separation anxiety was no longer blinding, but we were back in an unfamiliar civilian environment, which meant the physical tether was absolutely mandatory.

We moved as a single, indivisible unit through the grand, echoing marble corridors of Union Station, pushing through the heavy double doors and out into the bustling Portland afternoon.

Jessica didn't even have to look at a map. She banked her wheelchair right, leading us directly toward the local transit lines.

"We are transferring to CTS," Jessica instructed, referring to the Columbia Transit Service. "We need the MAX Light Rail."

We walked the short distance to the transit stop and boarded the sleek, electric CTS train. I guided Jessica’s wheelchair into the designated accessible zone, while Rachel immediately boxed me into the nearest window seat, her knee locking firmly against mine.

The light rail accelerated smoothly, carrying us right out over the dark, churning waters of the Willamette River via the massive, industrial Steel Bridge. I watched the city skyline reflect off the water. The psychological distance from the Green River Valley was immeasurable.

A few minutes later, the automated voice echoed through the train car. *"Next stop: Convention Center."*

We stepped off the CTS platform. Looming just a block away was the massive, glass-and-steel facade of the *Crown Regency*.

I pushed Jessica’s wheelchair through the heavy revolving doors and into the sprawling, luxurious hotel lobby. It was quiet, smelling of expensive floor wax and fresh linen.

"Wait here," Jessica directed, wheeling herself up to the polished mahogany reception desk.

I stood with Rachel and Kristin near a carpeted seating area. I glanced up at the digital clock mounted behind the concierge desk. It was 1:45 PM.

My analytical processor caught the logistical snag instantly. Standard corporate hotel check-in wasn't until 3:00 PM. If our room wasn't ready, we would be forced to sit in the exposed, public lobby for over an hour, leaving Rachel's perimeter highly vulnerable to the unpredictable flow of civilian foot traffic.

But Jessica was a Quantum Senior Technical Program Manager. She didn't leave variables to chance.

"Checking in," Jessica told the front desk clerk, sliding her corporate credit card and ID across the polished wood. "The reservation is under Ragsdale. I confirmed an early arrival for an ADA-accessible Double-Queen."

The clerk typed rapidly on his keyboard, offering a bright, professional smile. "Yes, Ms. Ragsdale. Your room on the third floor was prioritized and is ready for you. We have you in our premium accessible layout."

He handed Jessica two electronic keycards. She wheeled back over to us, spinning the plastic cards expertly between her fingers.

"Third floor," Jessica announced. "Let's get off the grid."

We loaded into the spacious elevator, riding it up to the third floor. The hallway was perfectly silent, lined with thick, sound-dampening carpet. We found our room near the end of the hall. Jessica swiped the card, the electronic lock blinking green with a heavy, satisfying click.

I pushed the heavy wooden door open, and we stepped inside.

The room was exactly what the pack needed. Jessica hadn't booked a sprawling, multi-room suite that would separate us into different bedrooms. She had specifically reserved a massive, single room with two plush queen-sized beds and a large, roll-in bathroom. It provided the exact square footage Jessica required for her mobility, while guaranteeing the pack would remain locked within a single, impenetrable four-wall boundary.

The moment the deadbolt engaged behind us with a loud, metallic thud, the civilian world was officially severed. We had successfully established our forward operating base.

Jessica wheeled herself toward the wooden credenza beneath the television, pointing a sharp, authoritative finger at the small, glass-fronted mini-fridge glowing quietly near the floor.

"Before anyone unpacks, I need to lay down a perimeter rule," Jessica announced, shifting effortlessly into full corporate-deployment mode. "Do not touch anything inside that fridge. Do not pick up the twelve-dollar bottled water, and absolutely do not touch the alcohol."

I looked over, my analytical processor catching her warning tone. "Is it booby-trapped?"

"Financially, yes," Jessica explained. "I've been on enough Quantum deployments to know exactly how these high-end hotels operate. I’ve watched brilliant engineers learn the hard way that the company expense account doesn't cover the mini-bar. Every single bottle and snack in there is sitting on a digital weight sensor. If you pick something up for more than sixty seconds, or even just move a bottle of vodka out of the way to store leftovers, it automatically blasts a massive markup charge straight to my room folio. So, treat it like a museum exhibit. Look, but don't touch."

"Understood," I nodded.

The presence of alcohol in the room was a fleeting annoyance—a dark ghost of Delores’s toxic weaponization of booze and chaos—but inside the absolute safety of our fortress, it was entirely neutralized. If we needed drinks, I would just order apple juice from room service.

With the rules of engagement set, I dropped the heavy laptop bag onto the desk, rolling my shoulders with a long, exhausted sigh. Rachel didn't hesitate. She walked over to the queen bed nearest the window, kicked off her boots, and climbed right into the center of the mattress.

She patted the empty space right next to her.

I walked over and sat down on the edge of the bed. Rachel immediately wrapped her arms around my waist, resting her head heavy against my shoulder. We had spent the entire morning navigating buses, trains, and crowded stations. Her processor desperately needed to decompress, and she needed her anchor to do it.

"We made it," Rachel rasped softly against my flannel shirt.

"We're perfectly safe, Bug," I promised, leaning my head against hers.

Jessica parked her wheelchair near the small table by the window, while Kristin unzipped her duffel bag to grab her comfortable clothes. The zero-privacy baseline held completely; there was no retreating into the bathroom to change. We were locked inside the perimeter, and the mission to rebuild was officially underway.
