The hydrotherapy had worked absolute wonders on my spastic, locked-up muscles, but an hour of fighting the resistance of the water had completely drained our fuel reserves.

The second the heavy wooden door of our third-floor room clicked locked behind us, Jessica tossed the thick, leather-bound hotel compendium onto the desk right in front of me.

"We need calories," Jessica announced, wheeling her chair toward the small table by the window. "Michael, you are on logistics. Order whatever the pack wants. Do not look at the prices."

I opened the heavy leather binder to the Room Service section. My trauma-wired processor instantly began scanning the right side of the page, instinctively calculating the financial damage. The prices were astronomical—the standard, exorbitant markup of a luxury hotel.

For two years, Delores had controlled every single calorie I consumed. She weaponized the grocery budget to ensure my total subservience, screaming over the cost of a ten-dollar pizza and punishing me if I ever asked for anything extra.

But as I sat there, holding the menu in the safety of our fortress, a brilliant, vindictive realization washed over me. I smiled, a genuine, completely unburdened grin.

"What's so funny?" Kristin asked, dropping her wet towel into the bathroom hamper.

"I was just thinking about that movie," I chuckled, looking over at Rachel. "*House Guard 2: Lost in Manhattan*. Do you remember the scene where the kid gets access to his dad's credit card at the Plaza Hotel?"

Rachel’s eyes lit up with instant recognition. "He orders the massive ice cream sundae in the bed and racks up a nine-hundred-and-sixty-seven-dollar room service bill."

"Exactly," I grinned. "Delores would have a complete, catastrophic meltdown if I spent fifteen dollars without her permission. But Jess?"

I looked over at my older sister. Jessica, a Senior Technical Program Manager at Quantum, just offered a cool, corporate smirk, adjusting her glasses. She could drop \$967 on room service without even blinking. Her financial security wasn't just a convenience; it was a heavy, impenetrable shield guarding our perimeter.

"I don't think we'll hit nine hundred dollars," Jessica noted dryly. "But feel free to try."

I picked up the hotel phone and dialed the kitchen.

When the operator answered, I didn't hesitate or apologize for my order. My Bug and I operated on the exact same wavelength, especially when we were exhausted. We both desperately needed familiar, heavy comfort food.

"I need two double bacon cheeseburgers," I ordered smoothly. "But the build needs to be exact. Ketchup, mayonnaise, lettuce, and pickles. Absolutely no onions, and absolutely no tomatoes. I need a massive side of fries with both, and two large glasses of cold apple juice."

Rachel, sitting perfectly flush against my side on the edge of the mattress, gave an approving nod. We always ordered the exact same thing.

"For the third," I continued, glancing at Kristin, who gave me a quick thumbs-up. "A large plate of fettuccine alfredo. And for the fourth, a grilled chicken club sandwich with no spicy mustard."

I confirmed the lack of any alcoholic beverages on the tab, signed off with our room number, and hung up the phone.

"Forty-five minutes," I announced to the room.

"Alright," Jessica said. "Let's get presentable before the hotel staff arrives. I do not want anyone opening that door while we are still wearing athletic gear that smells like a swimming pool."

The zero-privacy baseline engaged effortlessly.

We didn't scatter. We stood in the open space of the large hotel room and stripped off the damp athletic shorts and undershirts we had worn through the hallways. Rachel stood right in front of me, her dark eyes locked onto mine as we changed into our heavy, incredibly soft Franklin's Market sleepwear. We maintained the absolute, unbroken visual tether, constantly verifying that the other person was still breathing, still real, and still safe. Kristin, fully integrated into the protocol, changed into her own comfortable loungewear right alongside us, completely normalizing the environment.

By the time the sharp knock echoed at the heavy wooden door, we were completely settled.

My muscles tensed instinctively at the sudden noise, but before I could even shift my weight on the mattress, Kristin was already moving.

"I've got the threshold," Kristin stated, her voice dropping into a low, authoritative register.

She stepped directly in front of the door. Rachel and I stayed safely anchored on the bed, perfectly insulated from the interaction. Kristin checked the peephole, verified the uniform, and cracked the door open just enough to slide the massive, silver-covered rolling cart inside.

She didn't let the hotel employee cross the threshold or linger to look into the room. She swiftly signed the receipt with a flourish, handed the man a cash tip, and slammed the heavy wooden door shut. The deadbolt engaged with a loud, metallic *clack*.

The sentinel kept the civilian world firmly out in the hallway.

We rolled the cart to the center of the room, spreading the heavy ceramic plates out across the desk and the small table. I grabbed my cold apple juice and took a massive bite of the hot, perfectly constructed bacon cheeseburger.

Eating in the warm, absolute safety of our locked room felt like a profound luxury. There was no screaming from the hallway, no suffocating tension hanging over the meal, and no fear of making a mistake. There was only the quiet hum of the mini-fridge, the soft chatter of my sisters, and the absolute certainty that the pack was whole.
