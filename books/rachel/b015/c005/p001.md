My internal clock was still wildly out of sync, but when my processor finally booted up in the pitch-black early hours of Sunday morning, the very first data point I registered was the absolute physical security of my perimeter.

I was pinned exactly in the center of the mattress. Rachel was completely wrapped around my right side, her leg hooked over mine and her face buried deep into the crook of my neck. Her breathing was slow and heavy, completely at peace. On my left, Jessica was a solid, unmoving wall of warmth. At the foot of the bed, Kristin was curled up into a tight ball, still fulfilling her role as the pack's designated sentinel.

It was cramped, hot, and logistically absurd. And it was the safest I had felt in two entire years.

"Alright, team," Jessica whispered a few minutes later, her voice cutting through the quiet dark. The digital clock on the nightstand read 5:30 AM. "Time to mobilize. Our extraction vehicle waits for no one."

The pack moved with the synchronized, frictionless efficiency of a military unit breaking down camp.

We threw off the heavy comforter. Rachel stood up right next to me. The zero-privacy baseline we had established was absolute and permanently locked in. As I pulled my sleep shirt over my head, Rachel stood squarely in front of me, stripping off her own oversized t-shirt at the exact same time. She didn't look away, and neither did I. We maintained a fierce, unbroken visual tether, constantly verifying that the other person was still breathing, still real, and still right there.

I reached into the shopping bag from yesterday and pulled out my newly forged armor. I pulled on the thick, dark denim jeans and buttoned up the heavy, incredibly soft flannel shirt. It didn't smell like Kent. It smelled entirely like my new life.

Rachel hooked her arm through mine the second her clothes were on, locking us together while I moved over to the desk to pack the command center.

I unplugged the massive, 17-inch piano-black rig. Before closing the lid, I pressed the tactile eject button on the Blu-Ray drive. The tray slid out, and I carefully snapped the base installation disc for *The Denizens 2* onto the spindle, sliding it closed. My analytical processor knew the software’s strict DRM architecture; it required an aggressive SecuROM disc-check to run, and I fully intended to lose myself in the digital neighborhood once we were safely in transit. I slipped the heavy laptop into my padded bag and hoisted it onto my free shoulder.

"Transit math is locked," Jessica announced, wheeling out of the bathroom in her sharp, dark travel clothes. "Let's move."

We stepped out of the apartment building, the freezing, damp November air immediately biting at our cheeks.

Rachel’s separation anxiety spiked instantly. The dark, unpredictable civilian world was still highly hostile territory. She pressed her shoulder flush against my bicep, gripping the thick fabric of my new flannel with both hands as we marched to the bus stop at Edmonds Avenue and NE 3rd Street.

The heavy diesel rumble of the local Route 105 broke the morning silence. We boarded, executing the first leg of Jessica's flawless logistics. The bus carried us the short distance down the hill to the Renton Transit Center.

The transfer was immediate. We crossed the concrete bay and boarded the Route 140, the heavy workhorse line that ran directly west through the valley. I sat next to the window, the heavy laptop bag resting on my boots, while Rachel sat pressed against my side, acting as a physical shield between me and the aisle.

Thirty minutes later, the bus hissed to a stop, dropping us off in the freezing pre-dawn air at the Tukwila ARTS station.

In 2006, the Tukwila stop wasn't a heavily fortified transit center; it was a temporary, exposed facility. It consisted of long, wooden plank platforms and a few basic, open-air metal shelters built next to the heavy freight tracks. There were no secure walls, no massive lobbies, and no controlled entry points.

The sheer vulnerability of the exposed platform sent Rachel’s threat-detection system instantly to Redline.

Her breathing hitched. She grabbed the lapels of my heavy winter coat and physically pulled me backward until my spine was pressed flat against the cold metal wall of the transit shelter. She didn't stand beside me; she stood directly in front of me, planting her boots on the wooden planks and creating a living, physical barricade between me and the scattering of other early-morning commuters waiting on the platform.

"I've got you, Bear," Rachel rasped, her dark eyes scanning the platform with terrifying ferocity. "Nobody comes near you."

"I know, Bug," I whispered, resting my hands gently on her waist to ground her. "The perimeter is holding."

We waited in the bitter cold for ten agonizing minutes, Rachel refusing to yield an inch of her defensive posture.

Then, the low, powerful blast of a train horn echoed through the valley.

The ground beneath the wooden platform began to vibrate. A massive headlight pierced through the morning gloom, and the sleek, aerodynamic European-style locomotive of the ARTS *Cascades* rolled into the station. It wasn't a standard commuter train; it was a sprawling, articulated line of heavy steel passenger cars, painted in sharp, authoritative colors.

To my exhausted, traumatized brain, it didn't look like public transit. It looked like a 500-ton, high-speed armored fortress. And the doors were opening just for us.
