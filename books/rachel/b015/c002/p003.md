We stood on the freezing, windswept concrete of the Kent Station platform, watching my older sister wheel her chair up the sloped concrete mini-high platform toward the open doors of the TAPS Commuter Rail.

The damp November wind whipped across the asphalt, cutting right through my thin t-shirt. I watched Jessica’s hands grip the push-rims of her chair. She was moving with absolute, tactical efficiency, completely focused on the logistics of boarding. She wasn't looking back. She was locking her perimeter down and executing her escape route.

My chest seized with a sudden, suffocating panic. She was leaving. The only barrier between me and the squalid nightmare of Aunt Lynne's house was about to disappear onto that carriage.

As her front casters crossed the threshold of the pneumatic train doors, the terror completely overwhelmed my trained compliance. I couldn't mask it anymore.

"Jess," I mumbled, my voice cracking under the weight of sheer exhaustion. I took a half-step forward. "Help me."

But the volume of my voice was entirely swallowed by the loud, mechanical hiss of the train’s air brakes and the freezing gust of wind sweeping across the tracks. Jessica didn't stop. She wheeled straight into the designated accessible bay and locked her brakes.

The heavy pneumatic doors hissed shut, sliding together with a solid, metallic thud.

The silver and green commuter train vibrated slightly and pulled away from the platform, carrying Jessica north toward Seattle. As I watched the last carriage disappear down the tracks, the profound, grounding perimeter my older sister had provided instantly vanished with it.

I was left standing on the freezing concrete platform with Delores.

"I know you haven’t seen your little sis in ages," Delores snapped, pulling her coat tightly around her shoulders and glaring at me. Her voice was laced with that familiar, dripping condescension that always made my stomach twist into knots. "But dragging me on a bus tour of Pierce County this early in the morning is ridiculous."

I kept my head down, shoving my hands deep into the pockets of the Quantum sweatpants Jessica had given me. "We just have to catch the buses," I muttered quietly, desperate to avoid triggering a public meltdown.

We followed Jessica's logistical map perfectly. We boarded the TAPS Route 565 down to Federal Way, sitting in tense silence. At the Federal Way transit hub, we transferred to the Route 500. By the time the heavy bus was navigating the urban streets of Downtown Tacoma, the damp morning fog had lifted into a solid, overcast gray.

When we were dropped off at the 10th & Commerce transit spine, we had a brief wait. I pulled my silver flip phone from my pocket, quickly typing out a message to Jessica to ensure my location was logged in her system.

*Downtown Tacoma about to board the 2, vehicle \#186.*

We boarded the Number 2 bus—the long route Jessica had specifically recommended to pass the time. We found seats near the middle of the carriage. Delores leaned against the window, her arms crossed, exuding a hostile, defensive energy. I just stared at the back of the seat in front of me, my anxiety spiking as we pushed deeper into the South Sound.

Later on in our journey, the bus operator called out. "Bridgeport & 67th. Freddy’s Market." The heavy air brakes hissed as the bus came to a stop to pick up a passenger at the massive grocery superstore. The pneumatic doors folded open, and a young woman stepped aboard.

I watched her flash a transit pass to the driver. She walked down the aisle and sat down in the seats directly behind Delores.

My heart hammered against my ribs. I froze, my eyes going wide. I was quite delighted, but utterly terrified to say a single word. I thought she looked incredibly familiar, but I dare not believe my eyes. It was Kristin. It was my baby sister’s best friend and roommate.

I knew it was her, but I completely locked my jaw. If I acknowledged her, if I showed even a fraction of a second of happiness at seeing another woman, Delores would absolutely freak out. She would interrogate me, accuse me of staring, and punish me for the rest of the day. So, I stared straight ahead, pretending the girl behind us was a total stranger.

Eventually, the bus pulled into the sprawling Lakewood Transit Center, the air brakes hissing one final time.

Delores and I stepped off the bus onto the concrete island. Across the massive parking lot stood a sprawling, big-box retail store with a giant red circular logo painted on the tan exterior.

Pretending I didn’t recognize Kristin, who had stepped off the bus right behind us, I turned to her and politely asked, "Excuse me, do you know where The Spot is?"

Kristin didn't miss a beat. She didn't smile, and she didn't greet me. She just pointed toward the massive red-themed store across the asphalt without saying a word.

"Thanks," I mumbled.

We made our way across the parking lot, with Kristin walking a few paces ahead of us. When we reached the entrance, the first set of automatic glass doors slid open, admitting us into a wide, enclosed vestibule.

Instead of continuing into the main store, Kristin stopped at the Guest Services counter, which was tucked securely inside the vestibule area. Delores and I hovered near a display of clearance items nearby.

I watched Kristin lean over the counter and speak quietly to the team member behind the register. The employee nodded, reaching down to unclip a heavy, black two-way radio from her belt. She pressed the button on the side. “Team Member Rachel?”

“This is Rachel. Can I help you find something?”

"Could you come to Guest Services please," the employee's voice crackled over the radio frequency.

“On my way,” I heard her voice over the two-way radio.

My chest tightened with anticipation. Hearing her voice over the radio meant alot, but I wanted to see her now. I kept my eyes locked on the interior set of automatic doors.

A minute later, she appeared.

Rachel was wearing the strict uniform of The Spot—a bright red polo shirt with her white nametag pinned to the chest, perfectly pressed khaki pants, black shoes, and black socks.

Because of the architectural layout of the entrance, I watched Rachel execute a highly specific path. She walked out of the main store, passing through the interior set of automatic doors into the vestibule. Then, she pushed through the exterior set of automatic doors, stepping briefly out onto the sidewalk, before immediately turning around and re-entering the vestibule through the entrance doors to access the front of the Guest Services counter.

The moment she cleared the doors, she saw me.

She didn't hesitate. She dropped a handful of return slips onto the counter and broke into a run.

"Michael! What are you doing out in Washington?" Rachel gasped, her raspy voice echoing in the glass vestibule.

She slammed into me, wrapping her arms around my neck in a fierce, desperate hug. I held her just as tightly, burying my face into her shoulder. After everything I had endured over the last two years, holding my baby sister felt like grabbing a lifeline.

When she pulled back, she didn't care about neurotypical boundaries or public appearances. She reached up, framing my face with her hands, and planted a firm, incredibly affectionate kiss directly on my cheek.

I caught Delores’s reaction in my peripheral vision. Delores’s face flushed a dark, furious red. Her jaw clenched so hard I thought her teeth would crack. She was absolutely seething with toxic jealousy, but she kept her mouth shut. The only reason Delores did not rage right there in the vestibule was because we were in a brightly lit public space surrounded by retail employees.

Rachel pulled back, her dark eyes scanning me from head to toe. Her expression instantly shifted from profound joy to a dark, calculating anger.

I knew what she was looking at. I was wearing Jessica's borrowed clothes. My hair was greasy, and I smelled like stale sweat and the squalor of Aunt Lynne's house. I hadn't been allowed to shower properly in days, and Delores smelled exactly the same.

Rachel didn't look mad at me. Her eyes drifted over my shoulder, locking onto Delores with a gaze of pure, unfiltered hatred. Rachel was highly observant; she knew exactly who was controlling my environment, and she knew exactly who was responsible for my deteriorating hygiene.

"I can’t really talk now," Rachel finally said, forcing her attention back to me and breaking the tense standoff.

"I needed a notebook anyways for class, that’s why I came in," Kristin chimed in smoothly, carrying a red spiral notebook to the Guest Services register to pay.

Rachel looked down at her watch, the practical reality of the bus schedules taking over. "Yeah, you need your 212A."

I hugged Rachel goodbye, squeezing her one last time before she had to return to the sales floor. Kristin took Delores and me over to the checkout to pay for her notebook, acting entirely like a helpful stranger.

We headed back across the parking lot to the transit center. As her bus pulled into the bay, Kristin finally dropped the act. She turned to me, wrapping her arms around my torso in a big, solid hug goodbye, completely ignoring Delores.

"Take care of yourself, Michael," Kristin whispered fiercely before stepping aboard her 212A route.

Delores and I walked over to our designated bay to catch the 204 toward the local mall. The moment we stepped onto the bus and took our seats, the fragile public peace shattered.

Delores gave me a venomous, sideways look. "Who was that?" she demanded, her voice a harsh, accusatory hiss.

"My sister’s roommate," I replied, my heart sinking. I wondered why I would even have to explain that, or why she was so furious about a brief interaction.

Delores snorted, a cruel, mocking sound. "You like a toothpick more than me?"

"She is my sister’s best friend," I stammered, my chest tightening as the familiar wave of psychological oppression washed over me. "And she already has a boyfriend."

She smirked, a dark, triumphant expression crossing her face. It wasn't a smile of relief; it was the look of a warden securing her prisoner.

"Good," Delores sneered, leaning closer to me so only I could hear. "Because you are spoken for by me! Besides, Brandi and I have plans for you as soon as we get back to the house…"

The threat hung in the damp air of the bus, heavy and coercive. My stomach violently dropped. The brief, beautiful sanctuary of seeing Rachel was gone, replaced by the terrifying reality of what was waiting for me in Kent.

We arrived at the sprawling local mall, stepping off the bus to wait at the shelter where our connection to Kent would pick us up shortly. I stood shivering in the cold, staring blankly at the asphalt, completely trapped.

Then, my silver flip phone vibrated in my pocket. I pulled it out.

The caller ID flashed a 425 area code. I had the number programmed into my contacts: *Jessica - Work*.

I flipped the phone open, entirely expecting to hear my sister's voice. Instead, the sharp, intimidating, high-level corporate register of Andromeda—Jessica's director at Quantum—came echoing through the speaker.
