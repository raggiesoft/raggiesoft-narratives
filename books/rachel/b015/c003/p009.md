Jessica stacked the empty plates on her lap tray and wheeled out to the kitchen, leaving Rachel, Kristin, and me in the bedroom. The exhaustion was finally catching up to all of us. The adrenaline of the escape, the massive software installation, and the crushing emotional weight of the day were settling deep into my bones.

"Time to wash up," Kristin announced softly, grabbing her own overnight bag. "I'll go first, then you two can have the bathroom."

While Kristin was getting ready, Rachel didn't move an inch. She sat pressed against my side on the edge of the mattress, her arm still hooked fiercely around my waist. Her dark eyes were tracking my every micro-movement. The separation anxiety radiating from her was palpable—a heavy, redlining panic that completely overrode any standard neurotypical social boundaries.

When Kristin returned to the bedroom, dressed in a set of comfortable flannel pajamas, Rachel immediately reached down and grabbed the heavy canvas duffel bag with her free hand.

"Come on," she whispered, her raspy voice tight with an unyielding determination.

We walked down the short hallway together, practically joined at the hip. We stepped into the small bathroom, and Rachel reached behind us, clicking the door shut.

Normally, two adult siblings sharing a bathroom at the exact same time would be an awkward breach of privacy. But normal didn't exist anymore. Kent had burned normal to the ground. My sister was in a state of absolute psychological emergency, and her need to physically ensure my existence superseded everything else.

"I'm not waiting in the hall," Rachel stated, her voice trembling slightly as she dropped the duffel bag onto the linoleum. "I am not letting you out of my sight. Not even for a minute."

"I know, Bug," I promised gently, leaning my forehead against hers. "I'm right here."

She finally, reluctantly, unhooked her arm from my waist so we could take turns using the toilet. But even as she turned her back to give me a shred of modesty, she stood directly in front of the sink, watching my reflection in the mirror. She needed the visual confirmation. She needed to *see* that I hadn't vanished into thin air.

We took turns washing our hands in the warm water, standing shoulder-to-shoulder at the small vanity.

Rachel unzipped the canvas duffel, pulling out our sleepwear. She handed me the long, familiar nightshirt I preferred for sensory decompression. As I pulled my Quantum t-shirt over my head, I caught her wincing in the mirror at the sight of the dark red welt across my back, but she forced herself to keep looking. She watched me pull the long nightshirt on, needing to verify that I was physically intact.

She quickly changed into her own sleepwear right beside me. The absolute second her shirt was pulled down, she closed the distance between us. Her arms wrapped fiercely around my torso, her hands locking together just below my ribs, completely avoiding the injury on my upper back. She buried her face against my chest, her breathing hitching as she absorbed the deep, grounding pressure of the hug. She wasn't letting go again.

"Let's go to bed," I whispered, resting my chin on top of her head.

With Rachel physically tethered to my side, we shuffled back down the hall and re-entered Jessica’s bedroom.

Jessica had already transferred from her wheelchair and was sitting up in bed, dressed in a dark, oversized sleep shirt. Her dark, tinted prescription glasses rested on the nightstand beside the glowing digital clock. Kristin was already sitting near the foot of the mattress, pulling the heavy comforter back for us.

The logistical puzzle of fitting four adults into a single bed was completely bypassed by the sheer, desperate need for proximity. This wasn't about comfort; it was about survival. It was the pack securing the perimeter.

I climbed carefully into the center of the mattress. Jessica immediately shifted closer on my left side, her presence a steady, unshakeable wall of pragmatic safety. Rachel scrambled under the covers on my right.

Rachel didn't just lie next to me. She rolled onto her side, pulling herself completely flush against my body. She threw one arm securely across my chest and rested her head on my shoulder, angling her face so she could stare directly at me in the dim light. She was absolutely refusing to turn her back. She needed to watch me breathe.

Kristin took the outermost edge of the mattress. She curled up near the foot of the bed, perfectly content to figure it out and sacrifice her own sleeping space. She acted as the silent, loyal sentinel—guarding the physical boundary of the mattress while fully understanding that I desperately needed my two sisters anchoring my sides.

Jessica reached over, clicking off the small bedside lamp.

The room plunged into darkness, save for the soft, ambient glow of the new Clarity desktop humming quietly in the corner, and the red digits of the clock.

I lay there flat on my back, sandwiched between the two women who had literally orchestrated my salvation. On my left, Jessica's steady, rhythmic breathing was a testament to the flawless, calculated logic that had pulled me out of the fire. On my right, Rachel's fierce, unyielding grip was the emotional anchor that kept my shattered processor from spinning out of control.

For the first time in two years, I didn't have to sleep with one eye open. I didn't have to constantly monitor the mood of the room or prepare for a sudden, coercive ambush. The door was locked. The perimeter was absolute.

I closed my eyes, letting the heavy, collective warmth of my pack sink into my exhausted bones, and finally allowed the darkness to take me.
