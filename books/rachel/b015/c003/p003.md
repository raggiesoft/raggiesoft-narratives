12:15 PM rolled around and Jessica gently prodded me awake.

"Ready for lunch, sleepyhead?" Jessica asked, a soft, encouraging smile on her face.

"Yeah, I think so," I mumbled, rubbing the exhaustion from my eyes.

"I’m going to make PB&J sandwiches—two for each of us—with chips and generic cola," she said. "Does that sound good?"

"Yeah, it’s simple and effective," I agreed, watching my sister expertly transfer from the mattress to her wheelchair.

She led the way to the kitchen area. "We’re going to be on the bus at 1:10 and will be at Broil’s around 1:45 or so."

She started assembling our lunch, the quiet, predictable mechanics of the task filling the apartment. Sitting at her dining table, eating a perfectly standard peanut butter and jelly sandwich, it felt incredible just to exist without the suffocating pressure of constantly calculating Delores's mood.

The Route 240 dropped us off quite a ways up Garden Avenue.

"I’ve been lobbying TAPS to get this stop moved closer for ages," Jessica explained with a tired sigh, locking her brakes for a moment as the bus pulled away. "We have to double back down the sidewalk to get to the entrance."

When we finally walked through the massive double doors of Broil's Electronics, I couldn’t believe my eyes. The place was a sprawling, cavernous warehouse themed with massive, retro-futuristic sci-fi props, with endless aisles dedicated entirely to silicon, circuitry, and screens.

"Jess, I don’t want to sound rude, but how much am I allowed to spend?" I asked, completely overwhelmed by the sheer volume of high-end hardware surrounding us.

"I think I can spare four grand or so," she stated casually, as if she were talking about buying a cup of coffee, “You get whatever your heart desires.”

I gasped, my feet stopping dead on the linoleum. "Four grand?!" I was absolutely not used to my big sister wielding that kind of financial artillery.

Before I could recover from the shock, a polo-shirted sales associate walked up. "Good afternoon, Jessica. What brings you in today?"

"My brother currently has an outdated Apex Meridian 110L, so I’m going to get him a real gaming rig," Jessica announced, immediately pushing her chair toward the main computer floor.

I never realized just how fast Jessica could propel her manual chair when she was on a mission. "Jess, slow down!" I called out, jogging to keep up.

"Oh, sorry," she laughed, banking smoothly into the laptop section. "Well, here we are. Now, you need a heavy-duty desktop replacement. I won’t settle for less than a bleeding-edge dual-core processor, 4 GB of RAM, and dual hard drives."

I started scanning the massive, glowing 17-inch models on display.

"Do you want a Blu-Ray drive?" Jessica asked, pointing to a sleek, piano-black machine boasting a top-tier mobile GPU.

I blinked. "You can afford that?"

"That’s no problem," my sister replied with a shrug. "Besides, it’s the new high-definition standard for movies, so you might as well future-proof it."

Another associate wandered over to our aisle, checking in. "Finding everything okay? Oh, good afternoon, Jessica."

"I’m getting my brother a real gaming rig," Jess repeated proudly.

I ran my fingers over the keyboard of the massive flagship model. "Jess, I think I found the one I like. The specs are incredible. But... the placard says it only comes pre-installed with Quantum OS XN Home Edition. It just has a mail-in coupon for Clarity Home Premium when it releases next year."

Jessica actually scoffed, a brilliant, corporate smirk crossing her face.

"Don't even bother with the mail-in coupon," Jessica instructed smoothly. "Clarity went RTM for enterprise clients last week. The public doesn't get it until January, but trust me, you are not going to be stuck running a legacy Home Edition on my watch. I have your operating system handled."

She turned her chair to face me. "I actually need to run a quick errand at my office in Building 35 after this anyway. Someday, when you're rested and fully recuperated, I’d love to take you on a proper tour of the Museum of Computer History on campus but today is strictly a quick in and out so we can get your rig operational."

I looked at the massive, dual-hard drive beast sitting on the shelf, imagining the absolute, glorious isolation of losing myself in a 17-inch high-definition widescreen. "Yes. This is the one."

Jessica nodded to the associate. "We'll take it. But we don't want to carry the massive cardboard box on the bus. Could you ring up a heavy-duty tech backpack to carry it in? And we'll need a weighted optical USB mouse and a premium mousepad to replace the trackpad."

"I’m going to put this on my Broil’s account," Jessica told the associate, before looking back at me. "Michael, would you like to grab some games while we’re here?"

"Yes, please," I said, a massive, genuine smile finally breaking across my face.

"Alright," Jessica commanded, spinning her chair toward the software aisles. "Games, then a few server parts for my own rig, and you’re going to need a high-speed transfer cable."

"A transfer cable?" I asked. "I don’t mind just reinstalling everything manually."

"You’ll be thanking me later," Jess promised. "I'll make sure you have the software you need, but if there are any specific games or titles you want that aren't on these shelves, I’ll order them online with rush overnight delivery."

When we finally reached the checkout lanes, I watched the associate carefully pack the 9.5-pound laptop, the power brick, the games, and the accessories into the new heavy-duty backpack.

"Are you sure there’s no time to drop this off back at the apartment first?" I asked, hoisting the heavy backpack over my shoulders.

Jessica checked her watch, her tactical transit radar pinging. "If we hustle, we might make the 564 express straight to Overlake. I still need to run to the office to grab something from my desk real quick, and if we miss this connection, the next bus isn't for an hour. I’m going to need you to push me. We have exactly five minutes. I’m trying to have it so that once we get back to the apartment, you don’t have to go back out."

We burst out of Broil's and sprinted down the sidewalk.

"There’s our bus coming around the corner!" Jess called out as the shelter came into view. "We just made it."

We rolled up the hydraulic ramp. Jessica flashed her Quantum Blue Badge to the operator. "I need to pay my brother’s fare. He’s going Two-Zone."

We skipped the apartment drop-off entirely, the heavy regional bus carrying us straight up Interstate 405. I watched the Washington landscape blur past, the heavy backpack full of expensive hardware resting securely between my feet.

When the bus operator finally called out, "Overlake Transit Center," I looked out the window and couldn’t believe my eyes. Sprawling across the landscape was a massive, pristine, heavily forested corporate city of glass and steel.

"So, this is where you work?" I asked, my voice hushed in pure amazement as I hoisted the heavy backpack onto my shoulders and stepped off the bus.

"Yep, this is it," Jessica smiled, adjusting her messenger bag. "It’s not much, but it does the job."

Of course, Jessica—completely accustomed to navigating the absolute epicenter of the global tech industry—was being modest. She knew this was my first time truly seeing the Quantum campus, and she let me soak in the sheer, overwhelming scale of her perimeter.
