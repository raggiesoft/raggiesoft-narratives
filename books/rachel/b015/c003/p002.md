The heavy TAPS Route 565 bus navigated the damp, gray morning streets, finally pulling into the Renton Transit Center.

I stared out the rain-streaked window, my heart hammering a frantic, exhausted rhythm against my ribs. And then, I saw her.

Sitting in her manual wheelchair by Bay 5, wearing a bright yellow raincoat that shielded her from the drizzle, was my older sister. Resting securely on her wheelchair’s lap tray were two small cups of coffee and two wrapped Danishes.

The air brakes hissed, and the pneumatic doors folded open.

There was a decent-sized line of commuters trying, as I was, to exit the bus. The sudden press of bodies and the claustrophobia of the aisle made the process incredibly nerve-wracking. My mind was racing in a million different directions, still half-expecting Delores to suddenly materialize from the shadows and drag me back to Kent.

The driver looked down at the wheelchair waiting on the curb. "My bus?" he asked over the intercom.

"No thanks," Jess answered, her voice projecting clearly through the rain. I stumbled off the bottom step, completely ignoring the other commuters, and practically threw myself onto my knees on the wet pavement, wrapping my arms fiercely around her waist.

"Just picking up my brother," Jessica finished, resting her chin on the top of my head and holding me tight.

She didn't ask questions. She wheeled backward, guiding us over to the loading area for the local Route 105. She picked up the cup and the pastry from her tray and offered them to me.

"Here. Something to hold you until we get to my place," Jessica said, her voice dropping into a steady, logistical cadence to keep me grounded. "It’s not a long ride at all, but please eat quickly."

"Quickly?" I asked, a fresh wave of exhausted sobs catching in my throat. I couldn't even hold the coffee cup steady.

"Because you’re not supposed to eat or drink on the bus," she explained smoothly, acting as if this were just a standard morning commute. "That’s why I only got us small ones."

I choked down the pastry, the sugar hitting my depleted system like a shockwave. We finished our drinks right as the heavy Route 105 pulled up to the curb. The doors opened, and the driver immediately smiled.

"Jessica! What are you doing up so early?" the driver called out.

As he got the hydraulic ramp deployed, my sister didn't mince words. "Long story short, Adam, my brother was left for dead by his girlfriend’s aunt this morning. I’m taking him to my place."

*Left for dead,* I repeated to myself, following her up the ramp. My brain was too sleep-deprived and traumatized to fully process the dramatic phrasing, but I figured it was entirely accurate. I took the fold-down seat directly across from the accessible bay as Adam secured her wheelchair to the floor restraints.

"Thanks," I sobbed, burying my face in my hands.

"It’s a short ride to my place on Edmonds Avenue," Jessica told me, ignoring the other passengers. "When we get there, I’ll show you my room, and then I’m going to make omelets with ham and cheese, exactly like Mom used to do on Sundays."

It wasn’t too long before Jessica reached up and hit the yellow stop-request strip.

"It’s that complex right there," my sister said, pointing through the window to a series of buildings across the street.

She called forward to the operator. "Wheelchair getting off, Adam."

We crossed the street and entered Jessica’s ground-floor apartment. The moment the deadbolt clicked shut behind us, the terrifying, unpredictable outside world was completely severed.

"Now, I need to be honest about things," Jessica said, wheeling into the quiet, perfectly organized living room. "I’m not mad at you at all, Michael. I was afraid this would happen, and I am kicking myself for not having you come with me yesterday... but you’re here now."

After showing me her bedroom and telling me to drop my junk in the corner, she turned her chair to face me, her eyes burning with an intense, protective fire.

"She was controlling you. That family is incredibly toxic. I hate to say this, because I know you are hurting, but I am so incredibly glad you were kicked out."

I started crying badly, my knees giving out as I sank onto the edge of her mattress.

"Don’t cry," Jess said softly. She wheeled out to the kitchen, abandoning the omelet idea entirely. She realized I didn't have the stamina to wait for a cooked meal. She gathered a box of cereal and a bowl. "Here. Have some breakfast."

As I sat there mechanically eating the cereal, I whimpered, "I can’t believe this is happening."

"Let me be honest," Jessica said, sitting across from me. "You will find someone who treats you well. Delores treated you like gutter trash. Look at me. Rachel, Kristin, and I are going to spend Christmas weekend with Dad and Cathy down in Oregon. I’m going to let them know you’re going to be with us, too."

She wheeled over to the table and pulled her cell phone out. She looked at the screen and sighed. "It’s only 6:35 AM. They won’t be up yet. Even I’m just now usually starting to get up on a Sunday."

"I’m sorry I woke you up, Jess," I sobbed, the guilt twisting in my gut.

"It’s okay," my sister replied immediately. "You needed to wake me, considering your situation."

"Thank you for not being mad at me," I whimpered, terrified that the sudden logistical burden of my presence would make her angry.

Jessica didn't use words to answer. She just leaned forward, wrapping her arms around my neck and hugging me fiercely to prove her loyalty.

"Let me text Rachel," she said, pulling back. "When she gets up and calls, I’ll explain what happened. Don’t be upset, Michael. None of this is your fault. You are completely safe with me here in Renton."

She quickly typed out a message to our younger sister: *Delores dumped Michael – he is safe with me in Renton.*

Jessica set her phone down and looked at me critically. "When you’re done with your breakfast, please take a shower. I’m sorry, but Delores was a disgusting slob, and I’d be surprised if that house didn’t fail a health inspection. You don’t need her. Didn’t you find it disgusting, all the cat shit on the floor? I am not mad at you; I am just worried about you."

The mention of that filthy house made my skin crawl. "You want me to take a shower?" I asked, my voice shaking.

"Yes. I’m sorry for what she did to you. I want you to get washed. You’ll thank me later."

"Jess, I’m sorry, but I just can’t," I started sobbing, pure panic spiking in my chest. The idea of going into a small, enclosed room and shutting the door behind me was terrifying. I couldn't handle the isolation. "Please. I don’t want you to... I don’t want to lose sight of you for even a single second right now," I snapped, my voice cracking with desperation.

"It’s okay. It’s okay," Jess said calmly, raising her hands to de-escalate.

Realizing I was exploding at the sister who had just rescued me, I burst into heavy tears and threw myself forward, burying my face in her shoulder.

"I am right here," Jessica promised, accepting my hug without hesitation. "Rachel will be up here later. I know you’re angry and scared, and you have every right to be."

She pulled back just enough to look me seriously in the eye. "I’d like to be honest with you. That morning at the Kent Train Station... I really wanted you to take the train with me to Seattle. I would’ve paid your fare, and I would’ve taken you straight to the Quantum campus with me."

I blinked, the tears blurring my vision. "I would’ve been welcomed where you work?"

"Yes. Given the situation, absolutely. My coworkers knew I wanted you out of that toxic environment, and yesterday afternoon, I was offered several rides to Kent to come physically extract you."

"This is why you sent the text message telling me to come to Quantum?" I asked.

"That’s right. That was my boss, Andromeda, on the phone while one of my coworkers was looking up the bus schedule. Remember the sandwich, drink, and chips you were offered at my desk? That was their direct response to you stating you hadn’t eaten since breakfast," Jessica replied gently. "To tell you the truth? Today, I was going to organize a team trip to Kent to drag you out of that slice of hell."

I hugged Jessica even tighter, completely overwhelmed by the profound, structural safety net she had built for me without me even knowing.

"Please don’t leave my side for even one second," I begged in a humiliated whisper.

"Okay, here is what we will do, if you want," Jessica offered smoothly, pivoting her logistics. "I will come into the bathroom with you. I can help you if you need me to. At the very least, I should probably show you how to use a wheelchair-accessible shower."

"You would be in the bathroom with me?" I asked nervously.

"Sure. You don’t want to be alone for even a second because you are terrified right now. I am your sister. It is nothing we haven’t seen before when we were kids."

"Thank you," I whispered. "I just feel so incredibly dirty. They treated me like I wasn't even a person."

"You are a person," Jessica said fiercely. "You are my brother. And we are going to wash that filth completely away."

I slowly, nervously got undressed. Jessica transferred to her wheelchair and wheeled into the bathroom, leaving the door wide open.

"I will stay right here the whole time, exactly as promised," she assured me. She handed me a towel and pointed to the controls. "Please, help yourself to my things. Shampoo, body wash, use whatever you need. We can always go out later to get something specifically for you. Please, sit on the shower seat, and take as long as you need to clean yourself."

I sat under the hot spray, letting the water scald my skin. I scrubbed my arms, my chest, and my hair, desperately trying to wash the suffocating stench of Aunt Lynne's house down the drain. Jessica sat quietly in her chair just outside the spray, acting as an unshakeable, physical anchor.

"Is there anything you’d like to do today?" Jessica asked as I finally turned the water off and reached for the towel. "Quantum knows that I need to comfort you, and I am using a Paid Time Off day today."

"Could I please get some sleep?" I yawned, the hot water finally pushing my exhausted body past its breaking point. "This is going to sound awful... but they kept me up all night."

"What happened?" Jessica asked gently, her voice dropping into a careful, investigative register as we moved back to her bedroom. "You started mentioning something before our showers."

She transferred from her wheelchair to her bed, turning the small radio on the nightstand to a low volume to provide some ambient white noise. "Please tell me everything. Take as much time as you need."

She pulled her laptop over, plugged it in, and turned it on, giving me the space to speak without demanding direct eye contact. "Take as long as you need," she reassured me.

I decided to pull my own cheap laptop out of my bag, plugging it in just to have something to hold onto.

"A 110L? Eh," Jessica observed the outdated machine. "How about this: remember me taking you and Rachel to Broil’s down in Oregon?"

"Yeah, why?" I sobbed.

"There’s a Broil’s that’s a hop, skip, and a 240, 564, or 565 away on Garden Ave. It’s about a twenty-minute bus ride from here. Would you like a better laptop? We can go later today after you’re rested."

I dropped the computer entirely. I practically threw myself across the mattress, hugging my sister very tightly, unable to hold it in anymore.

"They kept me up all night," I started, my entire body shaking violently. "They came into the room completely naked. They promised me a night of wild sex, but it wasn't a promise, Jess. It was a trap."

I sobbed into her shoulder. Jessica wrapped her arms around my back, holding me securely.

"They locked the door," I choked out, the horrifying memories rushing back. "Delores and her cousin just... they took turns. They wouldn't let me sleep. My internal clock was screaming because it was three in the morning East Coast time, and my brain was so scrambled I couldn't even process what was happening. Around two in the morning, Brandi snickered and said, *‘just wait until four.’* They just used me."

I sobbed some more, my chest heaving. "They forced me to touch them. I couldn't say no. I was trapped in that room, and Aunt Lynne was sleeping downstairs guarding the door. I just wanted to go to sleep."

Jessica's face hardened into a mask of pure, glacial fury. "They coerced you. You were exhausted, terrified, and they assaulted you."

I nodded, the tears soaking her shirt. Jess spoke the undeniable truth, but it was agonizingly painful to acknowledge it out loud.

"Suddenly I heard a pounding at the door," I finished, my voice a hollow whisper. "Aunt Lynne told me to get my junk and get out. Then I found myself shoved onto the 159 in the pouring rain at 5 AM. Then I called you, and here I am."

Jessica stroked the back of my head. "This is why you want me in the room with you right now?"

I nodded yes. My sister leaned back against the pillows, allowing me to snuggle securely into her arms. She was trying her absolute best to comfort me, fully understanding that my perimeter had been violently, catastrophically breached.

"You can sleep right here on my bed," Jessica suggested softly. "I’ll get you up for lunch. I usually eat around 12:30, and then we’re going to catch the bus to Garden Ave so you can get a new laptop. A fresh start."

"Please don’t leave my side," I begged, exhaustion finally dragging me under.

"I wasn’t going to," my sister promised. She allowed me to rest heavily against her as she started typing on her work laptop. "It’s 8:00 AM now. Please rest as long as you need."

"12:30 sounds fair," I yawned. Jessica gently moved my head to her pillows, pulled the heavy blanket over me, and, exactly as promised, she did not move an inch.

It sure felt good getting a decent, secure nap in.

I don't know how long I slept, but the radio on the nightstand slowly drifted into my consciousness.

"You’re listening to KSEA-FM – Seattle, Olympia, Mt. Vernon – playing Christmas Favorites of Yesterday & Today," the cheerful DJ announced. "Up next is Cram with This Christmas, followed by a listener request: Christmas, Don’t Be Late."

A few minutes later, the very familiar, bubbly, synth-pop percussive opening of a well-known holiday tune kicked in. It was *Last December* by Neon Horizon.

The moment the chords registered in my brain, the phantom sensation of the Quantum corporate lobby and the humiliating memory of Delores dragging me away flooded my system. I threw myself violently across the mattress, burying myself into Jessica’s arms, and wailed.

"What’s wrong?" my suddenly frightened sister asked, slamming her laptop shut.

Through heavy sobs, tears, and general wailing, I was able to etch out the agonizing connection. "This is what she did to me! This is exactly what she did to me the year we took the Pathfinder to Rhode Island! She traps me, and she hurts me, and the music just plays!"

My sister started tearing up, wrapping her arms around me and rocking me back and forth. She explained how she had cried all the way to work when she was dropped off at the Kent Train Station yesterday, because all she could think about was the horrific situation I was in. She reiterated how the Quantum team had been pleading with her to let them drive down to Kent to rescue me.

Suddenly, Jessica’s cell phone rang on the nightstand.

"It’s Rachel," she said, snatching the phone and immediately putting it on speakerphone so I could hear.

"Rachel," Jessica announced, her voice dropping into her fierce, tactical register. "Delores threw Michael out and left him for dead in Kent this morning. I want you to know he is safely in Renton with me right now."

"What happened?!" Rachel gasped, her raspy voice instantly filling with panic.

"Remember when I reported to you and Kristin about that shithole house in Kent, and how snappish and controlling Delores was being to him yesterday?" Jess asked.

"Yes, that no-good bitch," Rachel spat, her hatred absolute and unfiltered.

"Rachel, I need you and Kristin right now," I sobbed toward the phone, not holding any of my tears back. Jessica pulled me into the best hug she could manage from her seated position.

"When I get off work at 2:00, I will be on the very next bus to Renton," Rachel promised fiercely.

"I think Kristin should come too," Jessica added.

"She gets off work at 2:00 as well. I’ll ask her to meet me at Lakewood, and we will get on the next Route 574," Rachel confirmed. I was still crying, and I could sense that hearing me break down like this was completely shattering both of my sisters’ hearts.

"Have her bring overnight gear, including your long nightshirt, and the two of you come on up," Jessica instructed, managing the logistics perfectly. "I’ll figure out dinner when you both get into Renton. Also, Rachel... could you do me a big favor? Michael needs help feeling safe. He needs you to be here because Delores was an absolute monster."

"I'll be right there," Rachel said, her voice filled with profound, protective understanding.

Jessica gripped me tightly as the phone call ended. "You’re safe. You’re with me. I won’t let her into my apartment, and I don’t even think she knows where this building is. I swear as your big sister, I will protect you from her."

The conversation was over. Jessica allowed me to just curl up in her arms, realizing that I was in the middle of an uncontrollable emotional breakdown. But through the tears, my brain was finally starting to wake up and process the reality of the morning.

The nightmare was over. I was officially single again.
