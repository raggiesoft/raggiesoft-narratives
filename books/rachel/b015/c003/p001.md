I woke up to a violent, echoing pounding on the bedroom door.

My heart hammered against my ribs, panic instantly flooding my system. I had barely slept. I was running on maybe two hours of fractured, exhausted unconsciousness. Delores and Brandi had kept me awake almost all night, trapping me in a suffocating, coercive nightmare that had completely scrambled my brain. I felt physically sick, my body trembling and my mind detached, floating somewhere above the horrific reality of that cramped second-floor room.

"I want you out of my house!" a harsh voice demanded from the hallway.

It was Aunt Lynne. Being ordered out of the house at four in the morning seemed completely out of the blue, but as I sat up, the cold, ugly truth settled over me. She had orchestrated the entire night. I knew she didn’t really like me, but now I had the undeniable proof. She had used me for her niece's entertainment, and now that they were done, she was discarding me like trash.

"I’ll take you to a bus stop, and then good riddance!" she barked through the wood.

Basically, I had to get my junk and get out. There was no breakfast. There was no shower. I wasn't allowed to wash the grime and the trauma off my skin. I just had to shove my belongings into my bag and leave. I was frightened beyond belief, operating entirely on a hollow, mechanical survival instinct.

It was dark. It was freezing cold. It was pouring a relentless, driving rain—the kind of miserable Pacific Northwest downpour that meant it was just barely warm enough to be rain instead of snow.

Because the house was buried deep in the residential maze of Kent, Aunt Lynne drove me in suffocating silence to a main arterial road, dropping me off at an isolated TAPS bus shelter. She didn't wait. She just sped off, her taillights disappearing into the rain.

I was sitting in the bus shelter completely on my own. I was freezing, shivering violently in the damp air, and so upset I could barely draw a full breath. I had no clue what to do. I was extremely tired—my internal clock begging for rest—and it was just shy of five o'clock in the morning.

Through the darkness, the headlights of a heavy TAPS commuter bus cut through the rain. I found myself blindly boarding it with all my junk: the Route 159, heading towards Downtown Seattle.

It was 5:05 AM. The heater on the bus was running, but I was still shaking. All I knew was my older sister’s cell phone number. I knew she lived somewhere in the Seattle area—specifically Renton—but I wasn’t sure exactly where her apartment was.

I felt incredibly uncomfortable calling her at such a strange hour, but as the bus rattled down the dark street, the panic threatened to swallow me whole. I felt like I had no choice. I pulled out my silver flip phone and dialed her number.

I held the phone to my ear, listening to the dialing tone. *Please, Jess. Please pick up,* I thought to myself, desperately trying to hold back the hot tears stinging my eyes.

The phone rang again. I was so frightened.

"Who would be calling me at this hour?" a sleepy, groggy voice asked over the line.

I recognized Jessica’s voice immediately. Joy. Absolute, undeniable relief. Help at last. Jessica had picked up her phone, even though it was only five in the morning on a Sunday.

"Jess," I gasped, the dam breaking. "It’s your brother, Michael. I’ve been abandoned out here. I’m on a 159 heading to Seattle. My girlfriend dumped me, and her aunt brought me to a bus stop."

I started sobbing right there on the bus, completely unable to hold back the tears any longer. The crushing weight of the abuse I had endured all night finally spilled over.

Jess sighed, her voice thick with sleep and disbelief. "You’re bullshitting me, Michael. It is five in the morning..."

Before she could go any further, the drover’s voice over the bus’s PA system chimed loudly above my head: *"104th Avenue SE & SE 256th Street."*

There was a sharp intake of breath on the other end of the line. Jess sounded like she had suddenly bolted upright in bed. The sleepiness vanished instantly, replaced by a terrified, razor-sharp, tactical panic.

"I overheard the driver calling out a stop," Jessica said, her voice dropping into a rapid, worried command register. "You’re obviously not bullshitting me. Michael, what bus are you on?"

"The 159," I whimpered, wiping my nose with the back of my sleeve. "And the sign was marked Seattle."

"Good. It's good that you’re on a bus," Jessica affirmed, her brain clearly calculating transit grids at lightspeed. "Bear with me, I am going to give you instructions to Renton. You are coming to my apartment, right now. You’re on the bus which will take you to the Kent Train Station. When you get to the train station, catch the Route 565 marked Overlake. Get off at the Renton Transit Center and wait there. I’m coming to get you."

She paused, taking a breath. "If I’m not there yet, wait at Bay 5. Your bus will be letting you off in Renton at Bay 3. I’m going to be coming from Route 105. You should be pulling into Renton at 6:00. Let me get going so I can get dressed."

"Jess, slow down," I sobbed, my scrambled brain struggling to hold onto all the numbers.

"Okay, I'm sorry. Put simply," my sister repeated, her tone softening into that fierce, protective cadence I desperately needed. "When you get to the Kent Train Station, you want the 564 or 565 marked Overlake. It doesn’t matter which, as long as it’s a 564 or 565 and it is heading towards Overlake. You will be getting off at the Renton Transit Center, where I will be waiting. Have you eaten yet?"

"No, I haven’t," I cried, the tears flowing freely down my cheeks. "No food. No shower. No nothing. I was just thrown out. Please call Rachel... I’m just so scared right now."

I was so disoriented I just wanted all of my anchors at once, even though I knew Rachel was miles away in Lakewood.

"I will have something for you to eat when you get dropped off in Renton," Jessica promised smoothly, ignoring the logistical impossibility of getting Rachel there right now and focusing entirely on keeping me grounded. "Let me go so I can get ready and be there for you. I will keep texting you with my location, and I can track where you are based on the schedules."

"Thank you so much," I choked out. "I will text as I move from bus to bus."

When the Route 159 finally pulled into the Kent Train Station, the sky was still a bruised, dark gray. I wandered around the damp concrete terminal until I saw the signage indicating where the 564 and 565 were supposed to pull in.

I sat down on the cold metal bench inside the shelter in front of the 565’s bus bay and wallowed in my own misery for a bit. My phone vibrated in my pocket. It was a text message from Jessica.

105 dropped me off at Renton Center. What bus you on, and what is the vehicle number?

I needed to hear her voice again. I couldn't handle the silence of the transit center. I hit the call button.

"I’m at Kent," I told her as soon as she answered. "And this schedule on the pole says that there is a 565 in about five minutes."

"OK," Jessica replied, her voice loud and clear over the background noise of her own transit center. "I will see you in Renton very, very soon. I promise you that I’m waiting right here at the Renton Transit Center."

When the heavy, articulated TAPS bus finally pulled up and the doors opened, I climbed aboard, settling into a seat near the front.

"I'm on," I told her, reading the vehicle number off the placard near the driver. "I’m mostly just hungry right now."

"OK, message me when the bus exits the freeway again," Jessica instructed. "That will be your cue that you’re in Renton and getting closer to me."

I hung up, staring blankly out the window as the bus merged onto the dark, rain-slicked highway, carrying me north.

About twenty minutes later, the bus took the exit ramp, transitioning back onto surface streets. I immediately hit speed dial again.

"I’m on city streets," I whimpered into the receiver, trying to hold back a fresh wave of tears. "Looks like we just passed a Franklin's Hypermarket."

"I know exactly where you are," Jessica said, her voice a lifeline in the dark. "You’re about five minutes away, traffic pending. Let me get going so I can grab you something to eat, just to tide you over until we get back to my apartment."

"Okay," I breathed.

"See you in five minutes, Michael."

The call clicked off. I lowered the phone.

I was terrified. Logistically, I knew it was just five more minutes on the bus. But psychologically, those five minutes felt like an eternity. I was scared because I couldn’t hear my big sister’s voice anymore.

The bus made a few more turns, the air brakes hissing as it navigated the intersections. I gripped my bag so tightly my knuckles ached. I felt like I was going to explode from the anxiety. I just desperately needed Jessica at my side, and I needed her right now.
