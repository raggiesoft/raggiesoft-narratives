The ARTS train deposited us in Portland, and a quick connection on the local light rail brought us directly to the ticketing lobby of PDX.

The atmosphere inside the terminal was thick with the new, militarized tension of late 2001. The National Guard soldiers were still stationed at the doors, their woodland camouflage and heavy rifles providing a stark contrast to the holiday travelers.

We checked my bag and slowly made our way toward the security checkpoint.

This was the absolute limit. The new federal aviation rules mandated that only ticketed passengers were allowed past the metal detectors. Just two months prior, this exact barrier had been the site of our most agonizing trauma, when I had to watch Rachel disappear into the secure zone without me. I hadn't been able to walk her to the gate or sit by the window with her.

Now, the roles were reversed.

I stopped a few dozen yards shy of the chaotic, winding security line. I turned around to face my sisters.

"Well," Jessica sighed, pulling the brake levers on her wheelchair. She reached out, and I leaned down, wrapping my arms around her shoulders. "Have a safe flight, Michael. Tell Mom I'll fax her the updated school enrollment paperwork for Rachel on Monday morning."

"I will," I said, pulling back and looking at Rachel.

My little sister stepped forward. There were tears pooling in her dark eyes, but her breathing was steady. She wrapped her arms tightly around my waist, delivering that heavy, grounding pressure we both relied on.

I wrapped my arms around her ribs, locking my hands together.

"Be brave tomorrow at the new school," I whispered into her hair. "You know the muscle memory for the marimba. You have the notes. You're going to be fine."

"I know," Rachel sniffled, burying her face in my jacket for one last, deep breath of familiarity. "I love you, Michael."

"I love you too."

I released her. I didn't want to drag out the friction of the goodbye. I grabbed my carry-on, offered them both a final, reassuring nod, and turned toward the security queue.

I took my shoes off, emptied my pockets, and walked through the metal detector. As I grabbed my bag off the X-ray belt, I looked back over my shoulder across the vast ticketing lobby.

Jessica and Rachel were still right there. Jessica gave a small wave, and Rachel had one hand resting firmly on the back of Jessica's wheelchair, perfectly anchored to her new guardian.

I turned away and walked down the concourse toward my gate. My chest ached with the physical distance growing between us, but as I looked out the massive glass windows at the tarmac, I felt lighter than I had in a year.

The divorce had tried to destroy us, and the legal system had tried to tear us apart. But standing in the airport, knowing Rachel was safe in King County, I knew the truth. They had completely underestimated the pack.
