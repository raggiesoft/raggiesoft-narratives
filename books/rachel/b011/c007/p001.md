The Thanksgiving reprieve was officially over.

My duffel bag was packed and sitting by the front door of the Federal Way apartment. The emergency injunction had secured Rachel’s permanent residency in Washington, completely neutralizing Dad's custody, but the logistical reality of my own life still had to be addressed. I had a high school to get back to in Virginia Beach, and my non-refundable return flight was departing from Portland International Airport.

Because we had successfully fled Clark County to barricade ourselves in King County, getting me to my gate meant the three of us had to make the long transit trek back down the coast.

We locked the apartment and wheeled Jessica out to the street. The crisp, gray morning air of the Pacific Northwest was bracing, but my nervous system was profoundly calm.

We boarded a local TAPS bus, letting it carry us to the regional transit center in Tacoma. From there, we transferred to the southbound American Rail Transit Service (ARTS) train.

We claimed a block of seats near the ADA securement area. As the heavy train accelerated, tracing the lush, pine-covered coastline of Washington down toward Oregon, Rachel sat directly beside me. She didn't press her entire body weight against my ribs the way she did when she was terrified. Instead, she just laced her fingers securely through mine, resting her head lightly against my shoulder.

"I'm going to miss you," Rachel said, her voice quiet over the rhythmic clacking of the train cars.

"I'm going to miss you too," I replied, squeezing her hand. "But it isn't like last time. You aren't trapped in a walk-up with him. You're with Jess."

Rachel looked across the aisle at our older sister, who was reading a thick textbook for her upcoming university exams. The panic that usually accompanied a change in our routine was entirely absent from Rachel's dark eyes. The environment was safe.

"And I have the cards," Rachel added, tapping the pocket of her cargo pants where she kept the massive stack of prepaid calling cards Mom had given us. "We can talk every single day."

"Every single day," I promised.
