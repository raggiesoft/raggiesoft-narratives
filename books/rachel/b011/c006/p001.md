The morning light filtering through the Federal Way apartment was soft and gray, but the atmosphere inside the bedroom was incredibly warm.

I woke up exactly where I had fallen asleep: anchored in the center of the bed, perfectly wedged between my sisters. Jessica stretched on my left, letting out a quiet yawn, while Rachel shifted against my right ribs, her breathing steady and calm.

"Good morning everyone," Jessica whispered, her voice thick with sleep but laced with a profound, unshakeable relief. "Sleep well?"

"Yes," Rachel murmured, her face still buried half in her pillow.

"Best I've slept in months," I admitted, and it was the absolute truth. The threat matrix was offline. The perimeter was secure.

Jessica pushed herself up onto her elbows, a warm smile spreading across her face. "Let's stay in pajamas all day. All the stores are closed, and we aren't going anywhere."

"Pajama Day," Rachel agreed softly, the rigid safety of the new rule instantly settling her nervous system.

We slowly untangled ourselves and made our way out into the living room. There was no frantic rushing, no pacing, and no bracing for a slammed door. The apartment just smelled faintly of the compound butter and pie crusts we had prepped the night before.

I went into the kitchen and pulled three ceramic bowls from the cabinet. I grabbed the milk and a yellow box of Harvest Maize Grids—our absolute favorite, reliably crunchy cereal—from the pantry. I poured the precise, necessary ratio of cold milk to crispy corn squares and carried the bowls over to the coffee table.

Jessica had wheeled herself out to the living room and hit the power button on the television.

"Just in time," she said, taking her bowl.

The screen flickered to life, broadcasting the 75th Annual Hudson's Thanksgiving Day Parade live from the streets of New York City. The network hosts were bundled in heavy winter coats, their breath pluming in the cold November air.

Even three thousand miles away on the Left Coast, the atmosphere of the broadcast was heavy and distinct. It was November 2001. The national trauma of September was still incredibly raw, and the parade was leaning heavily into a message of national resilience. The television cameras panned over a massive, moving Lady Liberty float, followed immediately by a somber but beautiful tribute float honoring the city's first responders, surrounded by spectators holding American flags.

"Look," Rachel said, sitting cross-legged on the carpet next to my spot on the sofa, her spoon paused halfway to her mouth.

Floating down the urban canyon of the television screen was a massive, bright yellow balloon shaped like a very famous, very recognizable electric pocket monster.

"Volt the Electric Mouse," I noted, watching the handler crews in their matching jumpsuits struggle to keep the giant balloon steady in the crosswinds.

"And there's Tommy Proton," Jessica pointed out a few minutes later, as the massive balloon of the boy genius with the impossibly tall hair floated past a skyscraper. "And the Crawl-Kids."

Rachel hummed a soft note of approval. The predictability of the broadcast was a sensory dream. Every year, Timothy Turkey led the march, followed by the high school marching bands, the novelty balloons, and the structured, inevitable arrival of Santa Claus at the end to officially kick off the holiday season.

We sat there in the quiet living room, eating our Harvest Maize Grids in our soft cotton pajamas. We watched Buster the Hound float horizontally past the screen, followed by a massive, smiling bipedal red dog.

There were no screaming parents. There were no court orders hanging over our heads. There was just the mechanical crunch of our cereal, the cheerful, orchestrated noise of the Hudson's Parade echoing from the TV speakers, and the absolute certainty that our pack was exactly where we were supposed to be.
