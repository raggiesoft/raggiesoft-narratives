The microwave timer beeped from the kitchen, a sharp electronic tone that cut through the jungle music of the Super Nova 16.

Jessica closed her heavy university textbook and pushed it toward the center of the dining table. "Alright, pack," she called out, wheeling herself toward the kitchen. "Pause the game. It's three-thirty. Time for the final push."

Rachel and I saved our 101% file, powered down the console, and walked over to the kitchen island.

The apartment was overwhelmingly warm, completely saturated with the rich, savory smell of the roasting turkey. The environment felt incredibly safe, but Jessica had a specific, secondary objective for the afternoon. She wasn't just managing a timeline; she was actively reprogramming our survival skills.

Jessica locked the brakes on her wheelchair at the end of the counter and looked at both of us.

"Mom and Dad treated the kitchen like a hazard zone," Jessica stated, her voice calm but carrying a heavy, undeniable truth. "Whenever they cooked, it was a high-stress environment. They yelled if we got too close, they complained about the mess, and they never actually explained how anything worked. As a result, neither of you actually knows how to feed yourselves."

I shifted my weight, leaning against the counter. She was right. At the Delaney Street house, the kitchen was Mom's domain, and her anxiety always spiked when she was cooking. Dad just expected food to appear in front of him.

"That ends today," Jessica declared, pulling two aprons out of a drawer and tossing them onto the island. "If we are going to be a fully independent, functional pack, you both need to know how to navigate a kitchen without panicking. Today, we learn the algorithms of cooking."

Rachel hummed softly, picking up an apron and slipping the loop over her head. The word *algorithm* instantly activated her neurodivergent brain. Algorithms meant rules. Rules meant predictability.

"Rachel, you are on stuffing assembly," Jessica instructed, pointing to the massive Poly-Store Bin of torn bread squares from the night before, and the glass bowl of chopped celery and onions. "Cooking is just chemistry. If you follow the ratios, the outcome is guaranteed. I need you to combine the dry bread with the vegetables, and then slowly pour in exactly two cups of chicken broth while stirring."

Rachel went straight to work. She loved exact measurements. She carefully poured the chopped vegetables over the bread, grabbed a large wooden spoon, and began methodically folding the mixture together. The tactile, repetitive motion of stirring was perfectly regulating for her.

"Michael," Jessica said, turning her attention to me. "You are on potatoes. But we are going to do it safely."

She pointed to a five-pound bag of russet potatoes and a vegetable peeler. "Peel them, cube them into one-inch squares, and drop them into the large metal pot on the stove."

I took my station. The mechanical rhythm of peeling the potatoes—stripping away the rough brown skin to reveal the starchy white center—was deeply satisfying. Once I had a small mountain of cubes, I pushed them into the heavy pot.

Jessica wheeled over to the sink and pulled out the extendable faucet head, stretching it over to the stove to fill the pot directly on the burner.

"This is a structural workaround," Jessica explained, watching me. "A massive pot of water is incredibly heavy. When it’s boiling, it is a severe burn hazard. Because your CP affects your balance, you should never try to lift and carry a boiling pot of water across the kitchen to the sink to drain it."

I stared at the pot, suddenly realizing she had already analyzed my physical limitations and engineered a fail-safe. "Then how do I get the potatoes out?"

Jessica opened a drawer and handed me a large, stainless steel spider strainer.

"You don't move the water," she smiled. "You move the solid mass. When the potatoes are fork-tender, you turn the burner off, and you use the strainer to scoop the potatoes directly from the water into a mixing bowl on the counter. The heavy, dangerous water stays exactly where it is until it cools down."

It was a brilliant, simple modification. It completely eliminated the physical danger and the anxiety of losing my balance.

For the next forty-five minutes, the kitchen operated like a well-oiled machine. There was no yelling, no frantic rushing, and no one being told they were in the way. Jessica was a calm, authoritative teacher, guiding us through the sensory landscape of the kitchen. She showed Rachel how to tell when the stuffing had absorbed enough moisture, and she showed me how to test the potatoes by easily piercing a cube with the tines of a fork.

We weren't just following orders; we were acquiring fundamental life skills. The adults who were supposed to teach us how to survive had failed, so our twenty-year-old sister had stepped in to completely rebuild our foundation.

At 4:15 PM, Jessica reached up and hit the cancel button on the oven.

"Alright," Jessica announced, grabbing a pair of heavy oven mitts. "The bird is at temperature. Michael, open the door."

I pulled the oven door down. Jessica gripped the massive roasting pan and, with a coordinated heave, slid it out of the oven and onto the top of the stove to rest. The turkey was perfectly golden brown, the skin crackling slightly in the heat.

"Oven is clear," I said.

"Rachel, slide the stuffing and the green bean casserole in," Jessica directed.

Rachel carefully carried the foil-covered dishes to the oven and placed them on the center rack. I shut the door, locking in the final baking cycle.

"Excellent work, team," Jessica said, looking at the resting turkey and the bubbling pots. "Michael, scoop those potatoes. Rachel, start setting the dining table. It's almost time to eat."
