"No, Jess, you didn't miss Timothy Turkey," I said, pointing at the television screen with my spoon. "He hasn't come down the avenue yet."

Jessica parked her wheelchair back in her spot next to the coffee table and picked up her bowl of Harvest Maize Grids. The tense, rigid posture she had held during the phone call completely melted away as she took a bite, her eyes locking back onto the screen.

The broadcast cut away from the marching bands on the street and switched to the main camera stationed directly in front of the flagship Hudson's department store. The pavement was painted with a massive, colorful holiday star.

"Oh, quiet," Jessica hushed us playfully, waving her spoon. "The signature act is starting."

The 2001 parade, carrying the heavy emotional weight of the city's recent tragedy, had pulled out all the stops for its marquee performances. The television hosts, their voices thick with patriotic reverence, announced the arrival of the legendary Empire City Precision Dancers.

A massive line of women, all dressed in glittering, identical costumes, marched out onto the painted star. The music swelled—a triumphant, brass-heavy orchestral arrangement—and the dancers snapped into absolute, flawless synchronization.

Rachel leaned forward on the carpet, completely mesmerized.

For a neurodivergent brain, the Precision Dancers were a sensory masterpiece. There was no chaotic, unpredictable movement. It was pure, mathematical perfection. Fifty dancers moving as a single, unified entity, their legs kicking high in the air at the exact same millisecond, their arms linked to form an unbreakable chain.

"They move exactly the same," Rachel whispered in awe, her eyes tracking the mesmerizing geometry of the kick-line. "Nobody makes a mistake."

"It takes hundreds of hours of practice to get that perfect," Jessica explained softly, watching the screen. "They have to memorize the muscle memory of the routine until they don't even have to think about it anymore."

Rachel smiled at the phrase *muscle memory*. She reached down and tapped her fingers lightly against her own knee, mimicking the precise, repetitive rhythm of the marimba keys she had mapped out on the practice field back in August. She understood exactly how they did it.

I sat back on the sofa, eating my cereal and watching my sisters. The television screen was filled with bright colors, smiling crowds, and the reassuring, rigid structure of a choreographed dance routine.

Dad was sitting in a gloomy apartment three thousand miles away, sulking over a hung-up phone and a broken relationship. But here, inside the Federal Way apartment, we had built our own synchronized routine. We were linked together just as tightly as the dancers on the screen, moving to our own predictable rhythm, completely insulated from the chaos of the adults who had failed us.

As the dancers hit their final, flawless pose and the crowd on the television erupted into cheers, the camera panned up to the sky. The massive, wobbling head of Timothy Turkey finally floated into view, guided safely down the avenue by his handlers.

Rachel pointed excitedly at the screen, letting out a soft, happy hum. We were exactly where we needed to be.
