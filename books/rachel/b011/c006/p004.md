The final hour of the Hudson's Parade played out exactly as it always did, offering the profound, regulating comfort of a completely unchangeable routine.

The marching bands cleared the avenue, the final celebrity musical performances wrapped up on the painted star, and the television hosts' voices shifted into a tone of breathless, manufactured anticipation.

"Here he comes," I announced, setting my empty cereal bowl down on the coffee table.

Rachel sat up straighter, crossing her legs perfectly in front of her.

The massive, intricately carved sleigh float glided onto the screen, pulled by the animatronic reindeer. At the very top, waving cheerfully to the freezing crowds in Empire City, was Santa Claus.

It was the ultimate, predictable closer. The broadcast never surprised us with a different ending. It was a fixed, reliable anchor that officially closed the door on autumn and initiated the winter holidays. As the television cameras pulled back for a wide aerial shot of the avenue and the credits began to roll, the structured entertainment of the morning was officially complete.

Jessica reached over and hit the power button on the remote, plunging the living room back into a comfortable, quiet calm.

"Right," Jessica sighed, stretching her arms over her head. "The parade is over. The oven is preheated. It's almost time to put the bird in."

I stood up, picking up my bowl and Rachel’s empty bowl, and walked them into the kitchen. As I set them in the sink, a sudden, critical variable snapped into my analytical brain. I had chopped the celery, and I had seen the potatoes, but there was one piece of the Thanksgiving matrix I hadn't verified.

I turned around, leaning against the counter. "Jess. We need to talk about the cranberry sauce."

Jessica paused her wheelchair halfway to the kitchen. She raised an eyebrow. "What about it?"

"Did you buy the right kind?" I asked, my voice deadly serious. In the Delaney Street house, Mom's relatives always tried to bring homemade cranberry sauce—a terrifying, unpredictable compote of whole berries, orange zest, and chunks of walnuts that violated every sensory rule I possessed.

Rachel immediately pushed herself up off the carpet and walked into the kitchen to stand directly beside me.

"It has to be the can," Rachel stated, her voice quiet but possessing absolute authority. "It has to come out in the shape of the can."

"There is no other type of cranberry sauce," I agreed, crossing my arms. "If it doesn't have the metallic ridges printed into the side of the jelly, we don't recognize it. We don't accept it."

Jessica looked at both of us standing there, forming a united, neurodivergent front over a gelatinous fruit cylinder. A slow, knowing smile spread across her face.

She wheeled herself over to the pantry, opened the folding door, and reached down to the bottom shelf.

"Do you honestly think I would risk an unmapped sensory texture on our first independent Thanksgiving?" Jessica asked.

She pulled her hand out and set two heavy, aluminum cans onto the kitchen island. They were generic brand, solid, and completely devoid of any whole fruit chunks.

"Jellied," Jessica confirmed, tapping the top of the can with her fingernail. "It will slide out of this tin with a horrifying squelching noise, it will retain the exact shape of the cylinder, and we will slice it into perfectly uniform, quarter-inch discs. As the universe intended."

Rachel let out a long, happy breath. She reached out and touched the cold metal of the can, verifying its presence. The final, critical piece of the meal’s predictability was locked in.

"Excellent," I nodded, a massive wave of relief washing over me.

"Now," Jessica clapped her hands together. "If the Cranberry Committee is satisfied, who wants to help me carry this massive turkey to the oven?"
