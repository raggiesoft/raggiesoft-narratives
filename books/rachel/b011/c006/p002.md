The shrill ring of the kitchen landline cut straight through the cheerful, brassy marching band music of the Hudson's Parade.

Rachel froze instantly. The spoon hovering over her bowl of Harvest Maize Grids stopped dead in the air, her dark eyes darting toward the wall phone. Even with the legal shield in place, the sudden, unpredictable noise was a sharp reminder of the outside world.

Jessica didn't hesitate. She rolled her wheelchair back from the coffee table and navigated smoothly into the kitchen. She pulled the receiver off the wall hook and immediately pressed the speakerphone button, ensuring the entire pack had full situational awareness.

"Federal Way residence," Jessica answered, her tone brisk and entirely business-like.

"Jess, please," Dad’s voice filtered through the small plastic speaker. He didn't sound authoritative or demanding today. He sounded exhausted, pathetic, and entirely alone in his second-floor walk-up. "It’s Thanksgiving. Can we just have a truce for the holiday? Have a little mercy?"

Jessica’s expression didn't soften by a single millimeter. Her posture remained perfectly rigid in her wheelchair.

"That ship sailed the exact moment you ripped Michael and Rachel apart, Dad," Jessica replied, her voice dropping into absolute ice.

Dad let out a heavy, frustrated sigh, the sound crackling with static. "I'm just sitting here in this apartment—"

"Since you have so much free time today," Jessica interrupted smoothly, expertly pivoting the conversation to the ultimate leverage, "how is your studying going? Have you opened the high school equivalency book I bought you?"

Dead silence fell over the line. The faint, muffled sound of a television playing in the background of his apartment was the only response. He had absolutely no answer. He hadn't touched the heavy textbook.

Jessica scoffed, a short, clinical sound of disappointment. "I’m not surprised. But let me remind you of the deal we made. I already made your December car payment, so your sedan is safe through the holidays. But if you don't step up and get your high school equivalency, the bailouts stop in January. I am not funding your stagnation."

"You can't hold my car over my head every time we talk!" Dad’s voice began to rise, his fragile ego finally cracking under the humiliation. The desperate plea for mercy instantly morphed into his familiar, defensive anger. "You don't get to dictate my life!"

"I am dictating Rachel's life now, because you couldn't," Jessica fired back, her own anger flaring, backed by absolute, impenetrable legal authority. "I have the Virginia injunction, the Aero-Transit paperwork is on its way, and I am enrolling Rachel in the Federal Way public school system as soon as Monday rolls around."

"She is my daughter!" Dad yelled, his temper fully snapping. "Put her on the phone! Let me talk to Michael and Rachel right now!"

"No," Jessica stated, leaving absolutely no room for negotiation. "You may not talk to Michael, and you may not talk to Rachel."

"Jessica Marie, I am your father, and I demand—"

"Adjust your attitude before you call this number again," Jessica ordered, cutting right through his heavy parent voice.

She slammed her hand down on the receiver. The satisfying, mechanical *click* echoed loudly through the apartment, instantly severing the connection and terminating his access to our sanctuary.

Jessica took a deep breath, smoothing out the front of her t-shirt, and turned her wheelchair back toward the living room. The tense, crackling energy of the phone call dissipated almost immediately, replaced once again by the cheerful, predictable noise of the Hudson's Parade on the television.

Rachel slowly lowered her spoon back into her cereal bowl. The panic in her eyes had completely vanished. She looked at Jessica, then looked at me, a profound sense of safety washing over her features. Our older sister had just gone toe-to-toe with the man who had terrified us for months, and she had shut him down with effortless, structural precision.

"Now," Jessica said, grabbing her cereal bowl off the coffee table and offering us a warm, genuine smile. "Where were we? Did I miss the giant turkey balloon?"
