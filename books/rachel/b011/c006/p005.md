The oven preheat alarm beeped, a sharp, singular electronic tone that signaled the next phase of the timeline.

I walked over to the refrigerator and pulled open the heavy double doors. Sitting on the bottom shelf, taking up almost the entire width of the glass, was the massive, dark-enameled roasting pan containing our turkey.

When Jessica had originally engineered this holiday menu weeks ago, the variable parameters had been entirely different. She had planned the meal expecting a headcount of five: me, Rachel, Jessica, Dad, and Cathy. Because Jessica approached grocery shopping with the same over-engineered, fail-safe mentality she applied to her Quantum IT networks, she hadn't purchased a modest bird.

"Jess," I said, staring into the fridge, my analytical brain running the rough calculations based on the sheer volume of the poultry. "How big is this turkey?"

"Eighteen pounds," Jessica replied from the kitchen island, adjusting the brakes on her wheelchair so she was firmly locked into place.

"Eighteen pounds," I repeated, turning to look at her. "For three people."

"It was originally for five," Jessica reminded me calmly. "But the headcount changed, and you can't return a frozen turkey. Besides, the mathematical outcome is highly favorable."

Rachel, who had been standing quietly beside me, let out a sudden, sharp gasp of realization. She didn't reach for her AAC laptop. She just looked at me, her dark eyes wide with anticipation, and signed two words using rapid, excited ASL.

Bread. Meat.

"Leftover sandwiches," I translated out loud, the profound gravity of the situation hitting me.

There is an unspoken, universal truth about Thanksgiving: the actual dinner is merely the prerequisite for the leftover sandwiches. Cold, sliced turkey, a thick layer of jellied cranberry sauce, and a smear of compound butter between two slices of soft bread. And with an eighteen-pound bird for three people, our supply of sandwich material was essentially infinite.

Rachel hummed a loud, vibrating note of pure joy.

"Exactly," Jessica smiled. "Now, I need a structural assist. Eighteen pounds of dead weight plus a heavy roasting pan is a lot of torque on my shoulders from a seated position. Rachel, I need your help."

Rachel loved heavy work. For a neurodivergent brain, lifting heavy, solid objects provided deep proprioceptive input—it told her muscles and joints exactly where she was in space, acting as an intense, physical grounding mechanism.

She stepped up beside Jessica. I carefully slid the massive roasting pan out of the fridge and carried it the three steps over to the stove, resting it heavily on the front burners so they could get a proper grip.

"Okay," Jessica instructed, grabbing the left handle of the pan. "Grab the right handle. Keep your back straight. On three, we lift, and Michael opens the oven door. Ready?"

Rachel nodded, wrapping both of her small hands around the thick metal handle. Her posture was rigid and focused.

"One. Two. Three."

Together, they hoisted the massive pan. I pulled the oven door down, a wave of intense, dry heat washing over our faces. Moving in perfect synchronization, Jessica wheeled herself forward an inch as Rachel guided the right side of the pan until it was hovering directly over the bottom rack.

"Down," Jessica commanded.

The heavy metal pan landed on the oven rack with a loud, satisfying *clank*.

I pushed the rack inside and slammed the oven door shut. The heavy lifting was officially done.

"Perfect execution," Jessica praised, unlocking her wheelchair brakes and rolling back from the heat. She reached up and hit the timer button on the microwave, setting it for three and a half hours. "The bird is in. We have absolutely nothing to do until three-thirty."

Rachel looked at the glowing green numbers counting down on the microwave, then looked at the closed oven door. The timeline was locked. The parameters were set. She had infinite leftover sandwiches in her future, and she didn't have to navigate a single chaotic social interaction to get them.

She turned around, walked directly into the living room, and booted up her Super Nova 16 console. The holiday was running flawlessly.
