Rachel giggled, her dark eyes lighting up as she stepped fully into her element. The overwhelming stress of the last two months vanished, replaced by the rigid, predictable safety of a public transportation grid.

"Here in Portland, the airport is connected directly by the light rail," she explained rapidly, leading us toward the exit doors of the baggage claim. "Then we take a couple of buses across the river into Vancouver. Jess got a hotel since Daddy still lives upstairs in his apartment, and the hotel is right on the bus line."

We pushed through the heavy glass doors and out into the biting November air.

"Welcome to the Pacific Northwest," Rachel said, shivering slightly as both she and Jessica pulled the collars of their heavy coats tighter.

It was raining—a constant, blustery, sideways drizzle that instantly dampened my hair and smelled like wet pine and jet exhaust. It was a stark, sensory departure from the humid coastal air of Virginia, an apparent trademark of living in this part of the country.

We made our way down the covered walkway to the airport's train platform. Sitting beneath the glowing red digital arrival sign were a row of ticket vending machines.

"Hold on, pilot," Jessica instructed, tapping the brake on her wheelchair. "We need fare."

I parked her in front of the machine. Jessica pulled her wallet from her bag and started tapping the touch screen. "I'm buying us All-Day Passes for the Columbia Transit Service. It covers the trains and all the buses on both sides of the river."

The machine whirred, spitting out two thick, magnetically striped paper cards. She handed one to me.

"What about you?" I asked, looking down at Rachel.

Rachel reached into the pocket of her cargo pants and proudly produced a hard plastic card with her photo printed on the front. "I have a CTS Youth Pass. Dad had to buy it for me to get to school. It gives me unlimited rides anywhere in the entire system network. I know the whole map."

"She really does," Jessica smiled, tucking her day pass into her pocket. "She's basically a walking CTS schedule now."

The glowing red sign above us ticked down to zero. A long, sleek electric train glided quietly into the station, its doors sliding open with a soft chime.

We boarded through the front doors. I carefully pushed Jessica’s wheelchair over the small gap and maneuvered her into the designated ADA area, locking her brakes into place. Rachel immediately grabbed the seat right next to her, pulling a folded transit map from her pocket and spreading it open on her lap.

"Okay, look," Rachel said, tapping a red line on the glossy paper. "We need to take this train to Rose Quarter. Then we catch the Yellow Line up to Delta Park/Vanport. From there, we catch a bus across the river to Vancouver, then one more bus to the hotel."

I looked up from the map to the glowing digital destination sign at the front of the train car.

"Beaverton?" I asked, my brow furrowing as the doors chimed shut and the train began to accelerate. "I thought we were going to Portland."

"This train goes through Downtown Portland, then it crosses the river and continues all the way west to Beaverton," my little sister explained patiently, her finger tracing the route. "We are getting off before then. We change trains at Rose Quarter."

The rigid structure of the transit system was exactly what my brain needed. I leaned against the wall next to Jessica’s chair, letting the smooth, rhythmic sway of the train and the quiet hum of the electric motors soothe my frayed nerves. For thirty minutes, I didn't have to think about court orders or the divorce; I just had to follow the colored lines on Rachel's map.

We arrived at the Rose Quarter transit center, stepping out into the cold rain.

"This way," Rachel pointed, taking the lead without hesitation. "We have to change platforms for the Yellow Line."

Rachel took the handle of my heavy floral suitcase, dragging it behind her so I could keep both hands firmly on the push-handles of Jessica’s wheelchair. I piloted Jess through the crowded, wet transit plaza, following Rachel’s bright winter coat toward the northbound platform.

The Yellow Line train arrived a few minutes later, carrying us north out of the city center. The ride to the Delta Park station was short.

"Okay, end of the line for the trains," Rachel announced as we stepped off onto the platform. "The 6 will take us across the state line into Vancouver, and from there we catch the 4 to the hotel."

A massive, articulated CTS bus pulled up to the curb. The driver lowered the hydraulic ramp, and I pushed Jessica aboard.

As the bus merged onto the interstate and rumbled over the massive steel bridge spanning the dark, choppy waters of the Columbia River, the reality of the distance finally sank in. I was three thousand miles from home, riding a bus through the Pacific Northwest rain. But as I looked down at Jessica securing her wheelchair brakes, and watched Rachel excitedly tracking our progress out the window, the geography didn't matter.

The 4 bus dropped us off a block from the hotel. The rain was coming down harder now. Rachel grabbed my suitcase again, laughing as the cold wind whipped her hair across her face, while I pushed Jessica as fast as I could through the sliding glass doors of the hotel lobby. We were soaking wet, exhausted from travel, but our pack was finally back together.
