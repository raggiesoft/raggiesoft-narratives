We arrived in the room on the second floor, and Jessica swiped her keycard. The heavy hotel door clicked open, and we pushed inside, dragging the luggage out of the hallway.

I immediately pushed the door shut behind us, engaging the deadbolt and flipping the metal security latch into place. It was a reflex I hadn't been able to turn off since September. The perimeter was secured.

I turned around and took in the layout of the room. It was standard, generic hotel decor, but dominating the center of the space was a massive, king-sized mattress.

"One bed?" I asked, dropping my backpack onto a nearby armchair.

"Yeah," Jessica giggled from her wheelchair, a warm, knowing smile spreading across her face. "It’s big enough to share."

Before I could say anything else, Rachel stepped forward and wrapped her arms around my waist. It wasn't the frantic, desperate crash of the airport terminal. This was a deliberate, heavy, grounding squeeze. She buried her face into my chest, pressing her weight against me to create the deep physical pressure our nervous systems craved.

"We miss sharing the sofa," Rachel murmured into my shirt, her voice muffled but fiercely honest. "We miss triple sibling cuddles."

I wrapped my arms around her shoulders, locking her in tightly. "I missed it too," I admitted.

"Alright, as much as I love this, we are all soaking wet from the Portland rain," Jessica declared, taking on her familiar role as the oldest sibling directing the flow of the room. "Toilet, change into PJs, and let's start heading to bed."

"I suppose we both can help you," Rachel said, pulling back from my chest and looking at our older sister.

"Yes, please," Jessica replied, blushing slightly and giggling.

Because of Jessica's cerebral palsy, the physical mechanics of changing clothes, especially out of heavy, wet winter gear, could be exhausting for her to do alone. In the neurotypical world, needing help changing might be a source of embarrassment, but inside our pack, it was simply an established routine of care. There was absolute, unfiltered trust between the three of us.

We took turns using the bathroom, trading our damp street clothes for the incredibly familiar, soft textures of our worn-out cotton pajamas. Once Rachel and I were changed, we worked together to help Jessica out of her chair, carefully maneuvering her into her own sleepwear, completely devoid of any awkwardness.

When we were all finally in our familiar textures, we climbed into the massive hotel bed.

The hotel comforter was thick and heavy, providing an immediate, weighted blanket effect over my legs. We naturally fell into an arrangement that maximized physical contact, but this time, the geometry shifted to reflect exactly what we had been missing. I climbed into the absolute center of the mattress.

My sisters immediately closed the distance. Jessica settled in on my left side, resting her head gently against my shoulder, while Rachel practically fused herself to my right side, throwing her arm across my ribs and burying her face against my neck. I wrapped my arms around both of them, pulling them in flush against my sides.

I was the anchor, and for the first time in two months, my pack was properly tethered to me.

It was a tightly woven knot of limbs and shared body heat. The deep, restrictive pressure of having my sisters pressed against my ribs from both sides instantly sent a massive wave of regulation through my nervous system.

For two months, my brain had been operating in a state of high-alert, solitary survival. Now, squeezed tightly in the center of the bed, listening to the synchronized rhythm of their breathing, the threat level finally dropped to zero.

"I will admit," I whispered into the quiet room, looking left at Jessica and then right at Rachel, "I really missed sibling cuddles."

Rachel shifted her arm, her fingers curling tightly into the fabric of my t-shirt directly over my heart.

"Me too," Rachel breathed, her dark eyes completely soft, stripped of the anger and resentment she had been carrying all day.

I squeezed her shoulder, and then I squeezed Jessica’s. My chest tightened, not with anxiety, but with a massive, overwhelming surge of emotion that I didn't have a mathematical formula to process. The last time I had felt this exact sensation, we were standing in the foyer of the Delaney Street house, and I was handing Rachel over to a completely terrifying, unknown universe.

I looked at Rachel, taking in the reality that she was safe, and then at Jessica, who had orchestrated this entire miracle. We had survived the divorce. We had survived the grounding of the skies. We had defied the geography that tried to keep us apart.

"I love you," I whispered into the dark room.

It was only the second time those words had ever crossed my lips. We didn't use neurotypical emotional buzzwords; we proved our bond through action. But the sheer magnitude of this reunion demanded the verbal override.

Rachel’s breath hitched. She pressed her face harder into my neck, her body shaking with a sudden, silent sob of absolute relief.

"I love you too, Michael," Rachel whispered back, her voice breaking on the vow, echoing the exact same promise we had made on September 20th.

On my left, Jessica let out a soft, watery sob. As the neurotypical sister, she knew exactly how monumental and rare it was for her two autistic siblings to vocalize that phrase. She reached across my chest, resting her hand over Rachel's where it gripped my shirt, physically connecting all three of us.

"I love you both so much," Jessica wept quietly, a tear tracking down her cheek and soaking into my pajama shirt. "My beautiful, perfect pack."

We didn't say anything else. We didn't need to. We just pulled the heavy hotel blankets up to our chins, squeezed each other as tightly as we possibly could around the center, and let the rain beat against the windowpane outside, completely safe within our reunited perimeter.
