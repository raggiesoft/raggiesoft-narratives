The cross-country flight from Virginia had been a grueling, anxiety-inducing marathon. Flying just two months after the airspace had been weaponized meant the entire atmosphere of commercial aviation was thick with a heavy, unspoken tension. I had spent the last seven hours rigidly gripping the armrests, my nervous system completely frayed.

When I finally stepped off the jet bridge and walked down the sprawling, carpeted concourse of Portland International Airport, I was running on absolute fumes.

I followed the flow of passengers toward the exit of the secure area. The new reality of the world was on full display at the boundary line. A pair of National Guard soldiers stood on either side of the exit corridor, dressed in full green woodland camouflage fatigues, their M16 rifles slung across their chests. They were the new, permanent fixtures of the American airport, enforcing the strict rule that only ticketed passengers were allowed past the checkpoints.

It meant the traditional airport greeting was gone. I knew I had to cross the security threshold before I could find my anchors.

I stepped past the soldiers and out into the massive, echoing ticketing lobby, scanning the thick crowd of waiting families. I was fully expecting to see the rigid, stressed posture of my father standing next to Rachel.

Instead, my eyes locked onto a familiar mop of dark hair.

Rachel was standing near a concrete pillar, shifting her weight anxiously from foot to foot. But the real shock—the variable that completely short-circuited my brain—was the person sitting right beside her.

Jessica.

My eyes went wide. I stopped dead in my tracks for a fraction of a second, my brain struggling to process the visual data. Jessica lived in Washington State, near Seattle. She was supposed to be hours away at college. But there she was, sitting in her familiar manual wheelchair, a massive, bright smile breaking across her face as she spotted me.

My eyes lit up in absolute excitement. I dropped the heavy, analytical guard I had been holding up for the entire flight and ran forward.

I dropped my carry-on bag onto the polished floor and crashed into them. I wrapped one arm securely around Rachel's waist and leaned down to wrap the other around Jessica’s shoulders, pulling them both into a tight, desperate, grounding hug.

"I missed both of you so much," I said, my voice thick. The crushing, suffocating weight of the last few months—the broken doors, the grounded planes, the empty house in Virginia—finally lifted off my chest. The pack was together.

"I missed you too," they both said in unison, the synchronized response a perfect echo of our childhood.

I pulled back, keeping a hand firmly on Rachel's shoulder just to prove to my nervous system that she was actually real and physically present.

"I assume you need to claim baggage," Jessica said, looking up from her wheelchair, her eyes shining with unshed tears of relief.

"I do," I nodded, my brain finally catching up to the logistics of the room. I looked around the crowd again, confused. "Dad isn’t here?"

Rachel’s posture instantly went rigid. The joy of the reunion was momentarily eclipsed by a dark, simmering anger. She rolled her eyes, her jaw clenching. "He had to work. Ugh!"

The sheer resentment in her voice was radioactive. Dad had completely uprooted her life, dragged her three thousand miles away from her safe environment, and yet he couldn't even be bothered to request a Saturday afternoon off from his job at the storage facility to greet his son at the airport. He had pawned the responsibility off on his teenage daughter.

"Jess, I thought you lived up near Seattle. Yes, I’m happy to see you and everything, but how..." I trailed off, stepping behind her and instinctively gripping the rubber push-handles of her wheelchair. The tactile familiarity of piloting her chair instantly grounded me further.

We made our way through the crowded terminal toward PDX’s baggage claim area down on the lower level.

"I took my paid vacation from work," Jessica explained, looking over her shoulder at me with a triumphant grin. "I mapped out the entire transit schedule and planned everything with Rachel behind his back so we could both be here to greet you. I wanted our family together."

We reached the massive, rotating luggage carousel. The heavy buzzer sounded, and the metal belt started to move.

"That is my bag," I said, pointing as I stepped forward and pulled one of the heavy floral suitcases off of the belt. It was the exact same suitcase Dad had used to pack Rachel's life away two months ago, repurposed for my trip. I set it upright and gripped the handle. "Where to?"
