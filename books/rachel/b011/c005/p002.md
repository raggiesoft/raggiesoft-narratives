The quiet hum of the apartment had barely resumed when the landline rang a second time. Rachel’s shoulders immediately hitched up toward her ears, her dark eyes flashing back to the plastic receiver on the wall.

Jessica didn't hesitate. She rolled over and grabbed the phone, her posture still rigid from the fight with Cathy.

"Federal Way residence."

"Jess? It's Mom."

The tension in Jessica's shoulders instantly dissolved. She let out a long breath and hit the speaker button again so we could all hear.

"Hey, Mom. Is everything okay in Virginia Beach?"

"It's chaotic, but the paperwork is officially in motion," Mom’s voice crackled through the speaker, sounding exhausted but profoundly relieved. "I just walked out of the notary's office. I have the Power of Attorney for Care of a Minor, the Educational Delegation, and the medical consent forms all signed and stamped with a raised seal."

"Did you ship them?" Jessica asked, her brain instantly jumping to the logistics of Monday morning.

"I just dropped the envelope off at the Aero-Transit hub," Mom confirmed. "I paid for the absolute fastest overnight shipping they offer. Because tomorrow is Thanksgiving, the clerk said it will likely be delivered to your apartment on Friday morning. Saturday at the absolute latest."

I watched Rachel process the data from my spot at the kitchen counter. *Aero-Transit*. Physical, notarized papers.

"That gives us plenty of time," Jessica nodded, her tone turning brisk and professional. "The high school in Federal Way won't open their administration offices until Monday morning at 7:30 AM anyway. As soon as I have that Aero-Transit envelope in my hands, I have one hundred percent legal authority to walk in there and get Rachel enrolled as a King County student."

"I also faxed the Virginia injunction to Heritage High School in Clark County this morning," Mom added, ensuring the second half of the strategy was secured. "I formally withdrew her. If Tom tries to call them, they won't even speak to him. Rachel is officially out of his system."

Rachel slowly let go of the game controller. She looked down at the carpet, her chest heaving with a deep, shaky breath. The final, terrifying variable had been neutralized. She wasn't going back to the second-floor walk-up. She was staying here, in the safe zone, with us.

"Thank you, Mom," Rachel rasped softly toward the phone.

"I love you, baby," Mom replied, her voice thick with emotion. "You stay safe with your sister and brother. I'll call you guys tomorrow for the holiday."

The line clicked off.

Jessica turned her wheelchair to face us, a triumphant, brilliant smile breaking across her face. "Well. It's official. Rachel is a Washington state resident."

I walked over and sat down on the floor next to Rachel, bumping my shoulder firmly against hers. She bumped back, a small, genuine smile finally touching her lips.

"Now," Jessica clapped her hands together, shifting the energy in the room from legal warfare to domestic routine. "We have a Thanksgiving dinner to prepare, and I refuse to eat a dry turkey. Michael, grab the bag of Monarch Mills flour from the pantry. We need to start the pie crusts, and the dough needs to chill in the fridge overnight."

The shift to cooking was exactly what our brains needed. Following a recipe was a perfect, predictable algorithm. There were exact measurements, specific sequential steps, and a guaranteed, tangible outcome.

I stood up and went to the pantry, pulling out the heavy paper sack of Monarch Mills flour. Rachel pushed herself off the floor and went straight to the sink to wash her hands, the rhythmic splashing of the water helping to regulate her system after the back-to-back phone calls.

For the next two hours, the Federal Way apartment smelled of butter, flour, and roasting herbs. Jessica directed the kitchen from her wheelchair, measuring out the dry ingredients while Rachel and I handled the tactile, heavy work of cutting the cold butter into the flour.

The physical repetition of working the dough with my hands was incredibly grounding. There were no lawyers, no screaming girlfriends, and no frantic airport terminals. There was just the quiet, methodical safety of the kitchen, the predictable hum of the refrigerator, and the absolute certainty that tomorrow, for the first time in our lives, our pack was going to celebrate a holiday exactly on our own terms.
