By early evening, the pale, gray light of the Pacific Northwest had completely surrendered to the dark, and a steady, rhythmic rain was drumming against the apartment windows. Inside, however, the environment was completely insulated and warm.

We had successfully transitioned the kitchen into a highly efficient, closed-loop assembly line.

Thanksgiving in the Delaney Street house had always been a sensory minefield—packed with loud relatives, overlapping conversations, unpredictable schedules, and the crushing pressure to mask our discomfort and act "proper." But tomorrow, there would be no guests. There was no performance required. It was just the three of us, which meant we could completely dismantle the traditional holiday stress and rebuild it into a predictable, neurodivergent-friendly routine.

"Okay, the pie dough is chilling," Jessica announced, wheeling herself over to the refrigerator and pulling out a massive, heavy roasting pan. Sitting inside was the thawed turkey. "If we do the heavy lifting tonight, tomorrow we just have to put things in the oven and wait."

She parked her chair at the end of the counter. "Michael, you are on the chopping block. Onions, celery, and carrots for the stuffing. Rachel, I need you to tear the bread loaves into small squares."

Rachel happily abandoned her video game and took her place at the kitchen island. She pulled a loaf of bread from the plastic wrapper and began methodically tearing it into perfectly uniform, one-inch squares, dropping them into a massive Poly-Store Bin. It was a repetitive, tactile task—exactly the kind of low-demand physical loop that allowed her nervous system to coast in a state of complete calm.

I took my station at the cutting board. I gripped the heavy chef's knife and started on the celery. Thwack, thwack, thwack. The rhythmic, mechanical sound of the blade hitting the wood was deeply regulating. It was simple math: reduce the whole into equal, manageable fractions.

While we processed the dry ingredients, Jessica tackled the sensory-heavy work. She was mixing a compound butter in a glass bowl, mashing softened butter together with minced garlic, fresh sage, and rosemary. The sharp, earthy smell of the herbs filled the kitchen, but because it was happening in a safe, controlled environment, the strong scent wasn't an assault on our senses. It just smelled like safety.

"I'm going to rub this under the skin of the turkey and let it sit overnight in the fridge," Jessica explained, her hands expertly navigating the prep work. "It locks in the moisture and the flavor. After this, we’ll mix the green bean casserole, cover it in foil, and stick it in the fridge right next to the bird so it's ready to bake."

"It is very quiet here," Rachel noted softly, not looking up from her growing mountain of bread squares. She wasn't complaining; she was savoring the observation.

"It is," I agreed, sliding a pile of diced onions into a glass prep bowl. "No one is yelling."

"And no one is going to yell tomorrow, either," Jessica promised, carefully lifting the skin of the turkey to massage the butter underneath. "We sleep in. We watch the parade on television in our pajamas. We eat when we want to eat."

Rachel hummed a soft, happy note of approval, her hands working steadily.

I looked down the counter at my sisters. The emotional whiplash of the morning—the phone calls, the legal threats, the terrifying realization of how close Rachel had come to being dragged back to Clark County—had completely burned away.

In its place was a profound, unshakeable peace. We were chopping vegetables, tearing bread, and executing a simple holiday protocol, but it felt like a monumental victory. We had successfully defended our perimeter, and tomorrow, we were going to celebrate our pack exactly how we wanted to.
