We eventually arrived back at the hotel in Vancouver, soaked from the rain and exhausted from the massive logistical effort of the day. Jessica swiped her keycard, and the heavy door clicked open.

The rest of us followed her inside, dropping the heavy shopping bags—including Dad's new high school equivalency textbook—onto the carpet.

"Now that we are in private, Dad, let’s talk," Jessica said, parking her wheelchair near the window and turning to face him. "We want to have a nice family dinner. A real one. But we can't do that here on the second floor of a generic hotel."

Dad leaned against the doorframe, still wearing his damp jacket. He looked instantly guarded. "What are you suggesting?"

"Dad, we want you to come to my apartment for a few days," Jessica continued, proposing the next stage of our trip. "Come see where I live in Federal Way."

Dad’s eyes went wide. The geography hit him like a physical blow. "Federal Way? Jessica, that's practically Seattle. That's nearly a hundred and fifty miles north."

"It really isn't that far, considering how far away Rachel and Michael are from Virginia," Jessica countered smoothly. "And before you start complaining about your gas tank, I don't have a car, and we aren't driving up Interstate 5. I am buying us tickets on the ARTS train. We take the rail from Portland straight up to Tacoma, and from there, we catch the TAPS Route 500 bus directly to my apartment complex."

Rachel nodded eagerly, clearly already familiar with the Transit Authority of the Puget Sound schedules. "The train ride is beautiful, Dad. It goes right along the water."

"I'm paying for the tickets," Jessica added, tapping her wallet. "You don't have to spend a dime."

It was the perfect, logic-proof proposal. She had solved the transportation, removed the financial burden, and offered him a chance to actually participate in his children's lives.

But watching Dad from the edge of the hotel bed, my analytical brain saw the absolute panic rising in his chest. He was shaking his head before Jessica even finished speaking.

"I can't," Dad stammered, stepping backward toward the hallway as if the hotel room was suddenly shrinking. "I can't just jump on an ARTS train and disappear to Seattle for a few days."

"Why not?" Rachel demanded, her voice spiking with familiar frustration. "You manage a storage place! Ask for the days off! It's Thanksgiving week!"

"It's not just work, Rachel," Dad said, his voice rising defensively as he desperately reached for an excuse. "It's Cathy. I already crossed the river and spent the entire Sunday in Oregon. I left her at that restaurant."

"So? She was working," Jessica said, her tone flattening.

"She's Italian, Jessica!" Dad snapped, the real source of his terror finally bleeding through. "Do you have any idea how her family treats Sundays? Or the holidays? You don't just ditch an Italian mother on the week of Thanksgiving. She is already furious that you completely hijacked my morning. If I get on a train and leave Vancouver right now, she will lock me out of the apartment."

I stared at him, processing the pathetic reality of his situation. He was a middle-aged man terrified of his girlfriend's wrath. He had completely abandoned Mom, uprooted Rachel, and fractured our family to find "peace" on the Left Coast, only to land himself in a relationship where he was walking on eggshells, too terrified of an argument to spend a holiday with his own children.

But it wasn't just Cathy.

I looked at the shopping bag containing the massive test prep book. Dad was absolutely terrified of his oldest daughter. Sitting in a Vancouver hotel room while she paid for breakfast was humiliating enough. The idea of getting on a train, entering her fully independent, successful apartment in Federal Way, and sleeping under a roof funded by her Quantum tech scholarship would completely shatter whatever fragile fragments of his ego remained.

"I'm going back to my apartment," Dad said, his hand finding the doorknob. He looked at me, then at Rachel, his eyes filled with a cowardly, defensive guilt. "I'll see you before you fly back to Virginia, Michael. I promise."

"Don't bother," Jessica said. Her voice wasn't angry; it was entirely, devastatingly cold. The temperature in the room plummeted. "We aren't coming back to Vancouver. We're spending the holiday at my place."

Dad swallowed hard.

He didn't argue. He just opened the hotel door and slipped out into the hallway, pulling the door shut behind him with a quiet click.

I stared at the closed door, my brain rapidly processing the legal vulnerability of what Jessica had just declared. Rachel was fourteen years old. Dad had primary physical custody. We had already casually crossed state lines into Oregon this morning, and now Jessica was unilaterally announcing that she was taking a minor nearly a hundred and fifty miles north into King County without his explicit consent.

If Dad really wanted to challenge her, he absolutely could. He could have stood in the doorway. He could have threatened to call the police, citing the court order, and legally forced Rachel to stay in Vancouver.

But he didn't. He retreated.

Because Dad had run the threat matrix in his own head, and he knew he was completely outgunned. If he tried to use the legal system against Jessica, she would unleash her Quantum money. She wouldn't just argue with him; she would secretly fund a ruthless, high-priced attorney for Mom to revisit the custody agreement. She would drag his maxed-out credit cards, his lack of high school education, and his self-storage salary into a Virginia Beach courtroom and completely dismantle his life.

He had surrendered Rachel to Jessica because he was terrified of what Jessica could do to him.

Rachel let out a long, shaky breath, her shoulders slumping as the heavy tension finally drained out of the room. The brief, desperate illusion of a reunited family had completely shattered, leaving just the three of us again. But looking at Jessica, I realized we didn't need him. The pack had a new leader.

"Well," Jessica sighed, wheeling herself toward the center of the room. Her business-like demeanor quickly returned, a practiced shield to mask the sting of his rejection. She looked at Rachel and me, offering a soft, reassuring smile. "It's his loss. Rachel, pull up the ARTS train schedule. We need to figure out what time we're leaving for Tacoma."
