The journey from the Vancouver fast-food parking lot to the center of Portland was a masterclass in Rachel’s transit hyper-fixation.

Dad trudged behind us like a sullen, defeated teenager, his hands shoved deep into his jacket pockets. He had completely surrendered his parental authority to the laminated CTS Day Pass Rachel had handed him.

Rachel led the way, checking her digital watch against the printed schedules she had memorized. We caught the local 80 bus back to the Vancouver transit hub, immediately transferred to the 4, and then boarded the heavy, articulated 6 bus to carry us across the state line.

As the massive bus rumbled over the steel bridge spanning the dark, choppy waters of the Columbia River, I looked back at Dad. He was sitting alone in a window seat near the back doors, staring blankly out at the Oregon treeline. He looked incredibly small. Back in Virginia, he had commanded the rooms he walked into. Here, stripped of his car, his house, and his financial leverage, he was just a passenger in a system run by his daughters.

"Delta Park Station," Rachel announced as the bus hissed to a stop. "Everybody off. We catch the train here."

I piloted Jessica down the hydraulic ramp and out into the biting wind of the transit plaza. Dad followed silently, stepping off the bus and standing awkwardly near the ticket machines while Rachel guided us to the Yellow Line platform.

When the sleek electric train glided into the station, Rachel directed the boarding protocol. I locked Jessica’s wheelchair into the designated ADA spot, Rachel took the seat directly beside her, and I stood, holding the overhead rail. Dad awkwardly took a seat across the aisle, avoiding eye contact with all of us.

The train accelerated, carrying us south into the dense, urban sprawl of Portland. The rhythmic, predictable sway of the train car was deeply regulating. Rachel watched the digital stop indicator with absolute focus, her lips moving silently as she tracked the route.

Twenty minutes later, the train slowed, curving onto a raised platform.

"Rose City Center," Rachel announced, standing up and grabbing her coat. "Our stop."
