The train doors chimed open, dropping us off directly across the street from a massive, sprawling concrete and glass structure.

I pushed Jessica down the platform ramp and across the crosswalk, following Rachel through the heavy glass entrance doors. The sensory shift was immediate. The cold, damp wind was instantly replaced by forced hot air, the smell of roasted nuts, and the chaotic, echoing hum of thousands of holiday shoppers.

"Rose City Center," Rachel said excitedly, her eyes wide as she took in the three-story atrium. "The largest mall in Oregon."

We walked toward the center of the massive building, coming to a stop at a glass railing. I looked down in awe. In the absolute center of the bottom floor was a massive, brightly lit indoor ice skating rink. Skaters were gliding in wide circles to faint, piped-in holiday music. It was a completely different universe from the small, utilitarian strip malls of Virginia Beach.

Dad hung back, leaning against a concrete pillar a few feet away from the railing, looking entirely out of place in the festive environment.

"Why are you and Cathy being so stingy?" I asked quietly, looking expectantly at my father, then down at Jessica in her wheelchair as we resumed walking. The stark contrast between Jessica's confident spending and Dad's panicked penny-pinching was glaring.

"Money. I have relatively plenty, and they don’t," Jessica explained plainly as we made our way through one of the main halls of the massive building. She kept her voice low, but the analytical truth of it was sharp. "I want them to do better for themselves, but they don’t want to put in the effort. Just look at how Daddy responded at breakfast to me wanting him to get his education."

"I kind of wanted to have a family for Christmas," Rachel whimpered softly, her anxiety suddenly spiking. The structural tension between Jessica and Dad was fracturing the perfect, harmonious picture she had built in her head. She looked at the ice rink, then back at Jessica. "I didn’t care about money. I just wanted us to be together."

"We understand," Jessica said softly, her expression softening instantly. She understood that her sister was upset that the holiday was already starting off on the wrong foot, especially as our father played a miserable, silent caboose to our little train. She reached out and squeezed Rachel's hand. "Now, Dad. Get up here."

Dad slowly pushed off the pillar and joined us at the railing.

"We get it," Jessica told him, her voice dropping the combative edge she had used at the restaurant. "You don’t feel comfortable mentioning things in front of Cathy. She has her own kids, her own bills. But it is just us, your kids, now. You can drop the act."

Dad stared down at the ice rink, watching a young girl in a pink coat stumble and fall onto the ice. His shoulders slumped forward heavily.

"I miss your mother," Dad said quietly, his voice cracking on the admission.

The words hung in the air, heavy and pathetic. He had blown our entire family to pieces, dragged Rachel across the continent, and alienated his older children, all to run away from a marriage he couldn't control. And now, standing in a mall in Oregon, entirely dependent on his disabled daughter to pay his car loan, the reality of his escape had finally set in.

"We know that," Jessica said, her voice firm but lacking any pity. "And that is behind you now. Mom is moving on. You said you wanted to get far, far away, but none of us expected you would come all the way out here to the Left Coast. You ran."

Dad closed his eyes, gripping the glass railing.

"Now, we want to help you rebuild your life, starting with your education," Jessica continued firmly, taking on the role of the parent he desperately needed. "We think you can do a lot better than manager at a self-storage place. Stop thinking about the past, and let’s get your future squared away."

Dad didn't argue. He didn't use his heavy parent voice. He just nodded slowly, looking completely defeated by the truth.

"Okay," Jessica sighed, clapping her hands together to shift the dynamic back to the mission at hand. "Enough heavy family trauma for one morning. We are in the largest mall in Oregon, there is zero sales tax here, and I have a debit card. Lead the way, Rachel."

My two sisters, with Rachel eagerly taking the lead since she had mapped out the entire mall directory, started taking us from store to store to buy things. Dad trailed behind us, carrying the shopping bags like a pack mule.

I was surprised at how much Jessica’s wheelchair could carry, balancing bags on the back handles and across her lap. We stuck mostly to clothes, books, and small things, constantly running the logistical calculations of what we could comfortably carry on the public transit ride back to Vancouver. Despite the heavy conversation at the railing, being surrounded by my sisters, following a structured shopping plan, felt like the most grounded I had been in months.
