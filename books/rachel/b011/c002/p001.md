Morning rolled around, the pale, gray light of the Pacific Northwest filtering through the gap in the heavy hotel curtains.

I was the first to wake up. I lay perfectly still in the center of the massive king-sized bed, sandwiched between the grounding, rhythmic breathing of my two sisters. The emotional exhaustion of yesterday’s reunion had completely knocked us out, but my internal clock was violently clashing with the geography.

"Left Coast Time?" Rachel asked, her voice thick with sleep, as she shifted against my right side and rubbed a yawn out of her eyes.

"Left Coast Time," I replied softly, as Jessica also let out a groan and stretched against my left arm.

"When we finish waking up, we need the 7. We are going to meet Dad at Franklin’s," Rachel stated. Her brain didn't need coffee to boot up; her internal Columbia Transit Service map was already online and calculating the day's logistics.

"I should call. You know Dad sometimes forgets," Jessica said, reaching for her cell phone on the nightstand.

Rachel rolled her eyes, throwing the heavy hotel comforter off her legs.

"Something I need to know?" I asked my sisters, catching the undercurrent of annoyance.

"Oh, just Dad being Dad," Jessica grunted as we started the complex, coordinated routine of getting out of bed and helping her get dressed for the cold weather outside.

"Franklin's" was the colloquial name for Franklin's Hypermarket, a massive, regional chain store that sold everything from groceries and furniture to electronics and winter coats. It was a chaotic, fluorescent-lit sensory overload, but it had a massive parking lot and a major transit stop, making it the most logical neutral ground to meet our father.

Thirty minutes later, the three of us were heavily layered in winter coats, standing under the small plexiglass shelter of the hotel's bus stop. The morning air was biting, carrying that constant, damp chill that seemed to seep straight into your bones.

"We need the 7 heading towards Battle Ground," Rachel said, pacing slightly to stay warm while I kept my hands firmly gripped on the handles of Jessica's wheelchair.

"Doesn’t your 72 also go there?" Jessica asked, pulling her scarf tighter.

"Yes, on its way back from Orchards, but you have to cross the major street to catch it. We will want the 7," Rachel explained.

Down the wet asphalt, the bright headlights of a heavy CTS bus pierced through the morning mist. I immediately tensed, shifting my weight to prepare to push Jessica to the curb, but Rachel held up a gloved hand.

She squinted at the glowing orange digital headsign above the windshield as the massive bus hissed to a stop in front of us.

"74 to Mill Plain Transit Center," Rachel noted.

The heavy bi-fold doors hissed open. The driver looked down at us expectantly, his hand hovering over the controls for the wheelchair lift. If a wheelchair passenger didn't speak up, a driver might unnecessarily deploy the heavy mechanical lift, wasting time and messing up the route schedule. Rachel, perfectly fluent in transit etiquette, stepped right up to the bottom stair.

"We're not riding this one," Rachel called up to the operator politely but firmly. "We have a wheelchair, but we are waiting for the 7 going to Battle Ground."

The operator nodded gratefully, gave a quick wave, and closed the doors. The massive diesel engine roared, and the bus pulled away, leaving the stop clear.

"Good catch," Jessica smiled.

Less than five minutes later, a second set of headlights approached. Rachel checked the headsign and nodded sharply.

"Battle Ground," Rachel confirmed. "This is us."

The massive, high-floor transit bus pulled flush against the curb, its air brakes letting out a sharp, loud hiss. The operator opened the front doors, and Rachel immediately took charge of the boarding protocol.

"We’re riding," Rachel announced to the driver. "Wheelchair is going to Franklin’s on 117th."

"You got it," the driver said. "Stand back, deploying the lift."

Because it was an older high-floor model, it didn't just have a simple fold-out ramp. The actual metal stairs of the front entrance mechanically flattened out, accompanied by a loud, industrial warning beep and the heavy clanking of steel. The platform extended out and lowered all the way down to the wet concrete sidewalk.

"Alright, pilot, let's go," Jessica said.

I pushed her chair forward, rolling her carefully onto the metal grate of the lift and locking her manual brakes. The mechanical whine kicked in again as the hydraulic lift raised us up to the floor level of the bus.

I pushed her inside, maneuvering her into the designated securement area behind the driver. The sensory environment of the bus was intense—the smell of diesel fuel, the vibration of the idling engine vibrating through the floorboards, and the damp smell of wet coats. But as the driver secured the heavy nylon tie-down straps to the frame of Jessica's chair, and Rachel tapped her Youth Pass against the farebox, my nervous system remained perfectly calm.

The adult world was messy and terrifying, but inside the rigid, mechanical routine of the Columbia Transit Service, with both of my sisters right beside me, I knew exactly what the pattern was.
