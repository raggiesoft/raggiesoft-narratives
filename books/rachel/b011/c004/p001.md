Jessica’s apartment in Federal Way was a revelation.

It was on the ground floor of a modern, quiet complex, built with full ADA compliance. Inside, it was a perfectly orchestrated sanctuary of neurodivergent peace. The lighting was soft, the hum of the appliances was minimal, and the wide hallways meant Jessica could navigate effortlessly. Sitting on the living room rug, wrapped in a heavy fleece blanket, I watched my older sister operate not just as a college student, but as a completely independent, high-functioning adult.

It was a stark, intentional contrast to where Dad was currently living.

Dad and Cathy shared a two-bedroom unit at the Prairie View Apartments back in Vancouver. Rachel had the smaller ten-by-eleven bedroom, while Dad and Cathy took the master. But the most glaring, unforgivable flaw of the Prairie View lease was the stairs. It was a second-floor walk-up. Dad had deliberately chosen a physical perimeter that made it structurally impossible for his wheelchair-bound daughter to ever visit her little sister.

But the peace of Jessica's accessible apartment was currently overshadowed by a heavy, ticking clock.

Dad had retreated to his apartment yesterday, leaving us at the hotel near the Vancouver Westfield mall. By doing so, he had allowed Jessica to check us out of the hotel, board the ARTS train, and carry Rachel out of Clark County and into King County. Technically, Rachel was now AWOL from her custodial parent.

Jessica, however, was not waiting for Dad to make the next move. She was currently sitting at her dining room table, a headset pulled over her ears, her fingers flying across the mechanical keyboard of her Quantum-issued laptop.

"Yes, Mom, I know it's one o'clock in the afternoon there," Jessica said into the microphone, her voice carrying the clipped, hyper-focused cadence she used when she was writing code. "Just conference him in. I have the retainer fee ready to wire."

Rachel was sitting next to me on the floor, her knees pulled to her chest. "Who is she talking to?" she whispered.

"The lawyer," I murmured back, my analytical brain tracking the sheer volume of data Jessica was managing.

"Mr. Hayes," Jessica said suddenly, her posture straightening as the Virginia attorney joined the line. "Thank you for taking this on such short notice. I am faxing you an affidavit right now, signed by both myself and Michael, detailing exactly what happened yesterday. The key point is this: Thomas Ragsdale abandoned a fourteen-year-old minor at a hotel in Vancouver following a verbal altercation. She is currently in my care in King County, Washington, and she is terrified to go back."

There was a pause as the lawyer spoke on the other end, likely asking about the living conditions to build the "Material Change" argument.

"No, there are no other children in his apartment," Jessica clarified, her tone dripping with clinical disdain. "Cathy’s children are all adults. One son is in a Virginia state penitentiary, the other is failing on his own, and the daughter is entirely estranged. But the material issue, Mr. Hayes, is the physical structure. Thomas moved my sister into a second-story walk-up. I have cerebral palsy. He intentionally placed her in a housing situation that prevents her immediate family from accessing her."

She paused, listening to the lawyer confirm the strategy.

"Exactly," Jessica nodded. "What we need today is a temporary emergency order. An *Ex Parte* injunction. We need the Virginia Beach court to legally freeze the status quo so he cannot use local Washington law enforcement to remove her from my apartment during the Thanksgiving holiday."

Jessica's eyes scanned her laptop screen.

"Cost is not an issue, Mr. Hayes," Jessica stated, her voice icy and absolute. "I work for Quantum. Bill my account whatever you need to, but I want that emergency motion on a judge's desk before the courthouse closes tomorrow."

She hit a key on her laptop, initiating the wire transfer that would fund the war.
