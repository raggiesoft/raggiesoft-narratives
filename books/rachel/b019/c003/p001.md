The three-day Memorial Day weekend officially ended, and the civilian world was waking back up. But inside the Canadian Arch townhouse, the pack’s operational rhythm was already flawlessly clicking into gear.

At 6:40 AM, Jessica rolled her manual wheelchair out the front door, the warm, humid Virginia morning air greeting her. She didn't need Michael to drive her, and she didn't need to fight the unpredictable morning traffic on Independence Boulevard. Her transit algorithm for Town Center days was just as meticulously mapped as her routes in the Pacific Northwest.

She wheeled down the short concrete driveway, crossed Buckner Boulevard, and parked her chair at the sprawling transit transfer point right in front of the Community College of Hampton Roads.

It was an incredibly robust municipal hub. The CCHR stop serviced a half-dozen routes—the 12, 25, 26, 29, and 33—but Jessica was waiting for the workhorse of the corridor.

At exactly 6:45 AM, the heavy diesel engine of the Route 36 bus rumbled into the bay.

The operator deployed the hydraulic ramp. Jessica rolled aboard, locking her brakes into the securement bay. She didn't hold up her Quantum Blue Badge; local Coastal Virginia Transit Authority operators didn't care about a Seattle tech conglomerate. Instead, she flashed her standard CVTA 30-Day Pass. It was a frictionless transaction, automatically funded every month through Quantum's IRS pre-tax transit benefit program.

The heavy doors hissed shut, and the bus merged onto Holland Road, carrying the Director of Defense Integration straight into the commercial heart of the city.

It was a smooth, thirty-five-minute ride. The bus bypassed the early commuter gridlock, finally approaching the towering glass-and-steel epicenter of Virginia Beach.

"Stop requested," Jessica signaled, pulling the yellow cord.

Instead of riding all the way into the busy Pembroke East terminal, Jessica utilized the optimal drop-off geometry. At 7:22 AM, the bus pulled over at the intersection of Columbus and Independence. The ramp lowered, and she rolled onto the pavement, leaving the bus to finish its route while she executed the short, seamless roll directly to the gleaming front doors of the Armada Hoffler Tower.

If she left the office on time tonight, she knew she could catch the 36 heading back toward CCHR at the Columbus & Town Center stop at ten minutes past the hour. If the Navy deployment required her to stay past the final 6:10 PM bus, the pack was on standby. She just had to send a text, and Michael would be waiting at the curb in the transit van. The perimeter was entirely covered.

But right now, she had a bank to dismantle.
