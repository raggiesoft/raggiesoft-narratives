The Virginia morning was already thick with the familiar, suffocating coastal humidity by the time I steered the heavy transit van onto Moosewood Drive.

We didn't have to navigate any complex transit grids or fight the commuter rush on Independence Boulevard. The drive from the Canadian Arch townhouse to our newly acquired plot of land took exactly four minutes. I pulled the van flush against the curb, right across the street from the Silverleaf commuter lot, and put it in park.

The heavy, rotting structure of the foreclosed house sat in the overgrown grass, looking completely dead and pathetic in the bright morning sun.

"Alright, pack," Jessica announced from the passenger tie-down bay, her voice humming with a quiet, lethal satisfaction. "Deploy the lift."

We filed out of the van, executing our standard, frictionless extraction. I lowered the hydraulic lift, and Jessica rolled her manual chair onto the pavement. Kristin and Ashley stepped out, flanking us, while Rachel instantly took her designated spot pressed flush against my left side. She looped her arm securely through mine, resting her head lightly against my shoulder as we formed a solid line on the sidewalk across the street from the property.

Parked in the driveway of the rotting house was a massive, bright yellow heavy-duty excavator. A foreman in a neon vest and a hard hat was standing near the treads, holding a clipboard and a two-way radio.

He saw us assembling on the sidewalk and jogged across the asphalt. He took one look at our tight, unconventional formation and immediately locked eyes with the woman sitting in the wheelchair at the center of the perimeter. He recognized absolute corporate authority when he saw it.

"Ms. Ragsdale?" the foreman asked, stepping up to the curb. "We've got the permits cleared, the utilities capped, and the dumpsters staged in the back. We're just waiting on your green light to start swinging."

Jessica didn't hesitate. She didn't look at the house with any sort of nostalgic hesitation or buyer's remorse. To her analytical brain, the structure wasn't a home; it was an architectural failure standing in the way of our sanctuary.

"Tear it down to the dirt," Jessica ordered, her voice completely devoid of emotion.

The foreman grinned, tapped his hard hat, and jogged back across the street. He keyed his radio, and the massive diesel engine of the excavator roared to life.

The sudden, concussive boom of the engine sent a heavy vibration rippling straight through the soles of my boots. I instinctively reached up and clamped my heavy isolation headphones over my ears, dialing the volume of my CD player just high enough to muffle the sharp, abrasive mechanical shrieks.

Rachel didn't have her headphones on. The noise was astronomically loud, but her trauma-wired processor didn't throw a single error code. She didn't flinch, and she didn't try to hide behind me.

She knew exactly what that machine was about to do, and the sheer, mathematical logic of the destruction completely overrode her sensory overload. She tightened her grip on my arm, a massive, uncontainable smile breaking across her face as the excavator's heavy steel arm swung upward.
