The drive back from the clinic to the Canadian Arch townhouse was completely silent, but it wasn’t the tense, suffocating silence of a system lock. It was the heavy, profoundly exhausted quiet of a massive data transfer completing.

When I killed the engine of the transit van in the driveway, the late afternoon Virginia heat was still radiating off the pavement, but the internal temperature of the pack had fundamentally shifted. We executed our standard extraction, deployed the lift for Jessica, and filed through the front door of the townhouse.

In a world running on standard hardware, this was the exact moment where the perimeter was supposed to dissolve. The neurotypical script dictated that after an overwhelming, deeply personal medical revelation, a twenty-one-year-old woman and a twenty-three-year-old man should retreat to their own separate, private spaces to process the emotional fallout alone.

But the doctor had just handed us the clinical proof that we were not built for standard hardware.

We didn't scatter. The second the heavy front door clicked shut behind us, the deadbolt engaging with a sharp metallic snap, Rachel stopped dead in the middle of the living room carpet.

She didn't take her shoes off. She didn't drop her bag. She just stood there, her shoulders trembling, the sheer, staggering weight of the last twenty-one years crashing down on her all at once. Every time she had lost her words, every time the acoustic wall of a grocery store had driven her into a buzzing panic, every time an adult had looked at her like she was broken—it hadn't been a failure. It had been her neurobiology.

She looked up at me, her dark eyes completely overwhelmed. She didn't need to use her words. The telemetry was written all over her face. She needed the physical anchor.

"Come here, Bug," I whispered, my own voice cracking.

Rachel stepped directly into my chest, wrapping her arms around my torso with a desperate, crushing force. I wrapped my arms around her back, instantly locking in the deep-pressure protocol. I buried my face into her hair, tears silently spilling over my eyelashes and soaking into her collar.

For two decades, the outside world had tried to pry us apart. They told us our physical proximity was a liability, that we needed to grow up, separate, and stop hiding behind each other. But holding my sister in the middle of the living room, feeling her autonomic nervous system sync with my own, I realized the absolute truth: we didn't just survive *in spite* of each other. We survived *because* of each other. Our interdependence was the most flawless piece of engineering in the entire family.

We didn't want space. We wanted the perimeter.

"Don't leave us out," Jessica’s voice broke the quiet.

I opened my eyes. Jessica had wheeled her manual chair directly up to my right side, parking the tires flush against my leg. She reached her arms up, wrapping one around my waist and the other around Rachel's shoulder, pulling herself tightly into our gravitational pull.

Ashley and Kristin didn't hesitate. They didn't stand on the periphery waiting for an invitation. They understood their mechanical utility to the pack perfectly.

Ashley stepped up behind Rachel, wrapping her arms completely around Rachel's back and pressing her weight forward, amplifying the heavy, grounding compression. Kristin stepped up behind me, wrapping her arms around my chest and pressing her cheek firmly against my back.

It wasn't just a hug. It was a closed physical circuit. It was a massive, five-person deep-pressure firewall, built in the exact center of the townhouse living room.

The heavy, overlapping weight of my partners, my older sister, and my little sister acted like a literal cargo strap holding my fractured nervous system together. The buzzing panic of the clinical environment bled completely out of my muscles. The operational freeze that had haunted my entire childhood melted into the floorboards.

We stood there in a tight, impenetrable knot for a long time, the silence of the townhouse broken only by the sound of our synchronized breathing.

The Moosewood house was gone. The Dojang was dead. And as the crushing weight of the pack grounded me to the floor, I finally understood that there was absolutely nothing wrong with my brain.

The Base Camp was secure.
