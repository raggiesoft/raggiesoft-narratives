Moosewood Drive was a quiet, unassuming residential artery. The frantic, aggressive energy of the detoured traffic instantly vanished behind us. The street was lined with modest, suburban homes and mature pine trees, completely insulated from the roar of Independence Boulevard.

"Good call, Bear," Rachel called up from the back seat, her voice relaxed as the chaotic brake lights disappeared from her window.

I kept the van cruising at a slow, steady speed, mapping the alternate route in my head. But as we drove down the dark, quiet street, a glaring, ugly anomaly broke the suburban pattern.

"Michael. Pull over," Jessica commanded sharply.

I immediately tapped the brakes, easing the heavy transit van to a stop alongside the curb, putting the hazard lights on. "What is it?"

"Look at that," Jessica said, pointing out her passenger window.

Sitting on the right side of the street was a property that looked completely abandoned. The yard was overgrown with weeds. The roof was sagging, the gutters were rotting, and the windows looked dark and miserable. The house was an absolute, dilapidated disaster. But hammered into the dead grass of the front lawn was a crooked wooden sign: *FOR SALE - BANK OWNED.*

Ashley leaned forward from the back passenger seat, peering out the window at the rotting structure. Her face contorted into a mask of pure disgust.

"Jessica, you do not want that," Ashley stated, her practical Sentinel radar completely rejecting the site. "That place is a biohazard. It looks like it's literally sinking into the mud. You couldn't pay me to sleep in that thing."

Jessica didn't look discouraged. She unzipped her messenger bag, pulled out her smartphone, and rolled her window down. She held the phone up, snapping a high-resolution photo of the house, and then zoomed in to take a perfectly clear picture of the listing agent's phone number on the sign.

"Ashley, you're looking at the drywall," Jessica explained smoothly, her voice vibrating with absolute, corporate-level vision. She rolled the window back up. "I don't care about the drywall. I care about the dirt."

I shifted the van into park, looking at my sister. "The dirt?"

"It's a foreclosure," Jessica said, her eyes alight with tactical math. "The subprime market is collapsing, and the banks are desperate to unload dead assets. They will take pennies on the dollar just to get this rotting liability off their books. I can buy this lot in cash next week."

She turned her head, looking at the three women in the back seat, and then at me.

"I am not going to renovate it," Jessica declared, the sheer magnitude of her plan settling over the van. "The moment the deed is in my name, I am bringing in a bulldozer and leveling that trash to the foundation. We are going to build a brand-new house from scratch. We are going to build a fortress."

Rachel gasped softly from the center seat. "From scratch?"

"Custom architecture," Jessica nodded fiercely. "Zero cramped hallways. A massive, open-concept living space so we never lose our visual tether. Soundproofed walls. A dedicated, climate-controlled server room for Michael’s coding projects. And a master suite large enough to hold a custom-built, oversized mattress so our pack never has to sleep in separate beds again. I am going to buy this dirt, and we are going to build exactly what we need."

The heavy, profound realization of what she was proposing hit my chest. We weren't just searching for a house to adapt to; we were going to force the environment to adapt to us.

"It's right across the street from the Silverleaf commuter lot," Kristin noted from the back, her logistical brain catching up instantly. "Michael or I could drop you off in two minutes, and you could catch the Express bus straight onto the Naval Base without ever sitting in gate traffic."

"Exactly," Jessica smiled, tapping her phone. "The perimeter is perfect. Now, get us home, Michael. I have a real estate agent to call."
