The adrenaline rush of the initial reunification was beginning to fade, leaving a quiet, bone-deep exhaustion in its wake. But as we turned away from the security checkpoint and joined the flow of arriving passengers heading toward baggage claim, Rachel didn’t let her guard down entirely.

She reached out with her right hand, her fingers instantly finding mine. She didn't just hold my hand; she laced our fingers together firmly, establishing a continuous, tactile tether.

"Really, Rachel?" Mom sighed, walking a half-step ahead of us. She glanced back over her shoulder, her brow furrowing in a familiar, exasperated expression. "You're fourteen years old. Brothers and sisters don't need to walk around holding hands in public."

Rachel didn't say a word, and she didn't loosen her grip. She just stared blankly at the back of Mom's head.

"Leave it alone, Mom," Jessica said smoothly. She had bypassed the airport assistance completely and was propelling her own manual wheelchair with practiced, powerful thrusts of her arms, keeping perfect pace with us. "The environment is loud, and her processor is tired. It's a grounding technique."

I squeezed Rachel's hand, offering a silent agreement. We didn't care about neurotypical social expectations. If the physical pressure kept her from spiraling into a meltdown, I would hold her hand through the entire state of Virginia.

"So," I said, shifting the focus and looking down at Rachel as we navigated the long concourse. "Tell me about Federal Way. How is the new high school? Are the teachers actually listening to Jessica's IEP demands?"

Rachel's dark eyes brightened significantly. She looked at Jessica, a profound, quiet respect passing between them.

"They have to," Rachel rasped, her voice still rough from the flights. "Jess brings the binder to the office. She shows them the laws. They let me type on my laptop in class, and they don't make me read out loud."

"And her grades reflect the accommodation," Jessica added, glancing up at me with a fierce, proud smile. "She brought home her mid-term report card last week. Straight A's and two B's. The D's and F's from Dad's house are completely gone."

I stopped walking for a fraction of a second, staring at Rachel in absolute awe.

"A's and B's?" I repeated, a massive grin breaking across my face. "Rachel, that's incredible!"

When she lived with Dad, the constant stress and the forced, unaccommodated English-speaking environment had completely paralyzed her academic ability. Just three months in Jessica’s highly regulated, fiercely protected apartment, and she was thriving.

Rachel offered a small, proud nod, her fingers tightening around mine.

As we approached the escalators leading down to the baggage carousels, Jessica locked the brakes on her chair near a set of tiled archways.

"We need to execute a bio-break before we hit the luggage," Jessica announced practically. "Rachel, with me."

Rachel reluctantly unlaced her fingers from mine, giving my hand one final squeeze before stepping behind Jessica's chair to push her into the women's restroom.

I stood by the escalators with Mom, running my hand through my hair. The sheer relief of having them back was a physical weight lifting off my chest.

Less than five minutes later, the girls emerged. Rachel immediately bypassed the crowd, walking straight back to my side and seamlessly lacing our fingers back together.

We rode the elevator down to the lower level. The baggage claim area was a chaotic swirl of families and squeaking conveyor belts, but Jessica tracked the flight monitors with her usual logistical supremacy.

"Carousel four," Jessica pointed.

We waited near the edge of the metal belt. When the heavy canvas suitcases finally dropped down the chute, Rachel and Jessica instantly identified theirs. I grabbed the handles, hauling the heavy luggage off the belt while Rachel maintained her grip on my left hand.

"Alright, let's get out of here," Mom said, pulling her coat tighter. "Aunt Janet is circling the pickup lane in the sedan."

We pushed through the automatic sliding glass doors into the freezing Virginia night. The sudden blast of cold air was a shock after the stale, recycled air of the terminal, but the smell of the damp, salty coastal wind was undeniable proof that we were home.

Aunt Janet's silver sedan pulled up to the curb a moment later, the hazard lights flashing.

Mom and Aunt Janet immediately stepped out, moving toward the trunk to help me load the heavy suitcases.

"Jessica, honey," Aunt Janet called out cheerfully over the roof of the car. "We cleared out the front passenger seat for you so you'd have more legroom!"

"I'm sitting in the back, Aunt Janet," Jessica replied instantly, already maneuvering her chair toward the rear passenger door.

"Are you sure? It'll be a tight squeeze with the three of you," Mom protested, looking concerned about the logistics of transferring Jessica into the back seat.

"It's fine," Rachel interrupted, her voice firm. She looked directly at me. "Michael sits in the middle."

I laughed, instantly understanding the requested seating arrangement. It was our established, symmetrical anchor.

Mom and Aunt Janet reluctantly assisted with the transfer. Jessica locked her brakes, planted her hands on the doorframe, and expertly pivoted her weight from her chair into the back seat, sliding over to the far passenger side. I folded the frame of her manual chair, stowing it securely in the trunk before climbing into the middle seat.

Rachel slid in right after me, taking the driver’s side and pulling the heavy door shut.

The moment the doors closed, sealing us into the warm, quiet cabin of the sedan, the perimeter was locked.

I sat back against the upholstery. Instantly, Rachel leaned heavily against my left side, wrapping her arm securely around my torso and burying her face into my shoulder. On my right, Jessica leaned her head against my other shoulder, looping her arm through mine.

I wrapped my arms around both of them, pulling them flush against me in a tight, three-person huddle.

In the front seat, Mom adjusted the rearview mirror, her eyes catching the reflection of our entangled limbs.

"Honestly," Mom sighed, exasperated. "You three are adults and teenagers. You're practically sitting on top of each other. There is plenty of room to sit properly."

"Oh, let them be, Ellen," Aunt Janet chuckled softly from the passenger seat.

Jessica didn't lift her head from my shoulder, but her voice carried that cold, pragmatic logic that always shut down an argument.

"Would you rather we sat 'properly' and fought for forty-five minutes like standard, neurotypical siblings?" Jessica asked smoothly. "Or would you rather enjoy a quiet, completely regulated car ride where no one is screaming?"

The front seat descended into immediate silence. The adults couldn't argue with the math.

Mom put the sedan into drive and pulled away from the airport curb. Aunt Janet reached out and turned the radio dial.

"You're locked into WNVB, The Southside's Holiday Station!" a cheerful, pre-recorded DJ announced over the static. "Playing non-stop festive hits to get you through the season!"

A bright, acoustic guitar intro filled the cabin, followed by the familiar, upbeat vocals of The Winter Palace. It wasn't the heavy, symphonic rock of their famous rescue beacon; it was their lighter, acoustic holiday track, *Snowfall on the James*.

Rachel let out a soft, happy sigh against my shoulder as the music played. The heat from the vents, the quiet rhythm of the tires on the highway, and the heavy, grounding pressure of my sisters leaning against me created an absolute sanctuary.

We were finally, officially back together.
