The silver sedan pulled into the driveway of 1852 Delaney Street. Aunt Janet put the car in park, leaving the headlights illuminating the familiar, frost-covered front lawn.

Before Mom even cut the engine, a frantic, muffled cacophony of barking erupted from inside the house.

"They know," Rachel whispered, a brilliant, exhausted smile breaking across her face.

I didn't wait. I threw open the car door, stepping out into the freezing wind. Rachel scrambled out right behind me. We moved to the trunk, executing a routine we hadn't practiced in months, but one that was permanently hardwired into our muscle memory.

Rachel grabbed the heavy canvas suitcases, hauling them onto the pavement, while I lifted the folded frame of Jessica's manual wheelchair. I popped the wheels open with a sharp, mechanical snap, securing the frame, and rolled it to the rear passenger door.

Jessica was already waiting. She locked her brakes, grabbed the doorframe, and expertly pivoted her weight from the sedan seat into her chair.

"Thanks, Michael," Jessica said, immediately grabbing her push-rims.

"I've got the bags," I told her, slinging both heavy suitcases over my shoulders.

We moved up the concrete ramp toward the front door. The barking inside was reaching a fever pitch. Rachel didn't even wait for Mom; she reached past me, twisting the deadbolt and throwing the front door wide open.

The immediate rush of warm air was instantly overwhelmed by a massive, chaotic explosion of black fur.

Baby, the purebred Lab built like a small Great Dane, hit the entryway like a freight train. He didn't jump up—he knew better than to knock us over—but his massive tail was wagging so hard his entire back half was violently shaking. He practically tackled Rachel, burying his giant head into her stomach as she dropped to her knees on the linoleum, letting out a loud, rhythmic vocalization of pure joy.

Right behind him was Archie. His cloudy blue eye was wide with frantic excitement, and clamped securely in his jaws was a mangled, squeaky plush duck. He trotted rapidly in a tight circle around Jessica's wheelchair, desperately trying to show off his prize, emitting a high-pitched, muffled whine through the toy.

"Hi, boys. I missed you too," Jessica laughed, reaching down to scrub behind Archie's ears.

"Alright, let them breathe, you two," Mom sighed, stepping through the door and dropping her keys onto the entryway table. "It's late. Everyone needs to get settled."

Rachel looked up from where she was buried under Baby's massive paws. She looked at me, and then pointed directly toward the living room.

I didn't need to ask. I dropped the suitcases by the stairs.

"Sofa bed," I announced, walking into the living room.

Jessica rolled in right behind me. "I'll take the left side, Michael. Pull the cushions."

We worked together, ripping the floral cushions off the couch and grabbing the heavy metal handle hidden beneath the frame. With a loud squeak of springs, we pulled the hidden Queen-sized mattress out into the center of the room.

It wasn't a luxury setup. The mattress was thin, and the metal bar across the middle was notorious for digging into your spine. But for an entire year—from the terrifying day Jessica fell off the ladder in November of '99, until she left for college in August of 2000—this specific, uncomfortable bed had been the absolute center of our universe. It was where the three of us had slept every single night to ensure Jessica wasn't alone while she healed, and to ensure Rachel and I felt safe while the house fractured around us.

Mom walked into the living room, pausing as she saw the mattress fully deployed.

"You guys," Mom started, rubbing her temples. "There are three bedrooms upstairs. You don't have to sleep in a pile in the living room."

Her math was technically accurate, but logistically useless. The master bedroom belonged to her. The second bedroom at the end of the hall was mine. And the third bedroom—the room Jessica and Rachel had shared for years—was entirely inaccessible. Ever since the fall in '99, the staircase had become a hard, physical boundary. Jessica couldn't get her wheelchair up those steps, which meant the downstairs living room was her only viable option.

And where Jessica slept, the pack slept. We were never going to leave her down here alone.

Jessica didn't look up as she locked her wheelchair brakes near the edge of the mattress. "It's tradition, Mom. We're sleeping here."

Mom looked at the three of us. She looked at Rachel, who was currently wrestling with a hundred-pound Lab on the carpet, completely devoid of the terrified, silent tension she always carried when Dad was around. Mom let out a slow, defeated sigh.

"Fine. I am too tired to argue. Go get changed."

"Bathroom," Jessica instructed Rachel.

Rachel immediately stood up, pushing Jessica's chair toward the downstairs half-bath.

"I'll get the blankets," I told them, taking the stairs two at a time.

I rushed into my bedroom, changing quickly into my flannel pajamas and grabbing an armful of pillows and my heavy winter comforter.

When I returned to the living room, Rachel and Jessica were already waiting. Jessica had transferred from her chair onto the edge of the mattress, pulling a pair of sweatpants over her legs. Rachel was sitting cross-legged in her oversized sleep shirt, looking completely exhausted but refusing to lie down until the perimeter was secure.

I tossed the pillows onto the headboard and spread the heavy comforter over the mattress.

"Middle," Rachel demanded, her raspy voice vibrating with absolute certainty.

I climbed into the exact center of the bed, pulling the comforter up to my chest.

Instantly, the girls moved. Rachel practically dove under the blankets on my left side, wrapping both of her arms securely around my torso and burying her face into my shoulder. She threw her leg heavily over mine, anchoring me to the mattress.

On my right, Jessica slid down under the covers. She didn't use the suffocating, deep-pressure hold that Rachel required, but she wrapped her arm securely through mine, resting her head against my other shoulder.

I wrapped my arms around both of them, pulling them flush against my sides. The heat from the radiator, the heavy weight of the comforter, and the familiar, grounding pressure of my two favorite women completely short-circuited my exhausted nervous system.

The house was quiet. Baby had curled up on the rug near the front door, and Archie was asleep under the dining table, his plush duck still resting against his paws.

But as the minutes ticked by, none of us fell asleep.

I could feel Rachel's heart racing against my ribs. Her breathing was shallow. Jessica was perfectly still, but the muscles in her arm were tight.

"You can't sleep either," I whispered into the dark room.

"No," Rachel rasped quietly against my shirt.

"My brain is calculating the probability of a systemic failure," Jessica admitted softly, her pragmatic facade finally cracking just a fraction of an inch in the dark. "It feels statistically impossible that we actually pulled this off."

I squeezed them both tightly. I understood exactly what they meant. The last three months had been a living nightmare. We had been separated by court orders, airports, and the terrifying, oppressive shadow of Dad. Being back together, curled up on this terrible, lumpy sofa bed, felt like a dream. We were all terrified that if we closed our eyes, we would wake up back in our separate, isolated realities.

"It's not a dream," I promised them, my voice a low, steady rumble. "I'm right here. I'm not going anywhere. The perimeter is secure."

Rachel let out a long, shuddering sigh, her grip on my shirt tightening slightly.

"Northern Star," Rachel murmured into my shoulder.

I smiled in the dark, recognizing the reference to the song we had heard in Aunt Janet's car. It was our designated, unpunishable loophole. It was the absolute, undeniable truth.

"Yeah," I agreed, resting my chin against the top of Rachel's head. "You found the light. You made it home."

Slowly, agonizingly, the adrenaline finally burned out of our systems. The heavy, rhythmic breathing of my sisters began to synchronize with my own. I kept my arms locked securely around them, holding the perimeter tight, until the Virginia night finally surrendered, and we fell asleep.
