The massive twin engines of the aircraft roared, pushing us back into our plush leather seats as we accelerated down the runway.

I closed my eyes and gripped the wide armrests. Takeoffs were always an intense sensory event, but the physics of First Class completely changed the equation. In the back of the plane with Dad, the turbulence had violently rattled my bones, and the terrified energy of a hundred cramped passengers had suffocated me.

Up here in row one, the climb was smooth and heavily insulated. The thick leather absorbed the vibration of the floorboards. But the most profound difference was the spatial geometry of the seat itself.

I opened my eyes as the nose of the plane leveled out and the loud hum of the engines dropped to a quiet, steady drone.

I looked down at my legs. I had inches of empty space on either side of my hips. The center console separating me from Jessica was so wide that our elbows didn't even touch. Nobody was encroaching on my perimeter. Nobody was bumping my shoulder when they walked down the aisle. I was perfectly contained within my own designated, mathematically precise zone.

"Comfortable?" Jessica asked, looking over at me. She had already retrieved a thick textbook from her tote bag and rested it on her tray table.

"It's huge," I rasped quietly, running my hand over the soft, dark blue upholstery. "No strangers can touch me."

"Exactly," Jessica smiled. "That is the point of the premium cabin. We buy the physical space so your nervous system doesn't have to."

The heavy curtain separating us from the main cabin snapped shut, sealing off the noise of the boarding crowd. A moment later, the flight attendant approached our row. She rolled a sleek metal cart down the wide aisle, offering a warm, professional smile.

"Good morning, ladies," the attendant said smoothly. "Now that we are at cruising altitude, I can offer you a beverage before our meal service begins. We have fresh coffee, juice, and I can also pour you a mimosa or a glass of champagne if you'd like to start the holiday early."

Jessica offered a polite, practiced smile. "Thank you, but I'm actually twenty, and my sister is fourteen. So we will pass on the champagne."

The flight attendant didn't miss a beat, flawlessly executing a customer-service pivot. Her smile never wavered.

"Of course! My apologies," the attendant said cheerfully. "In that case, we have a wonderful hot chocolate, freshly brewed decaf or regular coffee, assorted juices, cola, ginger ale, and bottled water."

"Hot chocolate sounds perfect," Jessica replied. "We'll both take a hot chocolate, please. And we'll also need two glasses of water."

"Right away," the attendant nodded, pulling down two thick, ceramic mugs and two heavy crystal glasses.

I watched the interaction with quiet awe. There was no friction. There was no argument. Dad would have made a sarcastic comment or gotten annoyed at the interruption, but Jessica just stated the parameters, and the world adjusted around her.

The attendant placed the steaming mugs and the glasses of water onto our tray tables on small linen napkins.

"Enjoy," she said before moving to the row behind us.

Jessica reached into her tote bag and pulled out two small, orange plastic bottles. She opened the first one, shaking a white tablet into her palm, and then passed the second bottle to me.

"Medication protocol," Jessica instructed quietly.

I unscrewed the cap and tapped my daily pill into my hand. In September, Dad had purposefully packed my medication in the checked luggage, forcing my brain to navigate the terrifying environment of the airport completely unshielded. That single variable had caused my entire system to crash.

Jessica never made that mistake. She kept the logistics tightly controlled.

I placed the pill on my tongue and took a long drink from the crystal glass. The cold water washed it down, locking my chemical baseline into place. Jessica took her own medication with a sip of water, managing the chronic nerve pain in her legs.

Once the medical protocol was complete, I wrapped both of my hands around the thick ceramic mug. The heat radiated through the porcelain, warming my palms. I took a slow sip. The hot chocolate was rich and sweet, melting away the last lingering traces of tension from the Seattle security checkpoint.

Shortly after, the attendant returned with our lunch. Instead of the chaotic, crinkling foil wrappers I remembered from coach, the food was served on warm porcelain plates with heavy, solid utensils. It was chicken and rice, predictable and easy to process.

I ate my meal in complete silence, occasionally sipping my hot chocolate and looking out the window at the thick blanket of white clouds below us. The cabin was quiet. The food was warm. My sister was right beside me, completely in control of the environment.

The algorithm was perfect. And every second that the massive engines pushed us forward, we were getting closer to Michael.
