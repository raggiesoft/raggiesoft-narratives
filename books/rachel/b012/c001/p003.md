The Meridian First Class Lounge was an entirely different biome.

It didn't feel like an airport. The air smelled of dark roasted coffee and fresh citrus, completely masking the heavy, chemical scent of jet fuel that permeated the main concourse. The harsh, buzzing fluorescent lights had been replaced by warm, amber table lamps. But the most profound difference was the acoustic profile. The thick carpets and heavy velvet curtains absorbed the ambient noise, reducing the world to a soft, manageable murmur.

There were only about a dozen other people in the massive room. Most of them were businessmen in dark suits, reading newspapers in silence. Nobody was rushing. Nobody was yelling.

I parked Jessica’s wheelchair next to a low, polished mahogany coffee table, flanked by two oversized leather armchairs.

Jessica locked her brakes and looked at her watch. "It is seven o'clock," she stated quietly. "I engineered our timeline assuming we would be trapped in that security line for four hours. Because we bypassed it, we have a massive block of unscheduled time. Our flight to Chicago does not board until ten-forty-five."

For a neurotypical teenager, sitting in a waiting room for almost four hours might sound like a punishment. For my brain, it was a profound luxury. It was a guaranteed, uninterrupted block of processing time.

"I brought my laptop," I signed to her, tapping the heavy black bag on my shoulder.

"I know," Jessica smiled warmly. "And you have plenty of time to boot up Quantum OS and play your puzzle games. But first, we need to execute a breakfast protocol."

Jessica pointed toward the back of the lounge. There was a long, white marble counter glowing under soft recessed lighting.

"That is the buffet," Jessica explained. "It is entirely complimentary. I want you to go over there, select a plate, and bring breakfast back for both of us. Can you do that?"

My heart gave a tiny, sharp flutter of anxiety. She was issuing a solo objective in a public space.

I looked at the buffet, running a quick visual scan of the parameters. The marble counter was only fifty feet away. There was no line. There was no cashier to interact with, which meant zero requirement for verbal communication. The environment was completely contained and safe.

I gave Jessica a sharp, determined nod.

I slipped my laptop bag off my shoulder, setting it safely on the leather chair, and walked toward the food station.

The buffet was a masterpiece of predictable geometry. There were heavy, silver chafing dishes with domed lids, baskets woven with white linen napkins, and glass bowls perfectly aligned in a row. I picked up a heavy, warm porcelain plate. It felt solid and grounding in my hands.

I lifted the first silver dome. It was filled with scrambled eggs. I used the stainless steel tongs to transfer a perfectly calculated scoop onto my plate. I moved down the line, adding exactly three strips of crisp bacon. Next was the fruit station. I bypassed the unpredictable, messy berries and used a heavy silver spoon to select geometrically perfect, one-inch cubes of cantaloupe and honeydew melon.

I built a second plate for Jessica, mirroring the exact same nutritional ratios, and carefully carried them both back to our pod. I set them down on the mahogany table.

"Flawless execution," Jessica praised, picking up her fork. "Thank you, Rachel."

I sat down in my heavy leather chair, pulling my knees up and resting my plate safely on my lap. The food was warm and predictable. The leather chair wrapped around me like a protective shell. For the next three and a half hours, the universe was entirely contained within this quiet room. I ate my breakfast, booted up my laptop, and listened to the soft, rhythmic clinking of coffee cups.
