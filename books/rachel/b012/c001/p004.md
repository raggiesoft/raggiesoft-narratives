The digital clock in the lower right corner of my laptop screen ticked over to 10:45.

Exactly on schedule, a female lounge attendant in a crisp navy blue Meridian uniform walked over to our pod. She didn't try to touch Jessica’s chair, and she didn't speak to me like a child.

"Ms. Ragsdale?" the attendant asked softly. "Flight 402 to Chicago is ready for priority pre-boarding. If you'll follow me, we can get you settled before they open the gate to the main cabin."

"Thank you," Jessica nodded. She looked at me. "Laptop away, Rachel. You're the engine again."

I quickly shut down my system, packed my bag, and grabbed the rubber push-handles of her wheelchair. We followed the attendant out of the sanctuary of the lounge and back into the bright, noisy reality of the terminal.

The gate area was crowded, packed with people waiting to board the massive Boeing 757. But we didn't stop. The attendant swiped our boarding passes and opened the glass door leading directly down the jet bridge.

The jet bridge was steep, sloping downward toward the airplane door. I leaned back slightly, using my body weight and the friction of the rubber handles to keep Jessica's chair rolling at a slow, controlled pace.

When we reached the end of the tunnel, stopping right outside the open door of the aircraft, the parameters changed.

Waiting for us was a piece of specialized aviation equipment called an aisle chair. It looked like a standard wheelchair that had been violently compressed; it was incredibly narrow, with tiny wheels and no side armrests. Because commercial airplane aisles are only about twenty inches wide, a standard manual wheelchair physically cannot fit down the cabin.

"Alright," Jessica announced, locking the brakes on her everyday chair.

This was the vulnerable part of the algorithm. Jessica had to leave her custom-fitted, highly mobile chair and surrender to the airline's equipment.

Two baggage handlers were waiting. They were polite and efficient. Jessica unbuckled her waist strap, planted her hands on the armrests of her own chair, and smoothly pivoted her body, transferring her weight directly into the narrow aisle chair.

The handlers immediately strapped her in, securing tight nylon belts across her waist and chest to keep her from sliding out of the armless seat.

"Rachel," Jessica instructed, looking up at me as she was strapped in. "Grab the canvas tote bag from my chair. Then take your laptop bag, and follow right behind me."

I nodded, grabbing the heavy bags and slinging them over my shoulders.

The baggage handlers tilted the aisle chair back onto its rear wheels and carefully pushed Jessica through the aircraft door. I followed immediately behind her, stepping out of the cold jet bridge and into the warm, pressurized cabin.

We didn't have to go far. Because Jessica had weaponized her budget, our seats were in the very first row. Seat 1A and 1B.

The handlers parked the aisle chair next to the wide, plush blue leather seats. Jessica unbuckled the restrictive straps and executed a second physical transfer, sliding herself gracefully from the narrow transport chair into the deep comfort of Seat 1B.

"I've got the chair, ma'am," the handler said, pulling the aisle chair back. "We'll stow your personal wheelchair in the cargo hold and have it waiting for you on the jet bridge in Chicago."

"Thank you," Jessica nodded, officially taking command of our new environment.

I slid into the window seat, Seat 1A. The chair was massive. My shoulders didn't touch Jessica's, and my knees were nowhere near the bulkhead wall in front of us.

A flight attendant immediately appeared beside us, holding a silver tray with crystal glasses. "Pre-departure beverages? Champagne, water, or orange juice?"

"Orange juice for my sister, please," Jessica requested, taking a glass of water for herself.

I took the cold glass, wrapping both hands around it. The heavy, chaotic crowd of the main cabin hadn't even begun to board yet. We were already safe, seated, and insulated behind the heavy curtain that separated First Class from the rest of the plane.

I looked out the thick plastic window at the gray Seattle sky. In just a few minutes, the engines would start. The trajectory was locked. We were going east. We were going to Michael.
