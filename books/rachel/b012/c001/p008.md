The cold tile of the concourse floor was hard against my knees, but the steady, unyielding pressure of Jessica's hand on the back of my neck kept my system tethered to reality. Slowly, the ragged, guttural rumble in my chest quieted into a tired, rhythmic hum.

I was exhausted. A severe sensory meltdown drains the body’s battery entirely, leaving behind a hollow, heavy numbness.

"Okay," Jessica said softly, her voice close to my ear. "You're coming back to me. The storm is passing."

I nodded slightly against my arms.

"I need you to open your eyes, Rachel," Jessica instructed, her tone transitioning from fierce protector to pragmatic logistical commander. "We are halfway there. We survived the first flight, and we survived the tunnel. But we have a connection to catch. We have to get to the gate for Norfolk."

I forced my eyes open. The world was blurry and too bright, but the phantom neon lights were gone. I sniffled, slowly lifting my head from my knees.

As I sat up, a new sound cut through the ambient roar of the crowded terminal.

It was coming from the concourse's overhead public address speakers. Instead of a robotic boarding announcement, the speakers were broadcasting holiday music. A dramatic, slightly melancholic piano melody echoed down the hall, followed immediately by swelling, triumphant orchestral strings.

I froze, my audio processor locking onto the complex, layered sound. It was a song by The Arctic Symphony Project, a massive rock-orchestra ensemble that Michael frequently played in the background while building his computer networks.

The heavy, driving rhythm of the strings built a massive wall of anticipation, and then the vocalist’s powerful voice filled the concourse:

A thousand miles of winter gray

Another dawn, another day

The engine's hum, a weary soul

A heavy price, a heavy toll

My breath caught in my throat. I stared blankly at the metal spokes of Jessica’s wheelchair. The lyrics were a perfect, literal transcription of my algorithm. We had crossed a thousand miles of gray sky. We had paid a heavy emotional price.

I see your face on frozen glass

A memory that's built to last

A single light that I must find

To leave the endless dark behind

I closed my eyes. The face I saw wasn't just a memory; it was the absolute destination. It was Michael. He was the single light at the end of this terrifying, chaotic transit system.

Then, the orchestra swelled to a massive, soaring crescendo, and the chorus hit with the force of a physical tidal wave:

And the lights are bright, on this winter's night

Because you're coming home

Yes, you're finally coming home

Through the wind and rain, through the doubt and pain

You are not alone

No, you're finally coming home!

The mathematical reality of the lyrics slammed into my neurodivergent brain, completely overwhelming my emotional processor.

My chest seized. A second wave of intense physical vibration ripped through me, completely distinct from the first. The first meltdown had been caused by pain and terror. This one was caused by a joy so massive, so profoundly absolute, that my body literally could not contain the data.

I gasped, my hands flying up to cover my mouth as a loud, high-pitched vocalization of pure, unadulterated happiness tore out of my throat. New tears flooded my eyes, spilling hot and fast down my cheeks.

"Rachel?" Jessica asked, a spike of alarm returning to her voice as my shoulders began to shake violently again. She leaned forward, thinking my system was crashing a second time.

I shook my head frantically, my hands still clamped over my mouth. I couldn't form the words. I just pointed a trembling finger up toward the overhead speaker.

The towns fly by, a blur of light

I count the hours in the night

Remembering a whispered vow

It feels so close, it's coming now

Jessica paused, listening to the music echoing over the crowd. She looked at my shaking shoulders, the intense, brilliant light returning to my wet eyes, and the massive, trembling smile hiding behind my hands.

Her analytical brain instantly recalculated. She let out a long, breathless laugh, realizing I wasn't in pain. I was vibrating with anticipation.

The fire's lit, the table's set

A promise that I haven't met

But in my heart, the feeling grows

A warmth that melts the winter snows

"You're finally coming home," Jessica whispered, quoting the chorus right back to me, her own eyes growing slightly glassy. "He is waiting for you, Rachel."

As the song hit the instrumental bridge, a powerful, emotional electric guitar solo soared over the orchestra, echoing through the terminal. The music felt like pure energy. It wasn't a sensory attack; it was a power source.

I dropped my hands from my mouth. The overwhelming surge of joyful emotion fueled my exhausted muscles. I pushed myself up off the hard tile floor, wiping the happy tears from my cheeks with the back of my sleeve.

I stepped firmly behind Jessica’s wheelchair and locked both of my hands onto the rubber push-handles.

And the lights are bright, on this winter's night

Because you're coming home

Yes, you're finally coming home...

"Gate," I commanded, my raspy voice vibrating with absolute determination.

"Concourse C, Gate Twenty-Two," Jessica confirmed, her hands resting confidently on her lap as she entrusted the physical navigation entirely to me.

Coming home!

(Finally coming home!)

On this day!

As the orchestra and guitar built to a massive, triumphant final chord, I pushed the heavy wheelchair forward, steering us seamlessly into the flow of the holiday crowd. The exhaustion and the trauma of the tunnel were completely overwritten by the math of the final leg. I was the engine. We were halfway there. And I was going home to my brother.
