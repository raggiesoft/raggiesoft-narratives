I woke up to the heavy, rhythmic thumping of a dog’s tail hitting the side of the sofa bed.

I opened my eyes slowly. The living room was dim, illuminated only by the thin slivers of morning sunlight cutting through the closed plastic blinds. Archie was sitting on the rug right next to my head, staring at me with his one milky blue eye, his tail beating a steady tempo against the upholstery. At the foot of the bed, Baby let out a long, rumbling snore, still completely dead to the world.

I shifted my weight, my legs incredibly stiff from the awkward angle of the pull-out mattress. Rachel was still asleep next to me, her breathing deep and even, her forehead pressed against my shoulder.

For the first time in over a year, I didn't wake up bracing for the sound of our parents yelling in the kitchen. The Delaney Street house was entirely, beautifully silent.

"I'm awake," Rachel murmured, her voice muffled against my t-shirt. She didn't pull away; she just tightened her grip on the quilt.

"Morning," I whispered back, letting my head fall back onto the pillow.

We lay there in the quiet for a few more minutes, letting our brains slowly boot up and process the massive shift in reality. It was Saturday. Mom was in another state. Dad had moved into his rented room two days ago. For the next two weeks, we had absolute, unsupervised control over our physical environment.

"You know," I said quietly, staring up at the popcorn ceiling, "if this was a movie, or if we were normal teenagers, there’s a whole script for what we are supposed to be doing today."

Rachel shifted, propping herself up on her elbow to look down at me. Her hair was a messy, static-filled bird's nest. "What script?"

"The 'home alone for two weeks' script," I explained, tracing a pattern on the quilt with my thumb. "We're sixteen and fourteen. According to the rules of high school, the second Mom's plane took off, we were supposed to get on the rotary phone and invite fifty people over. We're supposed to be blasting loud music, breaking things, and sneaking our boyfriends and girlfriends in through the back door."

Rachel blinked, her face contorting in genuine horror at the idea. The thought of a house party—a massive influx of unpredictable strangers, loud, overlapping noise, and people touching our things—was a literal nightmare scenario.

"But I don't have a boyfriend," she stated, leaning heavily back against my side. "And you don't have a girlfriend."

"Exactly," I said. "We're failing the script."

"Good," she huffed. "The script sounds exhausting."

I smiled, resting my hand on her shoulder. "It is. But that's what people expect. Think about those kids looking at us at the mall yesterday. Society looks at two teenagers left completely alone in a house and assumes the worst. There's this massive elephant in the room about what teenagers are 'supposed' to do with zero supervision. They expect us to be reckless. They expect us to break all the big rules, or cross boundaries, or do something stupid just because nobody is here to stop us."

Rachel was quiet for a moment, processing the invisible neurotypical rules that always seemed to dictate how everyone else operated. She looked around our dark, quiet living room—the VCR still loaded with an animated dinosaur movie, the dogs sleeping peacefully, the heavy quilt pulled up to our chins.

"I don't want to cross boundaries or be reckless," Rachel said, her voice dropping to that serious, non-negotiable tone she used when establishing a permanent fact. "I just want to be right here. I want the blinds closed, and I want to know exactly where you are."

"I know," I promised, giving her shoulder a firm squeeze. "Me too."

The reality was that the "normal" teenage rebellion held zero appeal for us. We had spent the last twelve months surviving the chaotic, terrifying unpredictability of our parents' failing marriage. We didn't want the thrill of breaking the rules; we craved the absolute, iron-clad safety of a routine. Our rebellion wasn't throwing a wild party or sneaking people in; our rebellion was shutting the entire world out.

"So," Rachel said, her stomach letting out a loud, sudden growl. "Since we aren't throwing a massive party... what is our script for today?"

I ran the calculations for a perfect, low-demand Saturday. "We stay in our pajamas. We pour massive bowls of sugary cereal. We sit right back down on this sofa bed, and we watch whatever we want on TV without anyone telling us to go outside and be productive."

A wide, genuine smile broke across her face. "I like our script much better."

She pushed the heavy blankets back and climbed off the mattress. Archie immediately sprang up, grabbing his slobbery rope toy and trotting after her as she headed toward the kitchen. I sat up slowly, stretching my tight legs, the quiet morning air feeling lighter than it had in years.

The outside world could have their parties and their complicated social expectations. Inside the walls of the Delaney Street house, we were building our own rules.
