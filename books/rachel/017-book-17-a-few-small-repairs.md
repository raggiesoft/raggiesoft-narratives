# Book 17: A Few Small Repairs

## Chapter 1: Breakfast in Vancouver (Tuesday, November 21, 2006)

### Part 1: The Anchor's Rest – 7:00 AM (PST)

Waking up was not a slow, gradual process. It was a sudden, blinding spike of white-hot agony.

I opened my eyes, staring up at the generic, textured ceiling of the Crown Regency hotel room. I tried to shift my weight on the queen-sized mattress, and my lower back immediately locked up in a violent, paralyzing spasm. The toll of piggy-backing Jessica down the concrete arena steps, completely absorbing the shock of the Cruiserweight barricade crash, and then hauling her back out of the Rose City Center had finally come due.

I let out a sharp, involuntary hiss of pain through my teeth.

The sound woke Rachel. She was curled tightly against my side, her head resting on my chest. She blinked her dark eyes open, the grogginess clearing in a fraction of a second as her hyper-observant radar detected my distress.

She pushed herself up on one elbow, looking down at me.

"My back," I rasped, barely able to draw a full breath. "It's completely locked."

Rachel didn't panic. She didn't freeze. For my entire life, I had been her physical and emotional anchor, absorbing her meltdowns, shielding her from the crowds, and holding her together when the world threatened to tear her apart.

Now, the physics had shifted. The anchor was broken. It was her turn.

She looked down at her own body, and then at mine, realizing we were both still wearing the damp, sweat-stained street clothes we had worn to the AWE broadcast yesterday. She swung her legs over the edge of the mattress and stood up, her jaw set with a fierce, pragmatic determination.

She walked over to the corner of the room where our luggage was stacked. She unzipped our duffel bags, pulling out a fresh change of clothes for both of us, and grabbed two clean, white hotel towels from the rack near the bathroom door.

Rachel walked back to the bed and leaned over me. She slid her arm firmly under my shoulders.

"Arm," she instructed quietly.

I gritted my teeth and slung my heavy arm over her shoulders. She braced her stance, using her core strength to carefully hoist me into a sitting position. The pain was nauseating, but Rachel didn't waver. She wrapped her arm tightly around my waist, acting as a solid, living crutch, and slowly guided me toward the ADA-accessible bathroom.

In the neurotypical world, an adult brother and sister navigating a bathroom together would be completely derailed by the rigid, invisible rules of modesty and privacy. But those rules had never applied to our pack. Privacy was a luxury for people who didn't rely on each other for basic survival.

She helped me shuffle through the doorway, maneuvering me directly to the toilet so I could take care of business.

While I sat there, exhausted and in pain, Rachel completely ignored me. She methodically stripped off her own rumpled street clothes, tossing them into a pile in the corner, and turned the heavy chrome handles of the shower. Because the room was designed for Jessica, it featured a massive, roll-in shower with a wide, fold-down teak bench bolted to the tiled wall.

Once the steam began to fill the small room, Rachel turned back to me. She helped me stand up just enough to strip away my stiff denim jeans and heavy t-shirt. She supported my weight flawlessly as she guided me into the shower enclosure, gently lowering me onto the teak bench.

The hot water hit my shoulders and cascaded down my spine. The intense heat immediately began to loosen the suffocating, steel-cable tension in my lower back muscles. I closed my eyes, letting out a long, ragged exhale of relief.

Rachel quickly used the toilet herself, completely unbothered, before stepping into the hot spray with me.

She took the hotel soap and thoroughly helped me wash up, her hands moving with the same methodical, clinical care I had used when pulling her out of sensory meltdowns. Once I was clean, she carefully helped me stand, transferring me back to the closed lid of the toilet seat so I could dry off while sitting down.

She stepped back under the water to wash herself.

Sitting there wrapped in a warm towel, watching the steam billow against the mirror, I felt a profound, overwhelming sense of gratitude. She wasn't the terrified, mute little girl hiding under the bunk bed anymore. She was a fiercely capable woman protecting her pack.

Once she finished, she dried herself off, grabbed the clean clothes she had laid out on the vanity, and helped me get dressed. It took a few minutes of careful maneuvering to get my sweatpants and t-shirt on without triggering another spasm, but we managed it. Then, she quickly pulled on her own clean cargo pants and a soft, comfortable hoodie.

With my arm slung back over her shoulder, Rachel guided me out of the humid bathroom and into the main hotel room. She lowered me gently into one of the padded armchairs near the window.

"Thank you," I breathed, leaning my head back against the chair.

"You're welcome," Rachel replied softly, giving my shoulder a firm, grounding squeeze.

On the other queen bed, Jessica and Kristin were just waking up. Jessica sat up, taking in the scene—me sitting in the chair looking utterly exhausted, and Rachel standing over me looking fresh and completely in control.

"He's locked up," Rachel reported to Jessica, her tone perfectly mirroring our older sister's logistical cadence. "We are washed and dressed."

Jessica offered a soft, deeply appreciative smile. "Good job, Rachel. Give Kristin and me twenty minutes to cycle through the bathroom, and then we'll move."

I sat quietly in the chair, watching the morning traffic move along the wet Portland streets below, while Kristin and Jessica executed their own morning routines. The pain in my back was a dull, heavy throb now, manageable as long as I didn't move too fast.

Thirty minutes later, the pack was officially mobilized.

We gathered our bags, leaving the heavy, souvenir AWE folding chairs stacked safely in the corner of the room to retrieve later. I slung my arms over both Rachel and Kristin, letting the two women support my weight as we walked out into the carpeted hallway.

We took the elevator down to the lobby, pushed through the heavy glass doors of the Crown Regency, and stepped out into the freezing, damp Pacific Northwest morning.

Just a few hundred yards away, the platforms of the Rose City Center CTS station were waiting. It was time to cross the river.

### Part 2: Crossing the Columbia River – 8:30 AM (PST)

The damp, biting cold of the Pacific Northwest morning did nothing to slow our momentum. We left the quiet warmth of the Crown Regency, walking the short distance to the Rose City Center CTS station. My lower back was a tight, throbbing knot of exhausted muscle from carrying Jessica the night before, but having Rachel physically tethered to my side, acting as a living crutch, made the pain manageable.

We had a mission to execute, and Jessica had engineered the logistics flawlessly.

Rachel was in her element, leading the navigation with absolute, hyper-fixated precision. We boarded the northbound CTS light rail, riding the train to the end of the line at the Delta Park / Vanport station. From there, the transit algorithm shifted to heavy rubber tires.

We transferred onto a massive, articulated CTS bus, the diesel engine roaring as it carried us over the steel grating of the Interstate Bridge. We passed Jantzen Beach, crossing the invisible state line over the dark, choppy waters of the Columbia River, and descended into Downtown Vancouver.

"Transferring here," Rachel announced smoothly, guiding us off the bus and into the damp morning air.

We caught our next connection, riding steadily down Fourth Plain Boulevard until the sprawling concrete footprint of Vancouver Plaza came into view. From the massive transit island at the mall, we boarded our final leg: the Route 7.

As the bus hissed its way toward the Orchards section of Vancouver, I looked over at Jessica. She was staring out the rain-streaked window, her expression an impenetrable, tactical mask.

She had set the perfect trap for our father.

Dad was notorious for using cheap logistical complaints to avoid accountability. He always whined about the cost of gas, the traffic on the bridge, or the hassle of crossing into Portland. So, Jessica had simply eradicated his excuses. We were going to The Riverway Diner—a classic, vinyl-booth restaurant sitting right next to Franklin's Hypermarket. By car, the diner was exactly seven minutes away from his second-floor walk-up at the Prairie View Apartments.

She had invited his girlfriend, Cathy. She had offered to pay the entire breakfast bill, and she even offered to hand him cash for the gas he used to drive the seven minutes down the road. She had systematically removed every single roadblock he could possibly use to justify abandoning us.

### Part 3: The Tactical Seating Chart – 9:45 AM (PST)

The Route 7 dropped us off at the Franklin's Hypermarket transit shelter. We walked across the wet asphalt to The Riverway Diner. The air inside smelled of strong coffee, frying bacon, and maple syrup.

"Party of six, please," Jessica told the hostess. "We need a booth that can accommodate a wheelchair at the head."

The hostess led us to a large, spacious booth in the back corner of the restaurant. The table was designed to seat three people on each side. The second we reached the table, Jessica’s pragmatic, architectural brain took over. She didn't just tell us to sit down; she orchestrated the seating chart like a general arranging a battlefield.

She knew Dad would try to dictate the narrative, and she knew he hated the fact that Rachel relied on me for sensory regulation. He constantly tried to separate us, insisting that his teenage children shouldn't be so physically attached.

Jessica wasn't going to give him an inch of that leverage.

"Rachel," Jessica instructed, pointing to the booth bench against the far wall. "Inside seat. All the way against the window."

Rachel slid into the booth, her back pressing securely against the glass. It was the safest, most enclosed position in the restaurant, shielding her from the chaotic noise and movement of the diner.

"Michael," Jessica commanded. "Dead center. Right next to her."

I slid in, pressing my shoulder and hip firmly against Rachel's. I was the heavy, physical barrier between her and the rest of the table. Rachel immediately leaned her weight against my ribs, letting out a soft, grounded exhale. We were locked in.

"Kristin, take the aisle seat," Jessica finished.

Kristin slid in next to me, her posture tall and defensive. Our side of the table was a completely unified, impenetrable shield wall.

Jessica then wheeled her manual chair directly to the head of the table, locking her brakes with a sharp, mechanical click.

From her position at the head, she commanded the entire space. She could make direct eye contact with everyone sitting on our side, and she had a perfect, unobstructed view of the empty side of the booth—the two seats reserved for Tom and Cathy.

The psychological warfare of the seating chart was brilliant. When Dad arrived, he would be forced to sit opposite us. He would have to look at the undeniable, physical proof that his children were a fiercely united pack that he couldn't break, all while answering to his oldest daughter sitting at the head of the table paying the bill.

I rested my hands on the laminated tabletop, feeling the steady pressure of Rachel leaning against my side. The trap was set, the perimeter was secured, and all we had to do now was wait for the bell above the diner door to ring.

### Part 4: The Pathetic Text – 9:55 AM (PST)

By 9:50 AM, the diner was buzzing with the loud, chaotic morning rush. The clatter of heavy ceramic plates, the hiss of the espresso machine, and the overlapping chatter of a dozen conversations filled the air.

The two seats on the opposite side of our booth remained entirely empty.

Rachel was sitting perfectly still against my side. She hadn't taken her jacket off. Both of her hands were wrapped tightly around a thick mug of hot chocolate, her knuckles white. She kept her dark eyes locked unrelentingly on the glass entrance doors at the front of the restaurant, but the hopeful anticipation was beginning to curdle into a tense, suffocating dread.

I watched the digital clock on my silver flip phone tick over to 9:55 AM.

The tension in the booth was becoming radioactive. Next to me, Kristin was glaring at the empty seat, her jaw clenched tight enough to crack her teeth. At the head of the table, Jessica was staring at her cell phone resting on the laminated tabletop, her expression hardening into a mask of pure, glacial disgust.

Suddenly, Jessica's phone vibrated against the table.

It wasn't a phone call. It was a text message.

Jessica picked the phone up, her eyes scanning the tiny digital screen. I watched her read it. The last lingering shred of pragmatic, daughterly tolerance in her eyes completely died, replaced by an absolute, terrifyingly cold void.

"What does it say?" I asked, my voice low and tight.

Jessica didn't answer me verbally. She just turned the phone around and slid it across the table so I could read the glowing green screen myself.

Cathy got called in for a double shift and took the sedan. It's pouring out and my bad knee is acting up, so I'm not walking in the rain to the bus stop. Money is tight anyway. Have a safe flight back to Virginia.

I stared at the pixelated text. It was the ultimate, pathetic surrender. We had literally crossed a state line using public transit, in the exact same rain, to come to him. Jessica was paying the bill. He didn't even have the spine to dial the number and hear his oldest daughter's voice when he bailed. He hid behind a text message, blaming his girlfriend, the weather, and his physical ailments, completely ignoring the fact that his disabled daughter and his neurodivergent children had just navigated three buses and a train to sit in this booth.

"Jess?" Rachel whispered.

Her voice was raspy, trembling with a frantic, rising panic. She looked from the phone to Jessica’s face. "Where is he?"

Jessica let out a slow, devastated sigh. She reached across the table and placed her hand gently over Rachel’s, right over the hot chocolate mug.

"He's not coming, sweetie," Jessica said, her voice completely stripped of any sugar-coating. "He isn't coming."

The mathematical equation in Rachel’s brain stalled.

He's not coming.

She had endured the crushing crowds. She had braved the sensory nightmare of the transit system. She had swallowed years of trauma, abuse, and abandonment, all to sit in this diner for the singular, hyper-fixated goal of having a family breakfast.

The illusion finally, irreparably shattered.

I braced myself, tightening my arm around her waist, preparing for the devastating wave of grief to hit. I expected the jagged wheeze, the collapsing posture, the need to flee the loud restaurant.

But it didn't happen.

Rachel stared at the empty booth across from her. Her dark eyes, which had been wide and vulnerable just a moment ago, slowly narrowed. The heartbreak didn't break her; it instantly crystallized into something infinitely harder.

Jessica picked her phone back up. She didn't throw money on the table to run away. She calmly flipped her phone shut and picked up the laminated diner menu.

"Well," Jessica announced, her voice ringing with absolute, clinical finality. "I am starving. And, as it turns out, I am suddenly flush with a lot of extra cash every month."

I looked at my older sister, my analytical brain instantly catching the financial implication. "The car?"

"The car," Jessica confirmed smoothly. "The terms of my bailout were explicitly clear. If he wanted me to keep making his car payments, he had to prove he was working toward his high school equivalency. Given that he just refused to show up to a free breakfast because he was too lazy to catch a bus, I think we can mathematically guarantee he isn't studying for that exam."

Jessica looked directly at Rachel, offering a fierce, unyielding smile.

"The Quantum money stops today. If the bank repossesses his sedan, he can learn the Columbia Transit System schedules like the rest of us."

I felt Rachel stiffen against my ribs. I looked down at my little sister.

There were tears pooling in her eyes, yes, but they weren't falling. Instead, a brilliant, terrifying fire had ignited behind them. The neurodivergent need to fix the pattern, to repair the broken family unit, was completely burning away. She finally realized that the bridge didn't need to be repaired.

It needed to be burned to the ground.

Rachel took a deep, steadying breath. She uncurled her hands from her hot chocolate mug, sat up perfectly straight against the vinyl booth, and reached across the table to grab her own menu.

"I want the Belgian waffles," Rachel rasped, her voice completely devoid of a single ounce of grief. "With extra strawberries."

Kristin let out a sharp, triumphant laugh from my right side, grabbing her own menu. "I'm getting the lumberjack skillet."

I leaned back against the booth, staring at the three women sitting at the table. We weren't a broken family mourning an empty seat anymore. We were a fully realized, self-sustaining pack, completely and permanently severing the dead weight.

"I think I'll have the omelet," I said, a massive, genuine smile breaking across my face.

Tom Ragsdale was officially cut out of the system, and breakfast was finally served.

### Part 5: The Financial Guillotine – 10:15 AM (PST)

The waitress arrived balancing a massive tray, completely oblivious to the shifting tectonic plates of our family dynamic. She slid a plate of golden Belgian waffles smothered in strawberries in front of Rachel, dropped a steaming lumberjack skillet in front of Kristin, and set my omelet down.

The food smelled incredible, but the atmosphere at the table was sharp, tactical, and entirely focused on the boardroom execution of Tom Ragsdale.

Jessica took a slow sip of her coffee, her analytical brain already plotting the exact trajectory of the fallout.

"Here is the logistical reality of the car," Jessica began, addressing the table like a board of directors. "I have already processed the December payment. The bank drafted it last week, which means his sedan is legally secure through the end of the year. The question is how we handle January."

I set my fork down, my own processor booting up to run the variables. "You have two options. You either quietly cancel the automatic transfer and let him figure it out when the repo man shows up, or you tell him outright."

"Exactly," Jessica nodded. "Let's run the pros and cons. Option A: The silent treatment. If I quietly stop paying, there is zero immediate friction. I don't have to deal with his frantic phone calls or his defensive anger today. He simply defaults in January, and the consequences catch up to him naturally."

"But that gives him a loophole," I argued, seeing the flaw instantly. "If you ghost the payment, it gives him plausible deniability. He gets to play the victim. He’ll tell everyone that his vindictive daughter set him up to fail without warning."

Kristin swallowed a bite of her eggs, pointing her fork at Jessica. "Michael is right. He’s a coward. If you don't explicitly tell him, he’ll spin the narrative to make himself look like a martyr."

Rachel, who had been meticulously cutting her waffle into perfectly uniform, bite-sized squares, suddenly paused. She didn't look up from her plate, but her voice carried that raspy, immovable weight of an absolute decree.

"Tell him," Rachel stated.

She looked up, her dark eyes locking onto Jessica. The transformation in our younger sister was breathtaking. The terrified, mute girl who used to hide from his temper was completely gone, burned away by the sheer audacity of his text message.

"If you don't tell him, he gets to have a peaceful Christmas," Rachel reasoned, her logic razor-sharp and entirely unforgiving. "He doesn't deserve peace. He chose not to show up today. He should feel exactly what that costs."

Jessica’s lips curved into a fierce, predatory smile. "I completely agree. But there is one more variable we need to factor in: Cathy."

The mention of the girlfriend’s name made the temperature at the table plummet.

"She took the sedan to work today," I pointed out, recalling his pathetic text message.

"Exactly," Jessica said, leaning forward. "They share that vehicle. Cathy isn't just an innocent bystander in this. She sat back and watched him completely fracture our family. She tried to treat Rachel like a captive replacement child. She enables his laziness, and she actively benefits from the money I’ve been injecting into their household."

"She is just as guilty as he is," Rachel hissed, gripping her syrup-covered fork. "She didn't want him to come see us today, either."

"So if the car goes..." Kristin trailed off, grinning.

"If the car goes, Cathy loses her ride to work," Jessica finished smoothly. "They are both forced to rely on the Columbia Transit System in the freezing rain. If we drop the hammer today, we aren't just punishing Dad; we are destabilizing their entire household right before the holidays. They can share the misery."

The mathematical symmetry of it was absolute perfection. The punishment fit the crime flawlessly.

"Option B it is," Jessica announced.

She picked up her cell phone from the laminated table. She didn't bother making a phone call; Tom had set the terms of engagement by hiding behind text messages, so she was going to execute him on his own preferred battlefield.

Her thumbs flew across the small keypad.

Since you couldn't be bothered to catch a bus to see your children, I am officially waiving your requirement to study for your equivalency exam. Because my financial support ends today. December is paid. Consider this your 30-day notice to figure out how to fund your own life. Do not contact us again. Have a good life, Tom.

Jessica read the message out loud to the table.

"Send it," Rachel commanded, without a single microsecond of hesitation.

Jessica pressed the button. The phone flashed *Message Sent*.

The financial lifeline was officially severed. The Quantum money was gone. Tom and Cathy were effectively marooned on the Left Coast, completely at the mercy of their own disastrous choices.

Jessica set the phone face-down on the table and picked up her fork.

"Eat up, everyone," Jessica smiled, the heavy, suffocating weight of our father's shadow permanently lifted from her shoulders. "We have a train to catch, and we are not letting that man steal another minute of our vacation."

I took a massive bite of my omelet. Sitting shoulder-to-shoulder with Rachel, perfectly insulated within our united perimeter, I realized that I had never tasted a better breakfast in my entire life.

### Part 6: The 360 Area Code – 10:45 AM (PST)

The heavy, comforting atmosphere of our victory breakfast was settling over the booth when a sharp, sustained buzzing cut through the clatter of the diner.

Jessica’s cell phone was vibrating aggressively against the laminated tabletop.

All four of us stopped eating. Jessica looked down at the glowing digital display. The incoming number wasn't saved in her contacts, but the area code was glaringly obvious: *360*.

Clark County. Vancouver, Washington.

Rachel went entirely still against my side. The strawberry-covered piece of waffle she had been holding on her fork hovered motionless in the air.

"Is it him?" Kristin asked, her voice dropping into a low, defensive register.

"Who else would be calling me from a local Vancouver number right now?" Jessica replied, her tone completely devoid of surprise. She didn't reach for the phone. She just sat back in her wheelchair and watched it vibrate.

My analytical brain quickly ran the timeline. "He got the text message. He read the part about the car payment being cancelled in January, and he is panicking."

"Exactly," Jessica said, her eyes narrowing with a cold, absolute disgust. "He didn't call to apologize for missing breakfast. He didn't call to hear Rachel's voice. He is only calling because his wallet is suddenly lighter. He is frantic because he just realized his leverage is entirely gone."

The phone stopped ringing. The screen went dark.

Three seconds later, it lit up and started vibrating again. He was double-calling, trying to brute-force his way through the boundary.

Jessica picked the phone up. She didn't press the green 'Talk' button, and she didn't send him to voicemail. She pressed her thumb firmly against the power button on the top edge of the device and held it down until the screen completely powered off.

"Turn them off," Jessica commanded, looking around the table. "All of them. Right now."

I reached into my pocket and pulled out my silver flip phone, powering it down instantly. Kristin did the same with hers.

Rachel pulled her phone out of her cargo pocket, but she hesitated, staring at the blank screen.

"He pays for this one," Rachel whispered, the realization hitting her like a physical weight. "I'm on his plan."

"I know you are," Jessica said smoothly. "And Michael is still on Mom's plan. Kristin is paying for her own out-of-pocket, and I'm on a single line. The digital perimeter of this pack is completely fragmented, which leaves vulnerabilities."

Jessica waved the waitress over and placed her debit card on top of the breakfast check.

"If Tom realizes he can't reach me," Jessica explained, her strategic mind already ten steps ahead of his panic, "he is going to try to call Rachel's phone to scream at her. And when she doesn't answer, he will use his administrative access on the account to disconnect her line entirely just to be vindictive."

Rachel’s finger immediately clamped down on the power button of her device, killing the power to the phone before the thought could even fully materialize.

"So," Jessica smiled, a fierce, protective light returning to her eyes as the waitress handed her back the receipt. "It is time for the next small repair. Drink your coffee, everyone. We are going shopping."

### Part 7: The Unified Grid – 11:30 AM (PST)

We bundled back up against the freezing, damp Pacific Northwest air, leaving The Riverway Diner and crossing the wet asphalt back to the transit shelter.

The Route 7 bus arrived a few minutes later, its heavy air brakes hissing as it knelt to the curb. We boarded the bus, leaving the Orchards section of Vancouver behind, and rode steadily back down Fourth Plain Boulevard toward the massive concrete footprint of Vancouver Plaza.

By the time we navigated from the transit island into the sprawling, multi-level indoor mall, my lower back was throbbing again, but the adrenaline of the mission kept me moving.

Jessica bypassed the department stores and the food court, wheeling herself with absolute purpose down the main concourse until we stopped in front of a sleek, brightly lit storefront for Pacific Cellular.

It was a corporately owned flagship store, full of glass display cases showcasing the newest 2006 mobile technology.

"Follow my lead," Jessica instructed, rolling her manual chair straight through the doors.

A sales representative wearing a branded polo shirt immediately walked over to greet us. "Welcome to Pacific Cellular! How can I help you today?"

"I need to open a brand-new, four-line family account," Jessica stated, projecting the flawless, unshakeable authority of a corporate IT engineer. "I also need four brand-new devices. I will be purchasing the hardware outright today."

The representative's eyes widened slightly at the prospect of a massive, commission-heavy sale. "Absolutely, ma'am. We can get that set up right now. Are we porting over any existing numbers?"

"No," Rachel answered instantly, stepping up to the counter right beside Jessica. Her voice was raspy, but the conviction in it was absolute. "We want new numbers. Completely new."

"All four lines need fresh, unlisted numbers," Jessica confirmed. She reached into her messenger bag and pulled out her corporate ID lanyard, placing the heavy plastic card on the glass counter. "And please apply the Quantum corporate employee discount to the monthly data plan."

The representative nodded enthusiastically, leading us over to the display cases to pick out the hardware.

While Jessica handled the complex logistics of the contract and verified the data limits to become the sole, undisputed account holder, Rachel, Kristin, and I picked out our new phones. They were sleek, modern devices, a massive upgrade from the cheap, outdated flip phones Mom and Dad had previously saddled us with.

We brought the boxes back to the register. The representative clicked his pen, scanning his computer monitor.

"Alright, Ms. Ragsdale. I have your account approved and the hardware scanned in," the representative smiled cheerfully. "I'll go ahead and generate a block of local 360 numbers for the four devices..."

"Actually, no thank you," Jessica interrupted smoothly, her tone perfectly polite but leaving absolutely no room for debate. She tapped her Washington State ID resting on the glass counter. "My billing address is in Renton. I want 425 area codes for all four lines, please."

The representative blinked, his fingers pausing over the keyboard. He quickly deleted the data he had just entered. "Of course. Pulling from the King County pool right now."

Rachel looked down at the glass counter. A slow, triumphant smile spread across her face as she realized exactly what Jessica had just done. She wasn't just buying us new phones; she was building a digital moat. The 360 area code represented Clark County, Tom, Cathy, the squalid apartment, and the trauma of the divorce. By forcing the 425 area code, Jessica was permanently erasing Vancouver from our digital footprint.

Ten minutes later, the representative handed Jessica a sleek shopping bag containing our new activation slips.

We walked back out into the bustling mall concourse. Jessica stopped near a carpeted seating area and handed a sealed box to each of us.

"Take the old phones out of your pockets," Jessica instructed.

I pulled my old silver flip phone out. Rachel pulled out the clunky device Dad had given her. Kristin produced her old standalone phone.

"Throw them in the trash," Jessica commanded, pointing to the heavy metal waste bin a few feet away.

Rachel didn't even blink. She walked over to the bin and dropped the device Dad paid for directly into the garbage. I followed suit, tossing the phone tied to Mom's account right next to it, severing the last digital tether to the East Coast. Kristin tossed hers in as well, solidifying her place on our grid.

"From now on," Jessica said, looking at the three of us as we held our new, unopened boxes, "this pack is on a single, unified network. I control the account, I pay the bill, and neither Mom nor Dad will ever have administrative access to our communication again. If Tom Ragsdale wants to talk to us, he is going to have to find a way to earn our new numbers."

Rachel looked down at the sleek new phone box in her hands.

She stepped over to my side, immediately pressing her shoulder heavily against my ribs, claiming her anchor space. I slung my arm around her back, ignoring the ache in my spine, and pulled her flush against me.

"He's never getting this number," Rachel whispered fiercely.

"No, he's not," I agreed.

The bridge was burned. The lines were cut. The repairs were officially underway.

### Part 8: The Union Station Protocol – 12:30 PM (PST)

The heavy glass doors of the mall slid shut behind us, cutting off the chaotic, echoing noise of the holiday shoppers. We stepped back out into the freezing, damp Pacific Northwest afternoon, but the atmosphere within our pack felt lighter than it had in years.

Deep in our pockets, our four new cell phones sat perfectly silent. The 425 area codes were a digital fortress. Tom Ragsdale could scream, panic, and dial his phone until his fingers bled, and absolutely none of it would reach us.

We made our way across the wet asphalt to the massive Vancouver Plaza transit island. Rachel stepped up to the curb, her posture completely transformed. The fearful, hunched girl who had boarded the bus this morning was gone. In her place was a confident, hyper-focused transit navigator, completely in her element.

"Okay, pack," Rachel rasped, looking down the long row of bus bays. "We need the local route to Downtown Vancouver first. Then we transfer to the heavy bus to cross the river."

When the CTS bus hissed to a halt, the driver lowered the hydraulic ramp for Jessica. Kristin and I followed them aboard. We took our seats, and I immediately slung my arm around Rachel’s shoulders. The dull ache in my lower back was a persistent reminder of the Monday Night WAR, but the emotional relief flooding my system acted as a powerful anesthetic.

The bus carried us away from the mall, winding through the wet, gray streets until the dense, urban blocks of Downtown Vancouver rose up around us.

"Transferring here," Rachel announced as the air brakes engaged.

We stepped out onto the damp sidewalk. We didn't have to wait long. A massive, articulated CTS bus pulled up to the curb. We boarded our connection, the heavy diesel engine roaring as the driver accelerated away from the downtown core.

As the bus merged onto the highway approach, the sprawling, dark expanse of the Columbia River came into view. We rumbled over the heavy steel grating of the Interstate Bridge, the tires humming a loud, rhythmic vibration through the floorboards.

I looked out the rain-streaked window at the choppy water below. We were officially leaving Clark County. Every revolution of the bus tires put more physical distance between us and the squalid, toxic reality of Dad’s apartment. Passing the Jantzen Beach stop, we crossed the invisible state line, returning to Oregon.

The bridge wasn't just a piece of infrastructure anymore; it was a permanent, geographical boundary that we were never going to cross for him again.

The bus descended off the highway and pulled into the Delta Park / Vanport CTS station.

"End of the line for the buses," Rachel said, standing up and pulling the collar of her jacket tight against her neck. "We catch the light rail from here."

I piloted Jessica’s wheelchair down the ramp and onto the exposed concrete platform. The biting wind whipped across the transit center, but the cold didn't bother us. We were moving as a single, perfectly synchronized unit.

The sleek CTS light rail train glided into the station a few minutes later, its doors opening with a soft, melodic chime. We boarded the train, taking our familiar positions in the accessible seating area. The doors sealed shut, and the electric motors hummed as we accelerated south, cutting smoothly through the Portland suburbs.

The rhythmic swaying of the train car was deeply regulating. I closed my eyes for a moment, leaning my head back against the window, listening to the quiet murmur of Jessica and Kristin discussing college classes, while Rachel happily tracked our progress on the digital stop indicator.
