We chained our bikes to the racks outside the fine arts wing. As we walked toward the heavy double doors of the band room, a familiar figure pushed off the brick wall to meet us.

Ashley Raybourn had been waiting. She was wearing her matching white band t-shirt, her hair pulled back into a tight, utilitarian ponytail. As the pack sentinel, her radar was flawless. She didn’t need to ask how the morning at the Delaney Street house had been; she took one look at Rachel’s rigid posture and the exhausted set of my jaw and immediately knew the perimeter had been breached.

She didn’t say a word. She just stepped forward and pulled us both into a fierce, suffocatingly tight hug. It was the exact physical counterweight to the screaming we had left behind. I wrapped my arm around Ashley’s back, and Rachel melted into her other side. We stood there on the concrete for a long moment, letting the heavy, immovable gravity of our trio absorb the residual tremors of the morning.

"Welcome to high school band, Rachel," Ashley finally said, her voice a low, protective rumble as she pulled back, keeping a grounding hand on each of our shoulders. "You ready for this?"

Rachel gave a small, jerky nod. "Yeah."

"Good. Let's go grab the gear."

We pushed through the double doors into the cavernous, air-conditioned band room. It was percussion and leadership camp today, which meant the room was blissfully free of the chaotic woodwind and brass sections.

More importantly, it meant the room was completely free of Ashley Wiswell.

The clarinets weren't scheduled to join camp until Wednesday, giving me two full days of a secure, predictable environment before I had to deal with the ghost of last October. It had been nearly a year since the terrifying incident with her father, Paul Wiswell, but my nervous system still logged her entire existence as a lethal threat.

After that disastrous trip to Roanoke Rapids, North Carolina—an event that had completely shattered whatever fragile reality Wiswell was operating in—her social standing had plummeted. She spent her days walking the halls of Northwood High with a heavy, defensive chip on her shoulder, convinced that everyone was actively out to get her. She played the victim perfectly, seeing herself in an epic, tragic "her vs. the world" narrative.

But the math of the situation was entirely different. No one was out to get her. The reality was just cold, hard self-preservation. None of the guys at Northwood wanted to date her, sit with her, or even look in her direction because they were absolutely terrified of her father. Paul Wiswell’s reputation as an unhinged, intimidating force had spread, and no standard-hardware high school boy was going to risk dealing with a man who cornered teenagers. She was isolated by the very shield her father thought he was providing.

But for today, her toxic variables were entirely deleted from the equation.

"Alright, front ensemble, let's get the metal moving!" a sharp, authoritative voice called out over the dull roar of the drumline.

It was Samantha, our senior pit captain. She stood near the heavy timpani, her dark hair falling past her shoulders, her rectangular glasses catching the harsh fluorescent light. Unlike Ashley, Samantha wasn't integrated into our Pack. She didn't offer deep-pressure grounding or physical anchors. But she ran her section with an absolute, predictable discipline and a sharp, observant eye that my highly sensitive system deeply respected.

Following her command, the three of us grabbed the heavy handles of the front ensemble equipment. I helped Rachel wheel her marimba out the back doors, while Ashley pushed the vibraphone, the heavy rubber tires crunching over the dry grass as we made our way toward the sprawling, freshly cut practice field just before 8:00 AM.

We headed straight for the front sideline. This was our domain. The pit was a stationary collection of marimbas, xylophones, vibraphones, and timpani. There was no complex marching choreography to memorize while playing, just the pure, mathematical precision of striking the heavy wooden and metal keys. It was a haven for our rigidly analytical minds.

Rachel set up her mallets, meticulously lining them up in perfect parallel rows, her breathing finally starting to slow down.

Here, surrounded by the giant, resonant instruments and the strict, predictable schedule of Mr. Panoff's band camp, the chaotic adult problems of the Delaney Street house couldn't reach us. It was a perfect, structured sanctuary.
