At noon, Mr. Panoff blew his whistle, signaling the start of the hour-long lunch break.

The immediate mass exodus of teenagers was a noisy chaos of dropping mallets, unbuckling drum harnesses, and shouting voices. During band camp, the cafeteria was strictly off-limits. You either brought a packed lunch from home, or you stood out by the blistering hot curb waiting for a parent to drop off a fast-food bag.

Rachel, Ashley, and I didn't join the migration to the parking lot or the crowded outdoor picnic tables. We grabbed the heavy, hard plastic cooler and Ashley's insulated lunch box from the corner of the band room and slipped out the double doors.

We needed a hiding spot.

Directly across the carpeted hallway from the band room was a small, recessed alcove. It consisted of three short concrete steps leading down to a set of heavy, windowless wooden doors that opened up to the back of the auditorium stage. It was a liminal space—a dead end that nobody used unless there was a theater production running.

It was perfectly dark, perfectly cool, and perfectly defensible.

I sat down on the middle step, setting the heavy cooler beside me. Rachel didn't sit down on the concrete step. Instead, she took one look at the tight space and smoothly climbed directly onto my lap. She shifted her weight, sitting sideways so her back was pressed firmly against my chest, her legs draped over my knees.

Ashley didn't miss a beat. She dropped her lunch box and sat directly on my left side, pressing her shoulder and hip completely flush against mine, leaning her weight heavily into me to seal the perimeter.

I naturally wrapped my right arm around my Bug's torso, anchoring her to my chest, and draped my left arm securely around Ashley's shoulders. The three-way cuddle was immediate and automatic. In the wake of our parents' screaming match that morning, Rachel didn't just want to be near me; she needed the physical, undeniable proof that I was still there. And Ashley, as our sentinel, knew perfectly well that Rachel’s ultimate safe space had always been, and would always be, sitting securely on her brother's lap.

After a few minutes of deep, regulating compression, I released my arms so we could actually eat, though our physical geometry didn't shift an inch. Rachel stayed anchored on my lap, and Ashley remained leaning heavily into my side.

I popped the white plastic lid of the cooler open, and a glorious wave of freezing air rolled out from the blue freezer packs inside.

"I am so glad we don't have to wait out by the curb," Rachel murmured, leaning her head back against my collarbone. She was exhausted. The morning had taken a massive toll on her, both emotionally from the parking lot and cognitively from mapping out the muscle memory for "The Solfeggio Steps."

"Me too," I said, handing her a plastic bottle of generic, supermarket-brand cola. It was dripping with icy condensation.

I dug through the cooler and pulled out two crinkling plastic sandwich bags and a family-sized bag of plain potato chips. Ashley unzipped her own lunch box, pulling out a turkey sandwich and a chilled sports drink.

Rachel and I had a highly specific meal. Wheat bread, a thick layer of creamy peanut butter, strawberry jelly, and layered directly in the middle—sliced sweet pickles. The sharp, vinegary tang and the firm crunch of the pickles cut perfectly through the thick, sticky sweetness of the peanut butter and jelly. To anyone else, it was a bizarre combination. To us, it was the ultimate comfort food.

We sat there in the dim alcove, eating in complete, synchronized silence. The cold generic cola burned perfectly down my throat, and the rhythmic crunch of the potato chips was a soothing, predictable white noise.

We were out of the main traffic flow, but we weren't entirely invisible.

Because today was strictly reserved for the percussion section and the all-female color guard, the hallway traffic was sparse but concentrated. Over the course of the hour, scattered groups of upperclassmen walked past our alcove on their way to the vending machines or the restrooms. Every single one of them looked down into the stairwell.

I saw the looks. I saw the sharp, judgmental side-eyes from the older girls in the color guard. I heard the confused, whispered comments from the drumline guys. High school operated on a rigid, ruthless social hierarchy, and what we were doing completely short-circuited their standard-hardware brains.

They couldn't figure out the math. *Wait, he has two girls cuddled up with him? Which one is the girlfriend and which one is the sister? Why is a high school freshman sitting on a junior's lap? And why is the other girl completely okay with this?* At one point, a group of snare drummers walked past. Right in the center of the group was J.L., Ashley's ex-boyfriend from freshman year.

He slowed his pace, his eyes narrowing as he looked down into the alcove. It had been nearly two years since Ashley had ruthlessly dumped him after he delivered an ultimatum demanding she choose between him or the Pack. He was still incredibly bitter that the sentinel hadn't hesitated for a single second before choosing us.

He glared at Ashley, taking in the sight of her leaning happily against my shoulder while my sister sat on my lap. He scoffed, shaking his head in disgust at our "weird" dynamic, before turning and following his section down the hall.

Ashley didn't even flinch. She took a calm bite of her turkey sandwich, completely and utterly apathetic to his existence. His standard-hardware resentment meant absolutely nothing inside our walls.

A moment later, the heavy squeak of sneakers against the linoleum signaled another arrival.

Samantha appeared at the top of the concrete steps. She was carrying a worn clipboard and a bottle of water. She glanced down the hall at J.L.'s retreating back, let out a heavy, exasperated sigh, and then looked down into the alcove at our tangled three-way cuddle.

Our senior pit captain didn't sneer, and she didn't stare. She adjusted her glasses, completely unbothered by the fact that a fourteen-year-old girl was sitting securely on her sixteen-year-old brother's lap while another girl anchored his side. As far as Samantha was concerned, we were executing our music perfectly, and our physical geometry wasn't hurting anybody.

"Make sure you all hydrate," Samantha instructed briskly, tapping her pen against her clipboard. "We're running page two at thirteen-hundred hours. Don't be late."

She gave us a sharp, professional nod and continued down the hallway, effortlessly accepting our dynamic without a single ounce of judgment.

They didn't understand how our world worked. They didn't know why the noise of the cafeteria felt like physical violence, or why Rachel needed deep physical compression just to keep from falling apart, or the absolute devastation we had survived in the parking lot just a few hours prior.

And sitting there in the cool dark, serving as the literal furniture for the two most important girls in my life, I realized something profound: I didn't care.

Rachel took a bite of her pickle-and-peanut-butter sandwich, chewing slowly. She had seen the color guard girls staring at us, too. She just blinked her dark eyes, completely apathetic to their judgment, and leaned her head back a little harder against my chest.

The Delaney Street house was broken, and our parents had officially surrendered their roles as our protectors. But sitting on the concrete steps of the Northwood High stage, surrounded by the smell of valve oil and generic cola, we had drawn our own battle lines. The outside world could stare and whisper all they wanted. We were a fortress, and our walls were completely impenetrable.
