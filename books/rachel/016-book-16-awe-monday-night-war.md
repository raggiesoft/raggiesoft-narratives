# Book 16: AWE Monday Night War

## Chapter 1: The Portland Offensive (Monday, November 20, 2006)

### Part 1: The Morning Deployment – 6:00 AM (PST)

The sharp, rhythmic electronic beeping of the hotel alarm clock shattered the heavy, insulated silence of the third-floor room.

My processor booted up slowly, pulling me out of the deepest, most restorative sleep I had experienced in two years. I didn't wake up with a violently racing heart or the sudden, terrified urge to scan the room for threats. I just blinked against the dim, early-morning light filtering through the edges of the blackout curtains, my brain immediately registering the absolute, unshakeable physical geometry of my perimeter.

I was still pinned perfectly in the center of the plush queen bed. On my right, Rachel was completely tangled in the heavy white hotel comforter, her bare leg still securely hooked over my calf, her arm draped heavily across my chest. On my left, Kristin let out a soft, sleepy groan. She shifted her weight, pressing her bare shoulder flush against mine, and reached an arm out from under the blankets to blindly swat at the alarm clock on the nightstand.

Her hand found the snooze button, plunging the room back into quiet.

"Morning, Bear," Rachel rasped softly, her dark eyes cracking open.

She didn't move her arm. She just looked across my chest at Kristin, who was blinking sleepily, and then back down at me. A slow, highly amused smirk spread across Rachel's face as she realized the physical reality of our sleeping arrangement hadn't changed by a single inch since we turned the lights out last night.

"Delores is still screaming somewhere," Rachel whispered, her voice dancing with vindictive joy.

I let out a low, rumbling chuckle that vibrated against Rachel's arm. It was true. If Delores could somehow see this room, her mind would completely shatter. But as I lay there, feeling the soft, grounding warmth of Kristin resting comfortably against my left side, my analytical processor ran the logic.

Delores had absolutely no right to scream. She had voluntarily surrendered her territory. She was the one who had screamed at me, packed my bags, and violently thrown me out into the freezing Kent rain at four in the morning. She had broken up with me. She had literally thrown me away like garbage.

So what if I had found someone new? What if I was sharing a bed, completely stripped of armor, with a gorgeous, fiercely loyal twenty-year-old woman who actually protected me? It was none of her business anymore. Delores’s sick, twisted rules were dead.

Kristin felt the vibration of my laugh. She didn't pull away.

"Let her scream," Kristin mumbled playfully from my left. She nestled closer, resting her chin comfortably on my bare shoulder and closing her eyes again. "I'm comfortable. And she's a thousand miles away."

"Alright, strike the perimeter," Jessica’s voice suddenly cut through the darkness from the other bed, followed by the soft click of the bedside lamp clicking on. "It is 6:00 AM. Transit math requires us to be dressed and fully mobilized before the afternoon crowd hits the arena."

The pack moved with practiced, frictionless efficiency. We threw back the heavy comforter.

The zero-privacy baseline held absolute. There was no hesitation, no retreating to the bathroom, and absolutely no shame. Rachel, Kristin, and I stood up in the open space between the two queen beds, completely exposed in the soft lamplight, and began pulling clean clothes from our duffel bags.

For the second day in a row, my threat-detection system remained entirely dormant. The ghosts of Kent were gone. Kristin standing there, treating my physical form with total, unbothered normalcy, was incredibly medicinal. It proved to my trauma-wired brain that the female body was not a weapon.

I pulled on my thick denim jeans and a fresh, dark t-shirt, maintaining the unbroken visual tether with Rachel as she pulled her own signature red shirt over her head. We verified each other's presence, anchoring our nervous systems for the day ahead.

Once we were dressed, Rachel immediately dragged one of the heavy upholstered hotel chairs over to the polished mahogany desk. She sat down, pulling her knees to her chest, and patted the empty chair right next to her.

"You have time to build," Rachel reminded me.

I smiled, my processor buzzing with anticipation. I walked over to the desk and placed my hand on the cool, brushed-aluminum lid of the ten-pound, piano-black machine. In my head, the terminology had officially shifted permanently. It wasn't just a command center anymore. It was the **Extraction Rig**. It was the physical, humming vehicle of my liberation.

I pressed the silver power button. The dual 7200 RPM hard drives whined to life with a satisfying, mechanical rev, and the 17-inch screen illuminated the dark wood of the hotel desk.

I booted *The Denizens 2*, enduring the loud, aggressive SecuROM disc-check as the optical drive spun to maximum RPM.

Once the high-definition digital neighborhood loaded, I pulled up the hidden developer console. *bool_prop edit_citizen true*.

I clicked on the blank, default male avatar waiting in the family bin and initiated the *Force Create-A-Character* override. The screen transitioned to the blue-lit pedestal room, and I went to work.

Rachel immediately hooked her left arm securely around my waist. She leaned her entire body weight flush against my right side, resting her chin heavily on my shoulder so she could watch the screen perfectly.

With her physical pressure grounding me, I used the weighted optical mouse to meticulously sculpt the Bear. I adjusted the jawline and the height. But more importantly, I adjusted the posture. I deliberately bypassed the hunched, defensive slump that Delores had beaten into me over the last two years. I used the game's sliders to give the digital avatar the upright, confident stance of a survivor. I dressed him in a dark, comfortable flannel, perfectly mirroring the heavy armor I had worn on the train ride yesterday.

"There he is," Rachel whispered, her arm tightening around my ribs in a heavy, affectionate squeeze.

I moved my left hand over the keyboard, resting my finger above the 'C' key. Using the mouse, I rotated the digital Bear so he was standing perfectly shoulder-to-shoulder with the digital Bug I had built the night before.

*Click-whir.*

The simulated camera shutter echoed from the premium speakers, capturing the high-resolution screenshot and saving it directly to the Extraction Rig's hard drive. The visual documentation for the *Vertex Creative Studio* lore document was complete. Our digital sanctuary was officially populated, and we were standing tall.

I saved the game and powered down the Extraction Rig, the heavy cooling fans slowly spinning to a halt. My processor felt incredibly light. The digital armor was built. The physical perimeter was secure.

I leaned back in the heavy upholstered chair, looking across the room at my older sister, who was already reviewing transit routes on her corporate laptop.

"So," I said, a massive, unburdened grin breaking across my face. "What time do the arena doors open for AWE Monday Night WAR?"

### Part 2: The Logic of the Ban – 6:45 AM (PST)

At exactly 6:45 AM, a sharp, authoritative knock echoed against the heavy wooden door of our third-floor room.

Kristin, fully embracing her role as the pack’s designated sentinel, didn't hesitate. She marched to the threshold, checked the peephole, and cracked the door just enough to slide the massive, silver-covered room service cart inside. She didn't let the hotel staff cross the boundary. She swiftly signed the receipt, handed the employee a cash tip, and locked the deadbolt with a heavy, satisfying *clack*.

The civilian world was sealed back out in the hallway.

We wheeled the cart to the center of the room, distributing the heavy ceramic plates across the desk and the small table. Jessica took her scrambled eggs and coffee, Kristin claimed her massive Belgian waffle, and Rachel and I sat shoulder-to-shoulder on the edge of the mattress with our identical stacks of buttermilk pancakes, crispy bacon, and cold apple juice.

"I still can't believe the timing on this," I said, pouring a heavy layer of maple syrup over my pancakes. "We get front-row tickets to a live television broadcast, and it just happens to be the exact week Marcus Sterling decides to permanently ban all arena pyrotechnics. If they were setting off those massive concussion mortars and flash pots tonight, our perimeter would be completely shattered."

Rachel took a bite of her bacon. She looked over at me, her dark eyes sparkling with a bright, knowing amusement.

"Sterling didn't ban anything, Bear," Rachel rasped, her voice thick with the absolute, hyper-fixated confidence of someone who knew the hidden mechanics of the AWE universe inside and out.

I paused, my fork hovering over my plate. "What do you mean? We watched the promo on TV last week. He explicitly banned them to cut corporate costs."

"That's just the storyline," Rachel explained, her free hand moving excitedly as she broke down the reality. "I read the dirt sheets and the backstage production forums. The live-event touring department booked the Rose City Center six to eight months ago. Contracts are signed, basic logistics are set. But when you get to the permit phase—usually about four to six weeks out—the pyrotechnician team has to submit a highly detailed 'Pyro Plot' to the local Portland Fire Marshal and the venue's operations manager."

Jessica took a sip of her coffee, her corporate-logistics brain instantly engaging with the explanation. "Let me guess. They detailed every single mortar, gerb fountain, and flash pot, along with the required fallout zones, and the city rejected it."

Rachel nodded vigorously. "A hard denial. In mid-sized or older arenas like this, the Fire Marshal usually kills the pyro for a few specific reasons. Either the arena's HVAC system cannot cycle the smoke out fast enough, which would leave the building dangerously hazy for a two-hour live television broadcast. Or the central scoreboard and lighting grid hang too low, creating a severe fire hazard for vertical spark fountains. Or the local municipality just passed stricter indoor combustion laws that the touring production simply cannot meet."

"So the Fire Marshal killed the pyro," Kristin realized, cutting into her waffle. "Not the General Manager."

"Exactly," Rachel smiled, completely in her element. "Three weeks out, the Lead Pyrotechnician notifies the Head of Production that Portland is a dead zone for fireworks. The Head of Production takes it directly to the Creative Team. They realize that having zero pyro on a live Monday Night television broadcast—especially for stars whose entrances completely rely on the visual and auditory pop—will feel incredibly flat to the viewers at home and the live crowd."

"And they panic?" I asked.

"No, they adapt," Rachel said, her eyes shining with profound respect for the writers. "Two weeks out, the Head Writer looks at the upcoming card and realizes Marcus Sterling is already pushing a 'corporate cost-cutting' angle. They decide to lean completely into the skid. Instead of letting the fans think the company is just being cheap or having technical difficulties, they write the real-world venue restriction into the script."

The absolute brilliance of the maneuver clicked into place in my analytical processor.

"They sent Sterling out there last week to explicitly ban the pyrotechnics," I said, putting the pieces together. "Which achieves two massive goals. First, it perfectly lowers the expectations. The live fans in Portland, and the millions watching at home, are now explicitly expecting a show with no fireworks, so they aren't disappointed when it doesn't happen."

"And," Jessica added, a wicked, appreciative smirk crossing her face, "Sterling takes the blame for something the local government actually did. He absorbs all that real-world frustration and turns it into incredible, localized storyline heat. It perfectly sets up the stakes for whatever the main event is tonight."

"It's the absolute perfect marriage of behind-the-scenes logistics and on-screen storytelling," Rachel finished, taking a happy sip of her apple juice.

I looked at my little sister, overwhelmed by a deep, protective wave of pride. She wasn't just a fan watching a television show; she was a structural engineer analyzing the architecture of a fictional universe. Knowing the logical, real-world permit laws behind the pyrotechnic ban completely stripped away the unpredictability of the arena. It wasn't a chaotic, terrifying environment anymore. It was a highly controlled, permitted, and regulated production.

But as my processor ran the numbers, a massive logistical discrepancy suddenly halted my train of thought.

I lowered my fork and looked across the room at my older sister.

"Wait a minute," I said, my brow furrowing. "Jess... you bought us front-row Ringsider tickets for the *No Quarter* Pay-Per-View in Seattle this Sunday."

"I did," Jessica confirmed casually, taking another sip of coffee.

"Then how did we get *these* tickets?" I asked, gesturing vaguely toward the window and the Rose City Center across the street. "Portland is a massive detour from Renton. And AWE Ringsider tickets are astronomically expensive. You couldn't have afforded to buy front-row seats for a live broadcast *and* a Sunday Pay-Per-View in the same week."

Jessica just smiled, nodding her head toward Rachel. "Ask the VIP."

I looked back at Rachel. She was picking at a piece of bacon, a complicated mix of emotions washing over her face.

"Jessica didn't buy these," Rachel rasped quietly. "Dad won them."

My processor stalled. "Dad?"

"Two weeks ago," Rachel explained, staring down at her plate. "He was driving across the Interstate Bridge into Portland for work. He was listening to the local Classic Rock FM station, and they announced a 'Monday Night WAR VIP Front Row Giveaway.' He dialed the 1-800 request line and happened to be Caller 10. He won the four-pack."

"And he just gave them to you?" I asked, highly skeptical. Tom Ragsdale was notoriously manipulative. He was currently terrified of Jessica's financial guillotine regarding the car she was paying for, and he was completely estranged from us. Handing Rachel a massive VIP package for her ultimate hyper-fixation felt exactly like a trap designed to buy his way back into our good graces.

"He wanted to come," Rachel admitted, her shoulders tensing slightly at the thought. "He genuinely loves wrestling. He called me right after he won and told me we were going. I... I said yes, but I was terrified of having to sit next to him for three hours."

Kristin reached across the mattress and gently squeezed Rachel's knee in a show of quiet support.

"But then his schedule dropped," Rachel continued, her posture relaxing. "His boss at the self-storage facility wouldn't give him the night off. He's locked into the closing shift tonight. He tried to get out of it, but he couldn't. So, he called the radio station and legally transferred the entire VIP package into my name at Will Call. The tickets are one hundred percent mine. He's not coming."

I let out a long breath, the phantom tension of a Tom Ragsdale encounter leaving the room. But as I looked at the math of the situation, a profound realization washed over me.

"A four-pack of tickets," I murmured.

"Yeah," Rachel nodded softly. "I claimed one for me, one for Jess, and one for Kristin. But for over a week, I was just holding onto this vacant fourth ticket. I didn't want to invite a stranger. I just... left it empty."

The geography of the universe suddenly aligned with absolute, breathtaking perfection.

Rachel had been holding a single, empty front-row ticket to her sanctuary for eleven days. And at 4:00 AM on Friday morning, Delores had violently evicted me, initiating the extraction that sent me directly to Washington State.

The fourth ticket was never a spare. It was never an extra. It was a holding pattern.

I looked at my Bug, my heart swelling with a fierce, unbreakable warmth. I reached over, wrapping my arm securely around her shoulders and pulling her into a tight side-hug.

"It's a good thing you kept it empty," I whispered.

Rachel leaned her head heavily against my shoulder, a bright, unburdened smile finally breaking across her face. "It was always your seat, Bear."

I held my Bug close, the profound realization of the fourth ticket settling warmly into my chest. But as my analytical processor finished computing the timeline of Tom Ragsdale’s radio contest, it suddenly collided with a completely separate logistical discrepancy.

The math didn't add up.

I slowly pulled back from Rachel, my brow furrowing in deep confusion. I looked across the room at my older sister, who had gone back to quietly typing on her corporate laptop.

"Wait," I said, my voice dropping as my brain tried to parse the variables. "My processor just hit a massive snag."

Jessica stopped typing, peering over the top of her glasses. "What's the snag?"

"If Dad won the Portland tickets two weeks ago..." I started, pointing a finger in the air to track the timeline. "That means *you* specifically bought the four front-row Ringsider tickets for the *No Quarter* Pay-Per-View in Seattle. And knowing how fast AWE Pay-Per-Views sell out, you had to have bought those tickets months ago."

Jessica simply offered a calm, level nod. "The day they went on sale."

"But," I pressed, the gap in the logic widening, "months ago, I was trapped in Virginia with Delores. Even a week ago, I was stuck on a Pathfinder bus in the middle of nowhere. As far as you knew, I was three thousand miles away, and I wasn't allowed to leave her sight." I looked around the room at the pack. "Why did you buy four tickets?"

The hotel room fell completely silent. Rachel squeezed my hand. Kristin closed her paperback novel, resting it on the mattress and giving Jessica a quiet, knowing look.

Jessica didn't look away. She slowly closed the lid of her laptop, folding her hands neatly on top of the brushed aluminum chassis. The corporate-manager persona dropped entirely, replaced by the fierce, protective intensity of an older sister who had been playing a high-stakes chess game in the dark.

"Because I knew you weren't going to class," Jessica stated, her voice quiet but carrying the weight of absolute certainty.

I blinked, completely caught off guard. "What?"

"I check in on things, Michael. I have my ways," Jessica explained, her tone softening. "I knew you had completely stopped attending your courses at the Community College of Hampton Roads. The data was obvious. Your perimeter was shrinking. You were being isolated, cut off from your education, your family, and your life. Delores was suffocating you."

A cold phantom chill ran down my spine at the mention of the college, a dark reminder of the void Delores had deliberately engineered.

"The midnight rescue in Kent on Thursday?" Jessica continued, shaking her head. "That was never the original plan. That was a catastrophic emergency response because Delores forced the timeline. The original plan was a highly calculated extraction. I had a blueprint."

"What was the blueprint?" I asked, my voice barely above a whisper.

"The Golden Carrot," Jessica smiled, a fierce, tactical light shining behind her glasses. "I knew that if I just asked you to leave her, your threat-detection system would panic. Delores had you too heavily conditioned. I had to offer you something so massive, so logically undeniable, that it would short-circuit her control."

She pointed to Rachel, and then to herself.

"I bought the four *No Quarter* tickets the day they went on sale. The fourth seat was always yours. My plan was to call you in late October. I was going to tell you that Rachel and I had front-row seats to the biggest AWE event of the year, and that Rachel desperately needed her Bear sitting next to her."

Jessica paused, glancing affectionately at our little sister. "To be perfectly honest, if I could have only afforded two tickets, they would have been for you and Rachel. Because your sister absolutely despised Delores, and she wanted you out of that toxic relationship more than anything in the world."

Rachel nodded fiercely beside me, her dark eyes flashing with absolute conviction.

"I had thousands of dollars set aside in a specific deployment fund," Jessica revealed, laying out the sheer financial magnitude of her logistics. "I was prepared to pay through the absolute nose for a last-minute, direct, First-Class flight from ORF to SEA. I was going to send you the itinerary with one strict, non-negotiable condition: *The ticket is for Michael. Only Michael. Delores is not invited.*"

My processor stalled. The sheer audacity of the plan—the amount of corporate logistics and financial firepower my sister was preparing to drop on my abuser—was staggering.

"The choice would have been entirely yours," Jessica said softly. "Get on the plane, fly First-Class across the country, sit in the front row with your sister, and come back to the family. Or stay in the squalor."

"And the Extraction Rig?" I asked, looking down at the massive, ten-pound piano-black laptop resting quietly on the desk.

"That was Phase Two," Jessica grinned. "If I got you out here for the Pay-Per-View in November, I knew you'd be terrified of the fallout of going back. So, the laptop was going to be your Christmas present. The ultimate anchor. A brand-new, top-of-the-line digital sanctuary, given to you with zero strings attached, to permanently break her conditioning and keep you here in Washington for good."

I sat completely frozen on the edge of the mattress.

For two years, Delores had hammered into my brain that I was worthless. She convinced me that my family didn't care about me, that they were a threat, and that I had absolutely no value outside of serving her.

But sitting in this hotel room, the truth completely incinerated her lies.

Jessica hadn't forgotten me. She hadn't given up. While I was isolated in a decaying house on the East Coast, my older sister was actively building a war chest. She was plotting transit routes, buying front-row tickets, and securing bleeding-edge technology, engineering a flawless, impenetrable safety net to catch me the second I was ready to jump.

The fact that Delores had dragged me to Seattle on a miserable Pathfinder bus and thrown me out into the rain hadn't ruined Jessica's plan. It had just bypassed the flight logistics and forced the pack to execute the endgame a month early.

I looked at Jessica, my vision suddenly blurring with a thick, stinging heat. I didn't have the words to compute the magnitude of her loyalty.

I stood up from the mattress, crossed the short distance to the desk, and wrapped my arms tightly around my older sister's shoulders. I buried my face into her neck, letting out a long, shuddering breath. Jessica immediately hugged me back, her hands gripping my heavy flannel shirt.

"I've always got your back, Michael," Jessica whispered fiercely into my ear. "Always."

I pulled back, wiping my eyes with the sleeve of my shirt, and offered her a massive, watery smile.

"Well," I said, my voice thick with emotion as I looked around the fortress we had built. "The Golden Carrot worked. I'm right here."

### Part 3: The Holding Pattern – 10:00 AM (PST)

I said the words, but the sheer, crushing weight of what Jessica had just revealed finally broke through the last remaining walls of my defense.

For two years, my body had been locked in a permanent, rigid state of survival. I wasn't allowed to cry in Kent. If I ever showed a single ounce of emotional weakness, Delores would immediately weaponize it, screaming that I was pathetic or punishing me for making her feel bad. I had swallowed every tear, every ounce of terror, until my chest felt like it was packed with wet cement.

But sitting in this hotel room, realizing that my older sister had been willing to empty her bank accounts and drop thousands of dollars just to give me a single chance to escape... the wet cement completely shattered.

A low, guttural rumble ripped from the bottom of my lungs.

My legs gave out. I collapsed heavily back into the upholstered desk chair I had just been sitting in. I buried my face in my hands, and the crying hit me like a physical wave. It was loud. It was heavy. It was the ugly, breathless, earth-shattering sobbing of a hostage who finally realizes the war is actually over.

Instantly, the perimeter closed around me.

Rachel was there in a microsecond, dropping to her knees beside the chair and wrapping her arms fiercely around my waist, burying her face into my ribs. Jessica wheeled her chair in tight, her hands gripping my forearm with a firm, unshakeable pressure.

But then, the geometry of the room shifted.

Kristin dragged the second upholstered chair across the carpet, planting it directly next to mine. She didn't hesitate. She reached out, wrapped her arms around my shaking shoulders, and physically pulled my upper body against hers.

Because of her height—a sturdy, protective five-foot-nine—the angle was flawless. My face fell perfectly against her chest and shoulder. I didn't pull away. Instead, acting on a desperate, blind instinct, I leaned all of my weight into her. I gripped the fabric of her shirt, burying my face into the soft, secure curve of her neck as I sobbed.

She just held me, wrapping her arms around my back and applying a heavy, deep, crushing pressure that instantly started to ground my racing heart.

As I stayed buried against her, a sudden, blinding realization cut through the thick emotional fog in my brain.

Kristin wasn't my sister. She wasn't my cousin. She shared absolutely no blood with me.

Delores had spent two years projecting her sick, twisted jealousy onto my family, trying to isolate me from the sisters who loved me. But Delores’s ultimate, terrifying nightmare was me finding *another* woman. If Delores could somehow see me right now, completely enveloped in the arms of a gorgeous, taller, fiercely protective brunette... she wouldn't just be jealous. She would be completely, utterly defeated. She would realize her territory was permanently gone. She would have to leave me alone.

The logic locked into place. Kristin wasn't just offering comfort; she was a living, breathing shield against the monster.

Through my tears, I felt Rachel shift against my ribs. I couldn't see my sister's face, but I felt her hand reach up and gently pat Kristin's knee. I heard Jessica let out a soft, knowing exhale. They saw exactly what I was doing. They watched me completely bypass my terror of being touched by a non-relative, and they instantly understood the silent math. They knew why I was clinging to the roommate. It was the ultimate barricade.

The heavy, agonizing grief pouring out of my lungs slowly began to transform. The tears were still falling rapidly, but the suffocating darkness was gone. It was just pure, overwhelming, blinding joy. My brain was desperately trying to categorize the warmth radiating from Kristin, rewriting two years of horrific conditioning in a matter of minutes. She was safe. She was an absolute sanctuary.

Eventually, the heavy sobbing slowed to quiet, shuddering breaths.

I sat there, my face still pressed firmly against Kristin's shoulder. My throat was completely locked tight. The invisible, familiar wall had slammed down over my vocal cords, leaving me entirely without the ability to speak. But I didn't want to find my voice anyway. And I absolutely did not want to let go of Kristin.

She didn't make me. She just kept her arms wrapped securely around my back, resting her chin softly on top of my head while Rachel stayed anchored to my side. We stayed like that until the storm completely passed.

With the breakfast cart cleared and my tears finally dried, we settled into a long, restorative holding pattern.

The live broadcast didn't start until 6:00 PM, and Will Call didn't open until mid-afternoon. We had hours of dead time to kill, and absolutely zero intention of leaving the fortress to do it. The civilian world outside the Crown Regency was cold, damp, and filling up with thousands of unpredictable wrestling fans. We stayed off the grid.

Jessica sat at the polished mahogany desk, her corporate laptop open as she quietly mapped out the CTS transit schedules for tomorrow's excursion to Vancouver. Kristin eventually moved to the far bed, lying on her stomach and reading a paperback novel, completely relaxed in our shared space.

Rachel and I spent the rest of the morning anchored to the mattress nearest the window. I had my heavy, noise-isolating headphones resting around my neck, while Rachel had her own pair sitting safely in her lap. We didn't need to speak; the silence between us was loud and perfectly understood. I pulled a thick notebook from my duffel bag and began sketching out physical diagrams of the headless CMS architecture I wanted to build for my music catalog, occasionally pausing to point out the routing logic to Rachel. She would nod, leaning her heavy, comforting weight against my shoulder.

By 1:00 PM, the anticipation in the room began to physically hum.

Rachel's leg started bouncing in a rapid, rhythmic drumbeat against the mattress. She packed her heavy isolation headphones into her small bag, double-checked her wallet for her Washington State ID, and stood up from the bed.

"Armor up," Jessica announced, snapping her laptop shut and sliding it into her messenger bag.

We moved as a single, perfectly synchronized unit. I pulled on my thick denim jeans, my heavy work boots, and my warm winter coat. Rachel slipped into her cargo pants and a soft hoodie, securing her belongings. We didn't need to discuss the formation; the physical geometry was already permanently locked into our muscle memory.

At 2:15 PM, we stepped out of the hotel room.

### Part 4: The Plaza Approach – 2:20 PM (PST)

The freezing, damp Pacific Northwest air hit our faces immediately. For my overloaded processor, the sharp bite of the November wind was the exact physical reset I needed. It shocked my system, clearing the last lingering traces of the heavy emotional fog that had kept me completely silent since my meltdown that morning.

The low, heavy rumble of diesel generators vibrated through the soles of our boots. Her eyes were wide, darting frantically between the massive production trucks, the arena signage, and the growing crowd of early-arrival fans. Her threat-detection system wasn't spiking out of fear; it was simply paralyzed by the sheer, unadulterated magnitude of the Monday Night WAR.

My analytical brain saw the shutdown happening, and it triggered the absolute heaviest, most fundamental subroutine in my programming: *Protect the Bug.*

I swallowed hard, pushing against the heavy, invisible wall that had locked my vocal cords tight for the last four hours. I needed my voice. She needed her anchor.

"I've got you, Bug," I murmured.

The words came out as a quiet, raspy whisper, but the physical vibration of my chest against her shoulder made her blink. She looked up at me, her breathing shallow.

I wrapped my arm securely around her shoulders, pulling her flush against my side, and forced myself to speak again, pushing a little harder against the block.

"Look at the cables, Rachel," I said, my voice gaining a fraction more volume, stepping into the steady, mechanical cadence that always grounded her. "Look at the logistics. It's exactly what you told us at breakfast. It's not chaos. It's a highly controlled broadcast."

Rachel followed my pointing finger. She looked at the heavy black cables, neatly taped down to the concrete, routing perfectly from the production trucks into the massive loading bays. Her rapid breathing hitched, then slowly began to deepen as her brain latched onto the recognizable, underlying structure of the event.

"See?" I continued, the invisible wall in my throat finally shattering completely. The act of protecting her had acted as the ultimate system override. My voice dropped into its full, resonant, unwavering power. "It's all mapped out. Just like your production sheets. We are walking into a controlled environment, and our perimeter is completely solid."

Rachel let out a long, shuddering exhale. Her death grip on my winter coat relaxed into a steady, comfortable hold. She offered a small, grateful nod, her eyes shining with pure excitement rather than overload.

Jessica, who had paused her manual wheelchair to give us the space to regulate, offered a proud, subtle smirk. She wheeled forward smoothly over the wet concrete, retaking the point position to carve a path through the plaza. Kristin fell in line a half-step behind Rachel. Standing at a sturdy five-foot-nine, Kristin naturally eclipsed the rest of us, her broader frame effortlessly guarding her best friend's blind spot from the unpredictable civilian foot traffic.

We walked as a single, impenetrable unit past the towering, multi-story glass windows of the arena's main atrium, making our way directly toward the illuminated box office windows.

My vocal cords were fully online. My spine was straight. The Bear was fully awake. The trap Tom Ragsdale had inadvertently set for his daughter was about to be effortlessly dismantled, because Rachel wasn't walking up to that glass window alone.

## Chapter 2: Arrival at Rose City Center (Monday, November 20, 2006)

### Part 1: Will Call – 2:30 PM (PST)

The massive glass and steel facade of the Rose City Center loomed over us against the overcast, chilly Portland sky.

We made our way across the sprawling concrete plaza toward the box office. Jessica rolled smoothly beside us in her manual wheelchair, her breath visible in the damp November air. But now, five years later, he was forcing her to be the one to physically sign for his grand, expensive "olive branch." It felt like a trap wrapped in a gift box, but for tonight, we were playing along.

### Part 2: The Arsenal – 2:45 PM (PST)

If Rachel could have spoken, she wouldn't have had the words anyway. The sheer, validating joy of being recognized not just as a fan, but as a student of the craft by her ultimate hero, was a monumental moment. Kristin gently squeezed Rachel's shoulder, beaming with pride for her best friend.

The Rose City Center absolutely exploded. The dark matches had done their job perfectly. The crowd was hot, our snack perimeter was fully stocked, and the red light on the massive broadcast cameras began to blink.

## Chapter 3: AWE Monday Night War (Monday, November 20, 2006)

### Part 1: LIVE From Portland – 6:00 PM (PST)

At exactly six o'clock, the ambient hum of the Rose City Center was instantly obliterated.

High above the entrance stage, the massive TitanTron flared to life. The metallic AWE logo slammed onto the screen, sparking with digital interference and a heavy bass drop. The sheer auditory force of the bass vibrated straight through the concrete and into the soles of my boots. Normally, this would be the exact microsecond the stage would erupt in a terrifying, concussive volley of fireworks. But tonight, the arena remained dark and mercifully free of the sudden explosions that usually sent our nervous systems into a tailspin.

### Part 2: The Bottom Line – 6:02 PM (PST)

The TitanTron faded to black. For a split second, the massive arena held its breath.

I gritted my teeth, carefully walking her out of the tight seating row and lowering her gently into her own custom wheelchair parked nearby.

The moment her weight left me, my knees buckled.

"Michael!" Kristin gasped, stepping forward instantly.

I couldn't stand up straight. The muscles in my lower back had completely locked up into a solid, agonizing knot.

"I've got you," Rachel said fiercely, stepping up to my left side. Kristin immediately moved to my right.

I slung my heavy arms over both of their shoulders, letting the two women take the brunt of my physical weight. It was a complete role reversal; after spending the entire night acting as their physical anchor and shield against the chaos of the arena, my sister and her best friend were now physically carrying me.

Jessica balanced our massive VIP Prize Pack on her lap—the exclusive duffel bags, signed programs, and Rachel's prized foam finger—while Rachel and Kristin stabilized me.

"Alright," a familiar, gravelly voice called out over the dull roar of the departing crowd. "Let's get you guys out of this madhouse."

Walking down the aisle toward us was Dave "The Arsenal" Cross. He gave us a warm, tired smile, taking in our battered, exhausted formation, and gestured for us to follow him. Because navigating a wheelchair against the tide of 14,000 people pushing toward the main concourse stairs was a logistical nightmare, Dave personally escorted us through the backstage curtain to use the arena's heavy-duty freight elevator.

As we rolled through the backstage area, Dave fell into step right next to Rachel, who was currently acting as my left crutch.

"Hey, kiddo," Dave said gently, looking at Rachel. "I didn't forget. I just got off the phone with the merchandise department. Your Arsenal Skull t-shirt and hat? The order is officially in. The boys back in Stamford are going to process it first thing tomorrow morning."

Rachel stopped dead in her tracks, forcing Kristin and me to halt with her. She looked up at her absolute hero, her eyes wide with sheer disbelief.

Dave chuckled, patting her gently on the shoulder. "Don't cry on me now, kid. You've got a Pay-Per-View to get ready for this Sunday."

We reached the massive freight elevator, the hydraulic motor humming loudly as it carried us up to the street-level concourse.

When the heavy metal doors slid open, we were officially past the restricted backstage area and back in the public zone. We stopped to thank Dave profusely, shaking his hand and saying our goodbyes. A single tear of pure, unadulterated joy spilled over Rachel's eyelashes and rolled down her cheek. She was actually going to get the gear, and it was going to be shipped straight to our new front door in Renton, Washington.

We pushed through the glass exit doors out into the crisp, cold Portland night air.

Right across the plaza, the Rose City Center CTS station was an absolute madhouse.

The platforms stretched out beneath the shadow of the US-30 overpass, all harsh fluorescent light and concrete—but there was barely any platform left to see anymore. It was just people. A dense, shifting wall of bodies packed shoulder-to-shoulder from the tracks all the way out onto the sidewalks. Every few minutes, a train rolled in already full, doors sliding open to a wave of frustration as maybe three or four people squeezed in while everyone else got left behind.

“Thank God we don’t have to get on that,” Kristin muttered beside us.

I didn’t argue. I just tightened my grip on the handles and angled us right, aiming to skirt past the whole mess.

That plan lasted about ten feet.

The crowd had spilled way beyond the station, clogging the sidewalk in a slow, uneven current. I eased my pace immediately, nudging Jessica’s chair forward inch by inch, watching for gaps that barely existed. From up here, I could at least see over most of it—but that didn’t make it easier to move through.

“Excuse us—wheelchair coming through,” Rachel called, a step ahead, trying to clear a path.

Some people heard her. Some didn’t.

A guy in a wrestling hoodie took a step backward without looking, right into us. I braked hard, the footrests stopping just short of his heel.

“Oh—shoot, sorry!” he blurted, jumping out of the way. That little moment of awareness rippled outward—people shifting, turning, making space—but always just a second too late.

“Thanks,” I muttered, already guiding us into the next opening before it disappeared.

Another train screeched in under the overpass. Like a reflex, the entire crowd leaned toward it, compressing the sidewalk even more. For a second, we were boxed in on all sides.

I leaned down a little. “You good?” I asked quietly.

Jessica gave the smallest nod.

“Alright. I’ve got you.”

Short pushes. Stop. Pivot. Slip through. Repeat.

From where she sat, I knew it had to feel even tighter—nothing but legs and coats and bags, the noise bouncing off the concrete overhead. Up here, it was loud enough. Down there, it had to be overwhelming.

“Wheelchair—coming through, please,” Rachel tried again, firmer this time.

This time more people reacted quickly, stepping aside, holding their spots just long enough for us to pass.

We kept moving.

And then, almost without warning, it broke.

Half a block past the station, the pressure just… vanished. The crowd thinned into loose clusters, the noise dropped off, and suddenly I had space again—real space—to breathe and steer without fighting for every inch.

I glanced up—and there it was.

The Crown Regency. Glass front, bright lobby lights, calm like none of that chaos even existed.

“Almost there,” I said, more to myself than anyone else.

A minute later, we rolled straight up to the entrance, leaving the entire mess behind us like it was another world.

We took the elevator up to the third floor, dragging our souvenir chairs and VIP bags down the quiet, carpeted hallway to our ADA-accessible room.

The moment the heavy wooden door clicked shut behind us, the silence felt absolutely luxurious. The room featured two plush queen-sized beds with wide clearances for Jessica's chair.

"Easy now," Kristin instructed gently.

Rachel and Kristin carefully navigated me toward the bed nearest the window. Moving with perfectly synchronized teamwork, they slowly lowered me down until my back finally hit the soft mattress. I let out a long, agonizing groan of relief as the pressure was finally taken off my spine.

Jessica parked her chair near the foot of the beds, shedding her jacket with a tired sigh.

I barely had time to kick my boots off before the mattress dipped beside me. Rachel didn't even bother unpacking her VIP bag. She climbed right into the bed next to me, fully clothed, and instantly curled herself against my side.

She rested her head heavy on my chest, draping her arm over my ribs. The massive sensory and emotional high of the wrestling show was finally catching up to her processor, and she desperately needed her anchor to decompress.

I winced slightly through the pain in my back, but I wrapped my arm around her shoulders anyway, holding her tight. We had survived the Monday Night WAR, and the pack was exactly where it belonged.
