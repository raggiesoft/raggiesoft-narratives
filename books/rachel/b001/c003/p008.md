Dad kept his eyes locked on the road, his jaw tight as he steered the Izumi Bantam out of the sprawling mall parking lot, making a sharp right onto River Birch Run.

Every time he grabbed the stick shift to accelerate, his elbow brushed against Michael’s winter coat. He could see them in his peripheral vision as he banked left onto Eden Way North—Michael’s arms wrapped tightly around Rachel’s waist, her head thrown back against his shoulder. They were loud, practically screaming over the radio, completely lost in their own bizarre, shared world.

It made absolutely no sense to him.

Why would she cry if he moved her to the window? It was a simple, logical question that gnawed at him as he waited at the traffic light to make a left onto Volvo Parkway.

If Rachel was just scared of the loud truck or the crowds, why wasn't Jessica good enough? Jessica was thirteen. She was practically a third adult. She was soft-spoken, fiercely protective, and sitting right next to the window where Rachel would be completely out of the way of the gear shift. Any normal six-year-old girl who was feeling timid would naturally gravitate toward the older sister who babied her.

Instead, Rachel clung to an eight-year-old boy.

Dad hit the clutch and turned left onto Kempsville Road, the stiff suspension of the Bantam rattling hard over the pavement. Michael wasn't exactly a warm, nurturing figure. He was a quiet, rigid kid who didn't even speak his first words until he was five. He hated when his routine was interrupted, he hated loud noises, and he hated when his cousin Joey touched his things. By all traditional playground logic, Michael should be shoving his little sister away, complaining that she was heavy, or whining that girls had cooties.

But he didn't. He held onto her like it was his job. He anchored his arms around her like a seatbelt, and she squeezed him back with a desperate intensity that made Dad profoundly uncomfortable.

It wasn't just sibling affection; it was something heavier. Something almost frantic. If Dad tried to pry them apart, Rachel wouldn't just pout—she would completely shatter. She would scream, her face turning red, gasping for air like he was physically hurting her. And Michael would instantly panic, his whole body locking up, his eyes darting around like a trapped animal until she was given back to him.

Why? Dad wondered, his knuckles turning white on the steering wheel as he downshifted for the left turn onto Centerville Turnpike.

Grandma Helen called it "improper." Uncle Eddy called them "spoiled." Dad just thought it was weird.

He loved his kids fiercely. He would tear the world apart to keep them safe. But he didn't know how to protect them from whatever invisible thing was making them act like this. He wanted a normal son who played catch and complained about his little sister. He wanted a normal daughter who talked to her aunts and uncles instead of hiding behind her brother like a mute shadow.

He told himself it was just a phase. Rachel was just overly shy. Michael was just being a good big brother. Eventually, they would grow out of it. They had to grow out of it, because the alternative—that his kids were fundamentally, irreparably different from everyone else—was a thought he wasn't equipped to handle.

He finally made the right turn onto Brandon Boulevard, the familiar residential streets offering a brief reprieve from the heavy traffic. He shifted into third, dodging his son's knee again, letting them scream the lyrics to the radio. It wasn't proper, and it didn't make sense, but it was keeping the peace.

Dad made the final right turn onto Delaney Street. Pulling up alongside 1852, he saw Mom's sedan already parked in the driveway, meaning the rest of the loud, chaotic family was already inside.

With a heavy sigh, Dad parallel parked the Bantam against the curb, cut the engine, and let the mechanical silence wash over the cab. The ride was over, but the house was still waiting.
