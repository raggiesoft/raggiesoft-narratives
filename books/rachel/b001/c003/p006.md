The heavy brass tokens eventually ran out, and Jessica signaled that our time in the quiet, mechanical sanctuary of The Neon Quarter was over.

Rachel immediately grabbed my hand again as we crossed the threshold, stepping back into the bright, deafening chaos of the food court. We navigated through the maze of sticky tables and overflowing trash cans until we spotted our family. They had claimed a large cluster of tables near the center of the room. Uncle Eddy and Joey were eating massive slices of pizza, while Mom and Aunt Janet were surrounded by heavy plastic shopping bags.

Standing a few feet away from the tables, isolated from the rest of the group, were Dad and Grandma Helen.

Grandma Helen was a very traditional woman from Newport, Rhode Island, and she carried a rigid sense of East Coast etiquette wherever she went. She was currently speaking to Dad in a hushed, sharp whisper, her posture stiff. Dad was rubbing his temples, looking entirely exhausted by the conversation.

As Jessica led us over, Dad looked up. His expression immediately tightened into that familiar, authoritative mask.

"Michael," Dad said, stepping away from Grandma Helen to intercept us. "Tonight, you are going back to your room. You're going to sleep in your own bed."

My chest instantly seized up. The panic was sharp and fast. "But Joey is in there."

"I don't care," Dad replied firmly, keeping his voice low so the other tables wouldn't hear. "Joey is your cousin. You will share the room with him."

"We weren't fighting on the bottom bunk," I argued, my voice trembling slightly. I couldn't articulate the sensory math. I just knew that the lower bunk was safe and my bedroom was compromised. Rachel squeezed my hand so hard her knuckles turned white, sensing my rising panic.

"That's not the point, Michael," Grandma Helen interjected, stepping up beside Dad. She looked down at Rachel and me with a strict, disapproving frown. "It simply isn't proper."

I stared at her, completely lost. I had no idea what the word "proper" meant in this context. We were just sleeping. We were just hiding from the noise. It wasn't a puzzle or a game; it was a logistical solution to a broken environment. There was no logic to her argument.

"Why isn't it proper?" I asked, my frustration mounting because the rules were shifting again without any logical foundation.

Dad flushed red. He opened his mouth, then closed it, looking incredibly uncomfortable. He couldn't explain it to me. He was trapped between his mother's rigid, old-world sensibilities and the impossible task of explaining those unspoken adult anxieties to an eight-year-old boy.

Beside me, Jessica let out a loud, theatrical scoff.

At thirteen, she was standing squarely on the other side of the childhood divide. She was old enough to instantly decode the weird, puritanical panic Dad and Grandma Helen were projecting onto us. She knew exactly what they were implying about a boy and a girl sharing a bed, and she clearly thought they were being ridiculous.

"They're eight and six, Dad," Jessica said, her voice dripping with teenage exasperation. "They're practically toddlers. They're just trying to—"

"I WANT TO GO TO THE ARCADE!"

The sudden, ear-piercing shriek echoed across the entire food court.

Everyone jumped. Rachel flinched violently, burying her face into my side as her hands locked around my arm in a terrified vice grip.

Ten feet away, Joey was standing on his plastic chair, his face red with fury. Uncle Eddy had apparently just told him no, and Joey had chosen the nuclear option. He spiked his half-empty heavy plastic soda cup directly onto the table. Ice and brown soda exploded everywhere, splashing across Mom's shopping bags and Aunt Janet's coat.

"Joey, sit down right now!" Uncle Eddy bellowed, his voice booming over the ambient mall noise.

Joey screamed again, kicking the table leg so hard the entire cluster of trays rattled.

Silence swept over our small, isolated circle. Grandma Helen was staring at the chaotic scene, her hand clutching the collar of her coat, absolutely appalled by the total lack of decorum. Dad was staring at the spilled soda, his jaw tight, his previous lecture about the rules completely derailed.

Jessica didn't miss a beat. She looked at Dad, then pointed a finger directly at Joey, who was now throwing a full-blown tantrum while Uncle Eddy tried to wrangle him off the chair.

"You want to lock Michael in a room with that?" Jessica asked flatly.

Dad closed his eyes, taking a long, deep breath. He was already exhausted, completely overwhelmed by the crowds, the noise, and now his nephew causing a massive public scene. The thought of forcing me into a small bedroom with that kind of violent, unstructured energy was suddenly more trouble than it was worth.

Grandma Helen pursed her lips, her strict East Coast sensibilities highly offended. "Perhaps... given the circumstances, Michael sleeping elsewhere is for the best."

Dad opened his eyes, defeated by the sheer logistics of his own family. He looked down at me and Rachel, who was still trembling against my side.

"Fine," Dad sighed heavily, waving his hand in surrender. "Fine. But I don't want to hear a single peep out of you two upstairs tonight."

Jessica flashed me a subtle, triumphant smirk. The adults had tried to enforce their invisible social rules, but the sheer, undeniable reality of Joey's chaos had broken the system in our favor. The lower bunk was secure.
