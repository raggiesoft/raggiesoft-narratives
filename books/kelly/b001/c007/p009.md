# Part 10: What's Really Going On? 💬

Back at the hotel, the three of them said their goodnights. **Jessica** gave them both a warm, proud smile before quietly closing the adjoining door, leaving the twins alone in their room.

**Ryan** let out a long, weary sigh and reached for the TV remote, trying to pretend it was just a normal evening. But **Kelly** wasn't having it.

"Ryan," she said, her voice soft but firm. "Sit on the bed next to me."

He turned, a wary look on his face, but he relented, sitting on the edge of the other queen bed. The moment he did, Kelly moved to sit beside him, wrapping her arms around him in a tight, firm hug.

"It's time to talk about your leg," she said into his shoulder, her voice leaving no room for his usual dismissals. "I want to know what's really going on."

"It's nothing," he started to mumble, his voice muffled. "It's just my old job, I swear."

She held him tighter, refusing to let him pull away. "No," she said, her voice unwavering. "Don't hide it from me. Not anymore. I saw you today. You are in **genuine pain**, Ryan. I hate seeing you in pain. Please. Just tell me."

Held securely in his sister's arms, feeling her unconditional love and fierce concern, his defenses finally crumbled. He sagged against her, the exhaustion of the day winning.

"It was at **PriceSavers**," he began, his voice barely a whisper. "A few months ago. I was in the stockroom, getting a new VCR down from a high shelf. I was coming down the ladder, maybe a little too fast. The bottom step... I just missed it. I landed on my left leg, but it was all twisted and weird."

"Did you fall?" Kelly asked, her voice tight with worry.

"No, that's the thing," he said, shaking his head against her shoulder. "I didn't fall. I just... landed wrong. There was this sharp jolt in my hip, like a pop. It hurt for a second, really bad, and then it just... faded to a dull ache. I walked it off. It felt fine after an hour. I didn't think it was a big deal."

"Why didn't you tell me? Or Mom and Dad?"

"Because it was nothing!" he insisted, his voice cracking with a frustration that was aimed at himself. "It was a stupid misstep. I wasn't going to make a fuss and have everyone worry because I tweaked a muscle. I thought it would just go away."

When he was finished, she didn't say, "I told you so." She just pulled back slightly to look him in the eye, though she kept her hands on his shoulders. "Okay," she said, her voice full of a quiet, fierce resolve. "The first thing we're doing when we get back to Seattle is making a doctor's appointment. You are not ignoring this anymore. We'll figure it out."

He looked at his sister, his anchor, the person who had just become his staunchest advocate. He nodded, a wave of relief washing over him. He didn't have to pretend anymore. They would face this together.
