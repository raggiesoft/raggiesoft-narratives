The tour of the **Infinite Loop** campus was a dizzying experience for the twins. The corridors were long, pristine, and seemed to hum with a quiet, innovative energy. **David** and **Jessica** led the way, their conversation a comfortable, high-level shorthand of project names and technical specifications. **Kelly** and **Ryan** followed, trying to absorb every detail.

As they walked from one building to the next, Kelly noticed Ryan was starting to fall behind. His walk, usually so steady, had a slight but noticeable stiffness to it. He stumbled, catching himself against the wall of the corridor before quickly straightening up, a faint blush of embarrassment on his face.

"Ryan, are you sure you're okay?" Kelly asked, her voice a low whisper. "You're limping a little."

"I'm fine," he insisted, forcing a smile. "Seriously. It's just two years of standing on my feet all day at **PriceSavers**. My legs are just tired. It'll pass."

Up ahead, Jessica and David had stopped, their conversation ceasing. They had both seen the stumble. They exchanged a quick, silent look—a shared, unspoken moment of adult concern. It was a look that recognized this was likely more than just fatigue from a retail job.

Jessica turned her power wheelchair around, her expression calm and unreadable. "There are some benches just up ahead," she said, her tone casual but firm. "Let's take a five-minute break before we head into the OS lab."

Ryan didn't protest. As they continued the tour a few minutes later, a new, unspoken awareness settled over the group. Kelly was now watching her brother with a quiet worry, while Jessica and David observed him with a new, professional concern.

After another long corridor, the limp was no longer subtle. A sharp, shooting pain went up Ryan’s leg, and he winced, stopping for a moment and trying to discreetly stretch his hip. He was exhausted, and his pride was the only thing keeping him from asking to sit down.

Jessica stopped her wheelchair beside him. "Ryan," she said, her voice soft but direct, leaving no room for argument. "You're exhausted. Lean on the chair."

"No, I'm okay, really," he started, his pride flaring.

Jessica's gaze was steady, cutting through his denial with a look of professional concern. "As your supervisor, your well-being on this trip is my responsibility. Put your hand on the headrest of my chair and let it take some of the weight. That's an order."

He looked at his sister's worried face, then back at Jessica's firm, compassionate expression. Her insistence wasn't an act of pity; it was an act of a responsible leader. Defeated by both the pain and her undeniable logic, he relented. With a quiet sigh of gratitude, he placed his hand on the headrest of her power wheelchair, allowing himself the support. As they continued down the hall, the irony of the moment was lost on no one: Ryan, the anchor, now needed an anchor of his own.
