# Part 1: The Press Conference 🎤

The Grand Hyatt ballroom felt cold and cavernously empty. A few rows of chairs were set up facing a lonely podium, but only a handful were occupied by reporters from the tech press, their faces a mixture of professional obligation and cynical amusement. **Kelly Madsen** found a seat in the very back row, clutching the strap of her messenger bag. As promised, Ryan waited just outside the double doors, and their father was in the car downstairs. Kelly was here out of a morbid curiosity, a need to see the final, bizarre act of the disaster for herself.

**Evan Rothwell** strode to the podium, attempting to project an aura of defiant confidence. "Thank you for coming," he began. "There have been some misconceptions about a recent internal, informal brainstorming document that was taken out of context..."

Kelly almost sighed. She'd heard that exact phrase a dozen times in the office, usually right before he announced a terrible idea. He talked about "strategic restructuring" and "upcoming milestones for **AuroraOS**" as if his entire development team hadn't walked out the previous morning.

Then, the questions started. A reporter from **TechWire Daily** stood up. "Mr. Rothwell, how do you intend to ship a product with no engineers?"

Another followed before he could answer. "Are you aware that your Board has already announced a full cessation of operations? Is Aurora Systems not, for all legal purposes, defunct?"

The finality of the word "defunct" hit Kelly like a physical blow. It was truly, officially over.

With each sharp question, Evan deflected and spun, his confidence cracking. When a reporter asked if he would apologize to Jessica Taylor, he replied with a flat "no comment," and a visible shift occurred in the room. The journalists had their story: a delusional founder in complete denial.

One by one, they began to pack up. A reporter from a well-known tech blog stood, shook his head, and walked out while Evan was still mid-sentence. Soon, half the room was gone. Kelly watched as the power Evan once held over her evaporated under the harsh lights. He wasn't a CEO anymore; he was just a man talking to himself in an empty room.

She realized she was the only person left in the audience who wasn't being paid to be there. Quietly, Kelly stood up. Her movement was a small, personal declaration of freedom. Evan, lost in his own monologue, didn't notice her leave.
