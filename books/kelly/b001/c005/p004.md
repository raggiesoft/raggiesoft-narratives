The quiet in Jessica’s office was comforting. Kelly took a few deep, steadying breaths, the last tremors of her meltdown fading under the calm, accepting presence of her brother and her new mentor. She gave Ryan’s arm a gentle squeeze—a signal he understood as *I’m okay now*. He squeezed back before slowly releasing his hug.

Kelly turned her chair to face Jessica, her expression a mixture of embarrassment and resolve. She didn't want her first day to be defined by her meltdown; she wanted it to be defined by her work. She pointed a slightly trembling finger toward her new workstation. "Can I...?" she began, her voice quiet but clear. "Can I write something? Even just a 'Hello, World'. I just want to feel like I'm doing something."

Jessica’s smile was immediate and genuine. She understood perfectly. It wasn't about productivity; it was about reclaiming the day, about proving to herself that she belonged here.

"We can do better than 'Hello, World'," Jessica said warmly. She wheeled over to the whiteboard and drew a simple rectangle. "Okay, first assignment for both of you. The 'About' box for a new module in Aurora Presenter. It needs a title bar, an icon, a few lines of text for the version and copyright, and a single 'OK' button to close it. Nothing fancy. It doesn't even have to be connected to the main app yet. Just a standalone window that looks right and works."

It was the perfect first step. More than a simple line of text, but not a complex piece of the main presentation software. It was a self-contained, achievable task with a visible result—something they could build and own on their very first day.

"Ryan, you can build yours for the Quantum OS version," she said, pointing to one set of design specs on a shared server. "Kelly, you can take the MagnaOS version. The logic is the same, but the look and feel—the APIs you'll call to draw the window—are a little different. Your documentation is all set up. My door is open if you get stuck, but I have a feeling you won't."

Ryan nodded, a look of focused excitement on his face. He moved to his station, his earlier nervousness replaced by a programmer's curiosity. Kelly moved over to her own desk, her hands steady now. She saw the icon for the code editor, the same one the Aurora devs had shown her in the library, and took a deep breath.

With Ryan working quietly by her side and Jessica providing a steady, supportive presence from across the office, Kelly Madsen opened the editor and wrote her first official line of code for Quantum. She was finally doing the work she had always dreamed of.
