As they made their way to baggage claim, a man with a warm, friendly face and a MagnaByte employee badge clipped to his polo shirt approached them. "Jess! Welcome back," he said, giving Jessica a familiar, professional nod before turning to the twins. "You must be Kelly and Ryan. I'm David. It's great to finally meet you both in person."

David expertly helped them navigate the chaos of the baggage carousel, grabbing their bags with the ease of a practiced host. He then led them out to the passenger pickup curb, where a clean but decidedly not new, dark blue Stark Rancher van was waiting. It was a full-sized passenger van, the kind used for corporate shuttles, not a luxury vehicle. Its side was fitted with a sturdy, well-maintained wheelchair lift.

David handled the luggage while Jessica rolled her power wheelchair onto the lift. The twins climbed in, settling into a bench seat. The interior was spotless, but the upholstery was a durable, standard-issue gray cloth.

"Alright," David said as he got into the driver's seat. "First, we'll get you checked into the hotel so you can drop your bags. After that, we'll grab some food—there's a great burger joint near the office. Then, I'll give you the grand tour of Infinite Loop so you can get your bearings before we dive into the work tomorrow."

As they pulled out into the bright California sun, Ryan and Kelly stared out the windows, their faces a picture of pure awe. They drove past the low, sprawling campuses of the most famous technology companies in the world. For Jessica, it was a routine drive. For the twins, it felt like they were driving through the heart of the future.

The van pulled up to a sleek, modern hotel. The check-in was seamless, and soon the four of them were heading up in the elevator. David helped carry the twins' bags to their room. Jessica unlocked her own accessible room, then unlocked an adjoining door, revealing a second room with two queen beds.

"Oh, adjoining rooms," David said, placing the bags inside. "That was smart of your travel department. Makes it easy to coordinate."

Jessica caught Kelly's eye, a quick, questioning glance. Kelly gave a small, almost imperceptible nod of permission.

"It's a bit more than that," Jessica explained, her tone professional and direct. "Ryan is Kelly's anchor, her support system. Quantum's policy is to ensure that support is always available, especially on the road. This setup allows them to have their shared space while keeping the team together. It's a non-negotiable for us."

David's face immediately shifted to one of complete understanding and respect. "That makes perfect sense," he said. "It's a smart accommodation. Your company really looks out for its people."

He took a step back into the hallway. "Well, I'll let you three get settled. I'll be back in an hour to take you to lunch?"

"Sounds good, David. Thanks for everything," Jessica said.

As the door closed, Kelly and Ryan looked at each other. Their new boss hadn't just arranged for a hotel; she had advocated for their needs, professionally and without apology. They were safe here.
