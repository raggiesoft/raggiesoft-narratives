The rain lashed heavily against the single-pane windows of their modest, cramped living room, but Mary barely heard the storm. The worn, braided rug stretching between the faded couch and the television stand had been completely transformed into an active, high-stakes battlefield.

At seven years old, Mary sat cross-legged in the center of the rug, completely enveloped by the warm, safe presence of her two older brothers. The house was small, with low ceilings and creaky floorboards that stood in stark contrast to the sprawling, ostentatious McMansions going up in the wealthier suburbs, but to Mary, it was an absolute sanctuary.

Ethan, the eldest, was hunched over the centerpiece of their campaign: a sprawling, asymmetrical fortress constructed entirely of brightly colored Snap-Bricks. His brow was furrowed in deep architectural concentration. He carefully pressed a long, reinforced gray plate into the upper parapet, testing its weight-bearing capacity with his thumb.

Connor, sitting cross-legged on Mary's other side, sprang into action. As the tactician, he had spent the last twenty minutes meticulously organizing a defensive perimeter.

"Heavy cavalry is locked and loaded, Commander," Connor reported with a sharp, entirely serious salute.

He gestured to a fierce, perfectly aligned row of Morph-Mechanics. The plastic robots had all been shifted into their heavy vehicular and beast forms, creating an impenetrable wall of armor at the base of the Snap-Brick fortress. Behind them, crouching behind the legs of the coffee table, was a squad of Frontline Commandos. The rugged, military action figures were positioned flawlessly for a flanking maneuver.

Mary narrowed her eyes, completely immersed in the tactical weight of the decision. She looked at the gleaming Velocity Die-Casts waiting at the top of the ramp Ethan had built over the arm of the couch. She looked at the Frontline Commandos, and then up at her Stella Doll standing proudly on the balcony.

Neither of her brothers tried to rush her. They didn't snatch the toys, and they certainly didn't tell her that dolls couldn't lead military campaigns. In this house, on this rug, Mary's imagination was the absolute, uncontested law.

"We hold the fort," Mary decided, her voice dropping into a dramatic, hushed whisper. "Stella says we wait until they get past the rug fringe. Then, we release the Morph-Mechanics."

Mary giggled, burying her face into Ethan's sweater as Connor wrapped his arms around both of them from the side, turning the tactical huddle into a massive, tangled pile of hugs. They collapsed onto the carpet together, a mess of laughter and warm, physical affection, completely insulated from the gloomy rain outside.
