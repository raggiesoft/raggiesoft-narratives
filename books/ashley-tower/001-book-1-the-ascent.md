# Book 1: The Ascent

## Chapter 1: The Gathering Storm – Tuesday, February 9, 2021

### Part 1: Best Laid Plans – 7:30 PM

The deep, heavy rumble of his father’s oversized pickup truck pulling into the driveway vibrated right through the floorboards, but up here, behind a closed door, the noise was at least tolerable.

Donald let out a long, silent breath as Mary gently lowered him onto the edge of their shared mattress. His stomach always tied itself in knots during the agonizing carry up the sweeping, curved staircase. The sprawling suburban McMansion was built entirely to project wealth—vaulted ceilings, grand entrances, and absolutely zero considerations for a wheelchair. Down in the kitchen, the booming, arrogant laughter of Mary’s two older brothers began to echo off the walls, blending with the sharp clatter of their parents.

Donald hated the noise down there. He liked it right here, where it was safe and relatively quiet.

On the small, cracked screen of the television resting on their dresser, a local meteorologist gestured broadly to a digitally rendered map of the Texas panhandle.

"Looking ahead to next week, it seems we have a standard winter cold front moving into the region," the weatherman noted with a cheerful wave. "Expect a dip into the low forties by Monday and Tuesday, so keep those light jackets handy, but the skies should remain clear."

Donald only half-listened, his brain processing the information as a simple logistical fact: *I will need to make sure Mary grabs my heavy jacket for the party.*

He wasn't looking at the weather map anyway. His eyes were fixed on Mary as she reached under her pillow and pulled out a worn, spiral-bound notebook. A bright, humming vocalization slipped past Donald's lips, his hands moving in a rapid, excited rhythm against his thighs. He couldn't help it; his entire body practically vibrated with anticipation.

Mary grinned, the exhaustion of the stairs instantly melting away. She sat beside him on the bed, shifting closer until their shoulders pressed firmly together. Donald leaned into her, the deep, grounding pressure instantly helping to settle the excited tremors in his hands.

"I know," Mary whispered, her eyes shining in the dim light of the television. "Exactly one week. Next Tuesday."

She flipped the notebook open to a page completely covered in careful scribbles, highlighted dates, and hidden logistics.

"The girls have it all locked down," she said, pointing to a list of her friends' names. "They are covering the whole bill. Dinner, dessert, everything. They know neither of us has a dime, and they don't care. We're going to celebrate your eighteenth and my nineteenth exactly how we want to."

Donald let out a soft, breathy laugh. He reached for his sluggish, outdated tablet, his fingers tapping across the cracked glass until he pulled up a single, heavily encrypted text thread. He pushed the screen toward Mary, displaying a short message from Ashley.

*Flight confirmed. See you next week.*

Mary’s smile widened, though she instinctively lowered her voice, glancing toward the closed bedroom door. "And the absolute best part," she murmured, "is that the second the clock strikes midnight next Tuesday, your dad can't do a single thing to stop you from walking out that door. You'll be a legal adult. He can yell, he can threaten, but he won't have a shred of authority left."

Donald nodded emphatically. The thought of finally seeing his sister again after a decade in the dark—of never having to be carried up those terrifying stairs again, of leaving the suffocating shadow of the arrogant people downstairs—made his chest ache with a fierce, brilliant hope.

Everything was falling perfectly into place. He just had to make it through one more week.

### Part 2: The Anchor and the Watch – 8:15 PM

The narrow doorframe of the en-suite bathroom was just another architectural insult in a house built entirely for show.

Because his heavy manual wheelchair remained parked at the base of the sweeping staircase on the first floor, every movement upstairs required a grueling, highly coordinated physical transfer. Mary adjusted her grip around Donald’s waist, securely bearing his weight as she carried him from the edge of their shared mattress across the short distance of the bedroom. She expertly angled her shoulders to clear the cramped bathroom doorway, compensating for the complete lack of accessibility hardware.

With practiced, effortless strength born from a decade of necessity, she carefully lowered him, ensuring he was safely situated on the porcelain before stepping back.

Once he was secure, the routine shifted into its most critical phase. The intimate logistics of Donald's daily care were handled with a brisk, quiet dignity, entirely focused on his physical safety rather than the granular mechanics of the tasks.

Mary did not step out into the hallway to give him privacy. Instead, she sat down heavily on the edge of the bathtub, resting her elbows on her knees.

She locked her eyes entirely on his face. She didn't blink, and she didn't look away.

Donald let out a soft, humming vocalization, his stiff fingers resting on his thighs. He didn't mind her unwavering stare; in fact, he relied on it. He knew exactly what the rest of the family thought about this routine. Down in the kitchen, the four brothers routinely mocked the arrangement, loudly complaining to their parents that it was "stupid" and "weird" for a nineteen-year-old girl to hover over him like a warden. His father simply wrote it off as another one of Donald's pathetic anomalies.

They didn't understand the neurology, and they didn't care to learn.

Because of his specific neurodivergent profile, Donald was prone to silent, absence seizures—sudden, terrifying neurological misfires where his brain would simply pause. If one triggered while he was sitting unsupported on the hard porcelain, his body would instantly go slack. He would pitch forward onto the unforgiving tile before he could even make a sound.

Mary’s unbroken gaze was his absolute safety net. If his eyes glazed over, she was positioned to lunge forward and catch his dead weight in a fraction of a second. She watched him because she was the only person in the state of Texas who actually cared if he hit the floor.

When he was finished, the routine flowed seamlessly into the bathing protocol. The cramped shower stall was another miserable logistical hurdle, but they managed it with synchronized efficiency. Mary handled her own hygiene simultaneously, a necessity in a household where lingering too long in the bathroom usually invited heavy-fisted knocks and angry shouting from the older brothers in the hallway.

The rush of the hot water felt incredible against Donald's rigid muscles, temporarily melting away the spasticity and the dull ache of the carry up the stairs. The environment was purely functional, a clinical progression of soap, warm water, and heavy towels, executed with the profound, unspoken trust of two people who had spent their entire childhoods surviving the same hostile territory.

Twenty minutes later, Donald was dressed in incredibly soft, loose-fitting cotton sleepwear that wouldn't agitate his sensory boundaries.

Mary scooped him into another secure carry, hauling him out of the steamy bathroom and gently setting him down onto the edge of their shared mattress. She grabbed a towel, vigorously drying her own damp hair as she sat down next to him with a heavy, exhausted sigh.

Donald leaned his shoulder against hers, closing his eyes as the ambient noise of the house continued to vibrate through the floorboards.

His mind drifted back to the encrypted text message on his cracked tablet. *Flight confirmed.*

He hated airports. He knew the commercial terminal at DFW would be a massive, overwhelming sensory nightmare of glaring fluorescent lights, echoing announcements, and claustrophobic crowds. The thought of being crammed into a narrow, pressurized commercial airline seat next to hundreds of strangers usually made his chest tight with panic.

But as Mary threw her arm over his shoulders, pulling him into a warm, grounding side-hug, Donald realized he didn't care. He would endure the sensory overload of a commercial flight ten times over if it meant finally boarding a plane to Meridian City. The noise of the airport was temporary. The freedom waiting on the other side of it was permanent.

### Part 3: The Ten O'Clock News – 10:00 PM

The nightly transition into bed was a masterclass in quiet, unspoken efficiency. Moving with the practiced dignity of someone who had managed his physical boundaries for an entire decade, Mary carefully helped Donald change into his soft, sensory-friendly pajamas.

She pulled back the heavy quilt on their shared queen-sized mattress. Donald immediately transferred to his designated spot on the left side, shifting his weight until he was settled against the pillows. A moment later, Mary climbed in on the right.

For ten years, this specific arrangement had been his absolute sanctuary. Donald's AuDHD nervous system was highly sensitive to the dark, prone to severe spikes of nighttime anxiety in the sprawling, hostile house. Having Mary directly beside him—providing that constant, physical grounding presence—was the only way he could regulate his senses and fall asleep.

Mary grabbed the TV remote from the nightstand and clicked on the local ten o'clock broadcast. The anchor tossed the segment over to the meteorologist, who was standing in front of a digital map of the Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex.

"Just a heads up as we look toward the start of next week," the weatherman noted, gesturing to a sweeping blue line on the map. "We're tracking a cold front dipping down from the plains. Temperatures look to be dropping into the thirties and forties by Monday night into Tuesday."

"Hear that?" Mary whispered, pulling the covers up to her chest. "We’re definitely going to have to dig your heavy jacket out of the closet for the party."

Donald offered a soft, agreeable hum. A cold front was a minor logistical hurdle. As long as he was wearing a heavy coat, the temperature outside was entirely irrelevant compared to the excitement of the event itself.

Mary picked up her phone, her thumbs flying across the screen in the dim light of the television. "I’m just confirming the final headcount with the girls," she murmured, a bright, undeniable excitement bleeding into her voice. "They secured the reservation. It’s completely locked in."

She set her phone face-down on the mattress and turned her head to look at him, her eyes shining in the blue glow of the TV screen.

"Eighteen, Donald," Mary whispered, treating the number with absolute reverence. "It’s a massive milestone. In exactly one week, you are officially an adult."

Donald’s hands fluttered rapidly against the quilt, a quiet, joyful vocalization escaping his lips. Eighteen didn't just mean a birthday dinner. It meant legal autonomy. It meant the countdown was finally in the single digits, and he and Mary were just days away from walking out the front door and never looking back.

Mary reached over, resting her hand firmly over his wrist to apply that familiar, deep pressure.

"Get some sleep," she said softly, turning her attention back to the mundane local news. "Next week is going to change everything."

## Chapter 2: The Routine – Wednesday, February 10, 2021

### Part 1: The Departure – 7:00 AM

The alarm clock on the nightstand buzzed, pulling Donald from the deep, heavy sleep he only ever achieved when Mary was anchored beside him.

The morning routine was a rigid, necessary sequence. Mary was already up, moving with quiet efficiency. She helped him dress, her hands gently working his stiff limbs into a soft, comfortable sweater and jeans. His cerebral palsy made fine motor tasks like managing buttons impossible, and the chilly morning air always made his muscles feel tighter and more resistant than usual.

Then came the stairs. Donald braced his core, wrapping his arms securely around Mary's neck as she lifted his weight. The descent down the sweeping, ostentatious staircase of the McMansion was an agonizing, daily reminder of his father's priorities. Donald hated the vulnerability of the transfer, forced to rely entirely on Mary's physical stamina because the sprawling house was built to project wealth, not accommodate a wheelchair.

She reached the bottom step without faltering and gently settled him into the seat of his manual chair.

The kitchen was already a chaotic, abrasive sensory environment. The sharp clatter of ceramic bowls, the glaring overhead recessed lights, and the booming, arrogant voices of his father and older brothers made Donald’s shoulders instantly tense. He kept his eyes averted, staring at the polished granite countertop to block out the visual noise.

Mary ignored the rest of the household entirely. She prepared a bowl of warm oatmeal and pulled up a stool right beside his wheel. Because of the spasticity in his hands, attempting to use silverware was an exhausting, messy battle that usually ended in a severe sensory spike. Mary bypassed the frustration completely. With practiced, dignified patience, she picked up the spoon and carefully fed him, pacing the bites perfectly to his swallowing rhythm.

One of the older brothers scoffed loudly from the kitchen island, muttering something derisive about the arrangement, but Mary didn’t even flinch. She just offered Donald a small, reassuring smile, creating an insulated, protective bubble around the two of them that the rest of the family couldn't pierce.

By seven-thirty, the heavy rumble of a diesel engine vibrated through the front windows. It was time to leave.

Mary pushed his wheelchair through the heavy front doors and out onto the expansive concrete driveway. The Texas morning air was brisk and biting, carrying a sharp edge from the incoming cold front the news had mentioned the night before, but the sky above the affluent suburb remained deceptively clear.

The yellow special needs school bus idled at the curb, its amber lights flashing. The side door hissed open, and the mechanical, hydraulic whine of the wheelchair lift deploying filled the quiet street.

Mary wheeled him to the edge of the lift platform. Before she locked his brakes, she stepped around to the front of his chair. She leaned down, wrapping her arms securely around his chest in a tight, grounding hug. The deep pressure instantly settled the lingering, jagged anxiety left over from the kitchen.

"Have a good day," Mary whispered softly near his ear. She pulled back, brushing a stray lock of hair from his forehead, her eyes meeting his with absolute, unwavering certainty. "I'll be right here waiting when the bus brings you home."

Donald let out a bright, ascending vocalization, a small smile touching the corners of his mouth. He gave her a clumsy but firm nod.

Mary stepped back and gave the driver a thumbs-up. The hydraulic lift engaged with a heavy clunk, smoothly hoisting Donald up into the warm cabin of the bus. As the doors folded shut and the bus began to pull away from the curb, Donald looked out the tinted window. Mary was still standing at the edge of the massive driveway, shivering slightly in the cold, watching the bus until it disappeared around the corner.

### Interlude: The Shifting Models – 12:15 PM

The microwave beeped three loud, shrill times, cutting through the ambient noise of the local midday news broadcast.

"Mary, it's done! Give it to us!"

Mary didn't move to open the stainless-steel door. She simply crossed her arms and leaned against the granite kitchen island, keeping her eyes fixed on the small television screen sitting on the counter. "You have to wait two minutes," she said calmly, not bothering to look at the two young boys sitting on the barstools. "The instructions say to let it sit so it finishes cooking and cools down. I'm not dealing with you two crying over burnt tongues."

Her two youngest brothers—the biological sons of her mother and Donald's father—groaned in unison, tossing their heads back with the exact same arrogant, entitled dismissal that their father wore every single day.

Mary tuned out their whining with practiced ease. Her attention was entirely captured by the meteorologist on the screen. The cheerful, dismissive wave from last night's broadcast was entirely gone. The weatherman was standing in front of a digitally rendered map of the country, tracing a massive, swirling purple band dropping straight down from Canada.

"Folks, we need to talk about the long-range models for next week," the meteorologist said, his tone carrying a sudden, grim seriousness. "This is no longer looking like a standard Texas cold front. A piece of the polar vortex has broken off and is funneling straight down the Great Plains. We are tracking a severe drop in temperatures starting this weekend, and by Monday and Tuesday, we could be looking at single digits across the metroplex. Now is the time to start thinking about wrapping those exterior pipes and preparing for some truly rough weather."

Mary’s brow furrowed. Single digits. That wasn't just a cold snap; that was genuinely dangerous.

"Mary! We're starving!" the younger brother complained loudly, slapping his hands flat against the granite counter.

"It's been two minutes," Mary said flatly. She pulled open the microwave door, retrieved the steaming plastic trays of processed food, and set them down unceremoniously in front of the boys. They immediately began inhaling the food without a single word of thanks.

Mary stepped back, letting out a slow, quiet breath as she looked around the sprawling, opulent kitchen. She despised this house. She despised the noise, the entitlement, and the way the entire family treated Donald like an inconvenience to be managed.

But as she watched the weather map cycle through the grim projections, a fierce, protective warmth bloomed in her chest. She didn't have to endure this much longer.

Next Tuesday was the birthday party. Her friends were covering the entire bill, giving her and Donald one perfect, normal night of celebration away from this toxic household. More importantly, Donald's sister Ashley was flying into DFW. Mary had spent months acting as a secure proxy, relaying heavily encrypted messages between Donald and his older sister. She was incredibly eager to finally meet the woman on the other side of the screen.

Ashley had made a promise, laid out in clear, unmistakable text: the absolute second Donald turned eighteen and aged out of his father's legal custody, they were leaving. Ashley had explicitly extended the offer to her, promising both Donald and Mary a permanent, safe home in Meridian, Washington.

Mary looked back at the TV, watching the blue and purple temperature gradients sweep down the Texas map. Let the rough weather come, she thought. It didn't matter. In six days, Donald was legally free, and she was taking him out of this house forever.

### Part 2: The Right Call – 3:45 PM

The sharp, high-pitched squeal of the school bus’s air brakes broke the quiet of the sprawling Prosper subdivision.

Through the tinted window, Donald watched the massive front doors of the McMansion swing open. Mary was already stepping out onto the concrete driveway, pulling a light cardigan tight across her chest against the biting Texas wind. She didn't wait near the porch; she walked right up to the curb, her eyes locked on the side door of the yellow bus.

The hydraulic lift whined, lowering Donald's manual wheelchair to the pavement. The second the platform settled, Mary was there. She stepped past the bus aide, leaned down, and wrapped her arms securely around Donald’s chest in a tight, grounding embrace. The immediate, deep physical pressure instantly neutralized the lingering sensory static of the chaotic bus ride.

Donald let out a long, shuddering breath, the tension leaving his shoulders. He was safe.

"Hey," Mary murmured into his hair, pulling back with a warm, relieved smile. "Let's get inside. It's freezing out here."

She grabbed the worn rubber handles of his chair, thanked the driver, and pushed him up the driveway and through the front doors. Leaving the heavy manual chair parked securely at the base of the stairs, she braced herself. The brutal, agonizing carry up the sweeping, curved staircase to their shared bedroom was a necessary evil. Balancing his weight securely while keeping his school backpack slung over her shoulder was exhausting, but once the bedroom door clicked shut behind them, the house's toxic atmosphere was entirely sealed out.

Mary settled him onto the edge of their shared mattress, propping a couple of pillows behind his back. She pulled up her rolling desk chair, unzipped his backpack, and laid his spiral-bound notebooks out on the small bedside table.

"Alright, how was it?" Mary asked, pulling his sluggish, cracked tablet from the front pouch of his bag and handing it to him. "Survive the gauntlet?"

Donald rested his stiff hands on the cracked glass. His fingers moved in slow, deliberate taps. He didn't just want to complain about the noise in the cafeteria; he needed to tell her about fifth period.

He pushed the tablet toward her. The text-to-speech was muted, so Mary simply read the screen.

*A girl in AP History asked me to go to The Catwalks with her during the passing period.*

Mary’s eyebrows shot up in absolute disbelief. "The Catwalks? The maintenance grates above the auditorium?"

Donald nodded, a dry, cynical amusement lighting up his dark eyes. He pulled the tablet back, typing furiously despite the tremor in his hands.

*I looked at my wheelchair. Then I looked at her. And I asked her exactly how she planned on hoisting a hundred and fifty pounds of dead weight and chrome up a twenty-foot vertical steel ladder.*

Mary stared at the screen for a split second before she burst out laughing, clapping a hand over her mouth. The sheer, staggering absurdity of the request perfectly encapsulated how entirely oblivious the rest of the high school was to Donald's physical reality.

"Oh my god," Mary gasped, wiping a tear of mirth from her eye. "What did she say?"

*She said she hadn't thought about that. I told her I was going to the nurse.*

Mary let out another breathy laugh, shaking her head. But Donald's smile faded. His jaw tightened, and he tapped the screen again, typing out the brutal, underlying truth that actually governed his existence.

*Even if it was physically possible, if a teacher catches you up there, it is an automatic suspension. The principal calls home. If the school called Dad and told him I was caught doing that...*

Donald stopped typing. He didn't need to finish the sentence. His heart hammered painfully against his ribs just visualizing the scenario. His father already treated him with absolute, arrogant disdain, viewing his cerebral palsy and his autism as pathetic burdens. If Donald brought the humiliation of a major disciplinary suspension to his father's doorstep, the ensuing verbal and physical abuse would be catastrophic. The risk wasn't just getting in trouble at school; the risk was surviving the punishment at home.

Mary’s laughter died instantly. The humor evaporated, replaced by a fierce, protective warmth. She leaned forward, wrapping her arms around him in another tight, heavy hug.

"You made the exact right call, Donald," Mary whispered fiercely, pressing her cheek against his temple. "It isn't worth it. None of it is worth giving him a reason to come after you. You protected yourself. I am so proud of you."

Donald closed his eyes, leaning into her hold. The validation was everything. He hadn't panicked; he had analyzed the variables and neutralized the threat perfectly.

Mary pulled back, clearing her throat to dispel the heavy tension, and immediately shifted the atmosphere back to their productive routine. She tapped the cover of his thickest textbook.

"Alright, enough high school drama. We have to conquer Calculus," Mary said, flipping the textbook open to his current assignment. "I know this chapter is brutal, but you know the concepts better than I do. You just have to walk me through the logic."

Donald smiled, shifting his focus to the complex, 12th-grade math problems printed on the page. Because his spasticity made gripping a pencil and writing out long equations nearly impossible, Mary acted as his physical scribe. She never solved the problems for him; she simply held the pen, waiting for his brain to do the heavy lifting.

"Okay, number four," Mary said, reading the prompt. "Integration by parts. We need to evaluate the integral of $`\int_{}^{}x\cos(x)\, dx`$."

Donald looked at the equation: $`\int_{}^{}x\cos(x)\, dx`$.

His analytical mind instantly segmented the variables. He tapped his tablet, breaking down the formula for her.

*Formula is* $`\int_{}^{}u\, dv = uv - \int_{}^{}v\, du`$*. Set* $`u = x`$*.*

"Okay, $`u = x`$," Mary narrated as she carefully wrote it down on the lined paper. "Which means $`du = dx`$. What's the $`dv`$?"

Donald tapped again. *Set* $`dv = \cos(x)\, dx`$*. So* $`v = \sin(x)`$*.*

"Got it," Mary smiled, her pen scratching against the paper as she assembled the pieces he was feeding her. "So if we plug it all back into the formula, we get $`x\sin(x) - \int_{}^{}{\sin(x)}\, dx`$."

Donald nodded emphatically, watching the math resolve perfectly on the page. He typed the final line.

*The integral of* $`\sin(x)`$ *is* $`- \cos(x)`$*. Final answer:* $`x\sin(x) + \cos(x) + C`$*.*

"Plus C," Mary echoed, drawing a neat box around the final answer. "Don't forget the constant. Nice job, boss. You're going to ace this test."

Donald let out a soft, breathy laugh, leaning his shoulder against hers. Outside the window, the Texas sky was beginning to turn a harsh, bruised gray as the cold front advanced, but inside the bedroom, the math was flawless, the perimeter was secure, and he wasn't facing any of it alone.

### Part 3: The Inventory – 6:30 PM

Dinner in the McMansion was always a loud, abrasive affair. Downstairs in the sprawling, aggressively lit kitchen, Donald’s father and Mary’s older brothers dominated the massive granite island, their booming voices echoing off the vaulted ceilings. They were loudly debating the upcoming weekend's football schedule, completely ignoring the television playing quietly in the corner of the room.

Mary sat beside Donald’s wheelchair, her attention split between carefully helping him eat and watching the screen.

The local evening news broadcast had returned from commercial, and the tone of the meteorologist had entirely shifted. The cheerful, dismissive banter from the night before was gone. The weatherman looked genuinely stressed, pointing to a massive, dark purple mass dominating the digital map of the Midwest, driving straight down like a spear into the heart of Texas.

"We cannot stress this enough," the meteorologist warned, his voice tight. "The long-range computer models are locking in. This piece of the polar vortex is not breaking up. We are looking at a historic, sustained freeze. Temperatures will plunge into the twenties by the weekend, and by Monday, we are tracking single digits. I urge everyone to wrap your exterior pipes, check on your neighbors, and prepare for hard winter conditions."

"Turn that garbage off," Donald's father scoffed around a mouthful of steak, waving his fork at the screen. "It's Texas. The news always panics for ratings. The ground is too warm for anything to stick for more than an hour."

Mary didn't say a word. She wiped Donald's mouth with a napkin, her jaw set tight. She looked at Donald, whose dark eyes were already fixed on her. He had heard the meteorologist. He understood the math of single digits.

"Alright, we're done here," Mary announced quietly, standing up and unlocking the brakes on his manual chair. She didn't wait to be excused. She navigated him away from the island, hauling him back up the sweeping, curved staircase to the safety of their room.

The second the bedroom door clicked shut, the abrasive noise of the house was severed.

Mary transferred Donald to the edge of the queen mattress, making sure he was comfortably propped against the pillows. Then, she walked directly to her closet. She didn't turn on the TV. She didn't pull out her phone to text her friends about the birthday party.

She pulled her worn, spiral-bound notebook from her bedside drawer, flipped to a blank page, and grabbed a pen.

Donald watched her, his hands resting quietly on his thighs. He let out a soft, inquisitive hum, his head tilting slightly as he tracked her anxious movements.

"Single digits, Donald," Mary whispered, looking back at him, her eyes wide with a sudden, fierce realization. "He thinks it's a joke. He's not going to do anything."

She started to write. *Water. Blankets. Flashlights.*

Mary stopped, staring at the paper. Her pen hovered over the lines as a cold, terrifying wave of clarity washed over her. She mentally walked through the sprawling, opulent rooms of the McMansion. They had massive, vaulted ceilings that trapped all the heat twenty feet in the air. They had an ornamental gas fireplace that barely radiated warmth. They drove gas-guzzling SUVs, but they didn't own a single piece of actual winter survival gear. There was no chopped firewood. There was no backup generator. There wasn't even a stockpile of non-perishable food in the designer pantry.

If the grid failed—if the heaters keeping the massive house warm suddenly shut off—the McMansion wasn't a fortress. It was an icebox.

Mary looked up from the notebook, her gaze locking onto Donald. Because of his cerebral palsy and lack of mobility, he couldn't generate his own body heat by moving around. His nervous system was incredibly vulnerable to temperature drops. If the house froze, his body would start shutting down long before anyone else's.

And his father wouldn't lift a finger to help him.

Mary dropped the notebook on the desk. She walked over to her closet, pulled down a heavy plastic storage bin from the top shelf, and began violently dragging every spare blanket, heavy quilt, and oversized hoodie she could find out of the depths of the wardrobe. She piled them onto the corner of their bed.

Donald watched her build the barricade of fabric. He reached out with a stiff, uncoordinated hand, his fingers brushing against the edge of a heavy fleece blanket.

Mary stopped. She knelt down beside the mattress, resting her hands firmly over his to provide that steady, grounding pressure.

"I don't care what they do downstairs," Mary promised him, her voice dropping into a fierce, unbreakable vow. "If the power goes out, I am not leaving this room. I will keep you warm. You are not going to freeze in this house, Donald. I've got you."

A brilliant, undeniable wave of joy crashed through Donald's chest, entirely overriding the grim weather report. His hands fluttered rapidly against the quilt, and he let out his signature happy squeal—a bright, ascending, high-pitched vocalization that cut beautifully through the heavy tension in the room. He leaned his head forward, bumping his forehead gently against her shoulder in a clumsy but profoundly trusting embrace.

Mary let out a watery, relieved laugh, wrapping her arms around his neck and holding him tight. The storm was coming, but as long as they had each other, they would survive it.

## Chapter 3: The Ice – Thursday, February 11, 2021

### Part 1: The Video Link – 7:00 AM

The harsh, digital buzz of the alarm clock sliced through the quiet of the bedroom.

Donald flinched, his eyes squeezing shut against the sudden noise. Beside him, Mary groaned, blindly reaching out to slam her hand against the snooze button. She rolled over, grabbing her phone from the nightstand, her eyes squinting against the bright screen.

Donald watched the tension instantly leave her shoulders. A wide, triumphant smile broke across her face.

"Well, look at that," Mary whispered, turning the phone so Donald could see the screen. It was an automated text message from the school district.

*PROSPER ISD ALERT: All campuses closed Thursday, Feb 11 due to hazardous winter road conditions. All classes canceled.*

Donald let out a bright, relieved vocalization. The grueling gauntlet of the high school hallways, the blinding cafeteria lights, and the freezing wait at the bus stop were officially canceled.

Mary threw the heavy quilt off and walked over to the bedroom window, pulling back the blinds. Donald craned his neck to look. The Texas morning wasn't a pristine, fluffy white wonderland. It was a bleak, bruised gray. A relentless sheet of freezing rain was actively glazing the sprawling concrete driveway below, encasing the manicured oak trees in a thick, dangerous layer of solid ice.

"Nope," Mary announced definitively, dropping the blinds. She walked back over to the mattress. "We are not doing zippers and denim today. Executive decision. Pajamas all day."

Donald’s hands fluttered against his thighs in absolute agreement. Staying in his ultra-soft, tagless thermal sleep pants was a massive sensory victory.

The morning routine was adjusted, but the physical reality of the house remained. Mary still had to safely transport him downstairs to eat. Donald braced his core as she wrapped her arms under his shoulders, enduring the agonizing, vulnerable carry down the sweeping McMansion staircase.

When they reached the bottom, the noise hit them like a physical wall.

The "snow day" had unleashed absolute chaos in the lower half of the house. Mary’s two younger brothers were sprawled across the living room couches, having dragged a mountain of blankets down from their rooms. They were violently mashing the controllers of the family's high-end entertainment console, screaming at the top of their lungs as digital race cars exploded in high-definition bursts of flashing light and aggressive bass across the massive television screen.

The sensory assault was instantaneous. Donald squeezed his eyes shut, his rigid shoulders pulling up toward his ears. The flashing lights felt like strobe lasers hitting his optic nerve, and the overlapping, aggressive shouting of the brothers made his chest tight with panic. He let out a sharp, distressed hum, his breathing hitching in his throat.

Mary didn't even attempt to negotiate. She didn't yell at her brothers to turn the volume down, knowing their father would just take their side anyway.

"Change of plans," Mary muttered, instantly grabbing the worn handles of his manual wheelchair. She spun him around, entirely bypassing the kitchen. She hauled him right back to the base of the stairs, scooped him into a flawless, secure carry, and marched him straight back up to the absolute sanctuary of their bedroom.

The moment the heavy bedroom door clicked shut, the shrieking audio of the video game was severed. Donald took a deep, shuddering breath, the rigid spasticity in his limbs slowly melting away as the quiet of the room wrapped around him.

Mary brought up a tray of dry cereal and a glass of water a few minutes later, refusing to subject him to the warzone downstairs. Once he was fed and comfortable on the edge of the bed, Mary walked over to her desk and booted up her computer.

Donald watched her launch the secure, open-source encrypted video client they used exclusively for the Meridian City backchannel.

It was barely 7:30 AM in Texas, which meant it was an agonizingly early 5:30 AM on the West Coast. Mary network-linked exactly as she had for months, expecting a quiet email or a brief chat. Instead, the incoming connection chime was already ringing brightly through the computer speakers. They were calling *her*.
