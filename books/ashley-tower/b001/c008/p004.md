# Part 4: The Siege – Tuesday Afternoon

The rest of Tuesday didn't feel like a birthday. It felt like a siege.

There were no text messages from Mary’s friends flooding her dead phone. There was no secret dinner out, no cake, and no coordinated extraction message from Ashley. The digital isolation was absolute, leaving them trapped in a freezing, silent bubble under the blankets.

The hours crawled by with agonizing slowness. To pass the time and keep their minds off the biting cold, Mary softly recounted the plan, whispering it into the dark space between them like a prayer. She reminded Donald that the Vanguard jet was sitting fueled in a hangar. She talked about what the Pacific Northwest might look like, and what it would feel like to finally meet Ashley.

And most importantly, she reminded him of the math.

"You're eighteen," Mary whispered, her thumb gently tracing the back of his stiff, freezing hand. "It doesn't matter that we're stuck in this bed. The clock still ticked. As of midnight, he doesn't own you anymore, Donald. You're a legal adult. We just have to wait for the ice to break, and then we walk."

Donald squeezed her fingers weakly, a small, exhausted smile touching his face. He was 18. She was 19. The legal chains were completely gone. Only the weather was keeping them here now.

Between them, the frozen water bottles were stubbornly fighting the thaw. It took nearly six hours of constant, shared body heat before the solid ice finally began to yield. By mid-afternoon, Mary was able to unscrew the cap and tip a few drops of agonizingly cold, slushy water into Donald's mouth. It wasn't much, but it was enough to keep severe dehydration at bay.

While the quiet reality of survival played out in the bedroom, the atmosphere on the first floor was undergoing a dramatic, terrifying shift.

Throughout Monday, the noise from downstairs had been defined by entitled annoyance—loud complaints about closed country clubs, ruined weekend plans, and dead video game consoles.

But by Tuesday afternoon, the McMansion’s interior temperature had dropped into the low thirties, and the arrogance finally broke.

Mary lay silently, listening to the muffled sounds echoing up through the floorboards. The heavy, booming authority in Donald’s father’s voice was completely gone. Instead, she heard the frantic, heavy thud of pacing boots. She heard one of the older brothers complaining, his voice tight and shaking with genuine physical pain.

They were finally realizing they were in actual danger.

*Clack. Clack. Clack.*

Mary heard the repeated, frantic clicking of the electronic ignition switch on the living room’s massive, decorative gas fireplace. It was followed by a heavy, furious kick against the stone hearth. The fireplace required municipal electricity to open the gas valve and run the blower. It was entirely useless.

"They didn't prepare," Mary whispered to Donald, her eyes fixed on the ceiling. "They thought it was just a regular snow day. They didn't stash any food upstairs, and they don't have enough blankets."

Donald let out a low, anxious hum, his muscles tensing slightly. He knew his father's temper. When the man felt out of control or threatened, he lashed out.

"Shh, it's okay," Mary soothed, pulling the blankets tighter around them. She had locked the bedroom door on Monday, and she wasn't going to open it for anything. "Let him panic downstairs. He doesn't know we have the ravioli, and he doesn't know we have water. We stay perfectly quiet. We don't exist until the rescue team gets here."

Downstairs, the father who had mocked Mary for worrying about the weather just twenty-four hours ago was now freezing in the dark of his own multi-million-dollar home, completely powerless to stop the cold.

Mary closed her eyes, resting her head against Donald's shoulder. Let the house freeze. Let the man pace. She had her perimeter secured, she had Donald, and as of today, they were both legally free.
