# Interlude: Radio Silence – Tuesday, 2:45 PM

Across the blacked-out expanse of the Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex, thousands of automated servers quietly purged unsent data.

In normal circumstances, a nineteenth birthday meant a flood of notifications. Mary’s friends, huddled under their own blankets in freezing apartments across the county, were trying to reach her. They typed out birthday wishes, asked if she had power, and begged for updates. But without the municipal grid, the cellular towers were dead. Every single text message sent to Mary’s phone hung in the digital void before violently bouncing back with a red *Message Send Failure* icon.

The digital isolation was absolute.

Inside the heated McKinney hotel suite, that same radio silence was driving Ashley Vanguard to the absolute brink of her sanity.

Ashley sat at the dining table, her phone resting face-up on the polished wood. The screen was completely blank. For the past twelve hours, she and Johnny had tried pinging Mary’s device using every encrypted backchannel, satellite relay, and SMS gateway they had access to. Nothing connected. The house in Prosper had simply vanished off the digital map.

"He's eighteen," Ashley whispered, her voice rough, staring at the dead phone. "He's legally free. Right now. And we can't even tell him we're waiting."

A sharp, agitated sigh cut through the room. Emma was pacing the length of the suite, her heavy boots thudding rhythmically against the carpet. She stopped at the window, glaring down at the solid sheet of ice coating the Dallas North Tollway. As a heavy machinery operator who routinely navigated eighty-thousand-pound Kodiak plows through the lethal, seventy-inch snowpacks of the North Cascades, the forced inaction was physically painful.

"Let me chain up the tires, Victoria," Emma practically begged, turning away from the glass. Her hands were curled into tight fists. "I can put heavy-duty chains on all four wheels of the lead SUV. I can put the suspension in off-road height, lock the differentials, and push through it. I know how to handle a sliding chassis."

Sitting quietly near the far wall, Victoria Vance didn't flinch.

"The extraction protocol has not changed, Emma," Victoria replied, her voice remaining that steady, immovable anchor. "You are not driving today."

"Victoria, they have no heat and no comms," Emma argued, her frustration bleeding through. "You know my driving record. I don't lose vehicles to the snow."

"And for the hundredth time, Emma, that is not snow out there," Morgan interrupted. The Chief Meteorologist didn't even look up from her ruggedized Quantum OS laptop. Her screen was filled with the raw, depressing telemetry data Marcus had sent her from the NWS desk. "The ambient temperature outside this hotel is currently seventeen degrees. Road salt is chemically useless below fifteen. You could put steel chains on the tires, but they won't bite into that ice—they'll just skate across it. It is a frictionless surface, and it is littered with abandoned, disabled civilian vehicles."

Morgan finally looked up, meeting Emma’s intense gaze. "If you try to push a 6,000-pound SUV through a Texas stack interchange right now, you are going to slide into a concrete barrier. We don't get a second transport if you wreck the first one."

Emma’s jaw tightened. She hated the math, but she couldn't dispute Morgan's physics. She turned back to the window, crossing her arms tightly over her chest.

Victoria stood up from her chair and walked over to the table, placing a gentle hand on Ashley's shoulder. She could feel the violent, coiled tension in the young woman's frame.

"I know it is agonizing, Ashley," Victoria said softly. "It goes against every instinct you have to sit in a warm room while your brother is in the dark. But we cannot compromise the asset by taking an unnecessary tactical risk. If we put Emma on that road today and she gets hit by a local driver who doesn't know how to drive on ice, Donald loses his extraction team entirely."

Ashley closed her eyes, letting out a jagged, trembling breath. "When, Morgan. Just give me the exact hour."

Morgan tapped her keyboard, pulling up the predictive thermal models. "The polar lobe is entirely stalled over us. We have to survive tonight first. Tomorrow morning, Wednesday, the temperature will start to climb. By 2:00 PM tomorrow, we should finally crest above thirty-two degrees. We need a few hours of that ambient warmth to rot the surface ice."

Morgan looked directly at Ashley. "Thursday morning. The second the sun comes up on Thursday, the ice will be soft enough for Emma's chains to bite the asphalt. That is when we breach."

Ashley opened her eyes, staring at the dead phone on the table. They just had to survive one more night.
