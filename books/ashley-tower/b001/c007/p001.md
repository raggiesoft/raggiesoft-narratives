# Part 1: Going Dark – 1:20 AM

Sleep was impossible.

Mary lay fully awake beneath the suffocating weight of five different blankets, her eyes tracing the faint, orange glow of the streetlights slicing through the window blinds. Beside her, Donald was rigid, his breathing shallow.

The house was fighting a losing battle. The McMansion’s massive, oversized central heating system was running constantly, putting out a loud, mechanical roar as it desperately tried to pump warm air into rooms with twenty-foot ceilings. Despite the heater running at maximum capacity, Mary could see her own breath pluming in the dark. The cold was radiating directly through the drywall.

At 1:23 AM, the sound of the house changed.

The low, heavy drone of the HVAC blower motor suddenly pitched down, shuddering as if it were choking on the air.

Mary sat up, clutching the edge of the quilt. Beneath the crack of the bedroom door, the hallway nightlight flickered. It dimmed to a sickly, anemic yellow, surged back to full brightness for a fraction of a second, and then dimmed again.

Donald let out a tight, anxious hum. He could feel the stuttering vibration of the dying motor through the floorboards. His hands curled into tight fists against his chest.

"It's okay," Mary whispered, her voice tight. She reached blindly into her hoodie pocket, her fingers wrapping frantically around the cheap plastic casing of the AA flashlight. "It's just the wind."

At exactly 1:25 AM, the Texas power grid collapsed.

It wasn't a slow fade. It was a violent, instantaneous severance. The glowing orange streetlights outside vanished. The green numbers on Mary's digital alarm clock blinked out of existence.

But the most terrifying part was the noise. The heavy, roaring fan of the central heater died abruptly, plunging the bedroom into an absolute, suffocating silence. Without the mechanical white noise of the house, the sheer violence of the storm outside rushed in to fill the void. The wind howled against the iced-over glass, and the heavy timber framing of the roof groaned as it contracted in the plunging temperatures.

Absolute, pitch-black darkness swallowed the room.

Donald let out a sharp, terrified squeal, his body locking into rigid, painful spasticity at the sudden sensory void.

Mary hit the button on her flashlight. A single, weak beam of pale light pierced the darkness, casting long, erratic shadows across the walls as her hand shook. She immediately swung the beam toward the bed, illuminating Donald’s wide, panicked eyes.

"I'm here," Mary said, her voice shaking as she crawled across the mattress, pulling the thickest fleece blanket securely around his neck. Without the heater pushing air, the ambient temperature in the room was already beginning to freefall. "I'm right here, Donald. I've got you."
