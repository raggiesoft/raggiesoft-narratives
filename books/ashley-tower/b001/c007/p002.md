# Interlude: The Gravity Trap – 2:30 PM

The tension inside the heated McKinney hotel suite was thick enough to cut with a knife.

By Monday afternoon, Morgan’s tactical weather maps had gone entirely red. The power grid had collapsed exactly as she predicted, and millions of Texans were currently plunging into the dark.

Emma stood in front of the massive television screen, her arms crossed tight against her chest. She was staring at the digital route Morgan had mapped between the hotel and the Prosper subdivision. Emma was practically vibrating with restless, coiled energy. She was the Vanguard’s heavy machinery specialist—the woman who spent her winters manning the massive, industrial Kodiak rotary plows, clearing lethal, seventy-inch snowpacks from the steep alpine switchbacks of Ruby Mountain.

"I can make that run," Emma stated, tapping her finger against the glowing blue line of the Dallas North Tollway on the screen. "Give me the keys to the lead SUV. I'll throw heavy chains on the tires and punch a hole straight down the tollway. I can have us at Donald's front door in under thirty minutes. The second the clock strikes midnight, we load him and extract."

Sitting at the head of the dining table, Victoria Vance didn't even look up from her datapad.

"Absolutely not," Victoria said, her voice a flat, immovable wall.

Emma frowned, turning away from the screen. "Victoria, I drive Kodiak rotary plows on the summit while blind in whiteout conditions. I know how to handle a sliding chassis better than anyone on the mountain. Let me go get him."

"You know how to handle *Cascade* winter conditions, Emma," Victoria corrected, finally looking up. Her gaze was sharp and entirely devoid of compromise. "You know how to drive on steep grades where there are snowbanks to ditch into, and where the highway patrol enforces chain laws. The rules of Ruby Mountain do not apply here. What is outside that window is not snow. It is a frictionless, two-inch sheet of solid black ice over untreated concrete."

Victoria stood up, walking over to the television. She tapped the screen, zooming in on the map where the tollway intersected with another major highway, displaying the sprawling, twisting concrete loops of a Texas stack interchange.

"If you take the tollway, you have to cross the flyovers," Victoria explained, her tone dropping into a grim, tactical lecture. "Those elevated concrete ramps are suspended eighty feet in the air, and they froze solid thirty-six hours ago. I don't care if you are the best driver in the Keep. I don't care if you have chains. If a heavy SUV loses lateral traction at the apex of a Texas stack interchange, your Kodiak experience won't save you. Gravity will pull that 6,000-pound truck sideways, and a dry-weather concrete jersey barrier is not going to stop you from going over the edge."

Ashley, who had been pacing near the windows, stopped and looked at the map, the terrifying reality of the route setting in.

"And that's assuming the road is empty," Victoria continued, her eyes locking onto Emma's. "It won't be. By tomorrow morning, this city is going to be desperate. You're going to have civilian drivers in rear-wheel-drive trucks with bald summer tires trying to find warming centers. To us, every other vehicle on that road is an unguided kinetic missile. If a local driver loses traction and comes sliding backward down a ramp toward you, you have absolutely zero maneuverability."

Emma’s jaw tightened, but she slowly lowered her arms. She stared at the twisting spaghetti of the elevated highway interchanges on the screen, recognizing the absolute, undeniable logic in Victoria's assessment.

"Surface roads only," Victoria ordered, finalizing the extraction protocol. "We stay completely off the elevated concrete. We take Highway 380 straight across. It is completely flat. We crawl at ten miles an hour, and we manually navigate around the abandoned vehicles. It will take three times as long, but it is the only way I am guaranteeing we make it to that boy's front door in one piece."
