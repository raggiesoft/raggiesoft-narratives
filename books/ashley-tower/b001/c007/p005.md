# Part 2: The Deep Freeze – 10:30 AM

By mid-morning, the ambient temperature inside the bedroom had plunged into the low forties.

The gray, diffused daylight filtering through the iced-over windows offered absolutely no warmth, only a harsh, pale illumination that caught the white plumes of their breath every time they exhaled. Without the forced air of the massive HVAC unit, the McMansion had become an echoing, freezing concrete box.

Mary sat near the center of the mattress, the massive nest of quilts, fleece throws, and heavy winter coats pulled up entirely over her and Donald. Her phone sat on the nightstand, nothing more than a dead brick of glass and lithium. Sometime around dawn, the cellular network had quietly vanished. Without the municipal grid to power them, the local cell towers’ backup batteries had drained, completely severing their only lifeline to the hotel in McKinney. Ashley and Victoria were fully locked out. They were entirely on their own.

"Okay," Mary whispered, her teeth chattering slightly as she forced herself to peel back the heavy layers of their cocoon. The freezing air in the room hit them like a physical wall. "We have to be quick, Donald. Just a minute out, and then right back under."

Donald let out a tight, shuddering breath, his muscles reacting instantly to the cold with a sharp spike of spasticity. His arms pulled rigidly toward his chest, his hands curling into tight, stiff fists.

Mary moved efficiently, ignoring the biting chill soaking through her sweatpants. She slid her arms securely under his shoulders and knees, lifting him from the mattress. The mechanics of the transfer were grueling in the heavy winter clothing, but she managed his weight carefully, carrying him across the freezing hardwood floor to the en suite bathroom.

She left the bathroom door wide open. There were no windows in the en suite, and the pale gray light spilling in from the bedroom was the only way they could see.

Once they had both used the toilet, Mary didn't waste a single second. She carried him straight back to the bed, dropping him gently into the center of the mattress before climbing in right beside him. She rapidly dragged the heavy pile of blankets back over their heads, sealing out the freezing air of the room.

Under the dark, heavy weight of the blankets, Mary wrapped her arms tightly around him, pulling him flush against her side. She hooked her chin over his shoulder, pressing her hands firmly against his back. It was the only defense they had left: trapping their shared body heat within the small, insulated pocket of the blankets and hoping it was enough to keep their core temperatures stable.

Donald leaned heavily into her hold, his breathing shallow but slowly evening out as the violent shivering began to subside. He let out a soft, low vocalization, resting his forehead against her collarbone.

Suddenly, a loud, muffled crash echoed up from the floorboards directly beneath the bed, followed by a heavy, booming shout.

Mary flinched, instinctively tightening her grip on Donald as the deep, furious cadence of his father's voice reverberated up through the vaulted ceiling of the living room below them. She couldn't make out the exact words through the thick insulation of their blanket fort, but she recognized the cadence perfectly. It was the sharp, aggressive tone he used when he felt slighted by the universe.

Another voice joined in—one of the older brothers, whining loudly in response.

Mary squeezed her eyes shut, pressing her cold cheek against Donald's hair. It was almost absurd. The grid had collapsed, the temperature was plummeting toward sub-zero, and the cell networks were dead. Millions of people were currently fighting for their lives in the freezing dark.

Yet, listening to the muffled, entitled rage echoing from the first floor, Mary knew exactly what was happening. They didn't grasp the sheer, physical danger of the dropping temperature. Donald's father was likely screaming because the country club remained closed and his golf weekend was ruined, while his sons were throwing a tantrum because their television and video game consoles wouldn't turn on.

Let them yell, Mary thought, pulling the heavy quilt tighter around Donald's shoulders. Let them pace around the freezing living room and complain about the inconvenience. She wasn't leaving this bed, and she wasn't letting Donald go.
