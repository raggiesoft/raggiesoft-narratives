# Part 3: Sanctuary – 9:30 AM

The heavy, soundproof door of the penthouse suite clicked shut, sealing them inside.

For Mary, the sheer sensory shock of the room was almost paralyzing. After three days trapped in a lightless, sub-zero concrete box, the McKinney hotel suite felt like stepping onto another planet. The massive overhead lights were blazing. The television was on, quietly broadcasting a local news station. And above it all was the glorious, heavy hum of the central HVAC system pumping seventy-two-degree air into every corner of the room.

Mary stood near the entryway, still instinctively clutching her duffel bag, staring up at a recessed ceiling light.

"I forgot what a lightbulb looked like," Mary whispered, her voice hollow and laced with absolute awe.

Beside her, Donald was sitting in his wheelchair, his dark eyes wide as he took in the bright, immaculate room. He let out a soft, breathy vocalization, his stiff fingers slowly uncurling in his lap as the ambient heat penetrated the layers of his clothing. He hadn't felt air this warm since Sunday.

Morgan Foster walked past them, casually tossing her heavy winter coat over a chair. The Keep's Gridmaster noted their bewildered expressions and offered a small, pragmatic smile.

"You're looking at a critical infrastructure loop," Morgan explained, gesturing toward the window. "This specific city block shares a municipal power substation with the surgical hospital two streets over. ERCOT legally cannot cut the power to this grid sector without killing patients on life support. So, as long as the hospital has juice, we have juice. Take your coats off. You don't need them anymore."

Mary let out a jagged, exhausted sigh of pure relief. She dropped the duffel bag to the floor and began to strip off the heavy, cumbersome layers of fleece and wool she had been practically living in.

Ashley stepped forward, pulling a plush armchair directly in front of Donald’s wheelchair. She sat down, suddenly looking incredibly small and nervous despite the absolute fortress of security surrounding them.

For the past three years, her entire relationship with her brother had existed strictly through the digital proxy of Mary's encrypted phone. Now, the screens were gone. There was no firewall, no father, and no distance. He was right here.

Ashley leaned forward, resting her hands tentatively on her knees. She looked at him, her eyes shining with unshed tears.

"Hi," Ashley whispered, her voice cracking under the weight of a decade of separation.

Donald looked back at her. The exhausted, shivering boy from the freezing bedroom was gone, replaced by a quiet, profound intensity. He recognized the shape of her eyes—they mirrored his own. He understood exactly who she was, what she had built, and what she had risked to get him out of that house.

"I know your spasticity is probably in overdrive right now, and I don't want to overwhelm your nervous system," Ashley said gently, her hands trembling slightly as she reached out, stopping just an inch from his arm. "But... can I hug you?"

Donald didn't hesitate. He didn't just nod; he leaned his entire upper body forward, pushing past the stiff, lingering ache in his muscles, and wrapped his arms directly around her neck.

Ashley let out a choked sob, immediately closing the distance. She buried her face in his shoulder, wrapping her arms securely around his back and pulling him in tight. Donald held on fiercely, his grip strong and absolute. It wasn't a fragile, careful embrace; it was a physical anchor. It was the closing of a ten-year wound.

Mary stood a few feet away, leaning against the wall, a quiet, tearful smile spreading across her face as she watched them. The absolute terror of the last forty-eight hours finally washed out of her system. She had done it. She had kept him alive, and she had brought him to his sister.

Over in the suite's kitchenette, Victoria Vance stood at the granite counter, her sleek tablet propped up against the tile backsplash. Glowing on the high-definition screen was a live, encrypted satellite feed straight from the industrial kitchen of The Prospector's Keep.

Diana Whitaker, the Keep's Culinary Architect, was leaning into the camera frame. Even from 2,000 miles away, wearing her crisp, double-breasted white chef's coat, Diana was completely commanding the operational space.

"Slow down with the heat, Victoria," Diana commanded, her voice sharp with clinical precision as she adjusted her earpiece. "I know your instinct is to give them a heavy steak or a massive breakfast platter, but their digestive tracks are under intense physiological stress. If you flood their systems with heavy proteins and complex fats right now, you'll trigger gastric distress or worse. We treat this like a refeeding protocol."

Victoria, who could dismantle an entire corporate legal team without blinking, looked entirely out of her depth holding a whisk. "I have the standard hotel kitchenette inventory, Diana. What am I prioritizing?"

"Broth and simple carbohydrates," Diana said, tapping her finger on her steel prep table for emphasis. "The body burns massive amounts of glucose during severe hypothermia from shivering. We need to replenish glycogen stores instantly without overtaxing the pancreas. Take that container of organic chicken bone broth from the fridge. Heat it until it's warm—not boiling. If it's too hot, it will shock their muted oral nerves. Add a pinch of sea salt for electrolytes."

Victoria carefully poured the broth into a saucepan, keeping one eye on the tablet.

"Good. Now look in the pantry. Find the sourdough bread," Diana continued, her advanced culinary chemistry background seamlessly driving the menu. "Sourdough is pre-fermented, meaning the wild yeasts have already broken down the complex starches. It's incredibly easy on a starved stomach. Toast it lightly—just enough to give it structure, but keep the interior soft. Spread a thin layer of salted butter. We need real, easily absorbable lipids to help jumpstart their metabolic heat generation, but keep it light."

"And for fluids?" Victoria asked, turning the burner to low.

"Hot, heavily sweetened black tea," Diana instructed firmly. "The glucose in the sugar will hit their bloodstream within minutes, and the warm liquid will radiate thermal energy directly from the stomach to the thoracic core. Do not give them dairy yet. Keep the milk out of it until tomorrow."

"Understood, Chef," Victoria said, a subtle, appreciative smirk playing on her lips.

"Go feed our family, Victoria. I'll hold the line up here," Diana said with a warm, supportive nod before the video feed cut to black.

A few minutes later, Victoria emerged from the kitchenette carrying a massive, steaming room-service tray. The rich, clean aroma of warm chicken broth and toasted sourdough hit Mary and Donald simultaneously, drawing an immediate, visceral reaction from their starving bodies.

Victoria set the tray down on the heavy wooden dining table. There was no freezing canned food here. There were steaming bowls of clear, golden broth, perfectly toasted sourdough bread with melting butter, and a large carafe of hot, sweet tea.

"Your core temperatures are stabilized, but your bodies have burned through massive amounts of caloric energy just trying to stay alive," Victoria stated, her tone shifting seamlessly from legal executioner to authoritative caretaker. "Diana just ran me through the chemistry of your first meal. You are both going to sit at this table. You are going to eat this food, and you are going to drink liquids that are not frozen solid. And then, you are both going to sleep for fourteen hours."

Donald pulled back from the hug, looking from the steaming food to his sister, a massive, genuine smile breaking across his face.

Ashley wiped the tears from her cheeks, laughing softly as she stood up and grabbed the handles of his wheelchair. "Come on. Let's get some real food."
