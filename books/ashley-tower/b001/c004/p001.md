# Interlude: The Vanguard Lands – 10:15 AM

The private jet hit the tarmac at McKinney National Airport with a heavy, violent shudder.

Outside the thick double-paned windows, the Texas sky was a bruised, oppressive gray. Freezing rain whipped across the runway in horizontal sheets, instantly glazing the concrete in a treacherous layer of solid ice. The jet’s massive engines roared as the thrust reversers engaged, fighting for grip against the slick tarmac before finally bringing the massive aircraft to a safe, controlled halt.

In the cabin, Victoria Vance unbuckled her harness, her expression entirely unreadable. Across the aisle, Ashley Sterling let out a slow, tightly controlled exhale. The sheer danger of the storm was no longer abstract data on a screen; it was physically battering the fuselage.

"Get it inside," Victoria ordered the flight crew the moment the cabin door unsealed. She didn't care about the exorbitant private aviation fees. "Do not leave this airframe on the ramp. Get it towed into the heated hangar before the control surfaces freeze solid. We need it ready to fly the second we have a weather window."

The extraction team moved with flawless, practiced precision. A convoy of pre-arranged, heavy-duty SUVs with winter-rated tires was waiting just inside the private gates. They loaded Johnny's manual wheelchair into the lead vehicle and carefully transferred him out of the freezing wind, Ashley and the rest of the team filing in closely behind.

The drive to the hotel was a white-knuckle crawl. The roads were already beginning to fail, littered with abandoned sedans that had spun out on the accumulating ice.

When they finally pulled under the covered awning of the luxury hotel—situated intentionally in the immediate shadow of the Baylor Scott & White Medical Center—Victoria handled the check-in with ruthless efficiency.

"The Vanguard block," Victoria told the concierge, sliding a corporate card across the marble counter. "And confirm the primary suite is fully accessible. Roll-in shower, zero-clearance thresholds, and heavy-duty environmental controls."

"Of course, Ms. Vance," the clerk nodded quickly. "We have the accessible suite prepared for your brother."

"It's not just for him," Ashley interjected quietly, stepping up beside Victoria. She exchanged a grim look with the older woman. "If we execute the extraction on Donald's birthday and this ice grounds the jet, we can't risk keeping Donald in a standard room. We need a secure, fully accessible perimeter right here until the airspace clears."

Once the team was secured in the sprawling, top-floor suite, the immediate priority shifted from logistics to intelligence.

Morgan Foster didn't bother unpacking her luggage. The Lead Industrial Engineer walked straight to the suite's large dining table, pulled a thick, ruggedized laptop from her tactical bag, and powered it up. The sleek boot screen of Quantum OS 10 Pro flashed for a fraction of a second before resolving into a highly customized, dual-pane desktop.

Morgan ran a video cable to the suite's massive flat-screen television, mirroring her display. She tapped a few keys, bypassing the generic weather channels, and pulled raw, unfiltered telemetry directly from the National Weather Service's Fort Worth office.

Ashley, Victoria, and Johnny gathered around the table, staring at the television. The screen was dominated by a massive, deep purple blob pushing violently down from the Dakotas, swallowing the entire state of Texas.

"Alright, listen up," Morgan said, her voice cutting through the quiet of the room. She didn't sound like a local news anchor trying to keep people calm. She sounded like an expert meteorologist looking at a catastrophe.

"The local news down here is treating this like an extended snow day," Morgan explained, gesturing to the brutal purple gradient covering the map. "They are dead wrong. This isn't a winter storm. It is a collapsed lobe of the polar vortex. It's stalling directly over the state, and the ground temperatures are going to plummet into the single digits. With the wind chill, we are looking at sub-zero survivability by Sunday night."

Ashley crossed her arms, her jaw tight. "So the roads freeze. They stay inside and turn on the heat. What's the critical failure point?"

Morgan tapped a key, and the radar shifted, replacing the weather map with a schematic of the power grid and its primary natural gas supply lines.

"The infrastructure," Morgan said bluntly, translating the complex industrial data into plain English. "Texas runs its power plants on natural gas. But unlike Washington, their wellheads and pipelines aren't winterized. They're entirely exposed to the elements. The moisture inside those pipes is going to freeze solid. The fuel supply to the power plants will literally choke to death."

Morgan looked away from the screen, meeting Ashley's eyes.

"By late Sunday night, the grid won't just struggle," Morgan warned, her tone absolute. "It will catastrophically trip offline. Millions of people are going to lose power, and because the houses down here aren't insulated for this kind of thermal drop, the interior temperatures are going to plunge to freezing within hours."

Ashley stared at the map, the terrifying reality of Donald's situation crystallizing in her mind.

"Because we are on the hospital's grid, this hotel will keep its power," Victoria noted, her voice low and steady. "But Donald's house won't."

"No," Ashley whispered, her hands balling into fists at her sides as she looked at the projected timeline. "He's going to be trapped in the dark with a man who won't even buy him a heavy winter coat."

"And it gets worse," Morgan added quietly. She minimized the browser window and launched a locally hosted, open-source atmospheric modeling suite, preferring to crunch the raw datasets entirely offline rather than relying on the lagging public web servers. "When the grid fails, the municipal water pumps fail with it. And shortly after that, the cellular networks. Within twenty-four to forty-eight hours of the blackouts starting, the backup batteries on the local cell sites will drain. He won't just be freezing. He'll be completely cut off from the outside world."

Ashley turned away from the television, pacing the length of the suite. The air in the room suddenly felt heavy, suffocating. She stopped at the floor-to-ceiling window, looking down at the iced-over, treacherous streets of McKinney.

"So we go now," Ashley said, her voice dropping into a hard, dangerous register. She turned back to Victoria. "We put chains on the tires. We take the heavy SUVs, we drive to Prosper, and we breach the house today. I don't care about the ice."

"The ice isn't what's stopping us, Ashley," Victoria replied, her voice remaining calm, steady, and immovable. She sat at the head of the dining table, her hands folded neatly over her phone. "The law is."

Ashley’s jaw tightened. "He is in imminent physical danger."

"And he is still seventeen years old," Victoria countered firmly, refusing to let emotion override strategy. "His father retains absolute, undisputed legal custody. If we put chains on the tires, drive to that house, and forcibly remove Donald today, it is not a rescue. It is a federal crime."

Johnny shifted in his manual wheelchair, leaning forward to reach the dedicated AAC tablet mounted to his armrest. His fingers tapped rapidly across the glass screen, the device's clear, synthesized voice cutting cleanly through the tension in the room.

*"She's right,"* Johnny's AAC broadcasted. *"If we hit that house right now, his dad doesn't even have to fight us. He just picks up the phone and calls the Prosper police. We get intercepted before we even make it back to the hangar. You and Victoria get arrested for interstate kidnapping, the jet gets grounded, and Donald gets handed right back to a man who will lock him down permanently. We lose the entire board."*

Ashley stared at Johnny, then looked back at the brutal purple gradient glowing on Morgan’s television display. The absolute, agonizing reality of the situation crashed over her. They had the money. They had the heavy transport. They had the tactical advantage. And absolutely none of it mattered against the ticking clock of Donald's birth certificate.

"We sit on our hands," Ashley whispered, her voice cracking slightly with the sheer, violent frustration of it. "While he freezes."

"We hold the line," Victoria corrected, standing up and walking over to place a heavy, grounding hand on Ashley's shoulder. "We are fortified. We have the heat, the comms, and the aircraft. But we cannot give that man the legal authority to stop us. We wait right here in this suite. The second the clock strikes midnight on his eighteenth birthday, he is a legally emancipated adult. At 12:01 AM, his father's legal authority completely evaporates, and we go get our boy. But not a second sooner."
