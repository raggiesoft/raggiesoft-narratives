# Part 3: The Inventory – 6:30 PM

Dinner in the McMansion was always a loud, abrasive affair. Downstairs in the sprawling, aggressively lit kitchen, Donald’s father and Mary’s older brothers dominated the massive granite island, their booming voices echoing off the vaulted ceilings. They were loudly debating the upcoming weekend's football schedule, completely ignoring the television playing quietly in the corner of the room.

Mary sat beside Donald’s wheelchair, her attention split between carefully helping him eat and watching the screen.

The local evening news broadcast had returned from commercial, and the tone of the meteorologist had entirely shifted. The cheerful, dismissive banter from the night before was gone. The weatherman looked genuinely stressed, pointing to a massive, dark purple mass dominating the digital map of the Midwest, driving straight down like a spear into the heart of Texas.

"We cannot stress this enough," the meteorologist warned, his voice tight. "The long-range computer models are locking in. This piece of the polar vortex is not breaking up. We are looking at a historic, sustained freeze. Temperatures will plunge into the twenties by the weekend, and by Monday, we are tracking single digits. I urge everyone to wrap your exterior pipes, check on your neighbors, and prepare for hard winter conditions."

"Turn that garbage off," Donald's father scoffed around a mouthful of steak, waving his fork at the screen. "It's Texas. The news always panics for ratings. The ground is too warm for anything to stick for more than an hour."

Mary didn't say a word. She wiped Donald's mouth with a napkin, her jaw set tight. She looked at Donald, whose dark eyes were already fixed on her. He had heard the meteorologist. He understood the math of single digits.

"Alright, we're done here," Mary announced quietly, standing up and unlocking the brakes on his manual chair. She didn't wait to be excused. She navigated him away from the island, hauling him back up the sweeping, curved staircase to the safety of their room.

The second the bedroom door clicked shut, the abrasive noise of the house was severed.

Mary transferred Donald to the edge of the queen mattress, making sure he was comfortably propped against the pillows. Then, she walked directly to her closet. She didn't turn on the TV. She didn't pull out her phone to text her friends about the birthday party.

She pulled her worn, spiral-bound notebook from her bedside drawer, flipped to a blank page, and grabbed a pen.

Donald watched her, his hands resting quietly on his thighs. He let out a soft, inquisitive hum, his head tilting slightly as he tracked her anxious movements.

"Single digits, Donald," Mary whispered, looking back at him, her eyes wide with a sudden, fierce realization. "He thinks it's a joke. He's not going to do anything."

She started to write. *Water. Blankets. Flashlights.*

Mary stopped, staring at the paper. Her pen hovered over the lines as a cold, terrifying wave of clarity washed over her. She mentally walked through the sprawling, opulent rooms of the McMansion. They had massive, vaulted ceilings that trapped all the heat twenty feet in the air. They had an ornamental gas fireplace that barely radiated warmth. They drove gas-guzzling SUVs, but they didn't own a single piece of actual winter survival gear. There was no chopped firewood. There was no backup generator. There wasn't even a stockpile of non-perishable food in the designer pantry.

If the grid failed—if the heaters keeping the massive house warm suddenly shut off—the McMansion wasn't a fortress. It was an icebox.

Mary looked up from the notebook, her gaze locking onto Donald. Because of his cerebral palsy and lack of mobility, he couldn't generate his own body heat by moving around. His nervous system was incredibly vulnerable to temperature drops. If the house froze, his body would start shutting down long before anyone else's.

And his father wouldn't lift a finger to help him.

Mary dropped the notebook on the desk. She walked over to her closet, pulled down a heavy plastic storage bin from the top shelf, and began violently dragging every spare blanket, heavy quilt, and oversized hoodie she could find out of the depths of the wardrobe. She piled them onto the corner of their bed.

Donald watched her build the barricade of fabric. He reached out with a stiff, uncoordinated hand, his fingers brushing against the edge of a heavy fleece blanket.

Mary stopped. She knelt down beside the mattress, resting her hands firmly over his to provide that steady, grounding pressure.

"I don't care what they do downstairs," Mary promised him, her voice dropping into a fierce, unbreakable vow. "If the power goes out, I am not leaving this room. I will keep you warm. You are not going to freeze in this house, Donald. I've got you."

A brilliant, undeniable wave of joy crashed through Donald's chest, entirely overriding the grim weather report. His hands fluttered rapidly against the quilt, and he let out his signature happy squeal—a bright, ascending, high-pitched vocalization that cut beautifully through the heavy tension in the room. He leaned his head forward, bumping his forehead gently against her shoulder in a clumsy but profoundly trusting embrace.

Mary let out a watery, relieved laugh, wrapping her arms around his neck and holding him tight. The storm was coming, but as long as they had each other, they would survive it.
