Morning sunlight pushed through the plastic blinds in thin, uneven stripes, cutting across the motel room like a barcode. The neon sign outside had finally flickered off, leaving only the distant hum of highway traffic and the muffled plumbing noises from the room next door.

On the bed closest to the TV, the triplets were already awake, bowls of off‑brand cereal balanced on their knees. A brightly colored cartoon channel played softly — slapstick sound effects, exaggerated voices, and the kind of chaotic energy that only Saturday mornings could deliver.

Amanda woke with a stiff neck and a highlighter stuck to her cheek.

She groaned, peeled herself off the desk, and blinked at the room.

Drake spotted her first.

“There she is,” he announced dramatically. “The Guardian of the Remote.”

Ashleigh snorted cereal milk out her nose. Jessika giggled so hard she nearly dropped her bowl.

Amanda rubbed her face. “What are you talking about?”

“Oh, you know,” Drake said, putting on a mock‑stern voice. “*‘That’s enough TV for tonight.’*”

Jessika chimed in, pitching her voice high. “*‘It’s bedtime.’*”

Ashleigh added, “You jumped up like the TV was about to catch fire.”

All three burst into laughter.

Amanda crossed her arms, trying to look stern, but the corners of her mouth betrayed her. “I was not that dramatic.”

“You were,” Drake insisted. “You moved so fast you left an after‑image.”

Jessika nodded vigorously. “You didn’t even wake up all the way. You just… activated.”

Amanda sighed, half amused, half exasperated. “Well, somebody had to turn it off.”

Drake leaned forward, eyes bright with mischief. “Come on, Amanda. Just once. Let us stay up past eleven on a Friday. We’re thirteen. We can handle it.”

Ashleigh and Jessika nodded in perfect unison — a triplet superpower.

Amanda’s expression shifted instantly. Not angry. Just firm. Protective. The same look she used when reminding them to lock the door or not leave food out overnight.

“No,” she said, without hesitation. “Absolutely not. That channel is off‑limits after eleven.”

“Why?” Drake groaned. “What even comes on?”

“Stuff that’s not for kids,” Amanda said simply.

Jessika frowned. “Like scary stuff?”

“No,” Amanda said quickly. “Not scary. Just… grown‑up. And not appropriate. And I’m not explaining it.”

Ashleigh tilted her head. “Is it boring?”

“Yes,” Amanda lied instantly. “Extremely boring.”

Drake narrowed his eyes. “You’re lying.”

“I’m the adult,” Amanda said, grabbing her textbook off the desk. “I’m allowed to lie if it keeps you three from watching garbage.”

The triplets exchanged a look — the universal teenage *she’s hiding something* look — but none of them pushed further.

Jessika scooted over on the bed. “You can sit with us. Cartoons are on.”

Amanda hesitated, then sat down between them, letting Jessika lean against her shoulder. Drake nudged her foot with his. Ashleigh handed her the cereal box.

The TV blared a ridiculous cartoon theme song.

The room felt lighter than it had in weeks.

Amanda let herself breathe.

She’d deal with the bills later. She’d deal with the studying later. She’d deal with the impossible future later.

Right now, she had three kids to protect, a bowl of cereal to steal bites from, and a Saturday morning that — for once — felt almost normal.

And that late‑night channel?

That could stay dark until further notice.


It was the first Monday of January, and the air inside Room 114 was thick with the smell of microwave noodles and the frantic tension of the Monday night ratings war.

Amanda was at the wobbly veneer desk, her hands pressed over her ears as she tried to memorize the cardiovascular system from a borrowed textbook. On the beds behind her, the triplets were heavily invested in the television.

Drake had control of the remote. For the last hour, he and Ashleigh had been stubbornly defending Channel 12, waiting for the massive, highly anticipated main event. Jessika was just watching for the sheer theatrical absurdity of it all.

On the screen, the two biggest stars of the Channel 12 roster finally stood face-to-face in the center of the ring. The crowd was buzzing. The commentators were shouting. The tension was at an absolute boiling point.

Then, the guy in the red and yellow colors raised his hand, extended a single index finger, and gently poked the guy in the black shirt in the chest.

The guy in black dramatically threw himself backward onto the mat. The guy in red covered him. One, two, three. The bell rang.

Silence fell over the motel beds.

Drake stared at the heavy CRT screen, his mouth slightly open. Beside him, Ashleigh just blinked, completely deadpan.

"Are you kidding me?" Drake said, his voice breaking in sheer thirteen-year-old betrayal.

"That was it?" Jessika asked, tossing a noodle back into her bowl. "That was fake even for *them*."

"That's it," Drake said, his face hardening with absolute disgust. "We're done. We're never watching Channel 12 again."

He raised the plastic motel remote like a weapon and aggressively punched the '1' button twice.

The screen blinked black for a fraction of a second before the Channel 11 broadcast snapped into focus. They had switched over at the exact perfect millisecond.

The commentators on the rival show were screaming in pure panic. In the center of the ring, a battered underdog in a flannel shirt was fighting for his life, trying to win the world title against impossible odds.

Suddenly, the unmistakable sound of shattering glass exploded through the cheap television speakers.

The arena crowd on the screen went absolutely, deafeningly nuclear. A bald, unhinged anti-hero marched down the ramp with a steel chair, swinging wildly. Chaos erupted. The underdog hit his finishing move, the referee's hand slammed the mat three times, and the crowd completely lost their minds as the new champion held the belt in the air.

Drake and Ashleigh physically leaped off the mattress, screaming at the top of their lungs, entirely caught up in the sheer, electric adrenaline of the moment. Jessika was laughing, clapping her hands at the sheer chaos on the screen.

At the desk, Amanda finally lowered her hands from her ears. She looked over her shoulder at her brother and sisters, who were currently shadow-wrestling on the threadbare carpet, arguing over who got to be the guy with the steel chair.

She let out a quiet, exhausted laugh and shook her head, turning back to her textbook.

They never turned back to Channel 12. From that Monday on, it was Channel 11 or nothing.


The morning sun pushed through the thin motel blinds in pale, dusty stripes, landing directly across the queen‑sized bed where the fifteen-year-old triplets had spent the night in a tangled, uncomfortable knot. The air conditioner hummed unevenly, rattling every few minutes like it was debating retirement.

Drake woke first. Or rather, he woke because he was being actively crushed from both sides.

Ashleigh was sprawled across his left arm, her knee jammed sharply into his hip. Jessika was curled tightly against his right side, her forehead pressed into his shoulder, her hair tickling his nose. Drake’s legs—already prone to tight, painful morning spasticity—were twisted in opposite directions. One was pinned dead-weight under Ashleigh, the other hanging off the edge of the mattress entirely, the muscle cramping.

He groaned softly, trying to pull his numb arm free. "Ow."

The sound set off a chain reaction.

Ashleigh shifted, mumbling something incoherent, and blindly elbowed him in the stomach. Jessika, startled by the sudden jerking movement, kicked out reflexively and sent the scratchy motel blanket flying to the floor.

Drake winced, rubbing his ribs. "Okay. This is… not working."

Jessika blinked awake, rubbing her eyes and groaning. "Why does my spine feel like it folded in half?"

Ashleigh sat up slowly, her blonde hair sticking out in every direction, rubbing at the stiff muscles in her neck. "Why is my everything sore?"

Drake tried to stretch, but his cramped legs protested violently. "I think we’ve reached the limit of what this bed can handle."

Jessika looked down at the mattress—the rumpled sheets, the missing blanket, the way all three of them, now teenagers with longer limbs and broader shoulders, had drifted into one impossibly cramped pile in the center.

"We don’t fit anymore," she said, her voice dropping, sounding strangely betrayed by the physics of the situation.

For Jessika, it was a simple, frustrating observation of mass and surface area. But for Drake's autistic brain, the words *We don't fit anymore* didn't process as a spatial problem. They processed as a catastrophic severing of his pack.

Drake's breath hitched. The ambient noise of the rattling AC unit suddenly dialed up to a deafening roar in his ears. His chest tightened painfully. He pulled his stiff legs up to his chest, wrapping his arms tightly around his knees, his knuckles turning white. He started to rock, his breathing turning rapid and shallow.

*Don't fit. Not together. Splitting up. Losing them.* Across the room, Amanda stirred at the wobbly desk, lifting her head from her open LPN study guide. She blinked at the three of them, bleary and exhausted. She saw Drake spiraling and immediately shifted into problem-solving mode.

"Hey, it's okay," Amanda mumbled, standing up and stretching her stiff back. "Jess, you can just sleep in the other bed with me from now on. There’s room. And you two—" she nodded gently at Drake and Ashleigh "—clearly sleep better next to each other anyway."

Amanda meant it as a logical, helpful solution. But to Ashleigh, who was already hovering on the edge of sensory overload from the bad sleep, the suggestion shattered her entire baseline.

*Jessika is leaving. The identical twin is moving away. The three are becoming two.* Ashleigh froze. Her hands flew up, clapping hard over her ears to block out Amanda's words. "No," she whimpered, her voice tight and panicked. "No dividing. Three. We are three. We sleep in threes. No dividing!" She squeezed her eyes shut, her breathing syncing with Drake's frantic, hyperventilating rhythm.

The physical reality of the bed didn't matter anymore. Their sanctuary was being torn apart.

Amanda dropped her pen. The exhaustion vanished, instantly replaced by fierce, protective adrenaline.

Jessika didn't even hesitate. She scrambled across the wrinkled sheets and threw her arms around Ashleigh. She didn't say a word; she just applied deep, heavy pressure, wrapping her identical twin in a tight, grounding bear hug, pressing her chin firmly onto Ashleigh's shoulder.

Amanda reached the edge of the bed and climbed right onto the mattress. she moved right into Drake's space, prying his white-knuckled hands away from his knees and pulling his head firmly against her chest. She wrapped her arms around his shaking shoulders, applying the firm, consistent pressure she knew his nervous system desperately needed to reboot.

"I'm here," Amanda said, her voice dropping into a low, steady rumble that vibrated through her chest and into Drake. "I've got you. Nobody is leaving. Nobody is splitting up."

"Three," Ashleigh sobbed into Jessika's shoulder.

"I'm right here, Ash," Jessika whispered, squeezing tighter. "I'm not going anywhere. I'm right here."

They stayed like that for a long time. The only sounds in the room were the hum of the AC and the slow, gradual deceleration of Drake and Ashleigh's breathing. Amanda kept rubbing firm, rhythmic circles into Drake's back, acting as a physical anchor until the rigid tension in his muscles finally began to melt. Ashleigh slowly lowered her hands from her ears, her fingers gripping the fabric of Jessika's t-shirt like a lifeline.

When the panic had finally bled out into exhaustion, Amanda pulled back just enough to look Drake in the eye, then reached over to gently brush Ashleigh's hair out of her face.

"You are not being separated," Amanda said, her voice quiet, absolute, and unshakeable. "You are just moving three feet to the left so Drake's legs don't cramp. But listen to me."

She waited until she had all three of their eyes focused on her.

"If we ever get out of this motel," Amanda vowed, the promise burning with a fierce, iron-clad certainty. "And when I start making real money... I will buy a king-sized bed. The biggest, most ridiculous one they make. And the three of you can go back to sleeping together in a giant pile if you want to."

All three triplets froze.

Drake blinked, the last of the panic fading from his eyes. "Really?"

"Really," Amanda promised. "I swear it."

Ashleigh’s tear-streaked face slowly brightened. "A giant bed?"

Jessika finally smiled—a real one. "Like… a bed we can actually fit in?"

"A bed you can sprawl in," Amanda said, offering a tired but warm smile. "A bed you can grow in. A bed that doesn’t try to break your spine in your sleep."

Drake let out a long, heavy, relieved breath. His shoulders finally dropped. "Okay. That helps."

Jessika nodded, loosening her death grip on her twin. "Yeah. That helps a lot."

Ashleigh leaned her head against Drake’s shoulder, fully regulated again. "I still want the left side."

Jessika grabbed a pillow and lightly smacked Ashleigh in the arm with it. "You always take the left side."

Amanda laughed quietly, climbing off the mattress. "You’re all impossible. But at least eventually, you’ll be impossible with enough room to sleep."

The triplets groaned, stretched, and began untangling their limbs from the sheets, already bickering about who had kicked whom and whose elbow had done the most damage. But underneath the familiar noise, the shift felt right.

They weren’t separating. They were just adapting until Amanda could build them a bigger sanctuary.


The air inside the Wizard's Keep arcade on the second floor of the mall was exactly what a sanctuary was supposed to smell like: stale popcorn, floor wax, and the metallic tang of thousands of hot, buzzing glass monitors. It was a chaotic, sensory-overload environment for most people, but for the fifteen-year-old triplets, it was perfectly ordered, predictable noise.

Drake, Ashleigh, and Jessika stood in front of the absolute crown jewel of the arcade floor: a massive, four-player linked bank of *Redline Overdrive* sit-down stock car cabinets. The glowing numbers 1 through 4 shone brightly above their heads.

"Expert track?" Jessika asked, cracking her knuckles. "Or are we taking it easy on Drake today?"

Drake scoffed, sliding his long, gangly legs under the steering column of Player 2. He immediately checked the pedals, feeling the heavy, satisfying resistance of the brake and the smooth travel of the accelerator. He gripped the force-feedback wheel, his thumbs resting perfectly on the rigid spokes. "Mammoth Gorge. Four laps. And I'm not using automatic transmission like you casuals."

Ashleigh slid into the hard plastic bucket seat of Player 1, directly to his left. She didn't say a word, just immediately reached out and aggressively slammed her hand down on the four-speed H-shifter, testing the physical clunk of the gears. She hated the beginner oval track. She wanted the sharp, punishing hairpins.

Jessika took Player 3. The Player 4 seat remained empty, its screen flashing the vibrant, attract-mode demo of a polygonal stock car drifting through a tunnel.

"Alright," Jessika said, reaching into the pocket of her faded jeans and pulling out a fistful of brass tokens Amanda had handed them half an hour ago. "Let's go."

*Clink. Clink. Clink. Clink.*

The heavy thuds of the tokens dropping into the coin mechanisms echoed simultaneously across the three cabinets.

Suddenly, the attract-mode music cut out. A triumphant, synthesized brass fanfare blasted from the dual speakers built right into the headrests behind their ears, followed by a booming, digitized voice that echoed across the carpeted floor:

*"RED-LIIIIINE!"*

The screens flashed to the selection menu.

"Manual transmission, hard tires," Drake muttered to himself, his fingers flying across the glowing dashboard buttons. His brain completely detached from the reality of the arcade. The math of the track—the specific RPMs required for a flawless drift, the exact fraction of a second to downshift before the tunnel—became the only thing that mattered.

The countdown timer flashed in massive block numbers on all three screens.

*3... 2... 1... GO!*

The force-feedback wheels violently jerked to life in their hands. The massive subwoofers under their seats rumbled as the virtual engines revved to the redline.

Ashleigh immediately cut hard to the right, throwing her blue stock car directly in front of Jessika to block the racing line.

"Hey!" Jessika yelled over the roaring engines, slamming her foot on the brake pedal to avoid a catastrophic digital collision, the heavy wheel fighting her grip. "Dirty racing, Ash!"

"It's called defense," Ashleigh shot back, not breaking eye contact with the screen. She slammed the shifter into third gear, taking the first wide turn with clinical precision.

Drake ignored both of them. He was already pulling away from the pack. He knew the physics engine of *Redline Overdrive* better than he knew the layout of their cramped motel room. He approached the first sharp hairpin of Mammoth Gorge. He didn't hit the brakes. He simply dropped the heavy plastic shifter from fourth to second, jerked the wheel sharply to the left, and immediately slammed it back up to fourth.

His digital car entered a flawless, high-speed drift, the polygonal tires screeching through the speakers, the force-feedback wheel vibrating wildly in his hands. He cleared the apex of the turn perfectly, gaining a massive lead.

"He's doing the transmission exploit again," Jessika complained, wrestling her car back onto the track after clipping a concrete barrier. "He's literally breaking the code."

"It's a feature, not a bug," Drake yelled back over the booming soundtrack, a rare, genuine smile breaking across his face.

For the next four laps, they weren't homeless teenagers hiding from the state. They weren't wards of their exhausted older sister. They were just three professional drivers tearing through a digital canyon, completely immersed in the roaring, vibrating magic of the linked cabinets.

When the checkered flag finally waved on Drake's screen, declaring him the undisputed champion, the triplets sat back in their bucket seats, their hands still resting on the steering wheels, the adrenaline slowly bleeding out.

Drake let out a long breath, the tight spasticity in his legs momentarily forgotten. He looked over at Ashleigh, who was glaring at her "GAME OVER" screen in second place, and Jessika, who was laughing in third.

It was loud. It was chaotic. It was perfect.


"You guys are doing it all wrong."

Drake, Ashleigh, and Jessika jumped. Amanda was standing right behind Player 3, her arms crossed, watching the "GAME OVER" screen flash on Jessika's monitor. She was still wearing her faded maroon scrub top from her clinical rotation, her hair pulled back into a messy, exhausted ponytail.

Usually, when Amanda showed up at the Wizard's Keep, it meant the tokens were gone and it was time to march back to the reality of the motel.

Instead, she stepped around Jessika and casually slid into the empty, hard plastic bucket seat of Player 4. She held out her hand.

"Give me four tokens," Amanda ordered.

Jessika blinked, entirely caught off guard. She slowly reached into her pocket and dropped four brass coins into Amanda's palm.

"Amanda, this is Expert difficulty," Drake warned, his hands already gripping his steering wheel. "You don't even have a driver's license yet."

"I drive the narrative of this entire family, Drake," Amanda shot back, leaning forward to inspect the heavy, four-speed shifter. "I think I can handle a plastic steering wheel. Put your money in."

A wave of shocked, chaotic excitement rippled through the triplets. They scrambled to drop their tokens into the slots.

*Clink. Clink. Clink. Clink.*

The screens flashed in unison. The booming, digitized announcer voice shook the plastic housing of the cabinets:

***"RED-LIIIIINE!"***

"Okay, how does this work?" Amanda asked, suddenly looking a little less confident as the massive bass speakers under her seat began to vibrate. "Is the right pedal the gas?"

"Manual transmission, Ash! Block her out!" Jessika yelled, completely abandoning any concept of family loyalty the second the menu locked in.

*3... 2... 1... GO!*

The force-feedback wheels violently jerked. Drake, Ashleigh, and Jessika launched off the starting line in a perfect, synchronized roar of digital engines, their tires smoking as they slammed into second gear.

Amanda slammed her foot on the gas. Her bright yellow stock car lurched forward, but she severely underestimated the aggressive kickback of the heavy steering wheel. She overcorrected to the left, panicked, jerked the wheel to the right, and plowed head-first into the starting line's concrete barrier at 120 miles per hour.

A massive, digitized *CRUNCH* echoed from her headrest speakers. Her car's hood crumpled into a polygonal mess.

"Amanda, you have to shift!" Ashleigh yelled, taking the first turn at top speed.

"I am shifting! Why am I going backward?!" Amanda shouted over the noise, frantically wrestling with the heavy wheel as her car slowly reversed into a digital wall. "This wheel is actively fighting me!"

"Because you're in a wall!" Drake yelled, entirely in his element. He dropped his shifter into second, executed his flawless, physics-breaking drift around the Mammoth Gorge hairpin, and rocketed into the lead.

As Drake's car blasted through the first glowing checkpoint banner, the arcade announcer's booming voice echoed over the roaring engines:

***"TIME EXTENSION: Your time has been extended!"***

"I can't get out of first gear!" Amanda laughed, a genuine, unburdened sound that the triplets hadn't heard in months. She was aggressively mashing the pedals, her yellow car spinning out in the middle of the track while digital opponents flew past her. "Jessika, did you hit me?!"

"Welcome to the gorge, Amanda!" Jessika cackled, trading paint with Ashleigh as they fought for second place.

For four chaotic laps, the crushing weight of the motel, the LPN exams, and the state social workers completely vanished. They were just four kids screaming at each other in a dark, neon-lit room. Amanda was hopelessly, comically terrible at the game. She spent more time facing the wrong direction than she did actually racing, but she couldn't stop laughing.

On the final stretch, Drake hit the tunnel perfectly, maxing out his RPMs and crossing the finish line a full four seconds ahead of the pack.

The screens flashed wildly. The speakers erupted with a triumphant, deafening roar:

***"RED-LINE!!!!!!"***

Drake threw his hands up in victory, leaning back in the bucket seat with a massive grin. Ashleigh crossed second, Jessika third.

Ten seconds later, Amanda's smoking, heavily damaged yellow car finally limped across the finish line in dead last.

"Okay," Amanda breathed, letting go of the wheel and wiping a tear of laughter from her eye. "I concede. You are the superior drivers. I will stick to managing the budget."

She turned in her seat, looking at the three of them. They were flushed, smiling, and completely regulated. It was the exact opposite of the cramped, suffocating energy of Room 114.

Amanda's smile softened. She reached into her scrub pocket, her fingers brushing against a folded piece of paper.

"You guys having fun?" she asked quietly.

"I broke the track record," Drake said proudly, pointing at the glowing initials 'DRK' at the top of the leaderboard.

"Good," Amanda said, taking a deep breath. The laughter faded, replaced by something much deeper and more permanent. "Because I got a phone call today at the clinic. From an attorney."

The triplets went perfectly still. The ambient noise of the Wizard's Keep seemed to drop away.

"Uncle Victor found us," Amanda said.


Marcus Vance, the Chief Executive Officer of the Pebble Burrow Institute, was currently overseeing the construction of a highly complex, multi-million dollar aqueduct system.

It was going terribly.

He leaned back in his top-of-the-line ergonomic mesh chair, scowling at the custom 22-inch Ultra-Res Strategic Display dominating his desk. Beneath the faux-mahogany veneer of his workstation, the massive black computer tower roared. The dual-linked Vertex-Forge 9800 XL graphics array whined like a miniature jet engine as it rendered the sprawling, ultra-high-resolution city of his Urban Metropolis Builder save file.

Down on Level B2 of the facility, isolated from the noise and rust of the actual military contractors, Vance’s world was perfect. He took a sip of his double espresso. To his left, a reinforced interior window offered a pristine view down into the Level B3 executive gym, though he hadn't used the treadmills in months. To his right, his desk was littered with ignored manila folders—including the overdue maintenance logs for the deep-earth drill currently suspended miles below Level B4.

The drill could wait. His virtual citizens were out of water.

Vance reached for his mouse just as a soft, melodic ping echoed through the suite. He frowned. That was the arrival chime for the private, gold-painted elevator. Nobody was scheduled to come down from the B1 lobby.

The polished elevator doors slid open. Vance expected to see his assistant. Instead, six federal agents in sharp, dark windbreakers stepped out onto his faux-expensive wood floor.

"Marcus Vance?" the lead agent barked, flashing a silver badge. "You are under arrest for federal embezzlement, wire fraud, and misappropriation of classified government funds."

Vance froze. His hand hovered inches from the mouse. "This is a restricted black site. You can't—"

"Hands on the desk. Now."

Panic spiked in Vance’s chest. He lunged toward the bottom-right drawer of his desk—where he kept the encrypted flash drive containing the offshore routing numbers—but an agent was already there, slamming his wrist down onto the faux-mahogany surface. The espresso cup tipped over, sending a dark, hot puddle creeping toward a stack of falsified safety inspection certificates.

The sharp click of handcuffs echoed in the cavernous, quiet office.

"Wait, the buyout!" Vance stammered, his face pale as they hauled him to his feet. "Dr. Thorne is signing the paperwork today! I just need to call my lawyers—"

"Dr. Thorne officially took ownership twenty minutes ago," the lead agent said flatly, shoving Vance toward the gold elevator. "He’s the one who handed us your secondary ledger."

They didn't give him time to grab his coat. They didn't let him lock his workstation. They simply marched the former CEO out of his pristine, isolated fortress. They dragged him into the gold elevator, rode it up to the B1 lobby, hauled him past the massive, heavy-duty blast doors that separated the real facility from his fantasy land, and shoved him into the slow, clunky service elevator.

Minutes later, Marcus Vance was blinking against the humid 2003 Virginia sunlight, shoved into the back of a federal vehicle parked outside a completely unremarkable, rusted maintenance shed.

Far below the earth, the Level B2 executive suite sat in perfect, air-conditioned silence. The espresso continued to drip off the edge of the desk. The cooling fans of the massive black tower hummed steadily. And on the blindingly bright 22-inch monitor, Marcus Vance’s virtual city finally ran out of water, throwing flashing red warning icons across the screen.

The office was a perfectly preserved time capsule of corruption, just waiting for the new owners to step off the elevator.



“Good morning, I am Dr. Victor Thorne, and I’m here to assume ownership of the facility,” he said to the security officer stationed behind the heavy reinforced desk in the B1 lobby. “This is my daughter, Professor Erika Thorne. We’re moving in, and I was told to bring my family. My three nieces and my nephew.”

The heavy steel door leading from the employee checkpoint clicked open. “Dr. Thorne? Hi, I’m Dr. Peter Fratire, the head of facility management. We’ve been expecting you and the Professor,” he said, stepping out to shake their hands. He paused, his eyes drifting past the two billionaires to the four young adults standing behind them with duffel bags. “Ah. The manifest only listed the two of you.”

“A paperwork error, I assure you,” Dr. Thorne said smoothly. “These are the Wilcox siblings. My oldest niece, Amanda, and the triplets: Drake, Ashleigh, and Jessika. Ashleigh and Jessika are identical, and Drake is fraternal. I am their maternal uncle.”

“Of course, of course! Welcome to Pebble Burrow Institute,” Dr. Fratire recovered quickly, dabbing his forehead. He had no idea the previous CEO had just been hauled out in handcuffs, and he certainly wasn't prepared for four extra VIPs. “Right this way, please. I want to show you to your quarters. The previous administration left a rather... extensive living space.”

Dr. Fratire bypassed the massive, imposing blast door that led to the main facility and instead stopped at a small, completely nondescript door that looked like a janitor's closet. He tapped his badge, and the door slid open to reveal a pristine, wood-paneled elevator.

Behind Victor, the physical toll of the long journey was catching up to the siblings. Drake was unusually pale, his jaw locked tight against the deep, radiating ache in his legs. His condition was flaring up fiercely, the exhaustion stripping away whatever resilience he had left. Unable to bear his own weight, he had his right arm slung heavily over Ashleigh’s shoulders and his left arm anchored securely around Jessika’s neck.

His sisters stood flush against his sides, bearing his weight seamlessly. They acted as his living crutches, perfectly synchronized in their movements to keep him upright. It was an instinctual formation; they didn't complain, and they didn't waver.

Dr. Fratire gestured to a set of stairs adjacent to the elevator. “We can take the stairs down to—”

“Elevator, please,” Drake spoke up, his voice tight and strained with effort.

“Sure,” Dr. Fratire replied immediately, his eyes widening slightly as he finally registered the severe wobble in the young man's stance and the way the two girls were physically bracing him. “This takes you straight down to the executive living quarters on levels B3 and B4. It operates entirely independently of the main facility grid.”


The elevator bell rang, and the doors opened deep underground on Level B4.

As the wood-paneled doors slid apart, Drake let out a tight, shuddering breath. The descent hadn't magically cured the deep, radiating ache in his legs, and his knees buckled slightly as he tried to step forward.

Instantly, Ashleigh and Jessika adjusted their grip. It was a fluid, deeply practiced motion. Ashleigh hitched his right arm higher over her shoulders, her hand wrapping firmly around his waist, while Jessika mirrored the exact same hold on his left side. They didn't stagger under his weight; they simply locked their stances and moved forward in perfect, synchronized harmony to accommodate his faltering steps. To them, carrying their brother wasn't a burden—it was just what they did.

“Wow. This is a small space,” Drake said, his voice strained but laced with amusement as he let his sisters carry him out into what initially looked like a modest, if richly decorated, entryway.

“This is only the foyer,” Dr. Fratire smiled, waving them through the next set of heavy double doors. “Please, make yourselves at home.”

As Ashleigh and Jessika helped Drake through the doors, the true scale of the embezzled funds revealed itself, completely stealing the breath from the siblings' lungs.

The main living room was massive, but what caught everyone's attention was the rippling, aquatic blue light casting mesmerizing shadows across the plush, high-end furniture. One entire wall of the room was made of thick, heavy-duty acrylic, looking directly into the deep end of the Level B3 natatorium pool. The water gently lapped against the glass, making the room feel exactly like standing inside the observation deck of a luxury submarine.

Drake’s jaw dropped. He leaned heavily against Jessika to keep his balance, his eyes tracing the massive space. “All this is ours?”

Ashleigh shifted her footing to brace him as he leaned forward, her grip on his waist unyielding and warm. He glanced down the adjacent hall, his eyes widening even further. “Is that... a bowling alley?”

“Yep, a private bowling alley,” Victor sighed heavily, pinching the bridge of his nose as his brilliant, analytical mind rapidly calculated just how much taxpayer and investor money had been wasted down here. “There is a lot more for the five of you to explore.”

“Living quarters? This is a mansion shoved into a basement!” Amanda laughed, dropping her heavy duffel bags onto a faux-mahogany side table. She immediately turned her clinical eye toward the plush, oversized sofas bathed in the blue pool light, already calculating which one would be best for Drake to elevate his legs on.

Dr. Fratire’s pager suddenly beeped at his hip, the sharp noise cutting through the quiet hum of the underground air filtration. He glanced down at the digital screen. “Dr. Thorne, the B2 executive office suite is unlocked and ready for you upstairs.”

“Excellent,” Victor nodded, his posture shifting instantly from tired uncle to the ruthless CEO about to clean house. He turned to his daughter and nieces. “You five, feel free to unpack while I head up to B2 and begin sorting out the administrative mess. "Let's just give him a few minutes," Amanda decided. "He and Erika are busy sorting out the paperwork. We stay right here until they check in. Understood?"

The triplets nodded in unison. None of them wanted to wander out into the silent, cavernous hallways anyway. They sat back down in the living room, surrounded by working technology and perfect lighting, completely unaware that the sudden, eerie silence outside their door was the sound of the world not ending.


The heavy steel doors of the private elevator slid open with a soft ding, breaking the tense silence of the Level B4 foyer.

Amanda, Drake, Ashleigh, and Jessika practically jumped off the sectional sofa. They hurried toward the double doors, expecting to see their uncle looking annoyed about a blown fuse.

Instead, Victor and Erika stepped out looking like they had just walked through an oven. Victor had completely discarded his suit jacket and loosened his tie, his usually pristine dress shirt clinging to his shoulders with sweat. Erika looked even worse; her hair was pulled back into a messy knot, her sleeves were rolled up, and she carried a faint, acrid scent of sulfur and superheated rock that immediately clashed with the filtered, air-conditioned air of the luxury apartment.

"Uncle Victor! Are you okay?" Amanda asked, stopping in her tracks. "The power flickered, and the whole facility just went quiet."

"We're fine, Amanda," Victor said, his voice raspy. He walked straight past the high-end workstation and collapsed into one of the plush, faux-leather armchairs near the pool window. He ran a hand over his face, looking exhausted but fiercely alert. "The power flicker was intentional. We just manually severed the main feed to the lowest sector."

"What happened down there?" Ashleigh asked, eyeing the dirt on Erika's shoes. "Did something break?"

Erika walked over to the kitchen island, bypassing the leftover pizza to grab a large bottle of cold water from the stainless-steel fridge. She drank half of it in one go before turning to her cousins.

"Nothing broke," Erika said, her voice dead serious. "But it was about to. Tomorrow morning, at 0800."
