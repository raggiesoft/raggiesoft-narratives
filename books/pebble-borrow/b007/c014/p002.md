# The Burden of Ownership

Amanda let out a shaky breath, stepping back to lean against the kitchen counter. "So... what now? Do we pack up and leave? Hand it over to the government?"

"No," Victor said, standing back up and straightening his posture. The exhaustion in his eyes was rapidly being replaced by a cold, calculating fury. "Erika and I just spent billions buying this entire corporation and taking it private. We own every rusted pipe, every frayed wire, and every uncashed check in this mountain. We bought it to fix it, and we just averted the worst-case scenario. But if the previous CEO missed a supervolcano because he couldn't be bothered to read a geological survey, I need to know exactly what else he was ignoring."

Erika nodded, picking up her water bottle. "The labs on the upper levels are falling apart. The structural integrity is completely compromised. As the new owners, we need a full, immediate audit of every single room, hallway, and storage closet in this complex."

Victor looked at the five of them. "I need you all to stay here. The B4 and B3 levels are fully independent and structurally secure. Use the computers, watch a movie, or go bowling. Do not leave this suite. Erika and I are going back up."

"You're going back out there?" Drake asked, still looking a little pale.

"We own the Pebble Burrow Institute now," Victor said, buttoning his collar back up. "And we are going to see exactly what we bought."


The gold-painted elevator deposited Victor and Erika back on Level B1, just past the heavy blast doors. Instead of heading back to the abandoned B2 executive suite, Victor marched directly down the wide, sloped concrete corridor that led to the facility’s subterranean motor pool.

If the upper labs were a 1970s time capsule of neglected science, the motor pool was a graveyard of vehicular manslaughter waiting to happen.

The cavernous concrete garage smelled of old oil, damp exhaust, and rotting rubber. Fluorescent lights flickered overhead, illuminating a small fleet of vehicles that were supposed to be used for secure transport, site maintenance, and official contractor business.

Victor stopped at the edge of the parking bays, crossing his arms. "Erika, tell me I'm not looking at our official transport fleet."

Erika walked over to a white, windowless 1990s cargo van bearing a faded, peeling Pebble Burrow Institute logo. She ran a finger along the deeply rusted wheel well.

"I wish I could, Dad," she sighed, crouching down to inspect the tires. "The tread on these tires is practically non-existent. You can see the wire mesh poking through the rubber."

She stood up and walked down the line, her disbelief growing with every vehicle she passed:

The Security Patrol Cars: Two late-90s sedans with cracked windshields, sagging suspensions, and duct tape holding one of the side mirrors in place.

The Heavy Transport: A military-surplus flatbed truck that looked like it had survived Desert Storm but lost the war against Virginia humidity. A massive puddle of dark transmission fluid was actively pooling beneath its chassis.

The Inspection Stickers: Erika leaned in to look at the Virginia State Inspection sticker on the windshield of a beat-up utility pickup. "Dad, this inspection sticker is technically valid for 2003, but whoever signed off on it was either blind or bribed. The exhaust pipe is literally held up by a wire coat hanger."

It was a fleet designed for a shell company, not a multi-billion dollar defense contractor.


Victor didn't say anything. His eyes were locked on the far end of the garage, where an oversized, brightly lit parking bay had been cordoned off with velvet ropes.

Sitting in the center of the bay was the exact opposite of the rusted fleet.

It was a custom, completely pristine 2003 luxury SUV. It was a massive, blacked-out behemoth—heavily armored, sitting on custom chrome rims, and polished to a mirror shine. It looked like a vehicle designed to transport a head of state, or in this case, a corrupt CEO who wanted to feel like one.

Victor slowly walked over to the SUV, his polished shoes echoing in the quiet garage. He peered through the heavily tinted driver's side window.

"Custom cream leather interior," Victor muttered, taking inventory. "Burl wood dashboard. An integrated satellite phone system. And unless I am very much mistaken, those door panels are lined with Level IV ballistic plating."

Erika walked up beside him, shaking her head. "Why would he need a customized, armor-plated luxury tank to drive from a restricted military base to a maintenance shed?"

"Because he wasn't spending his own money," Victor said, his voice dropping into that icy, dangerous calm again. "He billed the Department of Defense for a 'High-Security VIP Transport Vehicle' and used it to commute to his taxpayer-funded bowling alley."

Victor stepped back from the gleaming SUV and looked back down the line of rusted, dangerous company cars. The contrast was physically nauseating. The staff who actually ran the facility were driving around in rolling deatraps, while Marcus Vance rode in air-conditioned, bulletproof luxury.

"Erika," Victor said, pulling a sleek silver pen from his pocket and making a note on his palm. "Add the motor pool to the absolute demolition list. I want every single one of those fleet vehicles sold for scrap metal by the end of the week. Order a fleet of brand-new, safely inspected trucks for the staff."

"And the armored SUV?" Erika asked, raising an eyebrow.

Victor looked at the pristine reflection of himself in the black paint. "Have the mechanics strip out the luxury interior, rip out the CD player, and sell the chassis back to the military at a steep discount. I refuse to have this monument to his ego sitting in my garage."


Victor and Erika were just stepping out of the B1 motor pool, the stench of old transmission fluid still clinging to their clothes, when the heavy main entrance doors to the facility swung open.

A sharp, synchronized marching sound echoed across the linoleum of the lobby. Four heavily armed Military Police officers stepped through, flanking a high-ranking Army officer.

Victor stopped in his tracks, his eyes narrowing.

The man striding toward them wore a pristine, impeccably pressed Class A uniform, his chest heavy with ribbons. But it wasn't the rank that caught Victor’s attention—it was the face. The jawline, the eyes, the exact structure of the man who had just been dragged out of the building in handcuffs hours ago. He looked like Marcus Vance, only hardened, disciplined, and utterly humorless.

"Dr. Thorne. Professor Thorne," the officer said, his voice a deep, gravelly baritone that echoed in the cavernous lobby. He stopped a few feet away, offering a stiff, practiced smile that didn’t reach his eyes. "I am Lieutenant General Peter Vance. I oversee the Department of Defense’s interests at this installation."

"General Vance," Victor replied, his voice dropping to a dangerously quiet register. He didn't offer his hand. "The resemblance is... clarifying."

Erika crossed her arms, instantly putting the pieces together. "So that’s how he did it. That's how a corrupt CEO manages to bleed a defense contractor dry for years without triggering a single federal audit. His identical twin brother was the military liaison signing off on the oversight reports."

General Vance’s stiff smile vanished. The MPs behind him shifted uncomfortably, but Vance held his ground, smoothly transitioning into his diplomatic strategy.

"My brother's... *administrative* issues are a matter for the Justice Department, Professor," the General said smoothly, clasping his hands behind his back. "I am here strictly in a military capacity. I was just informed that the power feed to the Mark IV Seismic Penetrator was manually severed. I assume this was an error during your transition of ownership."

"It wasn't an error," Victor stated flatly. "The drill test scheduled for tomorrow morning is permanently canceled."

"Dr. Thorne, let’s be reasonable," General Vance countered, his tone adopting the patronizing patience of a diplomat explaining the world to a child. "There are over two billion dollars of taxpayer money tied up in the Mark IV initiative. The Pentagon is expecting a successful penetration of the mantle boundary by 0800 tomorrow. Canceling this test jeopardizes national security readiness. I am asking you, as a partner in defense, to restore the power and let my team proceed."


"Partner?" Erika stepped forward, her eyes blazing. "General, your brother parked an armored, luxury SUV in a garage full of rusted deathtraps. He built a two-story aquatic mansion while his technicians worked under failing ventilation hoods. And worse—he completely ignored the geological reality of this site."

"The site was fully vetted—" Vance started to say.

"The site is an active, highly pressurized magma chamber!" Erika snapped, her voice echoing off the concrete walls. "If you turn that drill on tomorrow, you will trigger a VEI 8 volcanic event. You won't just destroy this facility, General. You will vaporize Middletown and bury Washington D.C. under thirty feet of toxic ash."

General Vance didn't flinch. He didn't look surprised. He just looked annoyed.

"There are always acceptable margins of risk in advanced defense testing, Professor," Vance said coldly, completely dismissing the apocalyptic science. He turned his attention back to Victor, stepping closer to try and use his physical presence to intimidate the billionaire. "Dr. Thorne. You are new to the defense sector. You don't want your first act as owner to be a breach of contract with the United States Armed Forces. Turn the drill back on."

Victor Thorne didn't back up an inch. He looked the three-star general dead in the eye, fully aware of the immense power the man held, and completely unimpressed by it.

"Let me explain exactly how this is going to work, General," Victor said, his voice practically dripping with ice. "I just bought the Pebble Burrow Institute. I took it private. I own the concrete we are standing on. I own the rusted vans in the garage. And I own the two-billion-dollar drill currently suspended over a supervolcano."

Victor leaned in slightly, lowering his voice.

"You and your brother used this mountain as a personal piggy bank. That ends today. The test is canceled. The drill remains powered down. And if you or any of your men attempt to bypass my security grid to turn it on, I will personally see to it that you share a federal prison cell with Marcus."

The silence in the lobby was deafening. The diplomatic mask completely fell off General Peter Vance’s face, revealing a terrifying, raw fury. His jaw clenched so tight the muscles ticked beneath his skin.

"You think a piece of paper makes you untouchable, Thorne," Vance sneered, his voice dropping into a vicious, thinly veiled threat. "That drill belongs to the defense of this nation. This isn't over. I'll be back. And next time, I won't be asking for your permission."

General Vance turned on his heel. "Move out," he barked at the MPs.

Victor and Erika watched in silence as the military contingent marched back out the heavy blast doors, the steel slamming shut behind them with a resounding, heavy thud.


To understand why General Peter Vance had the full backing of the Pentagon for so long, you have to understand the lie he sold them.

The Mark IV Seismic Penetrator wasn't just a drill; it was supposed to be the key to the Holy Grail of national security: absolute energy independence. Vance had convinced the Department of Defense that the Middletown anomaly was a massive, stable, super-heated rock bed. By punching a titanium-tungsten borehole deep into the mantle boundary, the military planned to pump water down and harvest the resulting high-pressure steam.

It was pitched as a localized, virtually unlimited geothermal power plant. A single successful test meant endless, clean energy to power the entire Eastern Seaboard and every naval base on the Atlantic Coast indefinitely.

That was the dream that justified two billion taxpayer dollars.

But on the morning of Day Four, the USGS data finally landed on the desks of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, completely shattering that dream. The thermal expansion coefficients, the dissolved volatile gas readings, and the sheer density of the magma chamber proved beyond a shadow of a doubt: they weren't tapping a stable heat source. They were putting a blasting cap on a VEI-8 supervolcano.

The dream of unlimited energy died at 0645 hours.
