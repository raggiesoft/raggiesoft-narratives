# Congress Responds

## Capitol Hill: The Joint Select Committee

The atmosphere inside the Hart Senate Office Building was unrecognizable.

Normally, a hearing of this magnitude would be a circus of partisan grandstanding, soundbites, and divided aisles. But as the newly formed Joint Select Committee on the Pebble Burrow Incident gave the gavel its first strike, the room was suffocatingly silent.

There were no divided aisles today. The terrifying reality that the entire Eastern Seaboard had been minutes away from a VEI-8 supervolcanic eruption had completely evaporated the political lines.

Sitting at the center of the witness table, bathed in the harsh light of a dozen camera flashes, was the Department of Defense's Chief Financial Auditor. Behind him sat General Thomas Harris, looking rigid and exhausted.

The Chairman of the Committee, a veteran Senator known for being a ruthless partisan bulldog, leaned forward and adjusted his microphone. When he spoke, there was no political theater in his voice—only a cold, unified fury.

"We are not here today as Democrats or Republicans," the Chairman began, looking directly at the DOD Auditor. "We are here as survivors. And we are here to figure out how the United States government funded the near-extinction of its own capital."

The DOD Auditor visibly swallowed, adjusting his glasses with a trembling hand. He pulled a microphone closer. "Mr. Chairman, the Department of Defense acknowledges a catastrophic failure in compartmentalization. The Mark IV Penetrator Initiative was classified under a Level-5 Black Budget designation. The oversight protocols were inherently restricted to prevent foreign intelligence from—"

"Stop right there," a Congresswoman from across the aisle interrupted. She didn't yield her time; the Chairman actively nodded for her to take over.

She held up a thick, red-stamped folder, slamming it down onto the dais. "Do not sit there and use national security as a shield for gross incompetence. We have the seized financial ledgers from Marcus Vance. Let’s talk about this 'compartmentalized' budget."

She pressed a button, and the massive monitors behind the witness table flared to life, displaying high-resolution crime-scene photos taken by federal agents.

"Exhibit A," the Congresswoman stated flatly. The screen showed the B2 executive suite, focusing on the massive, custom-built computer tower and the 22-inch monitor. "Marcus Vance used a multi-million-dollar defense grant to build a supercomputer so he could play a city-building simulation game while his actual facility rusted into the earth."

The screen clicked to the next photo: the rippling blue water of the B3 natatorium pool and the plush, submarine-style living quarters on B4.

"Exhibit B. A two-story subterranean luxury mansion, complete with a private bowling alley and an aquatic center, built directly into the bedrock above a dormant supervolcano. All of it paid for by the American taxpayer."

The Congresswoman leaned forward, her voice dropping to a dangerous whisper. "You are the Chief Financial Auditor for the Department of Defense. Two billion dollars vanished into faux-mahogany flooring and high-end graphics cards, and your office signed off on every single invoice. How?"

"Representative," the Auditor stammered, his face flushed red. "The invoices were heavily coded. The on-site military liaison provided sworn affidavits that the funds were being utilized for deep-earth structural reinforcement and thermal shielding. We had no reason to doubt the commanding officer's signature."

"Ah, yes. The commanding officer," the Chairman interjected, taking the reins back. He gestured to the monitors, pulling up two side-by-side military and corporate portraits.

"Lieutenant General Peter Vance. The military liaison assigned to oversee CEO Marcus Vance. Identical twin brothers." The Chairman let out a harsh, humorless laugh. "The Pentagon allowed a three-star general to act as the sole, unquestioned financial and operational auditor for his own twin brother's shell corporation. Did nobody in the E-Ring consider that a conflict of interest?"

The Auditor opened his mouth to reply, but General Thomas Harris stood up from his chair behind the witness table.

"With the Chairman's permission, I will answer that," General Harris said, his gravelly voice cutting through the tension. He stepped around the table, taking the seat next to the pale Auditor and pulling a microphone to himself.

"General Harris," the Chairman nodded respectfully. "You are under oath."

"I am, Senator," Harris replied, his posture perfectly rigid. He looked directly at the bipartisan panel. "The Department of Defense failed. We allowed a culture of absolute, unquestioned secrecy to completely override common sense. Peter Vance wore three stars, and in our establishment, that rank became a blindfold. We trusted the uniform instead of verifying the data. We didn't look at the geological surveys. We didn't send independent inspectors. We handed a loaded gun to a madman and walked away."

The chamber was dead silent. A four-star general taking absolute institutional accountability on live television was entirely unprecedented.

"Acknowledgment is the first step, General," the Chairman said softly, the fury in his voice replaced by a heavy, profound sadness. "But accountability doesn't rewrite history. It doesn't fix the civilian casualties."

The Chairman looked down at his notes.

"The American people need to understand exactly what it cost to fix your oversight error," the Chairman continued, looking into the C-SPAN cameras at the back of the room. "When General Vance locked the military out and turned that drill on, the Pentagon couldn't reach it. The FBI couldn't reach it."

The monitors behind them shifted one final time, displaying three simple, dignified photographs of Drake, Ashleigh, and Jessika Wilcox.

"Three eighteen-year-old civilians descended into a 140-degree furnace to do the job the United States military failed to do," the Chairman said, his voice echoing in the cavernous room. "Jessika Wilcox nearly died of systemic heat stroke. And as of this morning, the medical teams at Fairfax Hospital have confirmed that Drake and Ashleigh Wilcox suffered catastrophic crush injuries. They will be permanently paralyzed from the knees down. They traded their legs to save the Eastern Seaboard."

Several members of the committee wiped their eyes. The tragedy of it hung over the room like a physical weight.

"So, here is how we fix this," the Chairman announced, picking up a newly drafted, thick stack of paper. "Today, this committee is introducing the **Wilcox-Thorne Defense Oversight Act**."

A Senator from the other side of the aisle immediately turned on his microphone.

"Effective immediately," the Senator read, "no classified defense project exceeding fifty million dollars will be overseen by a single military branch. We are instituting mandatory, randomized, civilian-led audits for all black-budget expenditures. Furthermore, any falsification of safety, structural, or geological data by a defense contractor will now carry a mandatory life sentence under federal terrorism statutes."

The Chairman took the microphone back.

"This bill has two hundred and fifty co-sponsors in the House and eighty-five in the Senate. It will pass unanimously by the end of the week. The era of the blank check is over. We are going to tear the defense contracting system down to the studs, and we are going to rebuild it in the light of day. And we are going to do it in the name of the kids who paid our tab."

## What Happened and Why

The Chairman held up a thick, redacted ledger.

"Let the record show the facts," he stated flatly. "Marcus Vance, a civilian defense contractor, and Lieutenant General Peter Vance, his identical twin brother, conspired to embezzle billions of dollars from a classified geothermal initiative. They built a subterranean luxury mansion equipped with a bowling alley and a multi-million-dollar aquatic center, while the actual deep-earth drill was suspended over an active magma chamber."

A Congresswoman from across the aisle leaned into her microphone, seamlessly picking up the Chairman's momentum.

"The 'why' is what terrifies this committee," the Congresswoman said, her eyes narrowing at the Pentagon officials. "This happened because of a catastrophic, systemic blind spot in our black-budget oversight. General Vance was the sole military liaison signing off on his own brother's financial reports. Two billion dollars vanished into a hole in the ground in Middletown, Virginia, and nobody in the E-Ring bothered to double-check the math, let alone the geological surveys."

The Chairman leaned back in his heavy leather chair, his expression darkening as he looked directly at General Harris.

"But we all know exactly *why* the Pentagon looked the other way," the Chairman said, his voice echoing in the quiet room. "The Vance brothers didn't just sell you a drill. They sold you a miracle."

He held up a glossy, classified presentation folder that had been seized from the B2 executive suite.

"The Mark IV Seismic Penetrator wasn't just a drill; it was supposed to be the key to the Holy Grail of national security: absolute energy independence. General Vance convinced the Department of Defense that the Middletown anomaly was a massive, stable, super-heated rock bed."

The Chairman opened the folder, pointing to a cross-section schematic. "The pitch was intoxicating. By punching a titanium-tungsten borehole deep into the mantle boundary, the military planned to pump water down and harvest the resulting high-pressure steam. It was pitched as a localized, virtually unlimited geothermal power plant."

The Congresswoman nodded, tapping her pen against her microphone.

"A single successful test meant endless, clean energy to power the entire Eastern Seaboard and every naval base on the Atlantic Coast indefinitely ," she added, her voice sharp with disbelief. "That was the dream that justified two billion taxpayer dollars."

She leaned forward, locking eyes with the DOD Auditor.

"The military wanted that geothermal Holy Grail so badly, they willingly put on blindfolds. The promise of total energy independence was so alluring that nobody bothered to look at the thermal expansion coefficients or the dissolved volatile gas readings. Because if they had, they would have seen the truth: they weren't tapping a stable heat source. They were putting a blasting cap on a VEI-8 supervolcano."

The Chairman gave a grim, final nod. "You let the promise of a utopia blind you to the reality of an apocalypse."

## Who is Responsible?

The DOD Auditor swallowed hard, adjusting his glasses. "Senators, Representatives... the Department of Defense acknowledges a critical failure in the compartmentalization of the Mark IV project. General Vance exploited—"

"General Vance is a traitor currently sitting in a federal supermax facility," a Congressman interrupted sharply, slamming his hand on the dais. "We know who lit the match. We want to know who built the powder keg. The Pentagon allowed a culture of absolute secrecy to completely eclipse common sense."

General Harris leaned forward, turning on his microphone. He didn't offer excuses.

"The responsibility rests with the military chain of command, Congressman," General Harris said, his gravelly voice echoing in the chamber. "We trusted the uniform instead of verifying the data. The Pentagon failed."

The Chairman didn't let the silence linger. He turned his severe gaze down the length of the witness table, moving past the military brass to the row of civilian agency directors who had been hoping to escape the spotlight.

"General Harris is right to take ownership of the military's blind spot," the Chairman said, his voice dropping to a dangerous, measured baseline. "But the Pentagon doesn't operate in a vacuum. A two-billion-dollar drill doesn't get built without environmental waivers, geological sign-offs, and energy grants. This was a catastrophic failure across the entire spectrum of the Executive Branch."

The Chairman pointed a heavy finger at a woman sitting two seats down from General Harris. "Madam Secretary of Energy. Your department championed this project. You lobbied this very committee for the funding."

The Secretary of Energy leaned into her microphone, her face pale under the bright committee lights. "Mr. Chairman, the Department of Energy was operating on the promise of the Mark IV's yield. Total energy independence. We were told the science was sound—"

"You were told what you wanted to hear," a Senator from the left flank interrupted sharply. "You expedited the permits. You bypassed standard civilian oversight because the military stamped 'Classified' on the folder. You wanted the geopolitical win of domestic energy dominance so badly that you suspended the scientific method."

The Senator shifted his glare to the man sitting next to the Secretary of Energy. "And that brings us to the United States Geological Survey. Dr. Linus, you are the Director of the USGS. Your entire mandate is to monitor the tectonic and volcanic activity of the North American continent. How does an active VEI-8 supervolcano go completely unnoticed in the Shenandoah Valley?"

Dr. Linus adjusted his microphone, his hands visibly shaking, but his voice carried a sharp edge of frustration.

"Senator, we didn't just miss it. We were actively, legally locked out," Dr. Linus stated, pointing a finger at the redacted ledger on the Chairman's desk. "Marcus Vance used his status as a private defense contractor, completely shielded by his brother's Level-5 security classifications, to ban USGS personnel from the facility and the surrounding valley. Whenever my office requested site access to verify the localized thermal anomalies, General Vance threatened to have our geologists arrested under the Espionage Act. We were physically barred from placing our own seismometers."

"And you just accepted it?" the Congresswoman from across the aisle asked, her voice dripping with disbelief. "A civilian CEO hands you a report that says the mantle boundary is a perfectly dormant rock bed, and the premier geological agency in the world just takes his word for it?"

"We fought it, Congresswoman, but we were stonewalled by the Pentagon at every turn," Dr. Linus replied defensively. "The Department of Defense told us it was a matter of absolute national security. We were flying completely blind."

The Chairman leaned forward, narrowing his eyes. "If you were completely locked out, Dr. Linus, how did the White House finally get the accurate geological data on the morning of the crisis?"

"We didn't uncover it, Mr. Chairman," Dr. Linus admitted, the frustration in his voice giving way to profound respect. "The blackout ended because the facility changed hands. The moment Dr. Victor Thorne and Professor Erika Thorne assumed ownership of the Institute and descended into the deep-earth cavern, they knew something was fundamentally wrong."

Dr. Linus opened a slim file on the table in front of him.

"To understand how quickly this situation escalated, you have to understand who made the call," Dr. Linus explained, gesturing to the woman sitting next to Victor. "Professor Erika Thorne isn't just 'highly familiar' with volcanology and thermodynamics. She is a tenured professor of Volcanology at one of the most highly respected public universities in this country. She has authored dozens of peer-reviewed papers specifically focusing on magmatic pressure systems and subterranean thermal dynamics. When a scientist with her specific, irrefutable academic pedigree contacts the Director of the United States Geological Survey on a secure line and declares a Priority-One geological emergency, we do not ask for a second opinion. We treat it as absolute fact."

Dr. Linus looked directly at the bipartisan panel, making sure every senator and representative understood exactly who they were dealing with.

"Within an hour of inspecting the B4 levels, Professor Thorne cut through years of fabricated military reports. She reviewed the raw kinetic and thermal data that the Vance brothers had been suppressing, and she recognized immediately that the extreme ambient heat and kinetic vibrations weren't just anomalies—they were the exact signature of an active, highly pressurized magma chamber. If anyone on this planet was going to recognize a dormant supervolcano sitting under Middletown, Virginia, it was her. If she had not been the one to walk into that mountain, we would never have known the gun was loaded until it went off."

"Professor Thorne didn't ask for the military's permission. She personally contacted my office on a secure line, provided the raw telemetry, and demanded that the USGS deploy an emergency deep-earth mapping team to Middletown immediately, explicitly overriding the previous administration's ban. If she hadn't recognized the volcanic signatures and demanded our intervention, we never would have had the data to brief the Joint Chiefs and the President that morning. She broke the cover-up."

The Chairman struck his gavel once, a sharp, echoing *crack* that silenced the room's excuses.

"And there it is," the Chairman said, looking out at the packed gallery and the bank of live television cameras. "The perfect bureaucratic storm."

He leaned forward, his eyes locking onto the entire row of federal witnesses.

"The military blinded the geologists with security clearances. The Department of Energy was blinded by the promise of unlimited power. And the financial auditors kept writing the checks because nobody wanted to be the one to halt the Holy Grail of energy."

The Chairman shook his head, a look of profound disgust crossing his features.

"Marcus and Peter Vance built the powder keg. But every single one of your agencies handed them the gunpowder. It took a civilian professor and a billionaire to finally open the doors, and it took three teenagers to put out the fire. This wasn't just a military failure. It was an absolute, systemic collapse of the federal checks and balances."

## How Do We Fix This?

The Chairman nodded slowly, respecting Harris's blunt honesty, but the anger in the room didn't dissipate.

"Acknowledgment is the first step, General. But it doesn't buy back the legs of two eighteen-year-old kids," the Chairman said, his voice dropping to a heavy, emotional register.

The entire room fell utterly silent at the mention of the Wilcox siblings.

"Drake and Ashleigh Wilcox are currently lying in Fairfax Hospital, permanently paralyzed from the knees down, because they had to crawl into a 140-degree furnace to do the job the United States military failed to do," the Chairman continued, looking around the bipartisan room. "And their sister, Jessika Wilcox, nearly died of systemic heatstroke making sure they didn't die in the dark. We cannot legislate courage like that. But we can absolutely legislate the negligence that made it necessary."

"So, here is how we fix it. Today, this committee is formally introducing the **Wilcox-Thorne Defense Oversight Act**."

The Congresswoman beside him took over, reading from the drafted bill with sharp, unwavering authority.

"Effective immediately upon the President's signature, no classified defense project exceeding fifty million dollars will be overseen by a single military branch. We are instituting mandatory, randomized, third-party civilian audits for all black-budget expenditures."

She flipped to the next page of the thick document.

"Furthermore, any domestic project involving deep-earth drilling, seismic alteration, or geothermal tapping is permanently stripped of Level-5 classification shields. The United States Geological Survey and the EPA will have unrestricted, mandatory site access at all times. And finally, any falsification of safety, structural, or geological data by a defense contractor will now carry a mandatory life sentence in a federal penitentiary under domestic terrorism statutes."

The Chairman took the microphone back, looking directly into the C-SPAN cameras at the back of the room, addressing the nation as much as the witnesses.

"This bill already has two hundred and fifty co-sponsors in the House and eighty-five in the Senate. It will pass unanimously by the end of the week. The era of the blank check is over."

The Chairman stacked his papers, his expression hardening.

"But let me make this absolutely clear to everyone in this room, and everyone watching at home: passing a law does not close this committee’s doors. This is day one."

He pointed a finger directly at the center camera.

"We are going to tear the defense contracting system down to the studs, and we are going to rebuild it in the light of day. Tomorrow morning at 0900 hours, we are issuing federal subpoenas to the entire Board of Directors of Vance Industries. On Wednesday, we are pulling the financial ledgers of every subsidiary and shell corporation tied to the Mark IV project. We are going to track every single penny stolen from the American taxpayer, and we are going to ensure that the individuals who facilitated this near-apocalypse never see the outside of a prison cell again."

The Chairman reached for his heavy wooden gavel.

"You all have a lot of paperwork to gather before tomorrow," he said coldly to the row of federal witnesses. "This committee stands in recess until 0900 hours."

*Crack.*

The sharp strike of the gavel echoed through the Hart Senate Office Building, officially ending the first day of the most consequential congressional hearing in American history.
