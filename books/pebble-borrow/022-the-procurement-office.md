# The Procurement Office

The regional Department of Defense Medical Procurement Office in Alexandria was a monument to bureaucratic apathy. It was a sea of gray cubicles, beige walls, and government contractors who lived entirely by the rulebook.

Amanda sat in a stiff plastic chair across from Administrator Higgins, a mid-level procurement officer who had spent the last twenty minutes looking at his computer screen instead of at her.

"Miss Wilcox," Higgins sighed, adjusting his glasses and tapping a stack of requisition forms. "I understand that your uncle's legal team set up a specialized trust with the federal government to cover your siblings' medical expenses. However, you are requesting two ultra-lightweight, custom-molded titanium standing-frame wheelchairs."

"Yes," Amanda said, her voice tight but professionally composed. "Dr. Aris at Fairfax prescribed them. Drake and Ashleigh need them to temporarily bear weight on their femurs to prevent severe bone density loss and blood clots. It is a critical part of their rehabilitation."

"I don't doubt the medical efficacy," Higgins said in a patronizing, bureaucratic drone. "But those specific chairs are classified under strictly restricted, active-duty military rehabilitation assets. The DoD does not authorize expedited disbursement of active-duty tech to civilian dependents. The standard civilian review board takes six to nine months for approval."

Amanda gripped the arms of her chair. Six to nine months of her siblings sitting in standard chairs meant their muscles would atrophy further. It meant chronic pain.

"The White House authorized unlimited medical funding for their care," Amanda stated, her LPN training warring with her protective older-sister instincts. "President Sterling himself signed the executive order."

"An executive order guarantees funding, Miss Wilcox; it does not bypass the procurement supply chain," Higgins replied, finally looking up at her with a dismissive, exhausted expression. "I cannot authorize the release of military-grade hardware to civilians without a signature from a three-star general or a cabinet secretary. My hands are tied. You will have to wait for the civilian review."

He reached for a red stamp to deny the paperwork.

Amanda didn’t yell. She didn't threaten to call her billionaire uncle's lawyers, and she didn't beg.

She calmly unzipped the front pocket of her purse, reached inside, and pulled out the heavy, solid-gold medallion.

She leaned forward and dropped it directly onto the center of his desk.

*Clack.*

The heavy metal landed on top of the denial form with a resounding, authoritative thud.

Higgins stopped, his red stamp hovering in the air. He looked down at the coin.

Gleaming under the harsh fluorescent lights of the cubicle was the deeply engraved, unmistakable Seal of the President of the United States. Etched into the heavy gold rim were the words: *Presented by the Commander-in-Chief.*

In Washington D.C., and especially within the Department of Defense, a Presidential Challenge Coin is not a trinket. It is a physical manifestation of the absolute highest authority in the land. It means the person holding it has stood face-to-face with the President, and the President has personally vouched for them.

The color instantly drained from Higgins's face. He looked from the heavy gold coin to the twenty-three-year-old nurse sitting across from him. The patronizing boredom in his eyes vanished, replaced by sheer, bureaucratic terror. He suddenly realized *which* Wilcox family he was talking to.

"You said you needed a signature from a cabinet secretary to bypass the supply chain," Amanda said, her voice completely devoid of emotion, her eyes locked onto his. "I don't have a cabinet secretary. I just have the guy they work for."

She didn't raise her voice. She didn't need to.

"Does this bypass the civilian review board, Administrator Higgins, or do I need to call the Oval Office and let the President know his procurement office is denying my siblings their medical equipment?"

Higgins slowly put the red stamp down. His hand was visibly trembling.

"That... that won't be necessary, Miss Wilcox," Higgins stammered, pulling the paperwork out from under the coin and furiously stamping it with a green *APPROVED* seal. He reached for his phone, punching in a rapid sequence of numbers.

"Logistics? This is Higgins in Procurement," he barked into the receiver, his voice cracking slightly. "I have a Priority One-Alpha requisition. Two custom titanium standing-frames. I want them pulled from the active-duty reserve at Walter Reed and delivered to the Great Falls estate by 0800 tomorrow morning. Yes, tomorrow morning. Make it happen."

He hung up the phone and pushed the approved paperwork across the desk.

"They will be at your residence tomorrow morning, Miss Wilcox," Higgins said, refusing to meet her eyes.

Amanda calmly reached across the desk, picked up the heavy gold coin, and slipped it back into her purse.

"Thank you, Administrator," Amanda said, standing up and smoothing her scrubs. "Have a good day."
