# Dual Paths to Justice

## The Status of a Traitor

In the frigid, heavily guarded confines of the military brig at Marine Corps Base Quantico, Peter Vance stared blankly at the concrete wall of his cell.

Legally, he had not yet been stripped of his rank. Under the UCMJ, an officer retains their commissioned rank until they are formally convicted and a judge sentences them to a "Dismissal" (the officer equivalent of a dishonorable discharge).

However, maintaining the title of Lieutenant General was an empty, humiliating technicality. The very morning the B2 control room was breached by the FBI, Peter had been immediately relieved of command by the Secretary of the Army. His security clearances were permanently revoked, his access codes were wiped, and he was placed in maximum-security pre-trial confinement under flight-risk protocols. He was a three-star general who had to ask a heavily armed Military Police corporal for permission to use the sink.

"Stand away from the door," a voice barked through the steel mesh.

The heavy electronic lock clacked open. Two military police officers stepped inside, holding a crisp, perfectly pressed Army Class A Service Uniform on a hanger.

"It's time, Vance," the lead MP said coldly, intentionally omitting his rank. "Get dressed."

Military protocol dictated that he stand trial in his dress uniform. But as Peter buttoned the green jacket and pinned the three silver stars to his epaulets, the illusion of power was immediately shattered. The MPs stepped forward, pulling his arms behind his back to lock heavy, double-hinged steel handcuffs securely over the cuffs of his uniform. They chained the cuffs to a leather belly belt, ensuring the former commander couldn't even raise his hands to scratch his face.

## The Dead Walk

The heavy steel door of the brig slid open. Four heavily armed Military Police escorts, wearing rigid tactical vests and carrying assault rifles, surrounded Peter.

"Move," the corporal ordered, giving the former three-star general a firm shove toward the corridor.

Peter's heavy black dress shoes echoed against the sterile concrete as they marched him out of the maximum-security block. Because he was classified as an extreme flight and security risk, he wasn't just wearing the belly chain. As they reached the sally port, two more MPs forced him to his knees to secure heavy iron shackles around his ankles, connecting them with a short steel tether that restricted his stride to a humiliating, shuffling walk.

They led him out into the freezing Virginia morning. A heavily armored, windowless military transport van sat idling in the center of the loading dock, flanked by two tactical pursuit vehicles. There were no flashing lights or blaring sirens—just a silent, lethal convoy designed to move the most despised man in the United States armed forces.

They hauled him up into the back of the van, locking his belly chain directly to a heavy steel ring bolted to the floorboards. As the heavy doors slammed shut, pitching the cabin into near darkness, Peter closed his eyes.

For thirty years, he had commanded divisions, controlled billions of dollars, and held the fate of thousands of soldiers in the palm of his hand. Now, he was cargo.

## The Tribunal

The convoy arrived at the secure military courthouse at Fort Belvoir. The perimeter was entirely locked down, guarded by a ring of armed sentries.

Peter was unhooked from the floorboards and marched out of the transport van. As he shuffled down the pristine, polished hallways of the courthouse, the silence was deafening. There were no reporters, no cameras, and no civilian onlookers. This was a closed military proceeding, and the atmosphere was as cold and rigid as the marble floors.

The MPs pushed open the heavy wooden doors to the main courtroom.

The room was vast and imposing. Peter was led down the center aisle, the sharp *clinking* of his ankle chains and belly cuffs echoing against the wood paneling. He was brought to the defense table, taking a stiff seat next to his assigned Judge Advocate General defense attorney—a young Army Major who looked absolutely miserable to be sitting next to the greatest traitor in modern military history.

Sitting directly across the aisle was the lead Trial Counsel, a hardened military prosecutor who looked at Peter with a mixture of absolute disgust and cold determination.

But what truly stripped away Peter's remaining pride was the jury box.

Under the Uniform Code of Military Justice, a panel must ideally be composed of officers who outrank or equal the accused. Because Peter was a Lieutenant General, the Department of Defense had convened a panel composed of four-star generals. Six of the most powerful, highly decorated military leaders in the world sat in the box, their chests covered in rows of ribbons, staring down at the shackled man who had disgraced their uniform.

## The Reckoning

The bailiff, a towering Command Sergeant Major, stood up.

"All rise for the military judge," the bailiff bellowed.

A silver-haired Major General walked up to the bench, his expression severe and unyielding. He didn't look at the massive files stacked before him. He looked directly at the shackled former commander sitting at the defense table.

"Lieutenant General Vance," the judge began, his voice clipping through the room like a physical strike. "You are brought before this General Court-Martial charged under the Uniform Code of Military Justice with violating Article 94: Mutiny and Sedition, Article 108: Willful Destruction of Military Property, and Article 134: Conduct Hazarding the Nation."

The judge leaned forward, clasping his hands on the heavy oak bench, his eyes narrowing with undisguised contempt.

"The prosecution has provided undeniable evidence that you bypassed the civilian chain of command, ordered American soldiers to fire upon federal agents, and actively conspired to initiate a geological event that would have resulted in the deaths of millions of American citizens."

The courtroom was suffocatingly still. Even the defense attorney stared at the table, offering no objection.

"For these crimes against the United States of America," the military judge continued, his voice echoing off the walls, "the prosecution is seeking the maximum punitive action. This includes your permanent, dishonorable dismissal from the United States Armed Forces, total forfeiture of all pay and allowances, and life imprisonment in a federal military penitentiary without the possibility of parole."

The judge leaned back, the silence hanging in the air like an executioner's blade.

"How do you plead?"

## The Civilian Fall from Grace

Forty miles north, in a holding cell beneath the Alexandria federal courthouse, Marcus Vance was experiencing a very different kind of humiliation.

Marcus was used to wearing ten-thousand-dollar bespoke suits, commanding a fleet of private jets, and silencing his enemies with armies of expensive corporate lawyers.

Today, he was wearing a faded, oversized orange canvas jumpsuit that smelled faintly of industrial bleach. Because the Department of Justice had frozen every single financial asset, shell corporation, and offshore account tied to Vance Industries under the Patriot Act, Marcus was legally destitute. He couldn't afford a paralegal, let alone a high-powered defense team.

He was sitting across a scarred metal table from a twenty-eight-year-old, chronically overworked federal public defender.

"I don't think you understand the gravity of the indictment, Mr. Vance," the public defender said, rubbing his tired eyes as he looked over the mountain of paperwork. "They aren't just charging you with wire fraud and embezzlement. The DOJ is pursuing domestic terrorism, treason, and three counts of attempted murder regarding the Wilcox teenagers. The Attorney General is personally overseeing this docket. There is no plea deal. They are going to ask the judge to bury you under the prison."

Marcus gripped the edge of the metal table, his knuckles turning white. He scoffed, his arrogance completely untouched by the canvas jumpsuit.

"Attempted murder?" Marcus spat, shaking his head. "That is absolute legal fiction. I was locked in a holding cell on Level B1 when those kids went into the cavern. I didn't hold a gun to their heads. I didn't order them to crawl into that pipe. I didn't even know they were in the facility! You tell the DOJ to drop those three charges, or we will humiliate them in front of a jury."

## The "Depraved Heart" Reality

The young public defender didn't flinch. He just leaned back in his metal chair and looked at the fallen billionaire with a mixture of pity and exhaustion.

"Mr. Vance, you don't need to hold a gun to someone's head to be convicted of attempted murder in a federal court," the attorney said flatly, opening a thick legal textbook on the table. "The DOJ is pursuing these charges under the **Depraved Heart** doctrine of 18 U.S.C. § 1111."

Marcus frowned, the legal terminology finally piercing his armor. "What does that mean?"

"It means extreme, reckless indifference to the value of human life," the public defender explained, tapping a pen against the table. "You knew that mountain sat on an active magma chamber. You intentionally falsified the thermal data. You built a titanium drill directly over a supervolcano, and then you bypassed the safety grid. You created an active, 140-degree death trap that threatened the entire Eastern Seaboard."

"But I didn't put them in the cavern!" Marcus argued, his voice rising in panic.

"It doesn't matter," the defender fired back, his tone hardening. "Under federal law, if you commit a violently dangerous felony—like domestic terrorism or treason—you are legally responsible for any catastrophic bodily harm that results from it. When you and your brother initiated that drill, you forced an emergency response. Those three kids had to enter an unsurvivable environment to stop the apocalyptic chain of events *you* started."

The attorney pulled three glossy photographs from his file and slid them across the scarred metal table. They were the medical photos of Drake and Ashleigh's pulverized legs encased in external fixators, and Jessika covered in ice packs, fighting off systemic organ failure.

"Two of them will never walk again. The third nearly boiled to death from the inside out," the attorney said, his voice dropping to a harsh whisper. "You created the furnace. You lit the fire. A federal jury is going to look at these photos, and they are going to conclude that your extreme recklessness caused this. That is depraved heart attempted murder, Mr. Vance. And it carries a twenty-year maximum sentence for *each* kid."

Marcus stared at the photographs. For the first time since the FBI had breached his underground empire, the absolute reality of what he had done finally crashed over him. There was no boardroom maneuver that could fix this. He couldn't buy off a jury when the victims were paralyzed teenage heroes.

He was going to die in a cage.

"So," the public defender said, closing the file and standing up. "I strongly suggest you stop telling me what you didn't do, and start preparing for the sentencing hearing. Because the judge isn't going to give you an inch."
