# Amanda Interviews Care Nurses

The sprawling Great Falls estate was quiet at 7:00 AM, but it was a distinctly clinical kind of quiet. Beneath the usual sounds of a massive Virginia home waking up, there was the faint, rhythmic chirp of Jessika’s cardiac telemetry receiver down the hall, and the low, steady hum of the specialized, hospital-grade air filtration system Victor had installed to protect her compromised lungs.

Amanda sat behind the heavy oak desk in the ground-floor office she had converted into the central charting station. The morning sun filtered through the reinforced glass windows, illuminating a pristine, highly organized workspace.

On her left was a locked, temperature-controlled cabinet containing heavily regulated liquid narcotics, sterilized IV lines, and specialized compounding solutions. On her right was a dual-monitor computer system, currently displaying the triplets' overnight vitals and the day's grueling physical therapy schedule.

Directly in front of her, resting perfectly centered on the dark wood of the desk, was a neat stack of six manila folders.

These were the final candidates. Out of dozens of applications for the Private Duty RN positions under Wilcox Medical Management, PLLC, Amanda had mercilessly whittled the list down to six people.

She took a slow sip from her ceramic coffee mug, her eyes scanning the top resume. Richard Lawson. Fifteen years Level 1 trauma. Underneath his was Susan Gable. Twelve years step-down telemetry.

"You're up early," a quiet voice said from the doorway.

Amanda looked up to see Victor Thorne leaning against the doorframe, holding his own cup of coffee. He looked tired but deeply grounded, wearing a simple sweater instead of his usual tailored billionaire suits.

"I need to have the morning meds prepped before Drake and Ashleigh wake up," Amanda replied, tapping a bright yellow sticky note attached to Lawson's file. "And I wanted to review these one last time before the gate clears them. We need to hire at least two RNs today to establish a proper rotation, Uncle Victor. I can't keep pulling seventy-two-hour shifts, and you have a company to run."

Victor walked into the office and pulled up one of the guest chairs, looking at the stack of folders. He knew exactly how much power he had handed his twenty-three-year-old niece, and he didn't regret a single ounce of it.

"The trust has fully cleared the payroll accounts for the PLLC," Victor confirmed quietly. "One hundred dollars an hour, full W-2, complete medical coverage for them and their dependents, and we pay for their professional insurance. You have an unlimited operational budget, Amanda. But remember, you are interviewing them, not the other way around. If you don't like the way they look at you, if they hesitate when you explain the twins' deep pressure needs, or if they brush off Jessika's cardiac risk, you throw them out. We will pay for the absolute best, and we will tolerate absolutely nothing less."

Amanda nodded, her posture straightening. She reached into her scrub pocket, her fingers brushing against the heavy, solid gold of the Presidential Challenge Coin resting there. It was her physical anchor—a reminder that the Commander-in-Chief himself had trusted her to hold the line for this family.

"They answer to me," Amanda said, her voice dropping the exhaustion of the morning and taking on the crisp, unshakeable authority of a Care Coordinator. "I don't care how many letters they have after their name. If they can't check their egos at the door and respect how my siblings need to heal, they won't make it past this desk."

The intercom on the wall suddenly buzzed, glowing with a soft blue light.

"Miss Wilcox," the private security contractor at the estate's main gate announced over the speaker. "I have a Richard Lawson here for an eight o'clock interview, followed by a Susan Gable scheduled for nine. Do I have authorization to open the outer gates?"

Amanda looked at Victor, who gave her a single, supportive nod before standing up to leave the office so she could conduct her business.

Amanda pressed the intercom button.

"Clear Mr. Lawson through, and have security escort him to the ground-floor charting station," Amanda instructed calmly. She opened the first manila folder, smoothing the resume flat on her desk. "Let's see if he can follow directions."

## Richard Lawson, RN

Richard Lawson walked into the ground-floor charting station with the heavy, confident stride of a man who had spent fifteen years running a Level 1 Trauma floor. He was a broad-shouldered man in his late forties, wearing crisp maroon scrubs.

He sat in the guest chair, his eyes immediately dropping to the printed compensation contract Amanda had slid across the desk.

Richard stared at the paper for a long, silent moment. He read the numbers twice. "\$100 an hour," he read aloud, his voice entirely losing its practiced edge. "Paid for twelve hours, but the physical shift is only eight? And you’re fully funding professional malpractice insurance?"

"That is correct, Mr. Lawson," Amanda stated calmly, her hands folded over her legal pad. "My uncle is fully funding the PLLC. We are buying peak clinical alertness and absolute loyalty. In exchange, the medical standards in this house are zero-tolerance."

Richard cleared his throat, suddenly looking at the twenty-three-year-old sitting across from him with a very different level of interest. "Alright. I can work with that. What’s the clinical rundown?"

"It is highly complex," Amanda explained, tapping the top of his resume. "All three patients—my brother and my two sisters—suffer from severe, post-heatstroke dysautonomia. Their autonomic nervous systems are shattered. They require continuous, 24/7 cardiac telemetry monitoring. A sudden shift in elevation or temperature can cause their blood pressure to bottom out, leading to immediate syncope. Furthermore, my brother and one of my sisters survived bilateral crush injuries. They are paralyzed below the knee. When we transfer them to their titanium standing frames to maintain bone density, their dysautonomia makes the elevation change incredibly dangerous."

Richard nodded slowly, leaning back in his chair. The confidence was returning to his posture, shifting rapidly into arrogance.

"I've managed multi-system trauma for a decade and a half," Richard said, waving a hand dismissively. "Dysautonomia is tricky, but it’s manageable. And regarding the transfer protocols, don't worry about the standing frames. I'll take the lead on all physical therapy. I know how to handle paraplegic patients. We'll get them on a strict, hospital-style regimen. I’ll coordinate directly with the Chief of Orthopedics at Fairfax to streamline the orders."

Amanda didn't blink. The room's temperature seemed to drop a few degrees.

"You will not coordinate with Fairfax, Mr. Lawson," Amanda corrected, her voice perfectly level. "The Chief of Orthopedics writes the macro orders. But inside this house, you report directly to me. I am the Care Coordinator. I manage the shift charts, and I approve the daily physical therapy flow based on their telemetry."

Richard let out a short, patronizing chuckle. He looked at Amanda, entirely failing to register the absolute ice forming in her eyes.

"Listen, Amanda... it's great that you're an LPN, and it's sweet that you want to be involved," Richard said, offering a condescending smile. "But I have fifteen years of acute trauma experience. An LPN doesn't dictate a care plan to a veteran RN. I'm happy to keep you in the loop, but when it comes to the heavy clinical decisions, I'm going to run my shift the way I see fit."

Amanda reached across the desk. She didn't look angry. She looked entirely, clinically detached. She closed Richard's manila folder and pulled it out of his reach.

"Your clinical experience is extensive, Mr. Lawson," Amanda said, her voice dropping into a tone of absolute, unshakeable authority. "But your ego is a liability. You failed to read the chain of command explicitly outlined in the job description. This is not a hospital floor, and you do not run the shift. You execute the orders under my supervision."

Richard's patronizing smile vanished. "Excuse me? You're throwing away a fifteen-year trauma vet because of a title dispute?"

"I am denying your application because you lack the humility required to care for my siblings," Amanda stated, picking up her red stamp. Clack. "The interview is over. The \$100-an-hour contract is off the table. Security will escort you back to your vehicle."

Richard opened his mouth to argue, realized she possessed the sole authority over the most lucrative nursing contract he had ever seen, and clamped his jaw shut. He stood up, his face flushed with anger and embarrassment, and marched out of the office.

## The Smoke Screen

Amanda aired out the charting station for ten minutes before the intercom buzzed again, clearing the way for Susan Gable.

Susan sat across the desk, her posture eager. She had already agreed to the chain of command without batting an eye. She had no problem reporting to an LPN, and she had practically salivated over the \$100-an-hour, 8-hour shift structure.

"I am perfectly comfortable executing the orders from Fairfax and submitting my shift reports directly to you, Miss Wilcox," Susan promised brightly.

On paper, she was a perfect fit. But Amanda wasn't looking at the resume anymore. She was breathing in the air between them. Beneath the heavy, cloying layer of floral perfume Susan had sprayed on herself, the acrid, deeply ingrained stench of stale tobacco was unmistakable.

"Mrs. Gable," Amanda began, her tone shifting to a measured, clinical neutral. "On the initial employment application, there is a mandatory checkbox. It explicitly states that due to the severe autonomic vulnerabilities of our patients, we are a strictly non-smoking medical staff. You checked the box indicating you are a non-smoker."

Susan’s eager smile froze. A slight flush crept up her neck. "Oh. Well, yes. I mean, I don't smoke in the workplace. Ever."

"But you are a smoker."

"I just have one or two on my drive home to unwind," Susan backpedaled quickly. "I wash my hands meticulously. For a hundred dollars an hour and those benefits, I will follow any sanitation protocol you want."

Amanda leaned back in her chair.

"Third-hand smoke isn't just about washing your hands," Amanda explained calmly. "The chemical residue off-gasses from your hair, your skin, and your clothing long after the cigarette is put out. All three of my siblings are battling severe post-heatstroke dysautonomia. Their cardiac rhythms are incredibly unstable. If they inhale that chemical irritant while you are assisting with a pivot transfer, it could easily trigger a vasovagal response, bottom out their blood pressure, and put them into immediate syncope."

Susan opened her mouth to argue, but Amanda held up a single hand.

"The clinical hazard is massive, but it isn't the primary issue," Amanda continued, her eyes locking onto the older nurse. "The issue is that you saw the requirement, you knew why it was there, and you lied on a W-2 application because you wanted the salary."

The color completely drained from Susan's face.

"My siblings are recovering from catastrophic trauma," Amanda said softly, her voice carrying the terrifying weight of a guardian holding the line. "If a nurse is willing to lie to me about a daily habit to secure a paycheck, I cannot trust her to tell me the truth if she makes a medication error. In this house, integrity is a matter of life and death."

Amanda slid Susan's resume into the Rejected folder.

"Your clinical experience is excellent, Mrs. Gable, but I cannot hire you. Thank you for your time."

## The Intuition

By 10:30 AM, Amanda had aired out the charting station and was opening the third file of the morning. She desperately needed a win.

David Keller looked incredible on paper. Thirty-four years old. Ten years of acute neuro-rehabilitation experience at a top-tier facility in Maryland. The executive search firm had flagged his application with a sticky note that read: Highly recommended. Spotless background check. Top 1% of candidate pool. When Keller walked into the room, he matched the resume perfectly. He was tall, impeccably groomed in high-end navy scrubs, and carried himself with the quiet, competent confidence of an ICU veteran. He sat across from Amanda, reviewed the \$100-an-hour compensation sheet, and gave a respectful nod.

"The compensation is unprecedented, Miss Wilcox, but I understand why your uncle is offering it," Keller said smoothly. "You want absolute exclusivity and peak clinical alertness. I also have no problem reporting to an LPN. In a high-acuity home setting, having a dedicated family Care Coordinator prevents systemic communication errors. It's a smart structure."

Amanda felt a knot in her shoulders loosen. He was saying all the right things. He wasn't threatened by her, and he understood the stakes.

"Let's discuss the clinical realities of the house, Mr. Keller," Amanda said, leaning forward. "All three of my siblings survived severe hyperthermia. They battle catastrophic post-heatstroke dysautonomia. Their cardiac rhythms are incredibly unstable, and they require continuous telemetry. Furthermore, my brother Drake and my sister Ashleigh suffered bilateral crush injuries resulting in below-the-knee paralysis. When we transfer them to their titanium standing frames, the elevation change can trigger orthostatic hypotension and immediate syncope."

"Absolutely," Keller replied instantly, shifting into a crisp, clinical gear. "For Drake, I know the exact biomechanical protocol. Young men with bilateral crush injuries and severe dysautonomia need absolute precision. If his blood pressure bottoms out during a transfer, I will have the CNA immediately lower the frame while I push IV fluids. Total, rigid clinical control."

Amanda smiled slightly. It was a perfect, textbook answer. For the first time all morning, she thought she might actually be looking at her first hire. "Exactly. And for Ashleigh and Jessika?"

Keller relaxed back into his chair. His posture subtly shifted, the crisp clinical edge vanishing as he seemingly tried to build rapport with Amanda on a more personal level.

"Well, with Ashleigh and Jessika... female patients of that age require a very different approach," Keller said. His voice dropped an octave, taking on a soft, almost hushed quality. "Young girls dealing with trauma and sudden fainting spells are delicate. They need a much gentler, more intimate touch. I noticed Jessika's cardiac monitoring requires frequent electrode adjustments on her chest, and Ashleigh will need extensive assistance with her daily sponge baths. I'm very experienced in building... physical trust with vulnerable female patients."

The hairs on the back of Amanda’s neck stood straight up. A cold, primal spike of adrenaline dumped into her bloodstream, instantly erasing any relief she had felt moments ago.

"You mentioned the deep pressure therapy they use to co-regulate," Keller continued, leaning slightly forward, completely missing the absolute ice forming in Amanda's eyes. "I know you prefer the family to handle that, but I want you to know I am more than comfortable stepping in and providing that close, hands-on comfort for the girls when you need a break."

Every protective instinct Amanda possessed was screaming like a siren.

He had clinically respected Drake as a "young man" with a complex medical condition, but he viewed her sisters as delicate, vulnerable objects to be handled. He was a predator hiding behind a spotless medical license, and he had just shown his hand.

"The personal hygiene and bathing routines for my sisters are handled exclusively by me, or by female staff," Amanda stated, her voice flat, hollow, and terrifyingly calm. She reached across the desk and pulled his file away. "Furthermore, the deep pressure therapy is a strict, neurodivergent boundary. You do not touch them to comfort them. Ever."

Keller’s smooth smile faltered. A flash of arrogant irritation crossed his features. "Miss Wilcox, I am a highly trained professional. I was simply offering to take the burden of their intimate care off your shoulders—"

"The interview is over," Amanda interrupted, her voice cracking like a whip. "Your application is denied."

Keller stood up, the mask of the 'caring provider' slipping entirely to reveal the deeply unsettling man underneath. "You are making a massive mistake. You won't find another nurse with my neuro-rehab credentials willing to put up with this paranoid family dynamic."

"Security," Amanda said, pressing the intercom button without breaking eye contact with him. "Escort Mr. Keller off the estate. Immediately. If his vehicle approaches the main gate again, call the Fairfax County Police."

Keller scoffed, shaking his head, and turned on his heel, marching out of the charting station as two heavily armed private security contractors stepped into the hallway to flank him.

Amanda stood perfectly still until she heard the heavy front doors of the mansion close. Only then did she let out a shaky breath, her hands trembling slightly as she dropped his resume directly into the shredder next to her desk.

## The Mirror Image

Amanda didn't even have time to let her heart rate fully return to normal before the intercom buzzed again. She took a deep breath, praying the executive search firm hadn't entirely failed her.

Brenda Hayes was forty years old, with eight years of dedicated experience in spinal cord injury (SCI) units. Like Keller, her background check was flawless. Glowing recommendations from former nursing directors highlighted her dedication and bedside manner.

When Brenda walked into the charting station, she radiated a warm, maternal energy. She reviewed the \$100-an-hour W-2 contract, smiled warmly, and immediately agreed to the chain of command and the 8-hour shift structure.

Hoping the horror show of David Keller was just a statistical anomaly, Amanda started the clinical review again, carefully outlining the devastating combination of post-heatstroke dysautonomia and the below-the-knee paralysis.

"Let's start with Ashleigh and Jessika," Amanda said, keeping her tone strictly professional. "They require continuous cardiac observation for the dysautonomia, and Ashleigh needs total assistance with her lower-body hygiene and transfers."

"Of course," Brenda nodded, her tone crisp and entirely respectful. "Young women dealing with that level of physical vulnerability and sudden syncope need absolute professionalism and modesty. I always ensure the blinds are drawn, I explain every point of physical contact before I make it, and I maintain strict clinical boundaries while monitoring their telemetry without hovering. They deserve their dignity."

Amanda felt a genuine wave of relief. It was the perfect answer. Brenda understood the clinical boundaries for the girls flawlessly.

"Excellent," Amanda said, turning the page on her legal pad. "Now, regarding my brother, Drake. He suffered the exact same bilateral crush injuries and dysautonomia. His transfer protocols and hygiene needs are identical to Ashleigh's."

Brenda leaned forward, resting her elbows on her knees. The crisp, professional demeanor she had just displayed completely melted away, replaced by a cloying, overly familiar smile.

"Well, identical on paper, perhaps," Brenda said, her voice taking on a soft, almost breathless quality that made Amanda’s stomach instantly drop. "But taking care of a strapping young man who has lost his mobility... that's a very different dynamic. Boys his age, especially athletic ones, get so depressed when they lose their independence. They need a lot of... special attention."

Amanda went completely rigid in her chair. It was happening again. It was the exact same predatory frequency as Keller, just inverted.

"Drake's clinical needs are the same," Amanda stated coldly. "His dysautonomia requires strict cardiac observation."

"Oh, clinically, yes," Brenda agreed, waving a hand dismissively, completely oblivious to the ice in Amanda's voice. "But emotionally? I've found that young male patients really respond to a much more affectionate, hands-on approach. He's going to be so insecure about his paralyzed legs. During his bed baths and catheter care, I don't mind putting in the extra, intimate effort to really make him feel like a man again. You don't have to worry about him, Amanda. I love taking those poor, broken boys under my wing and giving them the close comfort they crave."

The hairs on Amanda’s arms stood up. It was the "Florence Nightingale" savior complex twisted into something deeply boundary-blurring. Brenda viewed Ashleigh and Jessika as patients requiring clinical dignity, but she viewed an eighteen-year-old, newly paralyzed, neurodivergent young man as a vulnerable project she could take advantage of under the guise of "nursing."

Amanda didn't even bother arguing. She simply picked up the red DENIED stamp and brought it down hard on the front of Brenda's application. Clack.

Brenda jumped slightly at the loud noise. "Excuse me?"

"The interview is over, Mrs. Hayes," Amanda said, her voice dangerously low, standing up from her desk and physically imposing herself between the nurse and the hallway that led to her siblings. "Your application is denied."

Brenda looked genuinely shocked, her maternal mask slipping into absolute indignation. "I beg your pardon? I just offered to give your brother the absolute highest level of dedicated care! Do you know how hard it is to find a nurse willing to do the heavy lifting for a grown man?"

"I know exactly how hard it is," Amanda fired back. "But I am not hiring a nurse who views my sisters as clinical patients and my brother as an 'intimate project.' Your approach to his personal care is entirely inappropriate."

"You're out of your mind," Brenda scoffed, snatching her purse. "I am a registered professional! You're just a paranoid little girl playing doctor with her uncle's money."

"Security," Amanda said, not breaking eye contact. "Escort her out."

## The Consummate Professional

By 1:00 PM, Amanda’s jaw ached from clenching it. She had spent her entire lunch break running rigorous, paranoid background checks on the remaining candidates, her trust in medical professionals severely fractured by Keller and Hayes.

When the intercom buzzed for her fifth interview, Amanda was fully prepared to throw another nurse off the estate.

Amanda opened the fifth manila folder. Benjamin Torres walked into the charting station a minute later. He was thirty-eight, dressed in clean, unwrinkled charcoal scrubs, and carried a thick leather portfolio. He had spent the last ten years as a charge nurse in the Neuro-Trauma ICU at Johns Hopkins.

He sat down and carefully reviewed the unprecedented compensation sheet.

"\$100 an hour, an eight-hour physical shift paid for twelve, and fully funded malpractice insurance," Benjamin read aloud, his voice calm but laced with genuine professional respect. He looked up at Amanda. "Your uncle isn't just funding a care team, Miss Wilcox. He’s engineering a zero-burnout environment. He is buying our absolute, uncompromised alertness."

Amanda’s shoulders dropped a fraction of an inch. He actually understood the logistics behind the money. But she wasn't ready to let her guard down yet. She had to run him through the gauntlet.

"Before we discuss the clinical specifics, Mr. Torres," Amanda began, her voice guarded and flat. "You need to understand the hierarchy here. The Chief of Orthopedics at Fairfax writes the orders, but you report directly to me. I am an LPN. You will submit your shift charts for my review. Is that going to be a problem for your professional ego?"

Benjamin didn't scoff. He didn't offer a patronizing smile. He simply nodded, his expression remaining perfectly calm and attentive.

"Not at all," Benjamin replied. "In a high-acuity home care environment, a single point of continuity is critical to prevent medication errors. You know their baseline better than I ever will. Having a dedicated Care Coordinator who is physically present and clinically trained is a massive asset. I understand from the search firm you're also currently enrolled at GMU for your DNP?"

Amanda blinked, slightly caught off guard. "Yes. I'll be attending classes during the day."

"That’s excellent," Benjamin said genuinely. "Translating clinical bedside experience into a doctoral practice makes for the best providers. You manage the macro-level care plan; I will execute the bedside orders while you are in class. It’s a highly efficient system."

Amanda took a slow breath. He had passed the ego check. Now for the medical reality.

"Let’s discuss the physical vulnerabilities of this house," Amanda said, her eyes narrowing slightly as she watched him for any shift in demeanor. "All three of my siblings survived severe hyperthermia. They battle catastrophic post-heatstroke dysautonomia. Their cardiac rhythms are incredibly unstable. Furthermore, Drake and Ashleigh suffered bilateral crush injuries resulting in below-the-knee paralysis. When we do a two-person pivot transfer to get them into their titanium standing frames, the elevation change is incredibly dangerous. How do you approach their transfers and personal care?"

Benjamin didn't lean forward. His voice didn't soften into that creepy, breathless register the predators had used. He remained perfectly, clinically detached.

"With absolute uniformity, continuous telemetry, and strict modesty protocols," Benjamin answered immediately. "Because of their dysautonomia, orthostatic hypotension is the primary threat during a standing frame transfer. My protocol is the same for Drake as it is for Ashleigh: the blinds are drawn, the door is locked. We monitor their live cardiac feed the entire time we elevate them. If their blood pressure bottoms out or their heart rate flutters, the transfer is immediately aborted and we lower them back down. I explain every point of physical contact before I make it, so they are never startled. I encourage them to do whatever they physically can for themselves to maintain their dignity, and I strictly step in only for what they cannot safely do. And with Jessika, it’s about proximity and telemetry monitoring without hovering so she still feels like she has space. They are patients, and my job is to keep them safe and respected."

It was perfect. It was the exact, sterile, highly respectful answer she had been waiting to hear all day. He viewed them as human beings requiring medical dignity, completely regardless of gender.

Amanda let out a slow, quiet breath. "And regarding their emotional regulation. Drake and Ashleigh are neurodivergent. They utilize deep pressure therapy—specifically, physical contact with each other or with me—to regulate their autonomic nervous systems. If they are holding onto each other, or if they are in distress..."

"...I step back and let the family handle it," Benjamin finished smoothly, nodding in complete understanding. "Massive sensory disruption is common with severe crush injuries, especially for neurodivergent patients. Proprioceptive input is a valid and necessary medical tool for them. I will manage the central lines, the IV fluids, and the telemetry feeds. If they need to co-regulate, I will give them the physical space to do so without interference."

Amanda stared at him. He hadn't just respected the boundary; he had accurately defined the clinical science behind it without pathologizing them.

"On your application," Amanda said, her voice finally losing its hard, defensive edge, "you checked that you are a non-smoker. Is that entirely accurate?"

Benjamin smiled, a genuine look of pride crossing his face. "I have never touched a cigarette or chewing tobacco in my life, Miss Wilcox. And since we are discussing the liabilities of a home-care environment with heavy liquid narcotics, I should also add that I have never had a drop of alcohol, either. Not even a beer on my twenty-first birthday. I understand the danger of dysautonomia triggers, so you never have to worry about my sobriety, my judgment, or any third-hand smoke affecting your siblings' heart rates."

He had practically handed her the keys to a stress-free shift.

Amanda looked down at his resume, tracing a finger over his impeccable references. She kept her face perfectly neutral, refusing to let her profound relief show. She still had to maintain her authority as the Care Coordinator, and she still had one more interview scheduled.

"I appreciate your candor and your clinical approach, Mr. Torres," Amanda said, her tone professional but notably warmer than it had been all morning. She stood up, extending her hand across the desk. "I have one final interview this afternoon to complete my due diligence. We will be making our final hiring decisions by the end of the week."

Benjamin stood up and shook her hand firmly. "I understand completely. Thank you for your time, Miss Wilcox. Take care of your family."

## The Fugitive

By 2:30 PM, Amanda was running on sheer adrenaline and lukewarm coffee. She pulled the sixth and final manila folder to the center of her desk. If this candidate passed the gauntlet and the reference checks, she would finally have her day-shift rotation.

"Send her in," Amanda told the intercom.

Valerie Higgins walked into the charting station. She was forty-two, dressed in a muted gray scrub set, with her hair pulled back into a neat, professional bun. Her resume boasted ten years of high-acuity home infusion therapy and neuro-rehabilitation. The executive search firm had rated her as one of their top prospects.

From the moment she sat down, Valerie seemed like a breath of fresh air. She reviewed the unprecedented \$100-an-hour, 8-hour shift structure, and the fully funded malpractice insurance with a calm, appreciative nod.

"I have zero issues reporting to an LPN, Miss Wilcox," Valerie said, her voice smooth and measured. "The letters on a badge don't dictate the flow of a home. You are the Care Coordinator. I execute the orders, you review the charts. It's the safest way to prevent communication breakdowns."

Amanda mentally checked the first box. Perfect.

"Let's discuss the clinical realities," Amanda said, leaning forward. "All three of my siblings survived severe hyperthermia. They battle catastrophic post-heatstroke dysautonomia. Their cardiac rhythms are incredibly unstable, and they require continuous telemetry. Furthermore, my brother Drake and my sister Ashleigh suffered bilateral crush injuries resulting in below-the-knee paralysis. When we transfer them to their titanium standing frames, the elevation change can trigger orthostatic hypotension and immediate syncope. How do you handle their transfers and personal care?"

"Strict modesty protocols and continuous cardiac observation across the board," Valerie answered without hesitation. "Below-the-knee paralysis requires a lot of emotional adjustment, and the dysautonomia makes every elevation change a cardiac event. I monitor the telemetry live. If their blood pressure bottoms out, we abort the transfer immediately. I maintain absolute clinical detachment. They are my patients, not my friends, and certainly not projects."

Amanda felt a knot in her shoulders loosen. Valerie was hitting all the exact same professional notes as Benjamin Torres. When Amanda explained the twins' neurodivergent need for deep pressure therapy, Valerie simply nodded, validating the clinical need for proprioceptive input and agreeing to step back and let the family handle emotional regulation.

She was a non-smoker. She claimed she didn't drink.

Amanda actually picked up a pen, ready to make a positive note on the margin of the resume. I found the second nurse.

Then, the intercom on the wall glowed with a flashing, urgent blue light.

"Miss Wilcox," the private security contractor at the main gate announced, his voice tight and completely devoid of its usual steady cadence. "I apologize for the interruption. I have two cruisers from the Fairfax County Police Department at the outer perimeter, accompanied by an agent from the DEA. They are requesting immediate entry."

Amanda stopped writing. The pen hovered over the paper.

Across the desk, Valerie Higgins went completely rigid. The warm, professional color drained out of her face in a matter of seconds, leaving her chalk-white. Her eyes darted frantically toward the heavy reinforced window, then to the heavy oak door.

"What is the nature of the request?" Amanda asked the intercom, her eyes locking onto the sudden, terrifying shift in the woman sitting across from her.

"They have a felony warrant for a Valerie Higgins," the guard replied. "Medical identity theft and interstate narcotic diversion. Do I have your authorization to open the gates?"

Valerie stood up so fast her chair scraped loudly against the hardwood floor.

"Miss Wilcox, there's been a massive misunderstanding," Valerie stammered, her voice suddenly high and breathless, the consummate professional mask shattering entirely. She grabbed her purse, backing away from the desk. "It's a clerical error with my last agency. If you just show me to the back exit, I can go sort this out with my lawyer—"

"Absolutely not," Amanda said. Her voice was ice.

Amanda didn't reach for the door. She reached under the lip of her desk, her finger hovering right next to the silent panic button that would drop the steel security shutters over the windows.

She pressed the intercom button.

"Open the main gates," Amanda ordered without a second of hesitation. "Escort the officers directly to the ground-floor charting station. Do not let them stop at the front desk. Bring them straight to me."

"Amanda, please!" Valerie pleaded, stepping toward the door. "I'm a good nurse! I just made a mistake with some paperwork!"

"Sit down," Amanda commanded, her voice cracking like a whip. "You are not leaving this room."

Valerie looked at the twenty-three-year-old sitting behind the desk. She looked at the cold, unyielding fury in Amanda’s eyes, and she realized instantly that running would do absolutely no good. Amanda Wilcox was a fortress.

Defeated, Valerie sank back into the chair, burying her face in her hands.

Less than two minutes later, the heavy oak door swung open. Two Fairfax County police officers, flanked by a plainclothes DEA agent and one of the estate's armed contractors, stepped into the room.

"Valerie Higgins?" the DEA agent asked, pulling a pair of heavy steel handcuffs from his belt.

Valerie didn't answer. She just stood up and put her hands behind her back.

The click of the handcuffs echoed loudly in the pristine charting station.

"Thank you for your complete cooperation, Miss Wilcox," the DEA agent said, looking at Amanda with genuine respect. "She’s been bouncing between high-net-worth private care gigs using forged references. She gains the family's trust, and then she starts siphoning off the liquid Fentanyl and Dilaudid prescriptions, replacing them with saline."

A wave of pure, absolute nausea washed over Amanda.

If she had hired Valerie... if she hadn't held the line... Drake and Ashleigh would have been screaming in agony from their crushed bones, their dysautonomic nervous systems misfiring in sheer panic, entirely unmedicated, while this woman pumped them full of useless saltwater and sold their painkillers on the street.

"Take her off my property," Amanda said, her voice shaking with a terrifying, contained rage.

The officers quickly escorted the disgraced nurse out of the office.

Once the door clicked shut, Amanda stood up, walked over to the locked, temperature-controlled narcotics cabinet, and double-checked the heavy steel deadbolt. She stood there for a long moment, staring at the vials of medication that kept her siblings out of agony.

She walked back to her desk, picked up Valerie's resume, and shoved it directly into the shredder.

## The Verification

Amanda sat in the deafening quiet of the charting station, the shredder beside her desk still radiating a faint heat from destroying Valerie Higgins's forged application. She pulled Benjamin Torres’s manila folder back to the center of the heavy oak desk.

Her hands were still shaking slightly from the adrenaline of the DEA raid. David Keller had been a predator. Brenda Hayes had been a boundary-blurring nightmare. Valerie Higgins had been a literal fugitive.

Benjamin Torres seemed like a unicorn, but Amanda was done trusting her gut. She picked up the phone, dialed the first number on his reference sheet, and prepared to dig.

### Call 1: The Surgeon

The first number belonged to Dr. Marcus Vance, Chief of Neuro-Trauma Surgery at Johns Hopkins. Amanda expected to be routed to a voicemail or brushed off by a receptionist, but when she stated she was calling regarding Benjamin Torres, she was patched through to the surgeon's office immediately.

"Miss Wilcox," Dr. Vance’s voice came through the receiver, crisp and hurried, but undeniably warm. "I only have about three minutes before I scrub in, but if this is about Ben Torres, I’ll make the time."

"Dr. Vance, I'm vetting Mr. Torres for a highly specialized, private-duty trauma role," Amanda said, keeping her tone strictly professional. "He would be managing below-the-knee paralysis and severe dysautonomia independently. Clinically, is he capable?"

Dr. Vance actually laughed. "Clinically? Ben is the only nurse on my floor who catches a vitals crash before the telemetry monitors do. He's hyper-vigilant. But if you want to know why you should hire him, it’s not just his charting. It’s his character. Last year, we had a nineteen-year-old kid wake up from a spinal fusion realizing he couldn't feel his legs. The kid was terrified, swinging wildly, completely inconsolable. Ben didn't sedate him. He just stood by the bed, held his hand, and talked him down for three hours straight after his shift had officially ended. Ben is a rock. You'd be incredibly lucky to have him."

### Call 2: The Social Worker

Amanda carefully noted Dr. Vance's exact words on her legal pad before dialing the second number. It belonged to Elaine Croft, the Lead Clinical Social Worker for the Hopkins SCI (Spinal Cord Injury) unit.

"Oh, Benjamin," Elaine sighed warmly when Amanda explained the nature of the call. "I was devastated when he put in his notice, but he told me he was looking for a quieter environment where he could focus on 1:1 patient care."

"My siblings require strict modesty protocols and have specific neurodivergent boundaries regarding physical touch," Amanda pushed, her voice hardening slightly as she remembered Keller and Hayes. "Does Mr. Torres respect physical boundaries?"

"Absolutely and unequivocally," Elaine replied instantly, her tone turning fiercely protective of the nurse. "In acute rehab, a lot of providers accidentally strip patients of their autonomy because it's 'faster' to just do things for them. Ben never does that. He treats every single patient—especially the young, vulnerable ones—with profound dignity. He asks permission before he touches them, he guards their privacy like a hawk, and he never infantilizes them. He is just a genuinely, deeply good human being, Miss Wilcox."

### Call 3: The Director of Nursing

Amanda’s shoulders had finally dropped away from her ears. The knot of anxiety in her stomach was unraveling. She dialed the final number: Helen Ruiz, the Director of Critical Care Nursing at Johns Hopkins.

Helen didn't mince words. "Miss Wilcox, I oversee four hundred nurses. Benjamin Torres is in the top one percent. Not just in skill, but in absolute, unshakeable integrity."

"He mentioned on his application that he is a lifelong non-smoker and has never consumed alcohol," Amanda asked, needing to verify the final, most critical piece of the puzzle given the liquid Fentanyl locked in her cabinet.

"It's true," Helen confirmed gently. "Ben is a straight arrow. He doesn't drink, he doesn't smoke, he doesn't gossip at the charting station, and he doesn't cut corners. If he tells you a medication was pushed at 0800, you can bet your license it was pushed at exactly 0800. If you are running a private medical wing for your family, there is no one on this earth I would trust more to guard their safety."

Amanda slowly hung up the receiver.

She stared at her legal pad. Three separate, high-level hospital executives hadn't just confirmed Benjamin’s clinical abilities; they had gone out of their way to explicitly praise his soul. He wasn't a predator. He wasn't a fugitive.

He was exactly who he said he was.

Amanda let out a long, shaky breath. She leaned forward, rested her forehead against the cool wood of her desk, and for the first time since she had started this brutal vetting process, she let herself cry a few quiet, exhausted tears of pure relief.

She picked up her phone, found Benjamin’s cell number, and dialed.

"Mr. Torres?" Amanda said, her voice finally warm and completely unguarded. "This is Amanda Wilcox. The position is yours. Can you start tomorrow morning?"

Amanda Wilcox was no longer just a terrified twenty-three-year-old sister trying to keep her family out of foster care. She was the CEO and Clinical Director of a multi-million-dollar, fully operational private medical fortress.
