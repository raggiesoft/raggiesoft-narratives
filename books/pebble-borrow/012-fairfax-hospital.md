# Fairfax Hospital

## The Fairfax Trauma Bay

The three Advanced Life Support ambulances screeched to a halt under the massive, brightly lit canopy of the Fairfax emergency bay. The hospital had been transformed into a fortress. Dozens of federal agents in tactical gear had established a hard perimeter, pushing civilian traffic away. Up on the roof, the distinct, heavy thrum of military Blackhawk helicopters was still winding down, having just dropped off the East Coast's top orthopedic and burn surgeons.

Right behind the ambulances, the matte-black federal SUV slammed into park. Amanda didn't wait for the FBI commander to open her door. She shoved it open and sprinted across the pavement, her LPN training completely overriding her physical exhaustion. She had managed their primary care for the last three years, and she knew the trauma doctors were flying blind without their medical histories.

The rear doors of the ambulances were thrown open almost simultaneously.

Drake was pulled out first. He was completely unresponsive, an oxygen mask strapped over his pale face, his chest rising in shallow, erratic gasps. The trauma team didn't even pause; they practically ran his stretcher through the automatic doors, shouting out his crashing vitals and the catastrophic nature of the bilateral crush injuries.

Amanda intercepted the lead military orthopedic surgeon sprinting alongside Drake's gurney. "I am his primary LPN!" she shouted over the rotors and sirens, falling into a rapid step beside them. "Patient is bradycardic at baseline, normally fifty-five BPM. EMS pushed sodium bicarbonate to fight the rhabdomyolysis and TXA for the internal bleeding. No known drug allergies!"

The surgeon gave her a sharp, appreciative nod, absorbing the critical data without breaking stride. "Copy the baseline! We're taking him straight to OR One!"

Ashleigh came next. She was awake, but barely. Her eyes were half-open, rolling lazily as she struggled weakly against the rigid splints binding her shattered legs. The morphine and the lingering 104-degree fever had stripped away her ability to speak, leaving her moaning softly as the flown-in specialists swarmed her gurney and rushed her inside.

Amanda peeled off from Drake's team to intercept Ashleigh's stretcher at the doors. "She has a naturally low baseline pressure, usually one-hundred over sixty!" Amanda called out to the burn specialist hovering over her sister's head. "She's in neurogenic shock. EMS pushed ten CCs of morphine, but the swelling is completely cutting off her pedal pulses!"

Then came Jessika.

Despite the blistering fever radiating off her skin, the youngest triplet was fiercely alert. The second her stretcher hit the pavement, she grabbed the sleeve of the nearest trauma surgeon.

"It was a heavy iron pipe!" Jessika yelled over the noise of the idling ambulances and the shouting agents. "It bucked under extreme pressure! It crushed them right below the knees! And we were in a hundred and forty degrees for an hour—they need ice, they need fluids!"

Amanda reached Jessika's side, grabbing her youngest sister's hand for a brief, desperate squeeze before looking directly at the trauma doc. "She has a mild mitral valve prolapse noted on her chart! Watch the chilled IV fluid flow rate so you don't throw her heart into an arrhythmia. She's critically dehydrated but needs a stepped-down flow!"

"We've got them, sweetheart," the surgeon said to Jessika, before giving Amanda a firm nod of professional respect, his voice steady and commanding as they wheeled her rapidly into the ER. "You did exactly right. We're taking them to surgery right now. You just worry about yourself.

## The Agony of Silence

Inside, the FBI tactical commander ushered Amanda, Victor, and Erika past the chaotic trauma bays and into a secure, sterile family waiting room on the surgical floor. An agent took up a post directly outside the closed door.

Just before the heavy doors sealed them in, a woman in dark blue scrubs—the surgical floor's Charge Nurse—stepped into the doorway. She looked at Amanda, noting her soot-stained clothes and the LPN badge still clipped to her pocket.

"Amanda?" the Charge Nurse asked gently. Amanda nodded, her throat too tight to speak.

"The medics told us what you did on the radio," the nurse said, her voice filled with profound professional respect. "Pushing the bicarb, managing the TXA, stepping down the IV flow for Jessika's heart... you bought them the exact window of time they needed to survive the transport. You did your job perfectly. Now, let us do ours."

Amanda managed a trembling nod as the Charge Nurse offered a reassuring smile before stepping back into the hall. The heavy doors swung shut with a soft click, sealing the family inside the quiet room.

The contrast was jarring. One minute they were tearing down a deserted interstate at ninety miles an hour, and the next, they were sitting in absolute, suffocating silence.

Amanda walked over and sat rigidly on a small sofa, staring blankly at a muted television in the corner. She didn't move toward the door or ask to scrub in. She knew the hospital protocols inside and out—treating immediate family in a trauma setting was a strict ethical lockout, and she had no legal privileges at Fairfax anyway. She had to play the waiting game, just like everyone else.

But that was the cruelest part of her medical training. She couldn't just sit there in ignorant hope. She knew exactly what rhabdomyolysis was doing to Drake's kidneys. She knew exactly what compartment syndrome meant for Ashleigh's legs. She could practically visualize the surgeons slicing into the fascia to relieve the catastrophic pressure. Knowing the grim, mechanical reality of what was happening behind those OR doors made the waiting a hundred times harder.

Victor had sunk into a stiff vinyl chair, his face buried in his soot-stained hands. He had built an empire on taking decisive action, on solving impossible equations, and on outmaneuvering corporate rivals. But here, under the buzzing fluorescent lights of the waiting room, he was entirely powerless.

Erika paced the length of the room, her arms crossed tightly over her chest, occasionally glancing at the clock on the wall. The second hand seemed to be moving through molasses.

Amanda pulled her knees to her chest, burying her face in her arms as the tears she had been fighting back since the cavern finally spilled over. Every minute that ticked by without a surgeon walking through that door felt like a physical weight pressing down on her chest.

## The Gathering Storm

Outside the hospital, the perimeter was holding, but the spectacle was impossible to hide.

Local news vans were already aggressively parking along the shoulder of the access roads, their satellite dishes deploying as reporters scrambled out with microphones and cameras. You couldn't land military Blackhawks on a civilian hospital and shut down Interstate 66 with a federal convoy without the world taking notice.

On the muted television inside the waiting room, a 'BREAKING NEWS' banner flashed in bright red across the bottom of the screen.

The camera showed a reporter standing just beyond the FBI barricades outside Fairfax, gesturing wildly to the flashing lights behind her. The footage cut to a shaky, aerial helicopter shot of the sleepy town of Middletown, Virginia, where dozens of federal vehicles were swarming the gates of the Pebble Burrow Institute.

Rumors were actively flying through the press circles. Sources within the Department of Justice had leaked that Marcus Vance, the billionaire CEO of a major defense contractor, had been dragged out of his facility in handcuffs earlier that morning.

But the press didn't have the whole story. They were wildly speculating about a corporate embezzlement ring, a hostage situation, or a domestic terrorism plot. They knew a massive, unprecedented federal response had converged on a subterranean bunker in Middletown, and they knew three critically injured people had just been rushed into surgery under presidential protection.

They had absolutely no idea that the soot-covered kids currently fighting for their lives in the surgical suites had just prevented the Eastern Seaboard from turning into a supervolcanic crater.
