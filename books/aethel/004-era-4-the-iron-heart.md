# Era 4: The Iron Heart

## Book I: The Magnetic North

### Chapter 1: The Art of Doing Nothing

#### Part 1: The Geometry of Sleep

Theme: sunstead-peace

Location: The Cottage on the Ridge

POV: Kaela

=

The morning suns were late, or perhaps Kaela was just early.

She lay in the grey twilight of the cottage, listening to the world wake up. The wind was brushing against the slate roof—the roof Kaelan had laid tile by tile two years ago—making a soft, scratching sound that used to terrify her, but now just sounded like home.

She didn't move. She couldn't move, even if she wanted to.

Kaelan was a heavy sleeper. Not just in depth, but in actual mass. He was sprawled across the majority of the narrow mattress, his left arm thrown over her waist like a seatbelt, pinning her to the sheets. His face was buried in the curve of her neck, his breathing slow and rhythmic, sending puffs of warm air against her skin.

Kaela stared at the wooden beams of the ceiling. She counted the dried bundles of sage and lavender hanging from the rafters.

*Seventeen bundles,* she thought. *We need more mint.*

She shifted her leg, trying to find a patch of cool sheet. Kaelan instantly grumbled in his sleep, his arm tightening reflexively, pulling her flush against his chest.

"You're a furnace," she whispered, poking his forearm.

"Structural... integrity," Kaelan mumbled, not opening his eyes. "Don't wiggle. You'll collapse the building."

"The building is stone, Kaelan. It's fine. My bladder, however, is not."

Kaelan sighed—a long, dramatic exhalation of a man being asked to perform a Herculean task. He rolled onto his back, releasing her from the clamp.

"Fine," he groaned, rubbing his eyes with the heels of his hands. "But you're letting the cold in."

Kaela sat up, shivering as the morning air hit her skin. She grabbed her shawl from the bedpost and wrapped it around her shoulders.

"It's summer, Sun. It's not cold. You just run at the temperature of a smelting pot."

She climbed over him—there was no space to walk around the bed—and padded across the cool floorboards to the washbasin behind the screen.

When she came back, Kaelan was awake. He was propped up on his elbows, watching her with sleepy, gold-blue eyes. His hair was a black disaster, sticking up in every direction.

"Tea?" he asked hopefully.

"If you start the fire," she countered.

"Deal."

Kaelan didn't get up. He just pointed a finger at the hearth across the room. He didn't use a spell; he just projected a tiny, concentrated thought of *heat*.

*Pop.*

The kindling in the fireplace smoked, then burst into a cheerful orange flame.

"Show off," Kaela smiled, filling the kettle from the water bucket.

"Efficiency," Kaelan corrected, falling back onto the pillow. "Come back here while it boils. You're too far away."

Kaela set the kettle on the swing-arm over the fire. Then, she walked back to the bed. She didn't hesitate. She climbed back in, curling up against his side, resting her head on his shoulder.

He wrapped his arm around her immediately, the circuit of their touch closing with a contented hum.

They lay there for ten minutes, watching the firelight dance on the whitewashed walls. They didn't talk about space. They didn't talk about wars.

"Horg says Clinker is teething again," Kaelan murmured, his fingers idly tracing patterns on her arm. "He chewed through the gate latch yesterday."

"We should bring him some apples," Kaela said. "The sour ones from the old tree. He likes the crunch."

"And Joren wants me to look at the watchtower steps. Says they're creaking."

"You built those steps a year ago. They're solid oak."

"Joren just wants company," Kaelan said. "He wants to tell me the story about the River Troll again."

"You should let him."

"I will."

The kettle began to whistle.

"Work time," Kaelan groaned, but he was smiling.

He kissed the top of her head. "I'll handle the fence today. You get the apples."

"Deal."

#### Part 2: The Baker’s Tax

Theme: sunstead-joy

Location: The Town Square POV: Kaela

=

Sunstead in the mid-morning was a bustling ecosystem of red dust and gossip.

Kaela walked down the main street, a basket hooked over her arm. She waved to the Tanner, who was stretching a hide in the sun. She nodded to the Miller’s children, who were chasing a hoop with a stick.

She didn't wear a hood. She didn't hide her face.

Two years ago, people used to stop and stare when the "Scions" walked by. They would bow, or whisper, or look terrified.

Now, they just waved.

"Morning, Kaela!" Mrs. Gable shouted from the open window of the bakery. The smell of yeast and cinnamon wafted out, thick enough to chew.

"Morning, Mrs. Gable," Kaela called back, leaning on the windowsill. "Do you have the dark loaves?"

"For you? Always." The baker bustled disappear and returned with two loaves of bread—dark, heavy rye with a crust like bark, exactly how Kaelan liked it.

"And," Mrs. Gable added, sliding a small paper sack across the counter with a conspiratorial wink, "a little something extra. For the boy."

Kaela peeked inside. It was a "broken" pie—a cherry tart that had collapsed in the oven. It looked like a mess. It smelled like heaven.

"He'll love it," Kaela laughed. "He says the broken ones taste better because the juice caramelizes on the pan."

"He says that so I don't feel bad about dropping them," Mrs. Gable huffed, wiping her floury hands on her apron. "He's a good lad. How's the wall coming?"

"It's... substantial," Kaela admitted. "I think he's trying to fortify the garden against a siege, not a deer."

"Let him build," Mrs. Gable advised. "Men like Kaelan need to move heavy things. Keeps them from thinking too much."

She leaned closer, her voice dropping.

"Is he... sleeping well?"

The town knew about the nightmares. In the first few months after their return from the South, Kaelan had woken up screaming more than once, shaking the cottage with accidental bursts of heat.

"He is," Kaela said softly. "No dreams for weeks. Just snoring."

"Good," Mrs. Gable nodded firmly. "You tell him to come down for supper on Freeday. I'm making roast mutton. And tell him to bring that appetite of his."

"I will. Thank you, Martha."

Kaela put the bread and the tart in her basket. She walked toward the orchard at the edge of town, her heart light.

She loved this. She loved that her biggest problem today was carrying enough apples to satisfy a giant, metal-eating badger. She loved that her brother was just "the lad" to the baker, not the Solar Engine or the God-Slayer.

She picked a basketful of the hard, green apples from the wild trees near the river.

As she walked back up the ridge path, she stopped.

She looked at the cottage.

From here, she could see Kaelan in the garden. He was shirtless, his back glistening with sweat in the midday suns. He was lifting a stone the size of a beer keg, setting it gently atop the wall.

He stepped back, hands on his hips, admiring the line.

He looked happy.

Kaela felt a swell of affection so strong it almost knocked the wind out of her.

*Stay like this,* she thought, projecting the wish into the universe. *Just stay like this forever.*

#### Part 3: The Fortress of Mint

Theme: sunstead-peace

Location: The Garden POV: Kaelan

=

The stone fit.

Kaelan ran his hand over the rough granite. It sat perfectly flush against its neighbor, locked in by gravity and friction.

"That," Kaelan declared to the empty garden, "is a good rock."

He wiped his forehead with his arm. He looked at the wall.

It was four feet high and two feet thick. It encircled the small plot of land where Kaela grew her herbs and vegetables.

Ideally, a garden fence should be a light, wooden thing.

This was a rampart.

"Overkill?" he asked himself.

He looked at the delicate green shoots of the mint plants peeking out of the soil. He remembered how upset Kaela had been three years ago when the deer ate them down to the roots.

"Nope," he decided. "Necessary security."

He picked up his water jug and took a long drink. The water was cool, drawn from the deep well.

He heard footsteps on the path. He knew the rhythm before he saw her.

*Step-light-step.*

Kaela came through the gate. She was carrying a heavy basket of apples and bread. She looked flushed from the walk, her copper hair escaping her braid.

"Delivery," she called out.

Kaelan walked over to her. He took the heavy basket from her arm as if it weighed nothing.

"You got the dark bread," he noted, peeking inside. "And... is that a pie disaster?"

"Mrs. Gable sent it. She says you need to keep your strength up for all this... siege engineering."

Kaela walked over to the wall. She pushed against it. It didn't budge. It felt as solid as the mountain itself.

"It's high, Kaelan," she noted, looking at a spot where he had built it up to five feet to block the prevailing wind.

"Deer can jump," Kaelan defended, breaking off a piece of the crusty bread.

"Deer, yes. I think this would stop a battering ram."

"Well, you never know," Kaelan grinned, sitting down in the grass with his back against his creation. "Rogue battering rams are a menace this time of year."

Kaela sat next to him. She leaned her head on his shoulder.

"It's beautiful," she said honestly. "It looks like it's always been here."

"I like the stone," Kaelan admitted, looking at his hands. "It's quiet. It waits for you."

He offered her a piece of the pie. They ate in silence, watching the clouds drift over the Razor Peaks in the distance.

Those mountains used to terrify him. They used to be the barrier between him and his destiny.

Now, they were just scenery.

"Horg wants us for dinner on Freeday," Kaela said, wiping cherry filling from her thumb.

"Mutton?"

"Mutton."

"I'm in."

Kaelan finished the pie. He brushed the crumbs from his chest.

He reached out and took Kaela’s hand. Her fingers were stained with apple dust; his were grey with stone dust.

"I was thinking," Kaelan said.

"Dangerous."

"I was thinking we should plant an apple tree. Inside the wall. Right there in the corner."

Kaela looked at the spot. "It'll take years to fruit."

"We're only twenty," Kaelan said. "We have years."

"Okay," Kaela squeezed his hand. "An apple tree. And maybe a bench. So I can watch you work without getting grass stains on my dress."

"I can build a bench," Kaelan said confidently. "I'll make it out of stone."

"Of course you will."

They sat there as the afternoon suns began to dip, bathing the red dust of Sunstead in a warm, golden light.

It was a perfect Tuesday. The wall was straight. The bread was good. The sister was safe.

Kaelan closed his eyes, listening to the hum of the bees in the clover, unaware that beneath him, miles below the crust, a clock he had set a billion years ago was about to strike midnight.

"Nap?" Kaelan suggested.

"Nap," Kaela agreed.

### Chapter 2: The Alarm Clock

#### Part 1: The Interest Rate

Theme: sunstead-peace-disrupted

Location: The Cottage Garden

POV: Kaela

=

The potatoes were coming in nicely.

Kaelan knelt in the rich, red soil of the garden, his hands caked in dirt. The stone wall he had finished yesterday stood solid and grey against the morning suns, a fortress protecting the mint.

It was a quiet Tuesday. Horg was hammering a plowshare down in the valley—a rhythmic *clang-clang* that was the heartbeat of the town.

Kaela was sitting on the new stone bench Kaelan had built into the corner of the wall. She was shelling peas, the pods popping with a crisp snap.

Kaelan pulled a potato from the earth. It was lumpy and brown. It was perfect.

He wiped his hands on his trousers and sat back on his heels. He looked at her.

"You missed a spot," he said, pointing to a pea pod that had fallen into the grass.

"I'm saving it for the ants," Kaela replied without looking up. "They have quotas too."

Kaelan laughed. He stood up, dusting off his knees. He walked over to the bench.

He didn't sit down. He stood behind her, wrapping his dirty arms around her shoulders, resting his chin on top of her head.

Kaela leaned back into him instantly, dropping the peas to rest her hands over his forearms.

"You're getting dirt on my dress," she murmured, but she squeezed his arm.

"It's clean dirt," Kaelan said. "It's our dirt."

He tightened the hug, rocking her slightly. The hum of the Circuit—that golden, warm connection between their minds—flared with contentment.

"You know," Kaelan whispered into her hair, "I was doing the math this morning. By my calculations, with the current interest rate of 'Forever,' I think I'm going to be paying this hug debt until the suns burn out."

"Longer," Kaela corrected softly. "You forgot the compound interest from the winter of '16."

"Right. The winter." He pressed a kiss to her temple. "So, eternity then? Is that the term?"

"Eternity seems fair," she agreed.

"Good," Kaelan said, his voice dropping to a rumble in his chest. "Because I'm never paying it off. I'm just going to keep defaulting on the loan so I have to keep holding you."

"I accept your terms, Sun."

They stayed there for a moment, suspended in the amber light of the morning. It was perfect.

Then, the world stopped.

It wasn't a sound. The birds were still singing. Horg was still hammering.

It was a vibration.

It started deep—so deep it felt like it was coming from the other side of the planet. It traveled up through the bedrock, through the red dust, and into the soles of Kaelan’s boots.

*Thrummmmmmm.*

It felt like a massive, subterranean generator cycling up.

Kaelan froze. He pulled back slightly, his hand going to the stone wall to steady himself.

"Kaela," he said. His voice was low, but it cut through the morning air.

"I feel it," she said. She stood up, the bowl of peas sliding forgotten to the grass.

She looked at her tea mug sitting on the bench. The liquid inside was rippling in perfect concentric circles.

"Is it an earthquake?" Kaela asked, grabbing his arm. "The tectonic plate?"

"No," Kaelan said. He closed his eyes. He engaged the Engine, pushing his senses down into the earth, past the crust, past the mantle.

He felt the iron.

He felt the massive, liquid metal ocean at the center of the world churning. It wasn't shifting naturally. It was spinning. It was aligning.

And it felt... familiar.

"It's not a quake," Kaelan whispered, his face draining of color. "It's a startup sequence."

Suddenly, the air in the garden changed. The hair on Kaelan’s arms stood up.

The iron shovel leaning against the stone fence rattled. Then, it lifted.

It floated three inches off the ground, suspended by an invisible magnetic grip.

"Kaelan," Kaela said, pointing to the porch.

The iron nails in the timber beams were groaning. The cast-iron pot by the door was sliding across the floorboards, pulled North.

"Gravity?" Kaela asked.

"Magnetism," Kaelan corrected. He stood up, wiping the dirt from his hands. "Massive, planetary-scale magnetism."

He looked at his own hands. The blood in his veins—rich with iron—tugged slightly, pulling him.

"Why?" Kaela asked. "Why now?"

Kaelan looked at the sky. He looked at the suns he had forged. He remembered a conversation from a billion years ago, in a library made of light.

*“I put a little extra iron in the core. Just in case.”*

"In case of what?" Kaela asked, reading his mind through the Circuit.

"I don't know," Kaelan admitted, terrified. "I don't remember setting the alarm. But I definitely just heard it go off."

The hum grew louder.

And then, for the first time in history, the compasses in Sunstead didn't point North.

They pointed Down.

#### Part 2: The Shadow of the Zenith

Theme: cosmic-dread

Location: The Void Beyond the Stars

POV: The Dark Master (The Unseen)

=

Far beyond the warmth of the Twin Suns, in the space between galaxies where light dared not tread, a Presence stirred.

It did not have a body. It did not have a throne. Malakor had built a tower of stone to mimic it; Seraphina had sought to birth a body to house it.

They were small minds. They thought in terms of matter.

The Master was not matter. It was the Null. It was the static at the end of the radio signal.

It had been waiting.

For eons, the Earth had been silent—a shielded experiment hidden in the arm of the Milky Way. The "Extra Iron" Solas had placed in the core acted as a dampener, a magnetic Faraday cage that hid the Scions' light from the deep dark.

But now, the cage was vibrating. The core was spinning up.

The shield was dropping to become a weapon.

The Unseen focused its attention. It didn't see with eyes. It saw with gravity.

It felt the pulse of the binary star system. It felt the awakening of the Iron Heart.

"Found you," the Void whispered across the light-years.

It didn't send an army. It didn't send a fleet.

It sent a Signal.

A single, discordant frequency—an "Anti-Song"—aimed directly at the vibrating core of the blue planet.

Malakor had wanted to rule the world. Seraphina had wanted to eat the stars.

The Unseen simply wanted to turn the page. And Earth was a typo it intended to erase.

#### Part 3: The Gathering of Iron

Theme: sunstead-action

Location: Horg’s Smithy

POV: Kaela

=

"My hammers are floating!" Horg bellowed, trying to grab a sledgehammer that was drifting lazily toward the ceiling.

The smithy was in chaos. Every iron tool—tongs, nails, horseshoes—was suspended in the air, vibrating with the low hum that was shaking the whole town.

Clinker, the massive iron-bristle beast in the pen out back, was howling—a sound like grinding gears—as the metal quills on his back stood straight up, magnetized.

"It's the core!" Kaelan shouted, running into the shop. He grabbed a floating anvil by the horn and hauled it down, slamming it onto its stump. "Horg! Get everyone outside! Away from the metal!"

"What did you do, lad?" Horg yelled, wrestling a cloud of nails. "Did you break the sky again?"

"I think I broke the floor this time!" Kaelan admitted.

Kaela stood in the doorway. She wasn't looking at the floating tools. She was looking at the horizon.

The sky to the North—over the Voidlands—was changing.

The Auroras were back. But they weren't the gentle green and violet curtains of the past.

They were red. Deep, blood-red ribbons of magnetic fire tearing through the atmosphere.

"Kaelan," Kaela projected, her mind sharp and urgent. "Look North."

Kaelan dropped the anvil. He stepped out.

He saw the red sky. He felt the pull in his blood.

"The core isn't just spinning," Kaelan realized. "It's projecting. It's opening a door."

"Where?"

"Underground," Kaelan said. "Deep. The Magnetic North just moved. It's not at the pole anymore."

He pointed to the center of the Voidlands, right where Malakor’s tower used to stand.

"It's there. The crater. It's an intake valve."

"An intake for what?" Horg asked, stumbling out of the shop, watching his hammers drift out the window.

"For us," Kaela whispered.

She looked at her brother. She saw the fear in his eyes—not fear of a monster, but fear of his own creation.

"The core is calling us home, Kaelan. We have to go down there. We have to answer the phone before..."

"...before the thing on the other end hangs up," Kaelan finished grimly.

He looked at his peaceful town, now rattling with magnetic tremors. He looked at the slate roof he had just finished.

"So much for the garden," Kaelan sighed.

He reached for his sword. It floated into his hand before he could grab it.

"We need the boat," Kaelan said. "But we aren't sailing on water this time."

"Magma?" Kaela asked.

"Magma," Kaelan confirmed. "We're going to the center of the Earth."

### Chapter 3: The Magnetic Highway

#### Part 1: The Land-Boat

Theme: sunstead-action Location: Horg’s Smithy POV: Kaelan

The *Iron Keel* sat in the middle of the dusty street outside the forge, looking completely out of its element. It was heavy, squat, and distinctly marine.

"You want to do *what*?" Horg asked, flipping up his welding mask. The air in the shop was thick with the smell of ozone and burnt hair; half his tools were currently bobbing against the ceiling, pinned there by the magnetic screaming of the North.

"I want to slide it," Kaelan said, patting the oak hull. "The pull is strong, Horg. It nearly ripped the shovel out of my hand in the garden. If we reinforce the keel with pure, conductive iron, I can lock it into the current."

"Like a leaf on a stream," Kaela added, looking at the map spread out on a barrel. "Only the stream is invisible, moving at sixty miles an hour, and aiming straight for a volcano."

Horg scratched his beard with a pair of floating tongs. He looked at the boat. He looked at the vibrating compass on his workbench, the needle pinned hard to the North.

"It's mad," Horg decided, a grin splitting his soot-stained face. "I love it. But you have a problem, lad."

"Just one?"

"Stopping," Horg said, kicking a dirt clod. "If you hook this tub to the magnetic line, you're going to be a missile. Momentum is going to be your enemy. If you hit a turn in the Red Canyon at full tilt, you'll smear yourself across the canyon wall. You need brakes."

"I can cut the connection," Kaelan argued. "I'm the switch. If I let go of the magnetic feed, we stop accelerating."

"Aye, you stop *accelerating*," Horg corrected. "But a two-ton oak boat moving at the speed of a galloping horse doesn't just stop because you asked it nicely. You'll slide for miles. You need friction."

Horg got to work.

The retrofit was brutal and loud. The town watched from a safe distance as the blacksmith and the Scion turned a fishing boat into a tank.

The Runners: Horg took the iron bands from the hull and welded thick, polished plates of soft iron over them. He shaped them like sled runners—wide and flat to distribute the weight over grass and gravel, but sharp enough to bite into ice when they reached the mountains.

"Pure iron," Horg grunted, fusing a seam with a shower of sparks. "Conducts the field better. You'll feel the road through these, Kaelan."

The Brakes: This was Horg’s masterpiece. He installed two massive, lever-operated iron "plows" on the stern, flanking the rudder.

"Drag-brakes," Horg explained, demonstrating the mechanism. "You pull this lever, and these teeth dig into the dirt. It'll tear up the road, and it'll shake your teeth out, but it'll slow you down before you hit a tree."

The Steering: The river rudder was useless in the air. Horg replaced it with a heavy-duty "Tail-Skid"—a reinforced iron fin that could be ground into the earth to force the boat to drift-turn.

"It's not precise," Kaela noted, testing the weight of the tiller. "It's brute force."

"It's a boat on dirt, lass," Horg chuckled. "Precision went out the window an hour ago. You steer with weight. You steer with grit."

The Canopy: Finally, they addressed the danger of rolling over. Horg bolted a cage of steel bars over the open cockpit, welding sheets of scrap metal to form a protective shell. It looked less like a cabin and more like a bunker.

By sunset, the *Iron Keel* was unrecognizable. It sat low and heavy on its new runners, the iron gleaming in the dying light.

"She's heavy," Horg warned, slapping the armored flank. "If the magnetism cuts out, you're going to be a very expensive lawn ornament."

"It won't cut out," Kaelan said, looking North. The red aurora was pulsing brighter than ever, a bleeding wound in the sky. "The earth is screaming."

"Then go," Horg said, handing Kaelan a pair of heavy leather goggles. "And bring it back. I'm running out of scrap metal to patch her up."

Kaelan put on the goggles. He climbed into the armored cockpit.

"Kaela?"

She climbed in beside him, strapping herself into the harness Horg had bolted to the bench. She checked the drag-brake levers.

"Brakes are set," she confirmed. "Map is ready. Navigation is... theoretical."

"Theoretical is my favorite kind of plan," Kaelan grinned.

He placed his hands on the iron dashboard Horg had installed. He closed his eyes. He reached out with his mind, past the wood, past the air, feeling for the singing, violent river of magnetic force tearing through the world.

He found it.

"Connecting," Kaelan whispered.

He pushed his will into the iron runners.

*CLANG.*

The boat lurched violently. The iron skids magnetized instantly, locking onto the field lines with a force that groaned through the frame. The boat lifted an inch off the ground, hovering on a cushion of magnetic repulsion.

"Hold on!" Kaelan yelled.

He released the magnetic brake.

The *Iron Keel* didn't roll. It *shot* forward.

It accelerated with a force that threw them back into their seats. They blasted down the main street of Sunstead, kicking up a rooster tail of red dust three stories high, leaving a stunned blacksmith and a cheering town in their wake.

They were surfing the planet.

#### Part 2: The Forest of Ghosts

Theme: adventure-speed-memory

Location: The Foothills of the Razor Peaks

POV: Kaelan

=

The plains of the Emerald Valleys blurred into a smear of green and gold.

Kaelan sat in the stern of the *Iron Keel*, his hands vibrating against the iron tiller. He wasn't steering so much as aiming. The magnetic pull was a relentless, screaming force that wanted to drag them straight North at terminal velocity.

"Speed check?" Kaelan shouted over the roar of the wind.

"Fast!" Kaela shouted back from the passenger seat. She was grinning, her hair whipped into a frenzy by the slipstream leaking through the viewports. "We're doing at least sixty! We just passed the Old Mill in three seconds!"

"Tree line coming up!" Kaelan warned. "The foothills are thick this time of year. We can't take them at this speed."

Ahead, the rolling grass gave way to the dense, dark pines of the lower Razor Peaks. There was no road here—only deer trails and the memory of a path Kaelan had walked alone three years ago.

"Brakes!" Kaela commanded.

Kaelan grabbed the heavy iron levers Horg had installed on his left. He hauled back.

*SCREEEEEEECH.*

The drag-brakes—massive iron plows mounted on the stern—slammed into the earth. Dirt and rocks sprayed thirty feet into the air behind them. The boat shuddered violently, fighting the momentum.

They slowed from a blur to a manageable, terrifying slide.

"Slalom!" Kaelan yelled.

They hit the trees.

Kaelan fought the tiller. He drifted the heavy boat between two ancient oaks, the iron runners scraping bark. He banked off a mossy boulder, sending sparks flying into the ferns.

It was chaos. It was exhilarating.

They slid into a small clearing and coasted to a stop, the magnetic pull humming impatiently in the hull, waiting for Kaelan to release the brakes.

The dust settled.

Kaelan took a deep breath, his heart hammering against his ribs. He looked out the reinforced canopy.

He froze.

He knew this clearing.

It wasn't just a patch of woods. To his left was a familiar, gnarled pine tree with a distinctive split in the trunk. And beneath it was a circle of blackened stones—the remains of a campfire.

"I slept here," Kaelan whispered.

Kaela looked at him. She followed his gaze to the fire pit. "On your way North?"

"Yeah."

Kaelan stared at the stones. A memory washed over him—not a vague recollection, but a visceral, sharp-edged echo of the past.

He remembered sitting right there, three years ago. He had been cold. He had been eating a piece of dried meat that tasted like sawdust.

But he remembered what he had *felt*.

He hadn't felt lonely yet. The loneliness hadn't set in until the bear attack.

That night, sitting by that fire, he had felt... relieved.

*Iron does not cuddle,* the Wedge had whispered in his mind.

He remembered looking at the empty space where his sister should have been and thinking: *Finally. No more noise. No more weakness. I am a warrior now. I don't need anyone.*

He had hugged himself, shivering in the thin blanket, and told himself that the shivering was just weakness leaving the body. He had been proud of his isolation. He had mistaken the numbness of the Wedge for the hardness of a shield.

"Kaelan?" Kaela’s voice pulled him back.

She wasn't looking at the fire pit. She was looking at him, her silver eyes filled with concern. She could feel the shadow of the memory through the Circuit—the cold, oily residue of Malakor’s programming.

Kaelan looked at her.

He looked at the small, iron-reinforced cockpit Horg had built. It was tight. The seats were bolted so close together that their shoulders were pressed firmly against each other.

There was no space here. No "dignity" of distance.

And it was perfect.

"I was such an idiot," Kaelan murmured, shaking his head. "I sat right there and congratulated myself for being alone. I thought I was free."

"You were sick," Kaela said fiercely. She reached out and took his hand, squeezing it hard enough to hurt. "You had a parasite in your head telling you that love was heavy."

"I know," Kaelan said.

He released the tiller. He didn't care about the magnetic pull. He turned in his seat, fighting the constraints of the safety harness, and pulled her into him.

He wrapped his arms around her, burying his face in her neck. He breathed in the scent of her—sage, sweat, and life.

He hugged her with the desperation of a man who knows exactly how cold the alternative is.

"I'm glad Horg built this small," Kaelan whispered into her hair. "I never want to be that far away from you again. Not even across a campfire."

Kaela melted against him, her head resting on his chest. "You won't be. We're a set, Sun. Forever."

"Forever," Kaelan agreed.

He thought about that word. *Forever*.

They were Scions. They were older than the trees outside. They had forged the stars before they were born into these bodies.

These human forms—Kaelan and Kaela—were mortal. They would age. They would eventually wear out, just like the boots he had worn on his first journey.

### Chapter 1: The Shadow at Noon

#### Part 1: The Cold Draft

Theme: ominous-warning

Location: The Cottage Bedroom

POV: Kaela

=

Kaela woke up because the birds had stopped singing.

She lay in bed, staring at the timber beams. Usually, at this hour, the sparrows in the ivy were noisy. The rooster at the mill was crowing. The town was waking up.

But today, there was silence.

She shifted. Kaelan was asleep beside her, lying on his stomach, one arm thrown over her waist. He was a furnace, as always, radiating a steady 99 degrees of comfort.

But the air in the room felt wrong. It didn't feel like morning air. It felt... thin.

"Sun," Kaela whispered, nudging him.

Kaelan groaned into his pillow. "Is Horg here? Tell the door to suggest Luncheon."

"No," Kaela said, sitting up. She shivered. The quilt slipped down, and the air hit her skin. "Something is wrong. It's too quiet."

Kaelan rolled over. He opened his eyes. They were gold, but they looked dim, like a lantern running low on oil.

He sat up. He frowned.

"I feel..." He rubbed his chest. "I feel heavy. Like the gravity is turned up."

"I didn't touch the gravity," Kaela said immediately.

Kaelan got out of bed. He walked to the window. He unlatched the "Guillotine" gravity seal with a touch of his hand and pushed the shutters open.

He froze.

"Kaela," he said. His voice was flat. "Come here."

Kaela climbed out of bed, grabbing her robe. She walked to the window and looked out.

It was noon. The clock on the mantel said so.

But outside, the sky was not blue.

It was a sickly, washed-out grey. The twin suns were there, but they looked wrong. They weren't blindingly bright orbs of fire. They looked like pale, white discs, as if seen through heavy smoked glass.

The light hitting Sunstead was dim, colorless, and cold.

"Where is the heat?" Kaelan asked, reaching his hand out the window. "The suns... I can't feel them. I can't feel the connection."

He usually drew a constant trickle of power from the suns to recharge his internal battery. Now, he felt like he was sucking on a straw that had been pinched shut.

"It's the Unseen," Kaela whispered, gripping the windowsill until the wood cracked.

"How do you know?"

"Because," Kaela pointed to the garden. "Look at the shadows."

Kaelan looked.

The shadows of the trees, the fence, and the tub were not moving. The wind had stopped. The leaves were frozen in mid-sway.

"Stasis," Kaelan realized. "He's not attacking the core. He's dampening the atmosphere. He's trying to suffocate the energy."

"The Unseen is here. He's not sending minions anymore."

Kaelan looked at the shivering townsfolk. He looked at the dim suns. He felt the sluggish beat of the earth's heart, slowed by the cosmic dampening.

And the only thing standing between Sunstead and absolute zero was the internal fire of the Twins.
