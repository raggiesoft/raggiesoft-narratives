# Chapter 2: The Silence of the Hearth

## Part 1: The Fading of the Guardian

##### Scene 1: The Coldest Winter

Theme: sunstead-winter

Location: The Cottage on the Ridge

POV: Kaelan

=

The winter of their sixteenth year did not arrive; it invaded.

It started with a frost that killed the late pumpkins in the garden. Then came the wind, a shrieking gale that funneled down the Red Canyon, stripping the last dead leaves from the oaks and rattling the cottage shutters like a thief trying to break in. Finally, the snow came—not the dusting that usually dusted the high peaks, but a heavy, wet blanket that buried the garden gate and turned the world into a monochromatic prison of grey and white.

Inside the cottage, Kaelan was at war with the cold.

He knelt by the hearth, shoving a split log of seasoned oak into the flames with unnecessary force. The fire roared, climbing up the chimney, but the heat seemed to die the moment it reached the center of the room.

"It's the draft," Kaelan muttered, glaring at the door. "I need to repack the frame."

He stood up. At sixteen, he was nearly fully grown, his shoulders filling out the frame of the doorway. He radiated a dry, intense heat—his internal temperature spiking in response to his anxiety. The air around him shimmered like asphalt on a hot day.

He walked to the window and checked the seal. Ice was forming on the *inside* of the glass.

"Kaelan," a voice rasped from the bed.

Kaelan spun around.

Elara lay under a mountain of wool quilts. She looked small. The formidable woman who had taught him to hold a sword, who had stared down Varek in the market, was gone. In her place was a fragile collection of bones wrapped in paper-thin skin.

"I can make it warmer," Kaelan said, stepping toward the bed. He held up his hands, letting the red glow of his power pulse beneath the skin. "I can heat the stones under the floor."

"You will burn the house down," Elara wheezed. A cough rattled in her chest—a wet, heavy sound that made Kaelan’s stomach clench. "And I am not cold because of the air, little sun. I am cold because the fuel is gone."

Kaelan froze. "Don't say that."

"It is the truth," Elara whispered. She pulled a hand out from the covers to beckon him. Her fingers were trembling. "Come. Sit. Stop pacing. You make the room spin."

Kaelan sat on the edge of the bed. He took her hand. It was freezing. He poured his own warmth into her, trying to push the chill back, but it felt like trying to heat a glacier with a candle.

"The healer in the Capital," Kaelan started, the desperation rising in his throat. "Joren said he knows a path through the pass. I can run it. I can be there and back in three days."

"The Capital is a trap," Elara said, her eyes drifting shut for a moment. "And no healer can fix a wick that has burned down. I have been fighting gravity for sixteen years, Kaelan. I am tired."

Kaela appeared from the kitchen area, carrying a steaming mug of willow-bark tea. She looked pale, her eyes dark with a lack of sleep. She moved quietly, setting the tea on the bedside table.

She didn't speak. She didn't need to. Kaelan felt her mind pressing against his—a wall of heavy, silent grief that she was holding back so Elara wouldn't feel it.

*She's fading,* Kaela projected. It wasn't a question.

*Not if I can help it,* Kaelan shot back, stubborn and loud.

Elara opened her eyes. She looked at Kaela, then at Kaelan.

"Stop shouting," she murmured, a faint smile touching her lips. "I can hear you both thinking from here. It's too loud for a dying woman."

Kaelan flinched. "You're not dying."

"I am," Elara said softly. "And we have work to do before I go."

##### Scene 2: The Inheritance

Theme: sunstead-winter

Location: The Cottage on the Ridge

POV: Kaela

=

The tea went cold on the table. Elara didn't drink it. She seemed to be gathering her strength for something more important than sustenance.

Kaela sat on the stool beside the bed, her hands folded in her lap to keep them from shaking. She was using the Weave to monitor Elara’s heart.

It was a terrifying rhythm. *Thump... pause... thump... flutter.*

It sounded like a bird trying to fly with a broken wing.

"The chest," Elara commanded, pointing a shaking finger toward the foot of the bed. "Open it."

Kaelan got up. He knelt before the cedar chest—the one Elara kept locked, the one they were never allowed to touch as children. He lifted the heavy lid.

Inside, wrapped in oilcloth, were two items.

"Bring them," Elara whispered.

Kaelan carried the bundles to the bed. He unwrapped them gently.

The first was a cloak. It was made of heavy, midnight-blue wool, thick and soft. The clasp was silver, shaped like a diving hawk.

"For you, Kaela," Elara said. "It is woven with threads of obfuscation. It will hide you from eyes in the sky. It smells of home."

Kaela reached out and touched the fabric. It didn't just smell of home; it smelled of *Elara*—of sage, woodsmoke, and safety. She pulled it to her chest, burying her face in it to stifle a sob.

"And for you, Kaelan," Elara said.

The second item was a dagger. It wasn't a weapon of war like his iron sword. It was a tool. The handle was pale weir-wood, and the blade was a dull, grey steel that seemed to absorb the firelight.

"It is not for fighting," Elara rasped. "It is for cutting the threads that bind you. When the time comes... and it will come... use it to make your own path."

Kaelan picked up the dagger. He looked at it with disdain. He wanted a cure, not a knife.

"I don't want this," Kaelan choked out, his voice cracking. "I want you to stay."

"I know," Elara smiled, a ghost of her old, fierce expression appearing for a second. "That is why I must go. As long as I am here, you will always be children. You will always hide behind my skirts."

She coughed, her body seizing. The rhythm in Kaela’s mind stuttered alarmingly. *Thump... ... ... thump.*

"Elara!" Kaela gasped, reaching out to steady her.

Elara settled back against the pillows, her breath coming in shallow gasps. Her eyes were losing focus, looking past them, toward the ceiling, or perhaps through it.

"Listen to me," Elara commanded, her voice gaining a sudden, final strength. "The world is wide, and it is dangerous. But you are the dawn. Do not let the darkness tell you who you are."

She grabbed Kaelan’s hand with surprising strength. "Do not let your anger burn your house down."

She grabbed Kaela’s hand. "Do not let your silence become a cage."

She squeezed.

"Sing to me," she requested softly. "The Hearth Song."

Kaelan shook his head, tears streaming down his face. He couldn't speak. His throat was swollen shut.

Kaela looked at her brother. She saw the panic rising in him, the heat building. If she didn't act, he would explode.

She took a breath. Her voice wavered, thin and fragile in the cold room.

*"Sleep now, embers, deep and red..."*

She kicked Kaelan under the bed. *Sing,* she projected. *For her.*

Kaelan swallowed. He joined in on the third line, his voice rough and deep, cracking on the low notes.

*"Stone be strong and wood be dry,* *Let the storm go passing by..."*

Elara closed her eyes. Her breathing slowed, syncing with the rhythm of the song she had taught them to keep the thunder away when they were seven.

*"Gold for morning, Silver for night,* *Keep the circle warm and bright..."*

The grip on their hands loosened.

*"Brother’s shield and Sister’s eye..."*

The drumbeat in Kaela’s mind stopped.

*"Safe beneath... the stormy sky."*

The last note hung in the air, vibrating in the silence.

Elara didn't exhale. She was just... gone. The mountain had finally eroded.

Kaela sat there, holding the cooling hand. She didn't scream. She didn't wail. She just felt the world get suddenly, terrifyingly larger. The roof was gone. The walls were gone.

They were exposed.

##### Scene 3: The Frozen Earth

Theme: sunstead-winter

Location: The Ridge (Outside)

POV: Kaelan

=

Kaelan sat by the bed for a long time. He waited for the explosion. He waited for the rage to take him, to turn him into a supernova that would vaporize the cottage and the snow and the unfairness of it all.

But the explosion didn't come.

Instead, a terrible, heavy cold settled into his chest. It wasn't the winter air; it was the realization that he was now the oldest thing in the house.

"She's gone," Kaelan whispered.

"I know," Kaela said. She reached across the body. She touched Kaelan’s face. "We're alone, Kaelan."

Kaelan looked at his sister. He saw the terror in her eyes, reflecting his own. She looked small again.

"No," Kaelan said, his voice hardening into the first layer of the armor he would wear for the next two years. "We aren't alone. I'm here. I'm the shield now."

He stood up. He let go of Elara’s hand. He walked to the door and threw it open.

The blizzard rushed in, biting and cruel. Kaelan didn't flinch. He let the cold hit him. He welcomed it. It was better than the numbness.

"I will bury her," Kaelan said.

"The ground is frozen," Kaela whispered. "We'll have to wait for the thaw."

"I am not waiting," Kaelan growled. "She belongs in the earth. Not in here."

He walked out into the storm.

He grabbed the heavy iron shovel from the shed. He marched to the garden, to the spot under the old oak tree where Elara liked to sit in the summer.

The ground was like iron.

Kaelan raised the shovel and drove it down. It clanged against the frozen dirt and bounced off, sending a shockwave up his arms.

He swung again. And again. He barely chipped the surface.

"Break!" Kaelan screamed at the ground.

He dropped the shovel. He fell to his knees in the snow.

He placed his hands on the earth.

He didn't regulate. He didn't hold back. He channeled every ounce of his grief, his fear, and his anger into his palms.

*Burn.*

His hands turned white-hot. The snow around him hissed and evaporated instantly into steam. The frozen dirt smoked, then blackened, then glowed red.

Kaelan screamed, a raw, primal sound of agony, as he poured his life force into the ground. He melted the permafrost. He boiled the moisture in the soil. He turned the earth into mud, then into dry dust.

He dug with his hands. He scooped out the superheated earth, ignoring the way it singed his tunic. He dug until he was shoulder-deep. He dug until the grave was deep enough to hide the only mother he had ever known.

When he climbed out, he was exhausted. He was shivering, his internal fire dangerously low.

Kaela was standing there, wrapped in the blue cloak Elara had given her. She was holding a bundle—Elara’s body, wrapped in her favorite quilt.

Kaelan nodded.

Together, they lowered her into the warm earth.

Kaelan filled the grave. The snow began to cover it almost immediately, a white shroud for a white soul.

They stood there in the storm, two children who had been forced to become adults in the span of an hour.

"What do we do now?" Kaela asked, her voice lost in the wind.

Kaelan looked at the grave. He looked at the horizon, obscured by the snow.

"We survive," Kaelan said.

But as he looked at the cottage, he realized it wasn't a home anymore. It was a tomb. And he knew, with a sinking certainty, that he couldn't stay here. Not with the silence.

##### Scene 4: The Rejection

Theme: sunstead-winter

Location: The Cottage on the Ridge

POV: Kaela

=

The silence in the cottage was heavy. It wasn't the peaceful quiet of the forest; it was the suffocating silence of a held breath.

It had been one week since they buried Elara. The snow was still piling up against the door.

Kaela sat on the edge of the bed—their bed. The small wooden frame that had held them both through thunderstorms, nightmares, and leaks.

She was cold. The fire was dying down, and the draft from the window was biting.

Kaelan stood by the hearth. He wasn't feeding the fire; he was staring at it. He held the weir-wood dagger Elara had given him, flipping it over and over in his hand. Thwip. Catch. Thwip. Catch.

He looked different. His shoulders were set in a rigid line. The "hum" in his mind—the connection Kaela usually felt—was walled off. He was broadcasting nothing but static.

"Kaelan?" Kaela whispered.

He didn't turn. "What?"

"I'm cold."

It was the code. For ten years, "I'm cold" meant come hold me. It meant be the big spoon. It meant I need you.

Kaelan stopped flipping the knife. He stared at the flames.

"Put another log on," he said flatly.

Kaela flinched. She looked at the empty space beside her on the mattress.

"The wood is wet," she said, her voice trembling slightly. "It won't catch fast enough. Just... come here. Please."

She reached out her hand, palm open, waiting for him to take it.

Kaelan turned. He looked at her hand. Then he looked at her face. His eyes were hard, the gold in them dull.

He saw his sister needing comfort. But to him, it didn't look like comfort. It looked like weakness. He had held Elara’s hand while she died, and his warmth hadn't saved her. His softness hadn't stopped the winter.

The shield must be iron, he thought. Iron doesn't cuddle.

"No," Kaelan said.

He walked past the bed. He didn't touch her. He didn't even look at her as he passed.

He walked to the far corner of the room, near the loom. He grabbed a spare blanket from the chest—a scratchy, thin horse-blanket.

He threw it on the floor.

"Kaelan?" Kaela asked, her voice cracking. "What are you doing?"

"I'm sleeping here," Kaelan said, kicking the blanket into a rough shape. "The bed is too small. I can't sleep when I'm crowded."

"But... we always..." Kaela started, but her throat closed up.

Hot, stinging tears spilled from her eyes. She couldn't stop them. She wrapped her arms around herself, feeling the sudden, vast distance between them like a physical blow.

"Kaelan, please," she sobbed, the sound small and broken in the cold room. "Don't leave me alone."

Kaelan looked up from the floor. He saw her crying.

He didn't soften. His jaw tightened. To him, the tears weren't sad; they were annoying. They were proof that she wasn't ready for the world that had just killed their mother.

"We aren't children anymore, Kaela," he snapped, his voice rising. The air around him shimmered with a sudden spike of dry heat. "Stop crying. Stop acting like a scared little girl. Learn to keep yourself warm."

He lay down on the hard floor, turning his back to her. He pulled the scratchy blanket over his head.

Kaela sat there, her hand still outstretched toward empty air, tears dripping off her chin onto the quilt.

She felt the severance physically. It felt like a thread snapping in her chest.

She pulled her knees up, burying her face in them to stifle the sounds of her crying, because she realized he wasn't going to come. He was right there, five feet away, but he was gone.

She looked at the big, empty bed that suddenly felt like a frozen wasteland.

"Okay," she whispered to the dark, wiping her face with the back of her hand. "I'll learn."

That night, she didn't sleep. She wove a mental wall, brick by brick, to block out the sound of his breathing, because hearing him so close yet so far away hurt more than the cold ever could.

## Part 2: The Cage of Silence

##### Scene 1: Now that Elara has passed

The cottage on the ridge felt smaller than it used to.

Perhaps it was because Kaelan and Kaela were sixteen now, their limbs long and their presence filling the room. Or perhaps it was because the rocking chair in the corner—the one where Elara used to sit and whittle arrow shafts—had been empty for six months.

The morning sun filtered through the cracks in the shutters, painting dusty stripes across the single bed pushed against the far wall.

It was a narrow bed, built for one, or for two small children seeking comfort from a nightmare. It was not built for two young adults.

Kaelan lay on his back, staring at the thatched roof. His arm was draped over his eyes, his body radiating a heat that made the blankets stifle. Next to him, curled on her side, lay Kaela.

In their sleep, they had drifted together. It was an instinct carved into their bones before they could walk. Kaela’s head rested near his shoulder, her arm draped loosely over his chest. In the past, this was how they recharged—a circuit of energy, the Sun and the Moon finding equilibrium.

But this morning, Kaelan felt like he was suffocating.

He could feel Kaela’s dreams. They were soft, heavy things—memories of Elara, the smell of baking bread, the desire for the day to stay exactly as it was. Her contentment was a hum in his mind, a lullaby he didn't want to hear.

His own mind was screaming. It was shouting about the horizon. About the sword leaning against the wall. About the wasted days spent hiding in a town that didn't care about glory.

He shifted abruptly, pulling his arm away and rolling out of the bed.

Kaela woke instantly. The loss of his warmth was a physical shock. She sat up, rubbing the sleep from her eyes, her copper hair a tangled halo.

"Kaelan?" she murmured, her voice thick with sleep. "Is something wrong?"

"It’s hot," Kaelan muttered, not looking at her. He grabbed his tunic from the floor and pulled it on, his movements jerky and impatient. "The air in here is dead."

Kaela watched him. She could feel the jagged edges of his mood. It wasn't just the heat. It was her. Or rather, it was being here.

"I can open the shutters," she offered, reaching out a hand as if to touch his back.

Kaelan stepped out of reach before she could make contact. "Don't bother. I’m going out."

He walked to the small shrine they had built on the mantelpiece—a single candle burning in front of Elara’s old dagger. He stared at the flame, his jaw set.

"You’re going to the tavern again," Kaela said. It wasn't a question. "To listen to the travelers."

Kaelan spun around. "So what if I am? Someone has to know what’s happening in the world, Kaela. Elara is gone. We can't just... sit here. We can't just be dust in the wind forever."

"We aren't dust," Kaela said softly, pulling her knees to her chest. "We are safe. That’s what she wanted."

"Safety is a cage," Kaelan snapped. The air around him shimmered, distorting the light in the room. "I’m sixteen years old. I can lift an anvil over my head. I can fight three guards at once. And for what? To scrub pots? To hide?"

He paced the small room, his energy making the candle flame dance wildly.

"I heard them talking last night," Kaelan said, his voice dropping to a conspiratorial whisper. "The caravan from the North. They say there’s a new power rising in the Voidlands. A Sage."

Kaela froze. A cold shiver ran down her spine—a warning bell from deep within her intuition. "A Sage?"

"Not like the dusty old men in the Capital," Kaelan said, his eyes lighting up with a dangerous hunger. "They say he accepts anyone with power. He doesn't hide them. He trains them. He teaches them to be gods."

"Kaelan, no," Kaela whispered. "Elara warned us about the North. She said the Voidlands are cursed."

"Elara was afraid!" Kaelan shouted. The word cracked in the room like a whip.

Kaela flinched. They never spoke ill of Elara.

"She was afraid," Kaelan repeated, quieter but with more conviction. "She spent her whole life hiding. I don't want to hide, Kaela. I want to be something."

He walked back to the bed. He looked down at his sister. She looked small in the tangle of blankets, her eyes wide and wet with unshed tears. She looked like the past.

Kaela reached up. She wanted to pull him down. She wanted to wrap her arms around him and hold him until the "shouting" in his spirit quieted down, until they were just Kaelan and Kaela, back-to-back against the world.

"Please," she whispered. "Just stay. Just for today. Hold me?"

It was a plea for the comfort they had shared since birth.

Kaelan looked at her outstretched arms. For a second, he wavered. He remembered the warmth. He remembered the safety.

But then the "shouting" of his ambition drowned it out. He saw her love not as an anchor, but as a chain. If he lay back down, he feared he would never get up again.

He shook his head, a sharp, brutal motion.

"We aren't children anymore, Kaela," he said coldly. "Stop acting like one."

He turned his back on her. He grabbed his sword belt, buckled it around his waist, and walked out the door without looking back.

The door slammed shut.

Kaela was left alone in the silence. She stared at the empty space beside her in the small bed. It felt vast. It felt like a canyon.

She pulled the blanket up to her chin, breathing in the fading scent of him—iron and ozone—and for the first time in sixteen years, she wept not because he was hurt, but because he was gone.

Outside, Kaelan marched down the dusty path toward the town. He told himself he felt lighter. He told himself he felt free. But deep down, beneath the roar of his ambition, there was a small, quiet ache where his sister used to be.

He ignored it. He turned his eyes toward the North.

##### Scene 2: A Rough Bedtime

Theme: sunstead-rain

Location: The Cottage on the Ridge

POV: Kaela

=

The cottage smelled of burnt sugar and desperation.

It was raining—a cold, miserable spring drizzle that turned the red dust of the ridge into slick mud. Inside, Kaela sat at the small wooden table.

She had set two places. She had polished the wooden bowls until they shone. In the center of the table sat a lopsided honey-cake. It wasn't as pretty as the ones the Matron made—it was a bit dense, and the honey glaze was running off the side—but it was hot.

She had spent three hours bartering with the miller for the flour. She had spent two hours coaxing the damp wood in the hearth to burn evenly.

She smoothed her dress. She waited.

The suns had set an hour ago. The candle in the center of the cake was burning down, dripping wax onto the frosting.

Finally, the latch clicked.

The door swung open. Kaelan ducked to enter.

At seventeen, he was massive. The "growth spurt" Elara had predicted had hit him like a landslide. He was broad-shouldered, lean, and hardened by a year of grueling, self-imposed labor at the forge.

He was soaking wet. He smelled of coal smoke, wet iron, and old sweat.

He didn't look at the table. He didn't look at her.

He walked straight to the hearth and began unbuckling his tool belt. The heavy iron tongs and hammers hit the floor with a loud *clang-clang-clang*.

"You're late," Kaela said softly.

"Horg needed a rail fixed," Kaelan grunted, not turning around. He stripped off his soaked tunic and threw it into the corner—the corner that used to be Elara’s weaving space, now just a pile of his dirty laundry.

The sight of his bare back—muscled, scarred from forge burns, and tense—made Kaela’s chest ache. She remembered when that back used to be her pillow.

"I made dinner," Kaela said. "And cake."

Kaelan stiffened. He grabbed a rag and started scrubbing the soot from his arms. "I ate at the forge. Jace brought stew."

"It's honey-cake, Kaelan."

"I'm not hungry."

"It's our birthday," she whispered.

Kaelan stopped scrubbing. The silence stretched, tight as a bowstring.

For a second, Kaela hoped. She hoped he would turn around. She hoped he would smile, that the "Iron Shield" mask would slip, and he would be her brother again. She hoped he would walk over, pull her into a hug, and get soot all over her clean dress.

*Please,* she projected, a soft, desperate thread of invitation. *Just for tonight. Be soft.*

Kaelan felt the thread. And he cut it.

He tossed the rag down. "We aren't children, Kaela. We don't need cakes."

He walked to his corner—the drafty spot by the door where he slept on a pallet of furs now. He sat down and began unlacing his boots.

"I made you something," Kaela said, her voice trembling. She picked up a small bundle from the table. "It's a liner for your sword belt. Wool. So it doesn't chafe your hip."

She walked over to him. She held it out.

Kaelan looked at the gift. He looked at her hand.

If he took it, he would have to touch her. If he touched her, he might break. He might remember that he was lonely. And guardians couldn't afford to be lonely.

"Put it on the shelf," he said, staring at his boots. "I'm tired."

Kaela stood there. Her hand dropped.

"Why do you hate me?" she asked. The question hung in the damp air, raw and bleeding.

Kaelan looked up then. His gold-blue eyes were fierce, burning with a mixture of love and self-hatred that Kaela couldn't untangle.

"I don't hate you," he rasped. "I'm keeping you alive. Go to sleep, Kaela."

He lay down on the hard floor and turned his back to her. He pulled the rough blanket over his head, effectively building a wall in a room without one.

Kaela walked back to the table. She looked at the cake. She looked at the dying candle.

She blew it out.

She didn't eat the cake. She sat in the dark, listening to the rain, wishing he would just scream at her. Screaming was better than this. Screaming was contact. This... this was a ghost haunting a house she was still living in.

She walked to the big, empty bed. She climbed in, curling into a tight ball on the far edge, leaving enough room for him in case he changed his mind.

For an hour, the only sound in the cottage was the drumming of the rain and the occasional pop of a dying ember in the hearth.

Then, there was a groan from the floor.

Kaelan shifted. The floorboards were unforgiving. His muscles, already sore from the forge, were locking up in the cold draft. He tried to get comfortable, but the hard wood dug into his hip. He wasn't a martyr; he was just exhausted.

He sat up with a growl of frustration. He dragged his blanket with him and stalked over to the bed.

Kaela froze, her heart hammering. She didn't move. She pretended to be asleep.

The mattress dipped as Kaelan climbed in. He lay down on his side, facing away from her, keeping a rigid six inches of space between them. He pulled the covers up to his chin, his body radiating a tense, angry heat.

Kaela waited. The heat coming off him was magnetic. It was the only warmth in the room.

Slowly, carefully, she shifted. She scooted across the gap.

She reached out. She draped her arm over his waist, resting her hand flat against his chest. She pressed her forehead against his shoulder blades, seeking the familiar rhythm of his breathing.

In the old days—before the winter, before the grave—Kaelan would have sighed. He would have leaned back into her. He would have taken her hand and laced his fingers through hers, anchoring them together against the dark.

Tonight, he did nothing.

He didn't push her away. He didn't tell her to stop. He just lay there, stiff and unmoving.

It was like hugging a statue.

He tolerated her touch, but he didn't return it. He didn't relax into the embrace; he endured it. He held his breath, his muscles coiled tight, as if her affection was a weight he had to carry rather than a comfort he could share.

Kaela kept her arm around him. She squeezed him gently, a silent plea. *I'm here. We're safe.*

Kaelan stared at the wall. He felt the warmth of her hand on his chest. He wanted to grab it. He wanted to turn around and bury his face in her hair and tell her he was scared.

But shields don't get scared. Shields don't need hugs.

He forced himself to be stone. He forced himself to be the wall.

Kaela eventually stopped squeezing. Her hand went slack against his chest, though she didn't pull away. She fell asleep holding onto him, alone in the bed they shared, realizing that the brother she loved had turned into iron right under her fingertips.

##### Scene 3: The Silence at the Forge

Theme: sunstead-grief

Location: Horg’s Smithy

Time: Six months after Elara’s death (Age 16.5)

Horg clamped the red-hot plowshare onto the anvil with his tongs. "Hit it, lad!" he bellowed. "While the color is cherry!"

Usually, this was the moment the shop came alive. Kaelan would swing the heavy sledgehammer with a grin, Kaela (sitting on her usual barrel in the corner) would wince theatrically at the noise, and the two of them would banter.

“Too loud, Sun,” she would say. “Iron has to scream to change shape, Moon,” he would retort.

But today, there was no banter.

Kaelan swung the hammer. CLANG.

It was a perfect strike. Mechanically flawless. The iron flattened obediently.

CLANG. CLANG.

Horg frowned through the soot on his face. He looked at the boy. Kaelan wasn't smiling. His jaw was set so hard a muscle jumped in his cheek. He wasn't looking at the corner where his sister usually sat.

Horg looked at the corner. The barrel was empty.

Kaela had stopped coming to the forge three weeks ago. She used to bring a waterskin at noon. Now, she left it on the porch of their cottage, as if afraid to intrude.

"Ease up, Kaelan," Horg grunted. "You're bruising the metal. It’s a plow, not a warhammer."

Kaelan stopped mid-swing. He was breathing hard, sweat slicking his chest. The heat coming off him was intense—dry and angry, singeing the hair on Horg’s arms.

"It's done," Kaelan said shortly. He tossed the hammer onto the rack. He didn't wipe his face. He didn't ask how Horg’s bad knee was doing.

He just walked to the water bucket, drank from the ladle, and stared out the open door at the dusty road.

"Where's the sister today?" Horg asked, testing the waters. "I have a craving for those berry tarts she makes."

Kaelan stiffened. He didn't turn around. "She's at the cottage. Weaving."

"She hasn't been down in a while," Horg pressed gently. "The forge is quiet without her humming."

"It's a workspace, Horg," Kaelan snapped. The air around him shimmered with a heat haze. "It's supposed to be quiet. Distractions get you burned."

Horg watched the boy’s back. He saw the tension in the shoulders—the heavy, crushing weight of trying to be an adult too fast.

Horg remembered the little boy who used to panic if he lost sight of his sister in the market square. He remembered the twins sleeping in a heap under the harvest table.

"Iron gets brittle if you work it too cold, lad," Horg warned softly. "And it breaks if you quench it too fast."

Kaelan turned then. His eyes—gold and blue—were full of a misery so deep it looked like rage.

"I'm not brittle," Kaelan rasped. "I'm just busy."

He grabbed his tunic and marched out into the sunlight, leaving the blacksmith alone with the cooling iron.

Horg looked at the empty barrel in the corner. He sighed, a heavy sound in the quiet shop.

"You're not busy, son," Horg whispered to the empty doorway. "You're just lonely."

##### Scene 4: The Drunken Mule

Theme: sunstead-night

Location: The Drunken Mule Tavern

POV: Kaelan

=

The Drunken Mule was the loudest place in Sunstead, which was exactly why Kaelan was there.

The air inside was thick with the smell of cheap ale, unwashed bodies, and roasting onions. It was a sensory assault that drowned out the suffocating silence of the cottage on the ridge. Here, amidst the shouting drovers and laughing miners, Kaelan didn't have to listen to the sound of his sister breathing in a bed he was too afraid to sleep in comfortably.

He sat at a small, wobbly table in the darkest corner, nursing a tankard of spiced apple juice that had gone lukewarm.

He was trying to be invisible, but in a town this small, invisibility was impossible. Especially when you were six-foot-one, built like a siege engine, and sitting alone in a place where you historically never set foot.

A shadow fell over his table.

Kaelan looked up. Horg the Blacksmith loomed over him, holding a frothy mug of ale. The big man wasn't smiling. He wasn't doing his usual boisterous "King of the Tavern" routine. He looked concerned.

"You're taking up a paying seat, lad," Horg rumbled, though there was no heat in it.

"I paid for the juice," Kaelan muttered, looking back down at his cup.

Horg pulled out the chair opposite him and sat down. The wood groaned in protest.

"You don't drink spirits," Horg stated flatly. "And you hate crowds. You say they make your skin itch."

"I changed my mind," Kaelan lied.

"Did you?" Horg leaned forward, his massive forearms resting on the sticky table. He lowered his voice, breaking the unwritten rule of Sunstead privacy. "And where is the other half? I haven't seen Kaela since the market closed. Usually, where there's smoke, there's fire. Where there's Kaelan, there's Kaela."

Kaelan flinched. The names sounded too similar. They sounded like a set.

"She's at the cottage," Kaelan said, his jaw tight. "We aren't... we don't have to be everywhere together, Horg. We aren't toddlers."

Horg studied him. He looked at the tension in Kaelan’s shoulders, the dark circles under his eyes, and the way his hand kept drifting to the hilt of the weir-wood dagger on his belt.

"You're right," Horg said softly. "You aren't toddlers. You're a young man who looks like he's trying to fight a war that hasn't started yet."

Horg took a swig of ale.

"Go home, Kaelan," Horg advised gently. "Whatever is eating you... it can't be drowned in apple juice. And leaving her alone up there in the dark isn't going to fix it."

"I'm not leaving her alone," Kaelan snapped, a flash of heat warming the air around the table. "I'm keeping watch."

"Watch for what?" Horg asked. "The wolves are gone, lad. It's just us."

Horg stood up. He clapped a heavy, soot-stained hand on Kaelan’s shoulder.

"Go home."

Horg walked back to the bar, leaving Kaelan feeling more exposed than ever.

Kaelan stared at his juice. He felt a spike of shame. Horg was right. He didn't belong here. He was just hiding.

He was about to stand up when a voice from the table behind him cut through the noise.

"Quiet down, you lout."

Kaelan froze.

It wasn't a local voice. It wasn't the dusty drawl of the plateau. It was sharp, clipped, and carried the specific, nasal accent of the Northern borderlands.

Kaelan sank back into his chair. He tuned out the fiddle. He tuned out Horg. He focused entirely on the conversation behind his left shoulder.

"The roads are closed past the Twin Forks," a second voice murmured. It sounded nervous. "My cousin tried to run a shipment of grain last week. He didn't come back."

"Bandits?" the first man asked.

"Worse," the nervous voice said. "Silence. The patrols say the villages up there are just... empty. No bodies. No fires. Just gone."

Kaelan’s grip on his tankard tightened. The wood creaked loudly.

"It's him," the first man whispered, his voice dropping so low Kaelan had to strain to hear it. "The High Sage. They say he didn't die in the exile. They say he found something in the Voidlands."

"A weapon?"

"A tower," the man corrected. "A Shadowspire. He's gathering the old magic. He's recruiting, Jareth. Not soldiers. *Vessels*."

Kaelan felt a spike of heat in his chest that had nothing to do with the room temperature.

*Vessels.*

Elara’s dying words echoed in his memory: *The world is wide, and it is dangerous. You are the dawn.*

He wasn't just a boy with a temper. He was a vessel. He knew it. He felt it every time he touched a hot coal and didn't burn. He felt it in the restless energy that made his skin itch.

"If the Sage is back," the nervous voice said, "the King will send the army."

"The King is an old fool staring at stars," the first man scoffed. "He doesn't look at the dirt. By the time the Capital wakes up, the Shadow will have eaten the North. And then it will come here."

The men fell silent as the serving girl dropped a pitcher of ale on their table.

Kaelan sat perfectly still. The noise of the tavern—the laughter, the stomping boots, Horg’s booming voice—faded into a dull roar.

He looked at his hands. They were trembling. Not with fear, but with recognition.

*This is it,* Kaelan thought.

For two years, he had been suffocating. He had been trying to be a shield for a threat that wasn't there, guarding a cottage against nothing but the wind. He had been hurting Kaela because he didn't know what else to do with his strength. He was a weapon collecting dust on a shelf.

But this... this was a target.

A Shadow in the North. A monster that ate villages.

If he went there—if he fought this Shadow—he wouldn't be the dangerous brother anymore. He would be the Hero. He could burn all he wanted, and it would be *right*.

He stood up. He left a copper coin on the table for the juice he hadn't drunk.

He walked out of the tavern, ducking his head to avoid Horg’s gaze. He stepped into the cool night air of the street.

He looked North, past the canyon walls, toward the dark horizon where the stars seemed to be swallowed by an unseen mouth.

"I'm going," Kaelan whispered to the night.

He turned and began the long walk up the path to the cottage. He wouldn't ask for permission. He wouldn't ask for a hug.

He was leaving tonight.

## Part 3: The Weight of the Shield

The wind at the edge of the plateau tasted of freedom.

Kaelan stood at the Western Gate of Sunstead. It was three hours past midnight. The town was asleep, save for the drowsy guard in the watchtower who hadn't noticed the shadow slipping past the perimeter.

Kaelan shifted the weight of his pack. He had taken only what he needed: a waterskin, dried meat, a coil of rope, and the heavy iron sword he had forged himself.

He looked North.

Out there, beyond the jagged silhouette of the Razor Peaks, lay the Voidlands. And somewhere in that frozen waste was the Sage. The stories whispered in the tavern spoke of a teacher who didn't care about "balance" or "hiding." They spoke of a man who taught his students to burn bright enough to challenge the gods.

That is where I belong, Kaelan thought. Not here. Not scrubbing pots. Not stifling my fire so the neighbors don't get scared.

He took a step forward. The red dust crunched beneath his boot.

It was easy. Just one step, then another. The road stretched out before him, silver in the moonlight, promising glory. He imagined himself returning years later, a master of his power, a true hero who could scour the evil from the world. Kaela would understand then. She would see that he had to leave to become strong enough to save them all.

He took ten more steps. Then twenty.

Then he stopped.

He didn't stop because of a noise. He stopped because of the silence.

Without Kaela nearby, the constant, low-level hum in the back of his mind—the psychic echo of her presence—was fading. For the first time in sixteen years, his head was quiet.

It was what he wanted. It was what he had craved this morning in the suffocating heat of the cottage.

But now that he had it, it felt... cold.

He closed his eyes, trying to focus on the thrill of adventure. But instead, an image pushed its way into his mind. He saw Kaela sleeping in that narrow, empty bed. He saw the way she flinched when people raised their voices. He saw her dodging blows in the training circle—always defensive, never striking to kill.

She is soft, the voice in his head whispered. She is the Moon. She has no light of her own. If the Sun leaves, she goes dark.

Kaelan gripped the strap of his pack until his knuckles turned white.

If he left, who would watch her back? Who would step in when she hesitated? If the King’s hunters ever returned, Kaela would try to talk to them. She would try to understand them. And they would kill her.

A wave of nausea hit him. It wasn't fear for himself; it was a sickening realization of her vulnerability.

"Damn it," Kaelan hissed into the dark.

He looked at the Northern horizon one last time. He could almost feel the power waiting for him there—a magnetic pull, like iron to a lodestone. He wanted it. He wanted to tear down the evils of the world.

But I cannot build a legend on her grave, he thought bitterly.

He turned around.

The walk back to the cottage was harder than the walk away. Each step felt heavy, like he was dragging chains. He wasn't returning with joy; he was returning with resignation. He was a soldier reporting for guard duty at a post he hated.

He reached the cottage. The door was unlocked.

He pushed it open quietly. The room was dark, save for the dying ember of the candle on the mantelpiece.

He expected her to be asleep. She wasn't.

Kaela was sitting up in the narrow bed, her knees pulled to her chest. She wasn't crying anymore. She was just staring at the empty space where he should have been, her eyes wide and hollow in the gloom.

She looked up when he entered. She didn't speak. She didn't ask where he had been. She knew. She had felt the severance of their bond when he walked away, and she had felt the snap of its return.

Kaelan dropped his pack by the door. The thud sounded like a gavel falling.

He unbuckled his sword belt and leaned the weapon against the wall. He didn't look at her. He couldn't bear to see the relief in her eyes because it made him feel trapped.

He walked to the bed. He sat down on the edge, his back to her, pulling off his boots.

"You came back," Kaela whispered. Her voice was small, fragile.

"Someone has to watch the door," Kaelan grunted. He lay down, keeping a rigid distance between them. He stared up at the thatch roof. "You’d get yourself killed within a week without me."

It was a cruel thing to say, but he needed to say it. He needed to frame this as a burden, as a duty, to protect his own pride.

Kaela didn't argue. She didn't point out that she was faster than him, or that she sensed danger long before he did. She simply accepted his narrative because it meant he was staying.

"Thank you," she breathed.

She shifted, tentatively reaching out to bridge the gap. She rested her hand on his arm—a silent offer of the old comfort.

Kaelan didn't shake her off this time. But he didn't lean into it, either. He lay stiff, staring into the darkness.

"I’m not going to stay here forever, Kaela," he warned, his voice low and hard. "There is something growing in the North. I can feel it. And one day, I’m going to go there and crush it."

"I know," Kaela said. "But not tonight."

"No," Kaelan agreed, closing his eyes as the familiar, stifling hum of their bond settled back over his mind. "Not tonight."

He drifted into a restless sleep, dreaming of obsidian towers and a voice that promised him he could be a god, while Kaela lay awake beside him, holding onto his arm as if it were the only solid thing in a spinning world.

## Part 4: The Hollow Silence

The woods outside Sunstead were quiet, save for the crunch of dry leaves under boots and the rhythmic thwack of Kaelan’s machete clearing vines that didn't need clearing.

"You're making too much noise," Kaela said softly. She was kneeling near a patch of wild mushrooms, carefully cutting the stems. "If there’s a deer nearby, you’ve chased it halfway to the Capital."

"Let it run," Kaelan grunted, hacking at a innocent sapling. "I’m not in the mood to hunt. I’m in the mood to hit something."

He wiped sweat from his brow. It was another scorching afternoon. The air in the canyon felt heavy, like wool blanket draped over the sun.

"We have enough for the stew," Kaela said, standing up and brushing dirt from her knees. She looked at him, her eyes searching his face. "We can go back. You can go to the forge. You like the forge."

"I don't like the forge, Kaela," Kaelan snapped. "I tolerate it because pounding hot iron is the only time I don't feel like I’m suffocating."

He turned away from her, staring into the dense treeline. The resentment was a living thing in his chest now. Every time he looked at her, he felt the invisible tether holding him to this dusty town.

"I’m going to check the snare lines by the creek," he lied. He just wanted to walk five minutes without hearing her thoughts echo in his head. "Stay here."

Kaela opened her mouth to argue, but then she froze.

Her head snapped toward the East. Her eyes went wide, the gold flecks vanishing into dilated pupils.

"Kaelan," she whispered. The tone wasn't fear; it was dread. "Don't move."

"What?" Kaelan asked, annoyed. "Is it a wolf? I can handle a—"

"No," Kaela hissed. She dropped her basket. "Shadows. They don't... they don't have a sound."

Before Kaelan could sneer, the sunlight in the clearing died.

It didn't fade like a passing cloud. It was snuffed out. The shadows of the trees suddenly detached themselves from the ground, rising up like black oil, twisting into tall, humanoid shapes. There were six of them. They wore armor made of silence and held chains that smoked with cold vapor.

Malakor’s Retrievers.

"Run!" Kaela screamed.

But Kaelan didn't run. He grinned.

Finally, he thought. Something real.

"Get behind me!" Kaelan roared.

He drew his heavy iron sword. The air around him flash-heated. The dry leaves at his feet curled and blackened. He didn't wait for a strategy. He didn't listen to Kaela’s warning pinging in his mind (Left flank! Two of them!). He charged the nearest shadow.

"For Sunstead!" he yelled, swinging the blade with enough force to shatter stone.

The sword connected. There was a sound like a hammer hitting a wet sack. The shadow creature didn't bleed; it dissolved into mist, only to reform instantly behind him.

A chain lashed out, wrapping around Kaelan’s ankle.

The cold was shocking. It bit through his boot, searing his skin with ice. Kaelan howled, not in pain, but in fury. He planted his foot and pulled, channeling his heat into the chain. The metal glowed red, then white, and shattered.

"Is that all you have?" Kaelan laughed, the adrenaline intoxicating. He was a god of fire. He was untouchable.

He spun around to strike again, completely ignoring the other four figures closing in.

"Kaelan! The net!" Kaela’s voice screamed in his head.

He ignored her. He was too busy being the hero.

He didn't see the two Retrievers bypass him entirely. They weren't aiming for the raging fire; they were aiming for the quiet light.

Kaela was fighting, her quarterstaff a blur of motion. She struck with precision, hitting pressure points, dodging claws that swiped at her face. But she was defensive. She was fighting to create space, waiting for Kaelan to coordinate with her.

But Kaelan was fighting his own war ten yards away.

A weighted net, woven from the same obsidian magic as the chains, flew from the darkness.

Kaela sensed it. She twisted mid-air, avoiding the first one. But she couldn't dodge the second.

The black mesh enveloped her. It tightened instantly, binding her arms to her sides. The moment the magic touched her skin, her knees buckled. It was designed to suppress, to suffocate.

"Kaelan!" she cried out. It wasn't a warning this time. It was a plea.

Kaelan heard his name. He turned, his sword raised in triumph over a dispersed shadow.

He saw the remaining four figures surrounding his sister. One of them, taller than the rest, grabbed the top of the net and hauled Kaela up like a sack of grain.

"No!" Kaelan roared.

The arrogance vanished. The thrill of the fight evaporated, replaced by a cold, jagged terror.

He lunged toward them. He threw everything he had into a blast of heat—a wave of fire that scorched the earth in a line toward the captors.

The tall Retriever simply raised a hand. A wall of void energy absorbed the fire.

Then, the Retriever looked at Kaelan. It didn't speak, but a voice echoed in Kaelan’s skull, dry and rasping.

“The Sun is too hot to hold. We take the Moon.”

The shadows began to swirl, forming a vortex. A portal.

"Let her go!" Kaelan screamed. He dropped his sword and sprinted, reaching out with his bare hands.

He was fast. He was faster than he had ever been. He was mere inches away. He could see Kaela’s face through the mesh of the net. She was crying. She wasn't looking at the monsters; she was looking at him.

Her lips moved. I’m sorry.

Kaelan’s fingers brushed the edge of the net.

The portal snapped shut.

There was a thunderclap of displaced air. Kaelan stumbled forward, his momentum carrying him through the empty space where his sister had been. He crashed into the dirt, skidding across the dead leaves.

He scrambled up, spinning around, swinging his fists at the empty air.

"Come back!" he shrieked. "Fight me! Take me!"

But there was nothing. No shadows. No chains. No Kaela.

The sunlight returned to the clearing, indifferent and bright. A bird chirped tentatively from a high branch.

Kaelan stood in the center of the clearing, his chest heaving, his hands trembling.

He reached out with his mind. He pushed past the panic, searching for that familiar, annoying, constant hum. He searched for the echo of her thoughts, the feeling of her presence that had been the background noise of his entire life.

Kaela? he projected.

Silence.

It was a silence so absolute, so deep, that it felt like his eardrums had burst.

He dropped to his knees. He looked at his hands. They were burnt from his own heat. He looked at the spot where the portal had been.

"I’m alone," he whispered. The words sounded strange, foreign.

He remembered the morning. He remembered the anger. He remembered thinking, Safety is a cage. He remembered wishing she wasn't there to hold him back.

The woods were vast. The world was open. He could go anywhere. He could do anything. No one was there to tell him to be quiet. No one was there to tell him to be careful.

He was free.

Kaelan curled forward, his forehead touching the dirt, and let out a scream that tore his throat raw—a sound of pure, unadulterated regret that echoed through the empty canyon, with no one left to hear it.

## Part 5: The Armory of the Free

The walk back to Sunstead took an hour, but to Kaelan, it felt like a lifetime spent walking across the bottom of a dry ocean.

The silence in his head was deafening. Every few seconds, a reflex kicked in—an instinct to share a thought, to complain about the heat, to sense where Kaela was. And every time, his mind hit a wall of absolute nothingness. It was like reaching for a stair that wasn't there, a jarring vertigo that made him stumble on the flat road.

He reached the Western Gate as the sun began to dip, painting the red walls in the color of dried blood.

Joren was on duty. The old guard was leaning on his spear, whittling a piece of driftwood. He looked up with a smile as Kaelan approached, scanning the road behind him.

"Back early?" Joren called out. "Where’s your shadow? Did she beat you to the..."

Joren’s voice trailed off. He saw Kaelan’s face. He saw the streaks of dirt and ash, the burnt tunic, and the look in the boy’s eyes—a thousand-yard stare that belonged to a veteran, not a teenager.

Joren dropped his knife. He stepped out of the gatehouse, his hand gripping Kaelan’s shoulder.

"Kaelan," Joren said, his voice low and urgent. "Where is she?"

Kaelan opened his mouth. His throat felt full of glass. "The shadows," he rasped. "They... they took her."

Joren didn't ask "who" or "why." He looked North, his eyes narrowing. He swore a quiet, ugly curse. Then he turned to the boy. "Go to the square. I’ll ring the bell."

The bell of Sunstead didn't ring often. It was reserved for fires, floods, and war.

By the time Kaelan reached the town square, the people were already gathering. The baker, the tanner, the weavers—the people who had watched him grow up, who had scolded him for stealing apples and patched his scraped knees.

They saw him standing by the fountain, alone. They saw the heavy iron sword dragging in the dust. And they knew.

A ripple of anger went through the crowd. It wasn't the panic of a city under siege; it was the fury of a family whose home had been violated.

The Matron pushed through the crowd. She was old now, her hair white as bone, but her back was straight. She stopped in front of Kaelan. She reached up and took his face in her hands.

"Look at me," she commanded.

Kaelan forced himself to meet her eyes. "I couldn't stop them," he whispered. "I was... I was showing off. I didn't listen to her."

"We will mourn your mistakes later," the Matron said sharply, though her thumbs gently wiped a smudge of soot from his cheek. "Right now, we fix them. Where did they go?"

"North," Kaelan said. "A portal. They said... the Sun was too hot. They wanted the Moon."

"Malakor," the blacksmith, Horg, grunted from the side. He was a mountain of a man with arms like tree trunks. "He’s finally made his move."

"I’m going after her," Kaelan said. The declaration wasn't loud, but it cut through the murmurs of the crowd. He looked around at the faces of his neighbors. "I’m going to the Voidlands. I’m going to tear his tower down brick by brick until I find her."

"You’ll die before you cross the river in that tunic," Horg said bluntly. "And with that slab of iron you call a sword? You’ll tire out in a day."

Horg turned to the crowd. "Open the armory! Not the guard stashes—the Good stuff. The stuff we brought with us when we left the King’s service."

The square exploded into activity.

Sunstead was a town of refugees, retired soldiers, and master craftsmen. They had hidden their pasts, but they had kept their tools.

Kaelan stood by the fountain as the town mobilized around him. It was a surreal, dizzying whirlwind.

Horg returned ten minutes later carrying a bundle of chainmail that shimmered like liquid mercury.

"Stellium weave," he said, tossing it to Kaelan. "Forged from the heart of a fallen star. Lighter than silk, harder than diamond. It was my father’s. It will fit you."

A leatherworker brought a new scabbard and a belt with hidden pouches. "Stitched with rune-thread," she said. "It won't rot in the snow."

The baker brought sacks of waybread—hard, dense cakes that could sustain a man for days. "Don't eat it all at once, boy. It expands in the stomach."

Then, the Matron returned. She carried a bundle wrapped in oilcloth.

She laid it on the edge of the fountain and unwrapped it. Inside lay a cloak of deep, midnight blue wool, pinned with a silver clasp in the shape of a hawk.

"Elara’s," the Matron said softly. "She left it with me. She said, 'If the day comes when they must leave, give him this. It will hide him from eyes that watch from the sky.'"

Kaelan reached out, his fingers trembling as he touched the fabric. It felt familiar. It smelled of sage and old magic.

He put on the mail. He strapped on the belt. He swung the cloak over his shoulders.

Horg took Kaelan’s crude iron sword. He inspected it, frowning at the rough edge. He didn't offer a replacement. Instead, he took a whetstone from his pocket—a black stone that hummed.

"Your blade is ugly," Horg said. "But it has your heat in it. It’s part of you. Keep it." He ran the stone down the edge three times. Sparks flew, blue and hot. "Now it will cut spirits."

Kaelan sheathed the sword. He looked at the townspeople. An hour ago, he had wanted to leave them because he thought they were holding him back. He thought they were small.

Now, he realized they were the giant's shoulders he was standing on.

"I will bring her back," Kaelan promised. His voice was steady now. The frantic boy was gone.

"You bring yourself back, too," Joren said, handing him a rolled parchment map. "This shows the old trade routes to the Northern border. After that... you’re off the map, lad."

Kaelan nodded. He gripped the map.

He turned toward the North Gate. The crowd parted for him, silent and solemn. They touched his shoulder or his arm as he passed, transferring their strength, their hope, and their anger into him.

He walked out of Sunstead as the sun finally set.

The desert turned cold. Kaelan pulled Elara’s cloak tight around him. He reached out with his mind one last time, hoping for a flicker, a whisper, anything from Kaela.

There was only silence.

But this time, the silence didn't paralyze him. It focused him. It was a void that needed to be filled, a wound that needed to be stitched.

Kaelan began to run. He ran into the gathering dark, a single spark of fire heading into the heart of the winter, determined to burn the world down if that’s what it took to make the silence stop.

## Part 6: One Step at a Time

The red dust of the plateau did not give up easily. It clung to Kaelan’s boots for three days, a stubborn reminder of the home he was leaving behind. But on the fourth day, the red turned to the grey of shale and slate. The air, once thick with the scent of sage and heat, grew thin and tasted of pine needles and coming snow.

Kaelan walked.

It was not the heroic march he had imagined as a child. There were no bards singing. There was no galloping horse. There was just the rhythmic crunch of gravel, the rub of the pack straps against his shoulders, and the deafening silence in his skull.

Left foot. Right foot. Breathe.

He found himself reaching out with his mind a hundred times an hour. He would see a loose rock and instinctively project, Watch your step. He would see a hawk circling and think, Is that a scout?

And a hundred times an hour, the thought would hit the wall of silence and bounce back, mocking him.

The loneliness was a physical weight. It sat on his chest, heavier than the Stellium mail under his tunic.

By the fifth day, he reached the foothills of the Razor Peaks. The trees here were ancient pines, towering giants that blocked out the weak sun. The undergrowth was thick with thorns and shadows.

Kaelan slashed at a bramble with his machete, his movements angry and jagged.

"Stupid," he muttered to himself. "Just a stupid plant."

He was making too much noise. He knew it. In the past, Kaela would have corrected him. “You’re shouting again, brother. Step lighter. Be the wind, not the rock.”

"I am the rock," Kaelan growled at the memory. "Rocks break things."

A low growl vibrated through the soles of his boots.

Kaelan froze.

He spun around, his hand flying to the hilt of his iron sword.

Emerging from the shadows of two boulders was a Mountain Grey—a bear the size of a wagon, with fur matted with resin and scars. Its eyes were small, intelligent, and fixed on Kaelan.

Kaelan’s heart hammered. In the Dust Bowl, he and Kaela had sparred against imagined monsters. But this was real. This was five hundred pounds of muscle and teeth.

What’s the move? Kaelan’s mind screamed, waiting for the answer. He waited for Kaela’s voice to whisper, “Weak left leg. Blind in one eye. Roll right.”

The silence stretched. The bear roared—a sound that shook the pine needles from the trees—and charged.

Kaelan didn't dodge. He did what he always did. He met force with force.

"Come on!" he screamed, drawing his sword.

He channeled his heat. The blade glowed a dull, angry orange. He swung a massive, two-handed overhead strike, aiming to split the beast’s skull.

But the bear was faster than a rock. It swatted the sword aside with a paw as thick as an anvil. The impact jarred Kaelan’s bones, sending the weapon flying into the brush.

The bear didn't stop. It barreled into him.

Kaelan flew backward. He crashed into a pine tree, the breath driven from his lungs. The Stellium mail held—the bear’s claws didn't tear his skin—but the force of the blow cracked a rib.

Pain exploded in his side.

He slid down the tree trunk, gasping. The bear reared up on its hind legs, towering over him, preparing to crush him.

Kaela! Kaelan’s mind shrieked. Help me!

Nothing. Just the wind. Just the bear.

In that split second, staring death in the face, Kaelan realized the truth. He had never been a great warrior. He had been half a warrior. He had been the muscle, guided by eyes that weren't his own.

He was blind. And he was about to die.

No, a voice inside him snarled. It wasn't Kaela’s voice. It was his own. Get up.

He didn't try to stand. He didn't try to find his sword. He rolled.

The bear’s paws slammed into the earth where his chest had been a second ago, shattering a tree root.

Kaelan scrambled on hands and knees through the dirt. One step. Move.

He reached the brush where his sword had fallen. His fingers closed around the hilt. The iron was still warm from his earlier rage.

He stood up, clutching his side. The bear turned, snorting, ready to charge again.

Kaelan closed his eyes.

Stop shouting, he told himself. Stop waiting for her to tell you what to do.

He took a deep breath. He forced the fire inside him—the raging, nuclear panic—to condense. He pulled it inward, wrapping it around his core.

He opened his eyes. He didn't look at the bear’s terrifying size. He looked at the way it favored its right leg. He saw the patch of thinner fur near the throat.

He didn't scream. He didn't challenge it.

He waited.

The bear charged.

Kaelan didn't swing. He stepped. One step to the left, smooth as water.

The bear’s momentum carried it past him. As it passed, Kaelan didn't hack wildly. He thrust the sword upward, hard and precise, into the soft spot under the beast’s shoulder.

The bear howled and collapsed, sliding into the dirt. It twitched once, then went still.

Kaelan stood over the carcass, his chest heaving, blood dripping from his lip where he’d bitten it.

He wasn't triumphant. He felt sick. He felt exhausted.

He sank to the ground, leaning against the dead beast for warmth. He pulled a strip of cloth from his pack and bound his ribs, wincing with every loop.

"One step," he whispered to the empty forest.

He realized then that the journey to the High Tower wasn't going to be a sprint. It wasn't going to be a glorious charge. It was going to be this: Getting hit. Bleeding. Realizing he was alone. And standing up anyway.

He camped there that night. He ate the tough bear meat cooked over a small, smokeless fire. He cleaned his sword with snow.

The next morning, his body was stiff, his ribs burned, and his boots were cold.

He looked North. The Razor Peaks loomed ahead, a wall of white and grey. Beyond them lay hundreds of miles of frozen hell.

Kaelan stood up. He tightened his belt. He put his left foot forward. Then his right.

He started walking.

He didn't run. He didn't rush. He moved with a new, grim rhythm. He was learning the hardest lesson of his life: how to walk without a hand to hold.

One step at a time, he thought, the words falling into cadence with his boots on the stone. One step closer to her.
