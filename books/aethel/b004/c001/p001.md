# Chapter 1: The Art of Doing Nothing

## Part 1: The Geometry of Sleep

Theme: sunstead-peace

Location: The Cottage on the Ridge

POV: Kaela

=

The morning suns were late, or perhaps Kaela was just early.

She lay in the grey twilight of the cottage, listening to the world wake up. The wind was brushing against the slate roof—the roof Kaelan had laid tile by tile two years ago—making a soft, scratching sound that used to terrify her, but now just sounded like home.

She didn't move. She couldn't move, even if she wanted to.

Kaelan was a heavy sleeper. Not just in depth, but in actual mass. He was sprawled across the majority of the narrow mattress, his left arm thrown over her waist like a seatbelt, pinning her to the sheets. His face was buried in the curve of her neck, his breathing slow and rhythmic, sending puffs of warm air against her skin.

Kaela stared at the wooden beams of the ceiling. She counted the dried bundles of sage and lavender hanging from the rafters.

*Seventeen bundles,* she thought. *We need more mint.*

She shifted her leg, trying to find a patch of cool sheet. Kaelan instantly grumbled in his sleep, his arm tightening reflexively, pulling her flush against his chest.

"You're a furnace," she whispered, poking his forearm.

"Structural... integrity," Kaelan mumbled, not opening his eyes. "Don't wiggle. You'll collapse the building."

"The building is stone, Kaelan. It's fine. My bladder, however, is not."

Kaelan sighed—a long, dramatic exhalation of a man being asked to perform a Herculean task. He rolled onto his back, releasing her from the clamp.

"Fine," he groaned, rubbing his eyes with the heels of his hands. "But you're letting the cold in."

Kaela sat up, shivering as the morning air hit her skin. She grabbed her shawl from the bedpost and wrapped it around her shoulders.

"It's summer, Sun. It's not cold. You just run at the temperature of a smelting pot."

She climbed over him—there was no space to walk around the bed—and padded across the cool floorboards to the washbasin behind the screen.

When she came back, Kaelan was awake. He was propped up on his elbows, watching her with sleepy, gold-blue eyes. His hair was a black disaster, sticking up in every direction.

"Tea?" he asked hopefully.

"If you start the fire," she countered.

"Deal."

Kaelan didn't get up. He just pointed a finger at the hearth across the room. He didn't use a spell; he just projected a tiny, concentrated thought of *heat*.

*Pop.*

The kindling in the fireplace smoked, then burst into a cheerful orange flame.

"Show off," Kaela smiled, filling the kettle from the water bucket.

"Efficiency," Kaelan corrected, falling back onto the pillow. "Come back here while it boils. You're too far away."

Kaela set the kettle on the swing-arm over the fire. Then, she walked back to the bed. She didn't hesitate. She climbed back in, curling up against his side, resting her head on his shoulder.

He wrapped his arm around her immediately, the circuit of their touch closing with a contented hum.

They lay there for ten minutes, watching the firelight dance on the whitewashed walls. They didn't talk about space. They didn't talk about wars.

"Horg says Clinker is teething again," Kaelan murmured, his fingers idly tracing patterns on her arm. "He chewed through the gate latch yesterday."

"We should bring him some apples," Kaela said. "The sour ones from the old tree. He likes the crunch."

"And Joren wants me to look at the watchtower steps. Says they're creaking."

"You built those steps a year ago. They're solid oak."

"Joren just wants company," Kaelan said. "He wants to tell me the story about the River Troll again."

"You should let him."

"I will."

The kettle began to whistle.

"Work time," Kaelan groaned, but he was smiling.

He kissed the top of her head. "I'll handle the fence today. You get the apples."

"Deal."

## Part 2: The Baker’s Tax

Theme: sunstead-joy

Location: The Town Square POV: Kaela

=

Sunstead in the mid-morning was a bustling ecosystem of red dust and gossip.

Kaela walked down the main street, a basket hooked over her arm. She waved to the Tanner, who was stretching a hide in the sun. She nodded to the Miller’s children, who were chasing a hoop with a stick.

She didn't wear a hood. She didn't hide her face.

Two years ago, people used to stop and stare when the "Scions" walked by. They would bow, or whisper, or look terrified.

Now, they just waved.

"Morning, Kaela!" Mrs. Gable shouted from the open window of the bakery. The smell of yeast and cinnamon wafted out, thick enough to chew.

"Morning, Mrs. Gable," Kaela called back, leaning on the windowsill. "Do you have the dark loaves?"

"For you? Always." The baker bustled disappear and returned with two loaves of bread—dark, heavy rye with a crust like bark, exactly how Kaelan liked it.

"And," Mrs. Gable added, sliding a small paper sack across the counter with a conspiratorial wink, "a little something extra. For the boy."

Kaela peeked inside. It was a "broken" pie—a cherry tart that had collapsed in the oven. It looked like a mess. It smelled like heaven.

"He'll love it," Kaela laughed. "He says the broken ones taste better because the juice caramelizes on the pan."

"He says that so I don't feel bad about dropping them," Mrs. Gable huffed, wiping her floury hands on her apron. "He's a good lad. How's the wall coming?"

"It's... substantial," Kaela admitted. "I think he's trying to fortify the garden against a siege, not a deer."

"Let him build," Mrs. Gable advised. "Men like Kaelan need to move heavy things. Keeps them from thinking too much."

She leaned closer, her voice dropping.

"Is he... sleeping well?"

The town knew about the nightmares. In the first few months after their return from the South, Kaelan had woken up screaming more than once, shaking the cottage with accidental bursts of heat.

"He is," Kaela said softly. "No dreams for weeks. Just snoring."

"Good," Mrs. Gable nodded firmly. "You tell him to come down for supper on Freeday. I'm making roast mutton. And tell him to bring that appetite of his."

"I will. Thank you, Martha."

Kaela put the bread and the tart in her basket. She walked toward the orchard at the edge of town, her heart light.

She loved this. She loved that her biggest problem today was carrying enough apples to satisfy a giant, metal-eating badger. She loved that her brother was just "the lad" to the baker, not the Solar Engine or the God-Slayer.

She picked a basketful of the hard, green apples from the wild trees near the river.

As she walked back up the ridge path, she stopped.

She looked at the cottage.

From here, she could see Kaelan in the garden. He was shirtless, his back glistening with sweat in the midday suns. He was lifting a stone the size of a beer keg, setting it gently atop the wall.

He stepped back, hands on his hips, admiring the line.

He looked happy.

Kaela felt a swell of affection so strong it almost knocked the wind out of her.

*Stay like this,* she thought, projecting the wish into the universe. *Just stay like this forever.*

## Part 3: The Fortress of Mint

Theme: sunstead-peace

Location: The Garden POV: Kaelan

=

The stone fit.

Kaelan ran his hand over the rough granite. It sat perfectly flush against its neighbor, locked in by gravity and friction.

"That," Kaelan declared to the empty garden, "is a good rock."

He wiped his forehead with his arm. He looked at the wall.

It was four feet high and two feet thick. It encircled the small plot of land where Kaela grew her herbs and vegetables.

Ideally, a garden fence should be a light, wooden thing.

This was a rampart.

"Overkill?" he asked himself.

He looked at the delicate green shoots of the mint plants peeking out of the soil. He remembered how upset Kaela had been three years ago when the deer ate them down to the roots.

"Nope," he decided. "Necessary security."

He picked up his water jug and took a long drink. The water was cool, drawn from the deep well.

He heard footsteps on the path. He knew the rhythm before he saw her.

*Step-light-step.*

Kaela came through the gate. She was carrying a heavy basket of apples and bread. She looked flushed from the walk, her copper hair escaping her braid.

"Delivery," she called out.

Kaelan walked over to her. He took the heavy basket from her arm as if it weighed nothing.

"You got the dark bread," he noted, peeking inside. "And... is that a pie disaster?"

"Mrs. Gable sent it. She says you need to keep your strength up for all this... siege engineering."

Kaela walked over to the wall. She pushed against it. It didn't budge. It felt as solid as the mountain itself.

"It's high, Kaelan," she noted, looking at a spot where he had built it up to five feet to block the prevailing wind.

"Deer can jump," Kaelan defended, breaking off a piece of the crusty bread.

"Deer, yes. I think this would stop a battering ram."

"Well, you never know," Kaelan grinned, sitting down in the grass with his back against his creation. "Rogue battering rams are a menace this time of year."

Kaela sat next to him. She leaned her head on his shoulder.

"It's beautiful," she said honestly. "It looks like it's always been here."

"I like the stone," Kaelan admitted, looking at his hands. "It's quiet. It waits for you."

He offered her a piece of the pie. They ate in silence, watching the clouds drift over the Razor Peaks in the distance.

Those mountains used to terrify him. They used to be the barrier between him and his destiny.

Now, they were just scenery.

"Horg wants us for dinner on Freeday," Kaela said, wiping cherry filling from her thumb.

"Mutton?"

"Mutton."

"I'm in."

Kaelan finished the pie. He brushed the crumbs from his chest.

He reached out and took Kaela’s hand. Her fingers were stained with apple dust; his were grey with stone dust.

"I was thinking," Kaelan said.

"Dangerous."

"I was thinking we should plant an apple tree. Inside the wall. Right there in the corner."

Kaela looked at the spot. "It'll take years to fruit."

"We're only twenty," Kaelan said. "We have years."

"Okay," Kaela squeezed his hand. "An apple tree. And maybe a bench. So I can watch you work without getting grass stains on my dress."

"I can build a bench," Kaelan said confidently. "I'll make it out of stone."

"Of course you will."

They sat there as the afternoon suns began to dip, bathing the red dust of Sunstead in a warm, golden light.

It was a perfect Tuesday. The wall was straight. The bread was good. The sister was safe.

Kaelan closed his eyes, listening to the hum of the bees in the clover, unaware that beneath him, miles below the crust, a clock he had set a billion years ago was about to strike midnight.

"Nap?" Kaelan suggested.

"Nap," Kaela agreed.
