# Chapter 3: The Magnetic Highway

## Part 1: The Land-Boat

Theme: sunstead-action Location: Horg’s Smithy POV: Kaelan

The *Iron Keel* sat in the middle of the dusty street outside the forge, looking completely out of its element. It was heavy, squat, and distinctly marine.

"You want to do *what*?" Horg asked, flipping up his welding mask. The air in the shop was thick with the smell of ozone and burnt hair; half his tools were currently bobbing against the ceiling, pinned there by the magnetic screaming of the North.

"I want to slide it," Kaelan said, patting the oak hull. "The pull is strong, Horg. It nearly ripped the shovel out of my hand in the garden. If we reinforce the keel with pure, conductive iron, I can lock it into the current."

"Like a leaf on a stream," Kaela added, looking at the map spread out on a barrel. "Only the stream is invisible, moving at sixty miles an hour, and aiming straight for a volcano."

Horg scratched his beard with a pair of floating tongs. He looked at the boat. He looked at the vibrating compass on his workbench, the needle pinned hard to the North.

"It's mad," Horg decided, a grin splitting his soot-stained face. "I love it. But you have a problem, lad."

"Just one?"

"Stopping," Horg said, kicking a dirt clod. "If you hook this tub to the magnetic line, you're going to be a missile. Momentum is going to be your enemy. If you hit a turn in the Red Canyon at full tilt, you'll smear yourself across the canyon wall. You need brakes."

"I can cut the connection," Kaelan argued. "I'm the switch. If I let go of the magnetic feed, we stop accelerating."

"Aye, you stop *accelerating*," Horg corrected. "But a two-ton oak boat moving at the speed of a galloping horse doesn't just stop because you asked it nicely. You'll slide for miles. You need friction."

Horg got to work.

The retrofit was brutal and loud. The town watched from a safe distance as the blacksmith and the Scion turned a fishing boat into a tank.

The Runners: Horg took the iron bands from the hull and welded thick, polished plates of soft iron over them. He shaped them like sled runners—wide and flat to distribute the weight over grass and gravel, but sharp enough to bite into ice when they reached the mountains.

"Pure iron," Horg grunted, fusing a seam with a shower of sparks. "Conducts the field better. You'll feel the road through these, Kaelan."

The Brakes: This was Horg’s masterpiece. He installed two massive, lever-operated iron "plows" on the stern, flanking the rudder.

"Drag-brakes," Horg explained, demonstrating the mechanism. "You pull this lever, and these teeth dig into the dirt. It'll tear up the road, and it'll shake your teeth out, but it'll slow you down before you hit a tree."

The Steering: The river rudder was useless in the air. Horg replaced it with a heavy-duty "Tail-Skid"—a reinforced iron fin that could be ground into the earth to force the boat to drift-turn.

"It's not precise," Kaela noted, testing the weight of the tiller. "It's brute force."

"It's a boat on dirt, lass," Horg chuckled. "Precision went out the window an hour ago. You steer with weight. You steer with grit."

The Canopy: Finally, they addressed the danger of rolling over. Horg bolted a cage of steel bars over the open cockpit, welding sheets of scrap metal to form a protective shell. It looked less like a cabin and more like a bunker.

By sunset, the *Iron Keel* was unrecognizable. It sat low and heavy on its new runners, the iron gleaming in the dying light.

"She's heavy," Horg warned, slapping the armored flank. "If the magnetism cuts out, you're going to be a very expensive lawn ornament."

"It won't cut out," Kaelan said, looking North. The red aurora was pulsing brighter than ever, a bleeding wound in the sky. "The earth is screaming."

"Then go," Horg said, handing Kaelan a pair of heavy leather goggles. "And bring it back. I'm running out of scrap metal to patch her up."

Kaelan put on the goggles. He climbed into the armored cockpit.

"Kaela?"

She climbed in beside him, strapping herself into the harness Horg had bolted to the bench. She checked the drag-brake levers.

"Brakes are set," she confirmed. "Map is ready. Navigation is... theoretical."

"Theoretical is my favorite kind of plan," Kaelan grinned.

He placed his hands on the iron dashboard Horg had installed. He closed his eyes. He reached out with his mind, past the wood, past the air, feeling for the singing, violent river of magnetic force tearing through the world.

He found it.

"Connecting," Kaelan whispered.

He pushed his will into the iron runners.

*CLANG.*

The boat lurched violently. The iron skids magnetized instantly, locking onto the field lines with a force that groaned through the frame. The boat lifted an inch off the ground, hovering on a cushion of magnetic repulsion.

"Hold on!" Kaelan yelled.

He released the magnetic brake.

The *Iron Keel* didn't roll. It *shot* forward.

It accelerated with a force that threw them back into their seats. They blasted down the main street of Sunstead, kicking up a rooster tail of red dust three stories high, leaving a stunned blacksmith and a cheering town in their wake.

They were surfing the planet.

## Part 2: The Forest of Ghosts

Theme: adventure-speed-memory

Location: The Foothills of the Razor Peaks

POV: Kaelan

=

The plains of the Emerald Valleys blurred into a smear of green and gold.

Kaelan sat in the stern of the *Iron Keel*, his hands vibrating against the iron tiller. He wasn't steering so much as aiming. The magnetic pull was a relentless, screaming force that wanted to drag them straight North at terminal velocity.

"Speed check?" Kaelan shouted over the roar of the wind.

"Fast!" Kaela shouted back from the passenger seat. She was grinning, her hair whipped into a frenzy by the slipstream leaking through the viewports. "We're doing at least sixty! We just passed the Old Mill in three seconds!"

"Tree line coming up!" Kaelan warned. "The foothills are thick this time of year. We can't take them at this speed."

Ahead, the rolling grass gave way to the dense, dark pines of the lower Razor Peaks. There was no road here—only deer trails and the memory of a path Kaelan had walked alone three years ago.

"Brakes!" Kaela commanded.

Kaelan grabbed the heavy iron levers Horg had installed on his left. He hauled back.

*SCREEEEEEECH.*

The drag-brakes—massive iron plows mounted on the stern—slammed into the earth. Dirt and rocks sprayed thirty feet into the air behind them. The boat shuddered violently, fighting the momentum.

They slowed from a blur to a manageable, terrifying slide.

"Slalom!" Kaelan yelled.

They hit the trees.

Kaelan fought the tiller. He drifted the heavy boat between two ancient oaks, the iron runners scraping bark. He banked off a mossy boulder, sending sparks flying into the ferns.

It was chaos. It was exhilarating.

They slid into a small clearing and coasted to a stop, the magnetic pull humming impatiently in the hull, waiting for Kaelan to release the brakes.

The dust settled.

Kaelan took a deep breath, his heart hammering against his ribs. He looked out the reinforced canopy.

He froze.

He knew this clearing.

It wasn't just a patch of woods. To his left was a familiar, gnarled pine tree with a distinctive split in the trunk. And beneath it was a circle of blackened stones—the remains of a campfire.

"I slept here," Kaelan whispered.

Kaela looked at him. She followed his gaze to the fire pit. "On your way North?"

"Yeah."

Kaelan stared at the stones. A memory washed over him—not a vague recollection, but a visceral, sharp-edged echo of the past.

He remembered sitting right there, three years ago. He had been cold. He had been eating a piece of dried meat that tasted like sawdust.

But he remembered what he had *felt*.

He hadn't felt lonely yet. The loneliness hadn't set in until the bear attack.

That night, sitting by that fire, he had felt... relieved.

*Iron does not cuddle,* the Wedge had whispered in his mind.

He remembered looking at the empty space where his sister should have been and thinking: *Finally. No more noise. No more weakness. I am a warrior now. I don't need anyone.*

He had hugged himself, shivering in the thin blanket, and told himself that the shivering was just weakness leaving the body. He had been proud of his isolation. He had mistaken the numbness of the Wedge for the hardness of a shield.

"Kaelan?" Kaela’s voice pulled him back.

She wasn't looking at the fire pit. She was looking at him, her silver eyes filled with concern. She could feel the shadow of the memory through the Circuit—the cold, oily residue of Malakor’s programming.

Kaelan looked at her.

He looked at the small, iron-reinforced cockpit Horg had built. It was tight. The seats were bolted so close together that their shoulders were pressed firmly against each other.

There was no space here. No "dignity" of distance.

And it was perfect.

"I was such an idiot," Kaelan murmured, shaking his head. "I sat right there and congratulated myself for being alone. I thought I was free."

"You were sick," Kaela said fiercely. She reached out and took his hand, squeezing it hard enough to hurt. "You had a parasite in your head telling you that love was heavy."

"I know," Kaelan said.

He released the tiller. He didn't care about the magnetic pull. He turned in his seat, fighting the constraints of the safety harness, and pulled her into him.

He wrapped his arms around her, burying his face in her neck. He breathed in the scent of her—sage, sweat, and life.

He hugged her with the desperation of a man who knows exactly how cold the alternative is.

"I'm glad Horg built this small," Kaelan whispered into her hair. "I never want to be that far away from you again. Not even across a campfire."

Kaela melted against him, her head resting on his chest. "You won't be. We're a set, Sun. Forever."

"Forever," Kaelan agreed.

He thought about that word. *Forever*.

They were Scions. They were older than the trees outside. They had forged the stars before they were born into these bodies.

These human forms—Kaelan and Kaela—were mortal. They would age. They would eventually wear out, just like the boots he had worn on his first journey.

But the *Twins*... Solas and Lunara... they were eternal.

When these vessels failed, they would find others. Or they would return to the light.

But right now, feeling the thump of her human heart against his human ribs, Kaelan decided that he liked this version the best. He liked the grit. He liked the way she smelled like rain.

He liked that he had to work to keep her warm.

"Ready to go?" Kaela asked, pulling back slightly to look at him. "The mountain is waiting."

Kaelan kissed her forehead. He let her go, but only far enough to grab the controls.

"Ready," he said.

He released the brakes.

"Hang on, Moon. We're going up."

The *Iron Keel* surged forward, leaving the ghosts of the past in the dust, rocketing toward the Razor Peaks and the burning heart of the world.

## Part 3: The Truth about the Bridge

Theme: adventure-camp

Location: The High Chasm (The Bridge of a Thousand Truths)

POV: Kaelan

=

The suns dipped below the jagged rim of the High Chasm, painting the sky in bruises of violet and indigo.

"Camp," Kaelan said, his voice tight.

He pulled the heavy iron levers. The drag-plows bit into the shale, throwing up a cloud of grey dust. The *Iron Keel* shuddered and skidded to a halt just fifty yards from the edge of the cliff.

"We're stopping?" Kaela asked, unbuckling her harness. "We made good time today. We could push for the ridge."

"No," Kaelan said. He climbed out of the cockpit, his boots crunching on the loose rock. "I need to stop here."

He walked to the edge of the precipice. The wind whistled through the gap, a low, mournful flute note that he remembered from his nightmares.

Three years ago, he had stood here shivering, starving, and convinced that he was unworthy of love. He had seen a vision of his sister telling him to let go.

He looked down.

He expected the abyss. He expected the freezing mud ledge where he had landed, broken and weeping.

But the mud was gone. Or perhaps, it had never really been mud—only seen through the filter of his own misery.

Twenty feet down, fed by the spring thaw from the peaks above, was a pool of crystal-clear water. It sat on the wide stone ledge like a turquoise gem, calm and inviting.

"It's water," Kaelan whispered.

"It's a spring," Kaela said, appearing at his side. She looked down, fearless. "And we are dusty."

Kaelan looked at her. He looked at the water.

"I fell there," he admitted quietly. "I thought I was falling forever. But I just landed in a puddle."

"Then let's wash off the memory," Kaela said.

She didn't wait. She unclasped her cloak and laid it on the rocks. She pulled her tunic over her head. She kicked off her boots.

In the twilight, her skin seemed to glow with its own soft luminescence. She wasn't ashamed. She wasn't hiding. She stood on the edge of the cliff, naked and unbothered by the cold wind.

"Coming?" she asked, looking back at him.

Kaelan took a breath. He stripped off his heavy travel gear—the leather vest, the linen shirt, the trousers stiff with red dust. He left them in a pile next to hers.

"Yeah," he said.

They climbed down the narrow goat path Kaelan had once clawed his way up.

They reached the ledge. The air here was still, protected from the wind.

They stepped into the water. It was brisk, shocking the heat from their skin, but it wasn't the biting freeze of the Voidlands. It was clean mountain water.

Kaelan submerged himself. He let the water wash over his head, scrubbing the road dust from his hair. When he surfaced, he shook his head like a dog, spraying droplets everywhere.

Kaela laughed, splashing him.

He splashed her back.

They played. In the place where he had once wanted to die, they played like children.

Afterward, they sat on the warm stone ledge, letting the residual heat of the rock dry them. Kaelan opened the food pack.

He handed Kaela a chunk of sharp cheddar and a thick slice of Mrs. Gable’s dark rye bread.

They ate in silence, their shoulders touching, watching the stars come out over the chasm.

"I hated this place," Kaelan said around a mouthful of bread. "I thought it was the end of the world."

"It was the end of *a* world," Kaela corrected gently. "It was the end of the world where you thought you had to be alone."

She leaned her head on his shoulder.

"I like this place now. It has a nice view."

"It does," Kaelan agreed.

He finished his bread. He looked at his clothes drying on the rocks above. They wouldn't be ready until morning.

"We'll sleep in the boat," he decided. "But... thanks for coming down here with me."

"Always," Kaela said.

She stood up, stretching in the moonlight. She offered him a hand.

"Come on, Sun. Let's go home."

Kaelan took her hand. He climbed back up the cliff, leaving the ghost of the broken boy down on the ledge, drowned in the clear water of the truth.

They climbed into the cabin of the *Iron Keel*, curled up under the furs, and slept soundly on the edge of the abyss, untroubled by the dark.

## Part 4: The Scorch on the Ice

Theme: adventure-travel

Location: The High Chasm / The Outer Voidlands

POV: Kaelan

=

The suns rose over the High Chasm, turning the grey shale into gold.

Inside the armored cockpit of the *Iron Keel*, the air was still and warm, trapped by the heavy iron canopy and the fur blanket.

Kaelan woke up because his nose tickled. Kaela’s hair had escaped her braid during the night and was currently draped across his face.

He didn't brush it away. He inhaled. It smelled of the mountain spring water they had bathed in last night—clean, cold, and mineral-sharp.

He shifted slightly. Kaela was asleep on his chest, her arm thrown over his neck, her legs tangled with his under the furs. The contact was total. Skin to skin. Heart to heart.

In the years since the Star-Eater, this had become his baseline. If he wasn't touching her, he felt like he was holding his breath. When he held her, he could breathe.

"Morning," he rumbled, the vibration traveling through his chest into her cheek.

Kaela stirred. She didn't open her eyes. She just tightened her grip, nuzzling into his neck.

"Five more minutes," she mumbled.

"The suns are up, Moon. And our clothes are on the rocks."

"The rocks are cold."

"I'll warm them."

"You're already warm."

Kaelan laughed softly. He kissed the top of her head. "We have a schedule. The magnetic pull is getting stronger. If we don't move, the boat might leave without us."

They untangled themselves reluctantly. The morning air outside the blanket was biting, a reminder that they were heading North.

They climbed out of the boat, shivering in the dawn light.

Kaelan scrambled down the cliff path to the ledge where they had bathed. Their clothes were dry, stiffened by the wind and the residual heat of the stone.

He tossed Kaela her tunic. She caught it, pulling it on quickly.

They dressed standing on the edge of the precipice where Kaelan had once thought his life ended. Now, it was just a breakfast stop.

Kaelan pulled on his trousers and boots. He shook out his linen shirt.

"Do you remember," Kaela asked, fastening her belt, "when you told me iron doesn't cuddle?"

Kaelan winced, buttoning his vest. "I try not to."

"I do," she said. She walked over to him. She reached out and fixed his collar, her fingers brushing his neck.

"I remember thinking you hated me."

"I hated myself," Kaelan corrected. He covered her hands with his own. "I thought I had to be a wall. Walls don't have sisters."

"I like the door better," she said, looking up at him.

"Me too."

They climbed back up to the boat. They ate a quick meal of dried fruit and hardtack, washing it down with water from the spring.

Then, they got to work.

"Goggles," Kaelan ordered.

They pulled the heavy leather goggles down. Kaelan grabbed the iron levers.

He closed his eyes. He felt the hum of the earth. It was screaming now—a frantic, magnetic siren song pulling them North.

*Click.*

The runners locked in. The boat lurched.

"Hanging on?"

"Drive," Kaela said.

Kaelan released the brakes.

The *Iron Keel* launched forward. They cleared the rest of the High Chasm in minutes, blurring past the jagged rocks.

By noon, the terrain changed. The grey rocks gave way to the flat, obsidian sheets of the Outer Voidlands.

The temperature plummeted. The wind howled against the iron canopy.

They were surfing on black ice now.

"Kaelan," Kaela said, pointing ahead. "Heat signature. Old."

Kaelan looked through the viewport.

Miles ahead, in the middle of the featureless black plain, was a blemish.

It looked like a scar. The ice was pitted, melted, and refrozen in a chaotic, jagged pattern.

Kaelan slowed the boat. He didn't stop, but he eased back on the magnetic feed, letting them drift closer.

He knew this place.

Three years ago, there had been a tent here. There had been green trees, a hot spring, and a woman in a red dress offering him a cup of wine.

*Seraphina’s Oasis.*

"This is it," Kaelan said, his voice hard. "This is where she tried to buy me."

Kaela looked at the scar. Through the Circuit, she could feel the echo of the memory Kaelan was projecting. She felt the phantom smell of jasmine and rot.

She felt the temptation he had felt—the desperate desire to just *sleep*, to stop fighting, to let someone else carry the weight.

"She wanted to put you in a box," Kaela whispered. "She wanted to deliver you to the tower like a package."

"She almost did," Kaelan admitted. "The wine... it smelled good. I was so tired, Kaela. I was so cold."

He looked at the empty ice.

"She offered me warmth. She offered me pleasure. She told me I deserved to rest."

"Why didn't you take it?" Kaela asked, though she knew the answer. She wanted to hear him say it.

Kaelan turned to her. He looked at her copper hair, messy from the wind. He looked at her silver eyes.

"Because it wasn't you," he said.

"She was beautiful," Kaelan acknowledged. "She was perfect. But she was empty. I realized... if I drank that wine, I wouldn't wake up. And if I didn't wake up, who would find you?"

He reached out and took her gloved hand.

"I poured it out," he said. "I chose the cold. Because the cold meant I was still walking toward you."

Kaela squeezed his hand. She looked at the scar on the ice—the place where her brother had chosen pain over peace because he loved her more than he loved himself.

"I'm glad you poured it out," she said softly.

"Me too."

Kaelan looked at the scar one last time. It held no power over him now. It was just a burn mark on the floor of the world.

"Ready to leave it behind?" he asked.

"Burn it," Kaela said.

Kaelan grinned. He didn't use fire. He used speed.

He shoved the magnetic levers forward.

The *Iron Keel* surged. The runners shrieked against the ice.

They blasted right over the center of the scar, shattering the refrozen ice, leaving a fresh trail of iron shavings and victory in their wake.

They raced North, leaving the ghosts of the temptation in the dust, heading toward the true fire that waited in the deep.

## Part 5: The Last Clean Breath

Theme: adventure-camp

Location: The Edge of the Voidlands

POV: Kaelan

=

The magnetic river slowed as the terrain leveled out. They were at the very lip of the Voidlands proper—a desolate plateau where the red dust of the south met the black obsidian of the north.

"Brakes," Kaelan said softly.

He pulled the levers. The *Iron Keel* drifted to a stop near a cluster of jagged basalt pillars. The silence that rushed in to replace the roar of the wind was heavy.

It wasn't a peaceful silence. It was a hollow one.

Kaelan climbed out of the cockpit. His boots crunched on the black gravel. He looked North, toward the Crater of Silence, visible as a dark notch on the horizon.

He felt it immediately. The Echo.

It wasn't magic—Malakor was gone, his spells unraveled. It was the memory of the land itself. The rocks here remembered the weight of the Tower. The air remembered the taste of despair.

For a second, Kaelan wasn't twenty. He was eighteen, bleeding, starving, and dragging a heavy iron sword through the snow, convinced that he would never be warm again.

The ghost of that loneliness washed over him, a cold shiver that started in his marrow.

"Kaelan?"

He didn't turn around. He reached back with his hand.

Kaela took it instantly.

She stepped up beside him. She wasn't looking at the horizon; she was looking at him. She saw the shadow cross his face. She felt the dip in his internal temperature through the Circuit.

"It's just a memory," she whispered. "It can't bite."

"It has teeth," Kaelan murmured. "I can feel them. This place... it hates life, Kaela. It wants us to be quiet."

"Then we should be loud," she said.

Kaelan looked at her. The ghost of the lonely eighteen-year-old boy faded, replaced by the reality of the man holding his sister's hand.

He smiled. It started small, then broke across his face like sunrise.

"Come here," he demanded.

He pulled her in. He didn't wait for permission. He wrapped his arms around her waist and lifted her off the ground, burying his face in the soft wool of her tunic.

He squeezed her. He squeezed her until he felt the breath leave her lungs, until he felt the solid, undeniable resistance of her ribs against his.

"Payment," he mumbled into her shoulder.

"Accepted," she laughed, wrapping her legs around his waist, clinging to him like a koala. She kissed his cheek, a loud, smacking sound that echoed defiantly in the quiet air.

"You're warm," Kaelan said, letting her slide down until her feet touched the ground, but not letting go. "You're the antidote."

"To the Void?"

"To everything."

He looked past her. Nestled at the base of the basalt pillars was a small, steaming pool. It was fed by a thermal vent—the same geological unrest that was shaking the world was also heating the groundwater.

It was the last water before the Crater. The last clean thing.

"Lunch?" Kaela asked, following his gaze.

"Bath," Kaelan corrected. "Then lunch. I'm not going down into the Magma looking like a dust devil."

They walked to the pool. The water was crystal clear, bubbling gently over smooth black stones.

They stripped off their travel clothes—the heavy leather vests, the linen shirts stiff with red dust. They left them in a pile on the warm rocks.

There was no hesitation. They stepped into the water together.

It was hot—volcanic hot—but to them, it was perfect. Kaelan sank to his neck, letting the heat penetrate his muscles, loosening the tension of the drive.

Kaela splashed him.

It wasn't a gentle splash. It was a two-handed wave of water that hit him square in the face.

Kaelan sputtered, wiping his eyes. He grinned.

"Oh, it's war."

He lunged.

They wrestled in the water, laughing, splashing, making enough noise to wake the dead. The heavy, oppressive silence of the Voidlands shattered.

Kaelan caught her, pinning her against the smooth rock wall of the pool. He wasn't hurting her; he was holding her. Water sluiced down their skin.

Kaela was laughing, breathless, her eyes shining silver.

"You're wet," she pointed out, stating the obvious.

"You're beautiful," he said, staring at her.

The laughter died down, replaced by a warm, heavy contentment. He leaned his forehead against hers.

"I love you," he whispered. "Sun and Moon."

"Sun and Moon," she replied.

They washed the dust away. They washed the ghosts away.

Afterward, they sat on the edge of the pool, drying in the sun and the residual heat of the volcanic rock. They shared the last of the fresh bread and a wedge of hard cheese.

Kaelan ate ravenously, one arm draped over Kaela’s shoulders, pulling her into his side so he could chew and cuddle at the same time.

"It's deep," Kaelan said, looking toward the Crater. "The drop. I can feel the magnetic field folding in."

"We'll handle it," Kaela said, finishing her cheese. "Horg built the boat to take a beating."

"I'm not worried about the boat," Kaelan said. He kissed her temple. "I'm just glad I'm not falling alone this time."

"Never again," she promised.

They dressed in the warm afternoon light. Kaelan buckled his sword belt. Kaela braided her wet hair.

They climbed back into the *Iron Keel*.

Kaelan grabbed the tiller. The playfulness vanished, replaced by the steely focus of the Pilot.

"Ready?"

"Ready."

Kaelan released the brakes. The magnetic runners hummed, locking onto the field lines.

"Next stop," Kaelan said, grinning. "The center of the earth."

The boat surged forward, leaving the pool and the ghosts behind, racing toward the lip of the crater and the long, dark drop into the fire.

## Part 6: The Ghost Road

Theme: adventure-travel

Location: The Deep Voidlands

POV: Kaelan

=

The afternoon in the Voidlands did not bring warmth; it just brought a harsher, flatter light.

The *Iron Keel* was flying. The magnetic pull from the core was so strong here that the iron runners were humming a constant, high-pitched note, hovering two inches off the black ice.

Kaelan sat at the tiller, his eyes locked on the horizon. He knew this path.

Two years ago, this crossing had taken him four days. Four days of walking into a headwind that felt like knives. Four days of sleeping in cracks in the ice, listening to the hissing of Gloomwraiths circling his camp, waiting for him to freeze.

Now, they were covering the same distance in four hours.

"Slow down," Kaela said softly.

Kaelan blinked. He hadn't realized his grip on the levers had tightened. He pulled back slightly, engaging the drag-brakes. The boat shuddered, slowing from a blur to a manageable cruise.

"Why?" Kaelan asked. "We can make the crater by sunset if I push it."

"Because you're tense," Kaela said. She reached across the small gap between their seats and placed her hand on his knee. "You're projecting static. You're remembering."

Kaelan looked out the viewport. To his left, a jagged ridge of basalt rose from the ice like a spinal column.

"That ridge," he whispered. "That's where they herded me."

He pointed to a specific outcropping of rock.

"I stood there. Three Gloomwraiths blocked the path back. They didn't attack. They just... pointed. They wanted me to go to the Tower. They wanted me to be the battery."

He remembered the feeling of absolute helplessness. He remembered knowing he was walking into a trap and doing it anyway because he had nowhere else to go.

"They aren't there, Kaelan," Kaela said.

Kaelan looked. The ridge was empty. The shadows were just shadows—tricks of the light on black rock. There were no burning eyes. No hissing whispers.

"I know," Kaelan said. "But the cold... it feels the same."

"It isn't," Kaela corrected.

She unbuckled her safety harness. She turned in her seat, wiggling until she could slide over the center console.

The cockpit was tight—Horg had built it for function, not comfort—but she made it work. She squeezed into Kaelan’s seat, forcing him to shift back.

She sat on his lap, her back against his chest, her legs draped over the side of the chair.

"Better?" she asked, pulling his arms around her waist.

Kaelan buried his face in her shoulder. The smell of the volcanic spring and the clean scent of her hair filled his nose, chasing away the phantom smell of ozone and decay.

He felt the heat of her body pressing against his. He felt the steady, calm rhythm of her heart.

"Much better," he murmured.

"Drive," she commanded gently, resting her head on his shoulder. "Show the ghosts we aren't afraid."

Kaelan grabbed the tiller again, his arms bracketing her. He released the brakes.

The *Iron Keel* surged forward.

They drove through the heart of the Voidlands. They passed the frozen lake where Kaelan had fallen through the ice and nearly drowned. They passed the scree slope where he had twisted his ankle and walked on it for miles.

Every landmark was a scar. But now, they were blurring past them at sixty miles an hour, wrapped in iron and warmth.

The suns began to set, turning the sky into a bruised purple canopy.

Ahead, the horizon broke. The flat ice ended abruptly at a jagged, circular rim that stretched for miles.

The Crater of Silence.

Kaelan slowed the boat. They drifted toward the edge, the magnetic runners crunching over the loose obsidian gravel.

He pulled the full stop. The boat settled.

They sat there for a moment, looking out.

The Crater was massive. In the fading light, it looked like a mouth waiting to swallow them.

But it wasn't silent anymore.

From the center, miles away, a deep, rhythmic thrumming vibrated through the ground.

*Thrummmmm.*

And rising from the central vent was a glow—not the violet light of Malakor’s magic, but the deep, angry red of the planetary core.

"The intake," Kaelan whispered. "It's wide open."

"It's waiting for us," Kaela said.

She didn't sound afraid. She sounded ready.

"We camp here," Kaelan decided. "On the rim. I'm not going down that slide in the dark."

"Agreed."

They didn't set up a tent. The wind on the rim was ferocious, screaming over the edge of the crater.

They stayed in the boat. Kaelan engaged the thermal seals on the canopy.

They ate a supper of hardtack and dried fruit, sitting hip-to-hip in the pilot’s seat because neither of them wanted to move to the passenger side.

Outside, the Voidlands turned pitch black. The red glow from the crater cast long, dancing shadows against the iron hull.

"It feels different this time," Kaelan said into the dark of the cabin.

"How?"

"Last time, I came here to die," he admitted. "I thought if I burned the tower down, it didn't matter what happened to me."

He tightened his arms around her.

"This time, I have to live. I have to get us back to the garden. I have a fence to maintain."

Kaela smiled against his chest. "And a hug debt to pay."

"And a debt," he agreed.

They curled up together on the narrow bench seat, wrapped in the fur blanket. It was a tight fit, but that was the point.

Kaelan listened to the wind howling outside—the same wind that had tried to kill him two years ago. Now, it was just noise.

He closed his eyes, anchored by the girl in his arms and the iron beneath his feet, and slept on the edge of the volcano, ready to drop into the fire when the suns came up.

## Part 7: The Heavy Silence

Theme: adventure-camp-doom

Location: The Rim of the Crater

POV: Kaelan

=

Night in the Voidlands did not fall; it suffocated.

Outside the armored canopy of the *Iron Keel*, the wind shrieked. It wasn't the clean, sharp whistle of the High Chasm. It was a jagged, multi-tonal moan that sounded like voices.

*Kaelan... surrender...*

Kaelan flinched, his hand jerking toward the sword belt he had just unbuckled.

"It's just the wind," Kaela said softly. She was kneeling on the floorboards, unlacing her boots.

"It sounds like them," Kaelan whispered. He was staring out the viewport at the black obsidian shifting in the dark. "The Gloomwraiths. I can hear them hissing."

"There are no Wraiths," Kaela promised. She reached out with the Circuit, scanning the darkness. "The only living things for a hundred miles are us and a few very confused rock-rabbits."

"My head knows that," Kaelan said, rubbing his temples. "My gut thinks they're waiting for the fire to die."

He stood up. The interior of the boat was lit by the faint, blood-red glow reflecting up from the crater vent miles away. It cast long, twisting shadows against the iron ribs of the hull.

Kaela stood up and reached for the hem of her tunic. It was their routine—shedding the day, shedding the armor, sleeping skin-to-skin.

"Don't," Kaelan said sharply.

Kaela paused, her hands still on the fabric. She looked at him.

"Not tonight," Kaelan said. His voice was tight, bordering on frantic. "Keep it on. Keep the boots on, too. Actually... maybe we should sleep in the mail."

"Kaelan," Kaela said gently, lowering her hands. "We are safe. Horg built this boat to be a tank. Nothing is getting in."

"I know," Kaelan paced the small length of the cockpit. "I just... I can't be open tonight, Kaela. I feel like if I take my clothes off, the cold is going to touch me again. And if it touches me, I'll freeze."

He looked at her, shame burning in his eyes. He was the Solar Engine. He was the man who had surfed a boat across a continent. But here, on the edge of the pit where he had once come to die, he was a scared eighteen-year-old boy again.

Kaela didn't argue. She didn't tell him to be reasonable.

"Okay," she said. "Layers. Armor. Whatever you need."

She sat down on the narrow bench seat. "Come here."

Kaelan hesitated. The seat was narrow—too narrow for them to lie side-by-side without one of them hovering over the edge. And he felt too jittery to lie still.

"I can't sleep next to you," he admitted. "I'll twitch. I'm too wired."

"Then don't sleep next to me," Kaela said. She patted the bench. "Lie down on your back."

Kaelan frowned, but he obeyed. He lay down on the cushioned bench, his booted feet resting on the dashboard. He was stiff as a board, his arms crossed over his chest like a corpse.

Kaela climbed up.

She didn't lie beside him. She lay on top of him.

She settled stomach-to-stomach, her legs draping over his, her chest resting against his. She nestled her head into the crook of his neck, her hair spilling over his face like a curtain, blocking out the view of the red-lit canopy.

"Kaela?"

"You need weight," she whispered against his collarbone. "You need to know I'm here. If I'm on top of you, you can't forget."

Kaelan took a breath. The weight of her was heavy, solid, and undeniably real. It pressed the panic down.

He uncrossed his arms. He wrapped them around her back, locking his hands together. He pulled her down tighter, crushing her against him.

"Is this okay?" he asked, his voice muffled by her hair.

"It's perfect," she murmured. "It's warm."

The wind howled outside, sounding like a thousand dying ghosts. But Kaelan couldn't hear them anymore.

All he could hear was the steady *thump-thump* of Kaela’s heart beating directly against his own chest.

He closed his eyes. The tension in his muscles began to uncoil.

"Tomorrow," Kaelan whispered, "when we go down the tube..."

"Yeah?"

"You sit on my lap. In the chair. Just like in the forest."

"I wouldn't sit anywhere else," Kaela agreed. "I have to steer, remember? And I can't reach the tiller if you're hogging the seat."

"Right. Steering."

"And," she added, shifting slightly to get comfortable on the hard buttons of his tunic, "you have a debt to pay. This counts as double time."

Kaelan smiled in the dark. "Double time?"

"Hazard pay," she corrected. "For the ghosts."

"Fair enough."

He squeezed her, holding onto his payment plan like a lifeline. He wasn't the lonely boy in the snow anymore. He was a mattress for the most important person in the universe.

And as the red light pulsed outside, Kaelan slept without nightmares, anchored to the earth by the girl who refused to let him float away.
