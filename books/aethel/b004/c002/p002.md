# Chapter 2: The Smoke and the Fire

## Part 1: The Funeral Crashers

Theme: sunstead-tension

Location: The Town Square / The Graveyard

POV: Kaela

=

The death of a Matron usually brought silence. This time, it brought a carnival.

News traveled fast in the post-Iron Heart world. The red auroras had been seen for a thousand miles. The magnetic quake had been felt in the capital. And somehow, the rumor had spread that the epicenter was a small, dusty town called Sunstead, home of the late, wise Matron... and two "Others."

Kaela stood near the bakery, her hood pulled low.

The square was packed. Strangers in dusty travel cloaks crowded the stalls. They bought "Relic Bread" from a confused Mrs. Gable. They chipped pieces of stone off the town well as souvenirs.

They crowded the small graveyard where the Matron lay under her fresh cairn.

"She was a Saint," a man in a velvet coat proclaimed loudly, placing a wreath of expensive hothouse flowers on the rough stones. "She harbored the Celestials."

"They say the Celestials still live here," a woman whispered, scanning the crowd with hungry eyes. "They say they walk among us, glowing like lanterns."

Kaela shrank back into the shadows of the alley. She gripped Kaelan’s hand.

"They're treating her grave like a tourist attraction," she hissed. "And they're treating us like... prizes."

Kaelan’s jaw was set tight. He was wearing a heavy cloak, hiding his face, but Kaela could feel the heat radiating off him.

"I should go out there," he rumbled. "I should tell them to show some respect."

"If you go out there," Horg’s voice came from behind them, "they won't listen. They'll just try to tear pieces off your tunic for luck."

Horg stepped into the alley. He looked exhausted. He had spent the morning pulling people off the forge roof who were trying to get a better view.

"Go home," Horg advised gently. "Lock the gate. Joren and I will clear the graveyard by sundown. Let the Matron rest."

"We just wanted to buy carrots," Kaelan said, frustration leaking into his voice.

"I'll bring you carrots," Horg promised. "Go. Before someone sees your eyes."

They retreated. They walked up the back path, slipping through the shadows like thieves in their own home.

They reached the stone wall of the cottage. Kaelan slid the heavy bolt of the oak gate home with a definitive thunk.

"I hate this," Kaelan said, leaning his forehead against the wood. "I feel like a prisoner."

"We aren't prisoners," Kaela said, trying to convince herself. "We're just... private."

She looked at the garden. The high walls blocked out the noise of the town. The mint smelled sweet. The massive granite tub sat in the corner, inviting and safe.

"Bath?" she suggested. "To wash off the noise?"

Kaelan looked at the tub. He looked at the sky, turning violet with twilight.

"Bath," he agreed.

## Part 2: The Ladder

Theme: sunstead-anger

Location: The Garden Bath

POV: Kaelan

=

The water was hot. The night was dark. The wall was high.

Kaelan sat on the underwater stone bench, the water lapping at his collarbones. Kaela sat opposite him, her legs tangled with his. They had been soaking for twenty minutes, letting the heat seep into their bones, trying to forget the crowded square below.

They were naked, as they always were in the water. This was their sanctuary. The one place where they didn't have to wear hoods or hide their glow.

"Do you think they'll leave?" Kaela asked quietly, tracing a pattern on the surface of the water.

"Eventually," Kaelan said, eyes closed. "They'll run out of money. Or they'll get bored when they realize we aren't coming out to do magic tricks."

"I hope so."

It was quiet. Just the crackle of the heater fire and the wind in the trees.

Then, a sound.

Scrape.

It came from the south wall—the side facing the public path that led up the ridge.

Scrape. Thump.

Kaelan’s eyes snapped open.

"Kaelan," Kaela whispered, freezing.

Two wooden hooks appeared over the top of the seven-foot granite wall. Then, the top of a ladder thumped against the stone.

Before Kaelan could move, a head popped up.

It was a man. He held a sketchbook. Behind him, on another ladder, a woman appeared holding a lantern.

They looked down into the garden.

They saw the steam. They saw the stone tub. They saw the two figures sitting in the water.

"It's them!" the man shouted, his eyes widening. "The Golden One! And the Moon!"

He started sketching furiously.

"They're bathing!" the woman shrieked, swinging her lantern to illuminate the water. "Look! They are unclad! The divinity is revealed!"

The violation hit Kaelan like a physical punch.

He didn't think. He reacted.

He didn't stand up—that would have exposed them further. He slammed his hand onto the surface of the water.

BOIL.

He pumped a massive pulse of thermal energy into the tub. The water didn't boil, but the steam explosion was instantaneous. A thick, white cloud of fog erupted from the basin, rolling up and over the wall, blinding the peepers.

"Get down!" Kaelan roared, his voice shaking the stones.

"Hey!" a new voice bellowed from the other side of the wall. It wasn't a pilgrim. It was Horg.

There was a sound of wood splintering, a yelp of surprise, and the crash of a ladder falling over.

"You perverts!" Horg’s voice thundered. "Get off that wall!"

"We seek the truth!" the man screamed as he was apparently dragged away. "We only wanted to see the glory!"

"You're looking at my neighbors in the bath!" Horg shouted. "That ain't glory! That's peeping! Joren! Get the shackles!"

Inside the garden, the silence returned, heavy and thick.

The steam cloud slowly dissipated.

Kaelan sat in the water, his chest heaving. He was trembling—not from cold, but from a rage so pure it tasted like iron.

Kaela was curled into a ball on her side of the bench, her arms wrapped around herself, covering her chest. She looked horrified.

"They saw," she whispered. "They brought... lanterns."

Kaelan moved. He crossed the tub in a second. He pulled her into his arms, shielding her from the empty wall, shielding her from the sky.

"I'm sorry," he rasped, his eyes glowing dangerously bright. "I should have heard them. I should have..."

"It's not your fault," she said, her voice shaking. "They brought ladders, Kaelan. They hunted us."

There was a heavy pounding on the oak gate.

"Lad? Lass?" It was Horg. His voice was breathless and furious. "You decent?"

"No!" Kaelan shouted back. "We are in the tub, Horg!"

"Right. Sorry." Horg paused. "We got 'em. Four of 'em. Pilgrims from the South. They're in the jail cell now."

"Burn the sketchbooks," Kaelan commanded. His voice was cold. Dead cold.

"Already in the forge fire," Horg promised. "Listen to me. The town is rallying. The Miller, Mrs. Gable... they're mad as hornets. Nobody looks over a fence in Sunstead. Nobody."

"Get them out," Kaela said. Her voice was small but hard. "Get them all out."

"We're doing it now," Horg said. "We're clearing the ridge. No one comes up this path tonight unless they want to eat a hammer. You finish your bath. You're safe now."

Horg’s footsteps retreated, heavy and protective.

Kaelan held Kaela in the water. The heat was still there, the comfort was still there, but the sanctuary felt violated.

"They ruined it," Kaela whispered. "They ruined the bath."

"No," Kaelan said fiercely. He tilted her chin up. "They don't get to take this. This is ours."

He kissed her forehead. He held her tight, staring at the top of the wall, daring another head to appear.

"We need a roof," Kaelan decided grimly. "We're building a roof."

"A roof," Kaela agreed, burying her face in his neck.

They stayed in the water, defiant, refusing to leave their home, washing away the feeling of a hundred prying eyes with the heat of their own anger.

## Part 3: The Verdict of the Sun

Theme: divine-wrath

Location: The Sunstead Jail (Beneath the Watchtower)

POV: Kaelan

=

Kaelan did not dry off gently. He moved with a jerky, terrifying speed.

He pulled on his trousers. He pulled on his tunic. He didn't bother with underwear. He threw his heavy travel cloak over his shoulders, the one he had worn to the core.

"Kaelan," Kaela said. She was dressed too, sitting on the edge of the stone tub, her arms wrapped around herself. "What are you going to do?"

"I'm going to take it back," Kaelan said. His voice was calm. It was the unnatural, quiet calm of a hurricane eye.

"Take what back?"

"The image," Kaelan said. "They stole a look, Kaela. They have us in their heads. They have our nakedness in their memories. They intend to sell it. To draw it. To tell the world."

He turned to the gate. His eyes were not gold. They were white.

"I am not letting them keep it."

He walked out of the garden. Kaela followed him.

They walked down the ridge path. The night air was cool, but around Kaelan, the air shimmered with heat distortion. The grass wilted as he passed.

The town square was quiet. Horg and Joren had cleared the streets.

They reached the heavy wooden door of the jailhouse beneath the watchtower. Horg was standing guard, his arms crossed. He took one look at Kaelan’s face and stepped aside.

"They're in the back cell," Horg said quietly. "Four of them. They're still ranting about 'Divine Revelation'."

"Not for long," Kaelan said.

He pushed the door open.

The jail was a small, stone room with a single barred cell. Inside, the four pilgrims—the sketch artist, the woman with the lantern, and two others who had held the ladders—were sitting on the straw.

When they saw the twins enter, they didn't cower. They scrambled to the bars, faces flushed with fanaticism.

"It is them!" the woman shrieked, clutching the bars. "The Golden God! We saw you! We saw the holy vessels!"

"We have the truth!" the artist shouted, tapping his head. "I have it right here! I will paint it! The world will see the glory of the bath!"

Kaelan stepped up to the bars.

He didn't shout. He didn't raise his hand.

"You violated my home," Kaelan whispered. "You looked where you were not welcome."

"The Sun belongs to everyone!" the man argued, breathless with his own self-importance. "You cannot hide the light!"

"You want the light?" Kaelan asked.

He grabbed the iron bars of the cell door. The metal instantly glowed cherry-red, then white-hot. The pilgrims scrambled back, gasping.

"You wanted to see," Kaelan said, his voice overlapping with the harmonic resonance of Solas. "So I will let you see."

"Look at me," Kaelan commanded.

It wasn't a request. It was a Scion imperative.

The four pilgrims froze. They looked into Kaelan’s eyes.

Kaelan dropped the human mask. He engaged the Engine. He didn't unleash the heat; he unleashed the Light.

He flared.

For a fraction of a second, the jail cell was brighter than the surface of a star. It was a flash of pure, unadulterated solar radiance.

The pilgrims screamed, covering their eyes.

Kaelan dimmed instantly back to human.

"My eyes!" the artist wailed, rubbing his face. "I can't see! It's all white!"

"You aren't blind," Kaelan said coldly. "Your eyes will heal in an hour. But your memory... that is gone."

"What?" the woman gasped, blinking. "I... I remember... I remember the water..."

She frowned. She tried to summon the image of the naked twins in the tub.

But all she could see in her mind's eye was a blinding, white sheet of fire. The image of Kaelan and Kaela had been overexposed. Burnt out. Like film left in the sun.

"I can't see it," she whimpered. "I can't remember what you looked like. It's just... bright."

"That is your punishment," Kaelan declared. "You wanted the Sun? You have him. Every time you close your eyes... every time you try to sleep... every time you try to draw us... you will see this."

He pointed to his own glowing eyes.

"You will see the glare. You will never see the dark again. You will never have the peace of a shadow. You will carry the noon sun in your heads until the day you die."

The artist fell to his knees, sobbing. "It's too bright! I can't close it out!"

Kaela stepped forward. She looked at the sobbing men and women. She didn't offer mercy. She offered silence.

"And you will not speak of us," she said softly, weaving a thread of lunar dampening into their minds. "If you try to describe us, your tongues will be heavy. If you try to write our names, your hands will shake."

She looked at Horg.

"Open the cell."

Horg unlocked the door.

"Go," Kaelan commanded. "Run. Run back to the south. Tell them that the Sun is not a painting. Tell them the Sun burns."

The four pilgrims scrambled out of the cell, stumbling, shielding their eyes from the dim torchlight, screaming about the "Endless Light." They ran out the door, out the gate Joren opened for them, and vanished into the night, carrying their blinding punishment with them.

Kaelan stood in the empty jail, his hands trembling slightly as the adrenaline faded. The iron bars were still cooling, ticking as they contracted.

"Was that... too much?" Kaelan asked, his voice returning to normal.

"No," Horg said, staring at the door. "They stole your privacy. You just... seized the evidence."

Kaelan looked at Kaela.

"They can't remember," he said. "I burned the film."

"Good," Kaela said. She took his hand. It was hot. "Let's go home, Sun. I think we need to put that roof on the tub."

"Tomorrow," Kaelan agreed. "Tonight... we lock the door."

## Part 4: The Ward of Glare

Theme: sunstead-magic

Location: The Garden

POV: Kaelan

=

They stood in the garden. The water in the stone tub was still steaming, but the mood was ruined.

Kaelan looked up at the top of the seven-foot granite wall. It was solid rock, but it felt flimsy now. He kept imagining heads popping up over the rim like whack-a-moles.

"We could build a pergola," Kaela suggested, looking at the stars. "Grow vines over it. A green roof."

"Vines take years," Kaelan said, kicking a loose pebble. "And I don't want a roof. I want to see the Milky Way while I soak."

He paced the length of the wall.

"If we put a roof on it, they win," he muttered. "They force us into a box. I am done living in boxes."

"So what do we do?" Kaela asked. "We can't just hope Horg catches everyone with a ladder. There are hundreds of them down there."

Kaelan stopped. He looked at the granite. He remembered the pilgrims in the jail cell—how they screamed when he showed them the true nature of the Sun.

"We don't hide," Kaelan decided. "We trap."

He walked to the wall. He placed his hand on the cold stone.

"Kaela, I need a trigger. I need a sensor line exactly at the height of the stone."

Kaela walked over. She placed her hand next to his.

"I can do that," she said, her eyes glowing with a faint silver light. "I can weave a gravity tension line. If anything with mass—a hand, a face, a ladder—crosses the plane of the wall..."

"...it trips the breaker," Kaelan finished.

He closed his eyes. He poured energy into the stone. He wasn't heating it this time; he was charging it. He filled the granite with a dormant, high-yield solar flash.

"It won't kill them," Kaelan said, his voice grim. "I don't want dead pilgrims in my mint patch. But if they peek... they burn."

"The Artist's Curse?" Kaela asked.

"The Artist's Curse," Kaelan confirmed. "Permanent flashbulb memory. If they look over this wall, the only thing they will see for the rest of their lives is white light."

They worked together for an hour.

Kaela wove the detection web—a delicate, invisible dome of lunar sensitivity that stretched from the top of the wall to the chimney. It was permeable to rain, wind, and falling leaves. But if a biological mind tried to peer through it, the trap would spring.

Kaelan loaded the payload. He infused the granite with the blinding essence of the Zenith.

When they were done, the air above the wall shimmered slightly, like heat rising off asphalt in July.

"Done," Kaelan said, stepping back.

"It's aggressive," Kaela noted.

"It's a 'Do Not Disturb' sign," Kaelan corrected. "Written in the language of the sun."

He looked at the gate.

"I don't want them to worship us, Kaela. I don't want offerings. I just want to take a bath without ending up in a sketchbook."

"They'll learn," Kaela said. "The town knows. The pilgrims will find out."

She walked back to the stone tub. The water was still warm.

"Are we getting back in?" she asked.

Kaelan looked at the shimmering air above the wall. He looked at the gate, bolted shut. He looked at the water.

"We let the water get cold," he said.

He walked to the tub. He dipped his hand in. A pulse of heat brought it back up to temperature in seconds.

"Yes," he said. "We're getting back in."

They stripped off their clothes again—defiant now. They climbed back into the water.

They sat there, naked and unafraid, under the open sky.

Ten minutes later, there was a scuffling sound at the south wall.

Thump. A ladder.

"Here we go," Kaelan whispered, not moving a muscle.

A head popped up over the wall.

ZAP.

There was no sound of thunder. Just a silent, blinding flash of white light that illuminated the entire garden for a microsecond.

"MY EYES!" a voice shrieked from the other side of the wall. "IT BURNS! THE GLORY! IT'S TOO MUCH!"

There was a crash as the ladder fell over, followed by the sound of someone stumbling blindly away through the bushes, wailing about the "Endless White."

Kaelan didn't flinch. He didn't even look. He just reached out and took Kaela’s hand under the water.

"System functional," he noted dryly.

"Very functional," Kaela agreed, suppressing a giggle.

"Pass the soap?"

"Here."

They continued their bath, undisturbed by the world, protected by the very light they refused to rule with.
