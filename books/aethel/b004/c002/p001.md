# Chapter 1: The Privacy of Stone

## Part 1: The Hole Under the Floor

Theme: sunstead-peace

Location: The Cottage Kitchen

POV: Kaelan

=

The cottage smelled of damp earth, sawdust, and ambition.

Kaelan stood waist-deep in a rectangular pit in the middle of the kitchen floor. He had pried up the heavy oak planks near the pantry and was currently excavating the dense red clay beneath the foundation.

"Deeper," Kaela advised from the edge of the pit. She was sitting cross-legged on the floorboards, peeling an apple with a paring knife. "You're six-foot-one, Sun. If you make the ceiling six feet, you're going to have a permanent headache."

"I'm digging," Kaelan grunted, hoisting a heavy wicker basket of clay over his head. "But the bedrock is stubborn."

He set the basket on the floor. Kaela didn't lift it; she just tilted it, using a small telekinetic nudge to empty the dirt onto a canvas tarp she had laid out.

"Go to seven feet," she said. "We need clearance for the hanging onions."

"Seven feet it is."

The heavy wooden door of the cottage creaked open. Horg filled the frame, blocking out the morning sun. He was carrying a set of heavy black-iron hinges and a reinforced ring-pull.

"I hear we're undermining the foundation," Horg boomed, stepping inside. He looked at the pile of dirt on the tarp. "You trying to dig a shortcut back to the core, lad?"

"Just a cold room, Horg," Kaelan said, his voice echoing slightly from the hole. He stopped digging and looked up. The top of his head was now level with the floor joists.

"A root cellar," Horg corrected, walking over to the edge and peering down. "There's a difference, you know."

Kaelan wiped sweat from his forehead with the back of his dirty hand. "A hole is a hole, isn't it?"

"Bah," Horg scoffed, setting the hinges on the table. "A cellar is just a basement. It's dry. You put your tools there. You put your dry goods there. If you put a carrot in a dry cellar, it shrivels up like an old boot in a week."

Horg pointed a thick finger at the red clay walls of the pit.

"A root cellar is alive. It needs to breathe. It uses the moisture of the earth to keep the roots sleeping, not dying. You keep the floor dirt. You keep the temperature steady at fifty degrees. You do that, and a potato will last you until next harvest."

"Dirt floor. Humidity. Got it," Kaelan said.

He stuck his spade into the earth. "Okay. I'm at seven feet. Plenty of headroom for us giants."

"Now for the walls," Kaela said. "If we leave them as dirt, they'll crumble."

"Way ahead of you."

Kaelan placed his palms flat against the earthen wall of the shaft. He closed his eyes.

He didn't use the explosive, white-hot power of the sun. He summoned a low, slow heat—the kind of heat used in a kiln.

His hands glowed a dull, brick-red.

Hiss.

Steam curled off the clay as the moisture within the walls flash-boiled and escaped. The red dirt darkened, then lightened again as it baked. In seconds, the loose soil transformed into a solid, seamless sheet of terracotta ceramic.

He turned, repeating the process on all four walls. He baked shelves directly into the earth—nooks for butter, crannies for jars.

"Show off," Horg grumbled appreciatively. "Took me three weeks to line my cellar with stone. You did it in three minutes."

Kaelan climbed out of the hole, dusting his hands on his trousers.

The trapdoor opening was now a neat, square shaft lined with smooth, hardened pottery. It smelled clean, cool, and earthy.

"The floor stays dirt," Kaelan confirmed, looking at Horg. "For the humidity."

"Aye," Horg nodded. "Throw some gravel down there for drainage. And don't store your apples next to the carrots, or the carrots will taste like fruit."

Kaelan took the hinges from the table. He knelt to fit them to the heavy oak trapdoor he had cut.

"What's going down there first?" Horg asked. "I've got a keg of winter ale that needs a home."

"No ale," Kaela said, tossing her apple core into the compost bucket. "We don't drink spirits, Horg. You know that."

"Right, right. Dry as the desert, you two."

"Milk," Kaelan said, driving a screw into the wood with a twist of his wrist. "Mrs. Gable says fresh cream stays sweet twice as long down here. And the butter won't melt in July."

"Milk and potatoes," Horg sighed. "A warrior's bounty."

"It's a quiet bounty," Kaelan said, testing the lid. It swung shut with a solid thump, flush with the floor. He stepped on it. It didn't squeak.

"Phase One complete," Kaelan announced, standing up and stretching his back. The ceiling of the cottage was low, but he had just enough clearance if he didn't jump.

He looked at Kaela. He looked at the garden door.

"Now for Phase Two."

"The luxury upgrade?" Kaela asked, eyes lighting up.

"The luxury upgrade," Kaelan confirmed. "Horg, did you bring the chisel?"

"I brought the chisel," Horg said, handing over a heavy steel tool. "But if you're carving what I think you're carving... you're going to need a bigger rock."

"We have the rock," Kaelan grinned. "It's already in the garden."

## Part 2: The Fortress of Comfort

Theme: sunstead-peace

Location: The Garden

POV: Kaela

=

The suns were high, and the garden was a hive of activity.

Horg stood by the back wall, holding a long measuring stick. He bumped it against the top of the granite stonework.

"Seven feet, two inches," Horg announced. "Unless the Miller’s boy learns to fly, no one is seeing over this."

"Good," Kaelan said. He was standing waist-deep in a trench near the chimney, shovel in hand. "Now, the weak point. The gate."

The old garden gate was a charming, rustic thing made of willow branches. It was pretty. It was also see-through.

"Scrap it," Horg said. He walked to the cart he had pulled up the hill. He hauled down a slab of dark, stained oak, three inches thick and bound in black iron. "I built this for a bank vault, but the bank went bust. It fits your frame."

He and Kaelan lifted the heavy door into place. It fit the stone archway perfectly. There were no gaps between the planks. No cracks to peek through.

Horg drilled a single, tiny hole in the center, about eye-level. He fitted a small brass shutter over it on the inside.

"The 'Peep'," Horg explained, sliding the brass cover back and forth with a click. "You check who it is. If you don't like them, you don't open it. Simple."

"Perfect," Kaela said, testing the slide-bolt. It was heavy iron. When it thunked home, the garden felt less like a yard and more like a sanctuary.

"Now," Kaelan said, picking up his shovel. "The plumbing."

They moved to the corner near the chimney breast. Kaelan had already rolled the massive river-granite boulder into place.

"Drainage first," Horg commanded. "You don't want a mud pit every time you pull the plug."

Horg and Kaelan dug the trench, laying clay pipes that ran from the base of the rock, under the garden wall, and emptied into the mint patch on the other side.

"Mint likes water," Kaela noted. "It’ll grow like a weed."

"It is a weed," Horg grunted.

With the plumbing laid, Kaelan climbed onto the boulder. He knelt, placing his hands on the grey stone. He closed his eyes, his skin glowing with a low, steady gold light.

He didn't chip away the rock; he pressed it. Under his thermal influence, the granite softened like warm wax. He pushed down, scooping out the material, compressing the stone to make it denser.

He carved it wide. He carved it deep. He molded two distinct seats on opposite ends, facing each other, with enough legroom in the middle that two tall people wouldn't be kicking shins.

He smoothed the rim, rolling it over so it would be comfortable to lean a neck against.

He stepped back. The stone cooled instantly, hardening back into impenetrable granite.

"A two-person soak," Horg nodded, impressed. "That's a lot of water to haul."

"We have the hose," Kaelan pointed to the leather hose attached to the well pump. "And I'm the boiler."

"And the other business?" Horg asked, gesturing to the far corner of the yard, away from the house and the tub.

"The Privy," Kaelan nodded.

They walked over to the small wooden shack in the corner. It was drafty and leaned slightly to the left.

"Tear it down," Kaelan decided.

Using the leftover stone from the wall construction, Kaelan built a small, neat stone outhouse. He lined the pit with the same baked ceramic technique he used for the root cellar, ensuring it was clean and sealed from the groundwater. He carved a smooth stone seat (which he could warm up in winter—a luxury no king possessed).

He hung a solid oak door on it, complete with a latch.

"Stone walls," Kaela observed, running her hand over the cool masonry. "No drafts."

"Civilization," Kaelan agreed.

Finally, they addressed the sink. Inside the cottage, they kept the ceramic pitcher and bowl on the washstand for face-washing. But for garden work, Kaelan carved a shallow stone trough directly under the well pump spout.

He drilled a drain hole that fed into the vegetable patch.

"Wash the carrots before they come inside," Kaela said, pumping the handle. Clear, cold water splashed into the stone sink. "Keeps the floor clean."

Kaelan stood in the center of the garden. He looked at the high walls. He looked at the solid oak gate with its tiny peep-hole. He looked at the massive stone tub waiting to be filled.

"We keep the wooden tub inside," Kaelan said. "For blizzards. But this..."

He patted the granite rim of the new bath.

"...this is for us."

"It's a fortress," Horg said, packing up his tools. "Nothing getting in here but sunlight and rain."

"That's the plan, Horg," Kaelan smiled. "That's the plan."

Horg left, the heavy gate booming shut behind him. Kaelan slid the bolt home.

Thunk.

The world was locked out.

"Bath?" Kaela asked, eyeing the empty stone vessel.

"Bath," Kaelan confirmed, reaching for the hose.

## Part 3: The Scrub

Theme: sunstead-joy

Location: The Garden Bath

POV: Kaela

=

The new bath was not just a tub; it was a monument to relaxation.

Kaelan had sunk the massive granite basin into the earth, leaving only a foot of smooth, rolled stone rim above the grass. It was vast—easily eight feet across—with a continuous stone bench carved around the entire interior perimeter. Wide, shallow steps led down into the water, complete with a copper handrail Horg had bent into shape.

To the side stood the "heater"—a clever copper-coil contraption Horg had built, encased in a small brick furnace. A fire crackled inside the brick box, heating the water that circulated through the coils via a thermosiphon, keeping the massive volume of water at a steady, steaming temperature without Kaelan having to lift a finger.

"It's full," Kaelan announced, tossing a log into the furnace.

Steam rolled off the surface of the water, rising into the cool night air like a prayer.

They stripped by the stone bench, leaving their tunics folded neatly. The high walls and the bolted oak gate stood guard. The world was locked out.

They walked down the stone steps into the water.

"Oh," Kaela sighed, the water rising to her chest. "Oh, that is glorious."

It wasn't just hot; it was spacious. In the wooden tub inside, they had to fold themselves like origami to fit. Here, they could stretch. They could float.

Kaelan submerged to his chin, his long hair floating around him like a dark halo. He stretched his legs out; they didn't even touch the other side.

"Room to drift," he noted, sounding incredibly pleased with himself.

"And room to work," Kaela said. She grabbed the large sea sponge and the bar of lavender soap from the rim. "Come here. The ghost-itch is still driving me crazy."

Kaelan drifted over to the submerged bench where she sat. He turned around, sitting on the lower step between her knees so she could reach his back.

"Shoulders first," he requested. "That wool dug in deep."

Kaela lathered the sponge until it was a cloud of purple-scented bubbles. She began to scrub.

She didn't hold back. She pressed the rough sponge into his skin, scouring his broad shoulders, his neck, and down the long line of his spine. She scrubbed the tension out of his muscles. She scrubbed the memory of the tarp-kilt out of his pores.

Kaelan groaned, his head dropping forward.

"Harder on the left shoulder," he mumbled. "Right there."

"I got it."

She washed him methodically. She moved down to his arms, lifting each one to scrub the triceps and forearms. She washed his sides, her fingers digging into the ribs where the Gear-Breaker had bruised him.

"Turn," she commanded.

Kaelan turned on the step to face her. He looked relaxed, his eyes half-closed, his skin slick with soap and steam.

She scrubbed his chest, working the lather into the golden skin. She washed his stomach, his hips, and his legs, making sure every inch of him was clean, soft, and utterly free of the Voidlands' grit.

"My turn," Kaelan said, taking the sponge from her.

Kaela shifted, leaning back against the smooth stone rim. Kaelan moved closer, the water sloshing gently around them.

He lathered the sponge again. His touch was firm and thorough.

He started with her neck, scrubbing away the phantom itch of the wool collar. He washed her collarbones, her breasts, and her stomach. He lifted her legs one by one, scrubbing her calves and feet, massaging the arches where the magnetic boots had pinched.

It wasn't sexual; it was maintenance. It was care. It was two halves of the same soul ensuring the other half was whole.

"Better?" Kaelan asked, rinsing the soap from her arm with a splash of hot water.

"Much better," Kaela sighed. "I feel... human again. Or at least, like a clean Scion."

"Good."

Kaelan tossed the sponge onto the grass. He moved to the bench next to her.

He didn't move to the other side of the massive tub. He sat right beside her, their shoulders touching, his arm draping along the rim behind her head.

They sat in the steam, looking up at the stars framed by the high stone walls. The fire in the heater crackled softly. The water lapped against the granite.

"We should do this every night," Kaela decided.

"Every night," Kaelan agreed. "Unless it's raining. Then we use the indoor bucket."

"No," Kaela corrected, closing her eyes and leaning her head on his shoulder. "If it rains, we just get wet. I'm never squeezing into that bucket again."

Kaelan laughed, a low rumble in his chest. "Deal."
