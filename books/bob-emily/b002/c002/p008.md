# Chapter Notes: The Planetarium Promise

From the very first week of school in September, the highlight of Bob and Emily's academic year was a date circled on the Earth Science syllabus: the annual spring field trip. What made it special this year was the destination. Galax Community College, a 45-minute drive away, had just opened a brand-new, state-of-the-art planetarium the month before. The chance to see the stars in a new way became a beacon for the two astronomy-loving cousins. Their shared, excited chirps in the Heart-Hum whenever Mr. Shaneyfelt mentioned it were a constant, happy feature of his second-period class.

Then came the locker incident in January.

During the first, pain-filled weeks of his recovery, as Bob lay on the sofa in the living room, a new fear took root, one that had nothing to do with his medical prognosis. One afternoon, he got Emily’s attention and, using the Heart-Hum, made a series of sad, questioning moans while gesturing vaguely at the calendar on the wall. He then put his hands together and drew a large dome in the air, finishing with a soft sound like twinkling stars. Emily understood immediately. He was terrified he was going to miss the planetarium.

His fear was confirmed a few days later when his favorite teacher came to visit. Mr. Shaneyfelt sat by the sofa, talking about class, when Bob repeated the sad, worried sounds.

"He's afraid he's going to miss the field trip in April," Diane translated, her voice soft.

Mr. Shaneyfelt looked at Bob, his expression full of genuine compassion. "Bob, you listen to me," he said firmly but kindly. "That trip isn't for months. You focus on healing. If your doctors say you are cleared for travel by then, I promise you, you have a spot on that bus. We'll make it work."

As if on cue, the doctor making rounds entered the room to do a routine check. Overhearing the conversation, he smiled. "The teacher's right, Bob," he said, checking his chart. "From a medical standpoint, there is no reason you won't be stable enough for a simple bus trip by April. You'll be in your wheelchair, but you'll be able to go."

That promise became Bob's new beacon. It was a tangible piece of his old, normal life that he could still hold onto.
