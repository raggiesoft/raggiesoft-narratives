The first thing Bob was aware of was a sound. A slow, steady, rhythmic beeping that seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere at once. It was a sound that was trying to tell him he was alive.

He forced his heavy eyelids open. The world was a blurry, unfocused smear of white light and fuzzy shapes. A dull, throbbing ache pulsed from his leg, a deep and distant pain that was being held at bay by a warm, heavy fog that filled his mind. He tried to speak, to ask where he was, but all that came out was a weak, dry croak.

"Hey... hey, he's waking up." The voice was his mother Diane's, thick with exhaustion and worry.

A shape leaned over him. It was Emily. Her face was pale, her eyes red-rimmed and huge. She made a soft, questioning sound in the Heart-Hum, a sound so full of pain and love it almost hurt to hear. *Are you here? Are you with me?*

He tried to respond, to make the familiar, comforting hum that meant *I'm here*, but the mask made it impossible. His mouth couldn't move, and the steady push of air prevented any sound. He could only look into her eyes and weakly squeeze her hand.

The world slowly came into focus. He was in a small room filled with machines he didn't recognize. An IV was taped to the back of his hand, and he could feel the uncomfortable presence of a tube in his nose. This was the Intensive Care Unit.

His family was a constant, rotating vigil at his bedside. He would drift in and out of a medicated haze, and every time he woke, one of them was there—Diane, David, or Emily, their faces a mixture of love and profound sadness.

By that evening, a doctor came in after reviewing a series of charts. "He's stable," the doctor said to Diane and David. "His vitals are strong. The surgery went as well as could be expected. We can move him out of the ICU and to a private recovery room on the pediatric wing." As the nurses prepared him for the transfer, one of them gently removed the restrictive CPAP mask, replacing it with a small, clear oxygen tube that rested just under his nose. The moment his mouth was free, Emily let out a shuddering sigh of pure relief.

The transfer was a disorienting journey in his hospital bed, the ceiling tiles sliding by overhead. The new room was quieter, the beeping less insistent. It felt less like a crisis and more like a long, quiet battle had just begun. Emily was there, her hand gripping his, her presence the only anchor in his strange new reality. He was out of immediate danger, but his long, painful road to recovery was only just beginning. As they settled him in, Emily leaned close. He looked at her and managed the faintest, weakest hum. *I'm here.* It was the most beautiful sound she had ever heard.
