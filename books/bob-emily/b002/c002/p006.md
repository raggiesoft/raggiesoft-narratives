At 7:00 PM, the daytime staff rotated out, replaced by the quiet, focused energy of the night shift. By 9:00 PM, the family had settled in for their first unexpected night together. David made his bed on the sofa by the window while Emily curled up in the recliner. The chair was closer to Bob's bed, but it still wasn't close enough for the deep, grounding comfort they craved. They both hated it, but they understood. Doctor's orders were doctor's orders.

Bob, used to the routine, drifted into a light sleep. David, too, from his own past hospital stays, dozed on the sofa, aware the night would not be peaceful. But for Emily, this was a new and jarring experience.

At 11:00 PM, the room was flooded with harsh, fluorescent light. Emily jolted awake, her heart hammering as a nurse she didn't recognize entered. Her protective instincts flared, thinking something was wrong.

"It's okay, honey," David whispered from the sofa. "It's just a routine vitals check. They do this every few hours." The nurse offered a kind, apologetic smile as she quietly checked Bob's blood pressure. Just as quickly as they came on, the lights clicked off, plunging the room back into semi-darkness. Emily's adrenaline slowly subsided, leaving her wide awake.

She had just managed to drift off when, at 2:00 AM, the lights blazed on again. This time, Emily was annoyed but prepared. The nurse came in to give Bob his scheduled medication. Bob, now awake, indicated with a soft grunt that he needed to use the bathroom. The nurse, understanding, called for an assistant, and a moment later, two staff members were gently helping a groggy Bob to the private toilet and back into bed. Emily watched the entire process, a profound sadness settling over her at the sheer logistics of his new life.

At 5:00 AM, the lights came on again for the last overnight check. This time, Emily barely stirred, a weary resignation having settled in.

When the lights came on for good at 7:00 AM, it was the morning nurse with Bob's breakfast tray and his next dose of medication. Emily sat up, her body stiff, her eyes bleary from the broken sleep.

"Morning," David said softly from the sofa. "How about we go hunt and gather? I think the cafeteria is serving pancakes."

As they were putting on their shoes, the local news was running a "Storm Watch" update. "For those of you just waking up," the reporter said, "we have accumulations of at least a half a foot of snow, with more expected. All Carroll County schools and government offices are closed. Officials are advising everyone to stay off the roads."

They were officially trapped. David and Emily went down to the hospital cafeteria, got their breakfast to-go, and brought it back to the room. The three of them ate together, watching the blizzard rage outside the window, a tiny, resilient family in their own private snow globe.
