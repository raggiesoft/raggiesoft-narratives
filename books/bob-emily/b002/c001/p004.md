The air in Dr. Ragsdale’s office was thick with a tense, unnatural quiet. Dr. Ragsdale sat behind her desk, her posture rigid, her expression unreadable. In the two chairs opposite her sat Officer Miller, a veteran cop whose calm demeanor couldn't hide the weariness in his eyes, and Officer Davis, younger, sharper, and radiating a coiled energy. They were waiting.

A knock came at the door—a quick, almost arrogant rap.

"Come in," Dr. Ragsdale called out.

Caleb Anderson entered, a familiar, defiant smirk on his face. He took in the scene, and the smirk faltered, replaced by a flicker of confusion and then defensive annoyance as he saw the two uniformed officers.

"Have a seat, Caleb," Dr. Ragsdale said, her voice devoid of any warmth. She gestured to the empty chair beside Officer Davis.

He hesitated, then slumped into the chair, his eyes darting between the principal and the cops. "What's this about? Did I miss another detention?" he asked, trying to sound bored.

"We're well past detention, Caleb," Dr. Ragsdale stated coldly. "We're here to discuss what happened to Bob Scott this morning in the C-wing hallway."

Caleb’s attempt at nonchalance hardened into a sneer. "I have no idea what you're talking about."

"Don't lie in my office," Dr. Ragsdale shot back, her voice like ice. "And especially don't lie to them. We have the entire incident on security footage. We have you, Caleb, assaulting him and shoving him into his locker."

Panic flared in Caleb’s eyes, quickly masked by a surge of belligerent anger. "He deserved it! Him and that freak cousin of his, they've had it in for me all year! Always staring at me, whispering about me! It was just a prank to teach him a lesson! He's fine!"

Officer Miller, who had been silent until now, leaned forward slightly. His voice was unnervingly calm. "He's not fine, son. We just got off the phone with the hospital liaison. The boy has a severe leg injury. A permanent one."

Dr. Ragsdale drove the point home. "The doctors have confirmed that because of the hours he was trapped, he will have a progressive disability. He will eventually need a wheelchair. You did that, Caleb."

The color drained from Caleb’s face. The reality of the situation was finally crashing through his wall of arrogance. "No... I just... I just shoved him. It was a joke. I didn't mean for..."

"I think we've heard enough," Officer Miller said, sharing a brief, decisive glance with his partner. He pushed himself to his feet, his calm demeanor replaced by an undeniable authority. "Caleb Anderson, on your feet. Now."

Caleb stared at him, his mouth half-open, his mind unable to process the command. "What?"

"Stand up," Officer Davis repeated, rising smoothly from his own chair. "Turn around and place your hands behind your back."

"Wait, no! You can't!" Caleb stammered, a note of genuine terror in his voice as he was pulled to his feet. "It was a misunderstanding! I'm sorry!"

"It's too late for sorry," Officer Miller said grimly as Officer Davis secured Caleb's wrists behind him. The sharp, metallic click of the handcuffs echoed in the small office. "Caleb Anderson, you are under arrest for aggravated assault and felonious restraint. You have the right to remain silent. Anything you say can and will be used against you in a court of law."

As Officer Miller continued reading him his rights, the officers guided the now silent, trembling Caleb out of the office. The door clicked shut, leaving Dr. Ragsdale alone in the sudden, heavy silence. She took no satisfaction from the scene, only a deep, profound sorrow for the lives that had been shattered in her hallways that day.
