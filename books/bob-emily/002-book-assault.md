# Book: Assault

## Chapter 1: The Locker

### Part 1: The Assault

The legend of the "Emily Jenkins Rule" gave the cousins a wide berth of respect and safety in the crowded hallways of Bluewater High. Bullies and troublemakers, having heard the story of the girl who was stronger than half the football team, left them completely alone.

Everyone got the hint. Everyone except Caleb Anderson.

For Caleb, the weeks after the weight room incident were a torment of festering rage and humiliation. Every time he saw Emily, he didn't see a girl to be feared; he saw a prize that was being unfairly kept from him. In his twisted, obsessive mind, his failure to impress her wasn't his own fault. It was Bob's. Bob was the obstacle. Bob was the reason she didn't see him.

It all came to a head on a Tuesday in January.

Bob was at their shared locker between classes, swapping out his books. The hallway was momentarily quiet. He didn't notice Caleb approach until the senior slammed a hand against the locker next to his head, trapping him.

"You think you're so clever, don't you?" Caleb snarled, his 175-pound frame looming over the much smaller boy. He smelled of stale sweat and anger. "You and her, laughing at me. Making a fool out of me."

Bob shook his head frantically, letting out a small, frightened whimper as he pressed himself back against the cool metal of the lockers.

Caleb laughed, a cruel, dismissive sound devoid of any humor. *Still won’t even speak to me, huh?* he thought, his rage boiling over. *I'll teach you to get in my way.* He saw Emily's strength as a challenge he couldn't meet, but this? This he could do.

"You need to stay away from her," he growled, his voice low and dangerous. "She's mine."

He grabbed Bob, whose feet scrambled for purchase on the linoleum floor. In a shocking act of violence fueled by weeks of impotent fury, Caleb began stuffing this smaller boy into the locker. Bob cried out, a sharp, piercing sound of pain and terror. His leg was twisted at a sickening, unnatural angle as Caleb used his full weight to force him into the cramped metal box.

There was a final, agonizing cry from Bob, and then the locker door was slammed shut, plunging him into a world of darkness and white-hot pain.

Caleb stood there for a moment, his breath ragged, his heart pounding with a sick sense of victory. He hadn't just punished Bob; he had removed the obstacle. He turned and walked away laughing, ready to go find his "prize," leaving Bob broken and trapped in the dark.

### Part 2: The Discovery

The hallway was quiet, filled with the muffled sounds of teaching from behind closed classroom doors. Emily approached the locker she shared with Bob, a textbook held to her chest, a soft, happy hum in her throat as she thought about their plans for the weekend.

But as she got closer, the hum died. She heard a sound from inside the locker—a faint, rhythmic whimper.

Her blood ran cold. It was a sound she knew with every fiber of her being. It was Bob’s specific whimper of true, helpless pain.

A panicked, high-pitched chirp escaped her lips. Her hands, suddenly clumsy and shaking, flew to the combination lock. The numbers seemed to swim before her eyes. Once, twice, her fingers slipped off the dial. A surge of pure, primal adrenaline coursed through her. In the back of her mind, a frantic thought formed: *If this doesn't open on the next try, I will rip this door off its hinges.*

On the third try, her trembling fingers finally found their purchase. The lock clicked open. She yanked the metal door with all her strength.

Bob tumbled out, landing in a heap on the linoleum floor. He was pale and slick with sweat, his leg twisted at a sickening, unnatural angle beneath him. He tried to push himself up, his hands scrabbling weakly at the floor, but he couldn't move his leg. A low moan of agony and terror slipped from his lips.

Seeing him crumpled and broken on the ground, Emily’s system overloaded. Her own form of a scream tore through her—not a word, but a frantic, desperate chattering. A cascade of sharp clicks, guttural moans, and high-pitched chirps filled the empty hallway, the sounds of pure terror and a desperate plea for help.

The alarm had been raised, but in the cold reality of the echoing hallway, hours of critical time had already been lost forever. She dropped to her knees beside Bob, hovering over him, her own frantic noises mixing with his pained whimpers. For what felt like an eternity, they were alone in their bubble of horror, invisible and unheard.

Then, the world shattered. The harsh, jarring buzz of the school bell signaling the end of the period cut through the air. Doors flew open, and the hallway instantly flooded with the loud, chaotic rush of students. Laughter, shouting, and the slamming of lockers created a wall of oblivious noise. For a moment, the sea of students simply parted around the two figures on the floor.

Then, a girl shrieked. "Oh my God! What's wrong with him?"

"Look at his leg!" another voice shouted.

The chatter and laughter died in their immediate vicinity, replaced by gasps of horror. A small circle of shocked faces stared down at them.

"Teacher!" a boy finally yelled, his voice cracking with panic. "We need a teacher over here! Now!"

A teacher pushed through the gathering crowd, his face shifting from annoyance to pure shock in an instant. The alarm had finally been raised. But in the cold reality of the echoing hallway, hours of critical time had already been lost forever.

### Part 4: The Investigation

The quiet hum of the main office was a stark contrast to the violence that had unfolded in the hallway. Dr. Rachel Ragsdale sat at her desk, her expression a mask of cold, controlled fury. The school's guidance counselor, Mr. Henderson, sat opposite her, looking pale and shaken.

"I've reviewed the security camera footage from the C-wing hallway," Dr. Ragsdale said, her voice clipped and precise. She gestured to her monitor, which showed a grainy but clear image of a tall, lanky senior cornering Bob at his locker. "It's exactly who I suspected."

Mr. Henderson leaned forward, his shoulders slumping in grim recognition. "Caleb Anderson."

Dr. Ragsdale clicked open the student's digital file on her screen. "His disciplinary record is a mile long," she stated flatly, scrolling through the list of infractions. "Page after page of petty violations. Detention for insubordination, cutting class, disrespect to staff, vandalism... He’s a repeat offender who clearly thinks the rules are for everyone else."

"He's spent more time in detention this year than some students spend in study hall," Mr. Henderson confirmed, his voice heavy with frustration. "We've tried every internal tool we have. Warnings, parent conferences, more detention. Nothing gets through to him. And all year, he's been harassing the Jenkins girl."

"I know," Dr. Ragsdale said, her voice dropping to a low, dangerous tone. "So let me be perfectly clear on the facts. We have a student with a long-established pattern of defiance. On top of that, he spends the first semester as a 17-year-old harassing a 15-year-old girl. Then he turns 18, and as a legal adult, he violently assaults her 14-year-old cousin."

She looked up from the monitor, her eyes meeting Mr. Henderson's. "The school's ability to discipline him has reached its end. He has made that clear himself. This is a criminal matter now."

She picked up her desk phone, her movements sharp and deliberate. She pressed the button for her administrative assistant. "Patricia," she said, her voice firm. "Please get me Chief Brody of the Bluewater Police Department on my private line. Tell him it's an urgent matter regarding a violent felony committed by an adult student against a minor on school grounds."

She hung up the phone and looked at Mr. Henderson, her eyes like chips of ice. "I'm having him arrested. Today."

### Part 5: The Arrest

The air in Dr. Ragsdale’s office was thick with a tense, unnatural quiet. Dr. Ragsdale sat behind her desk, her posture rigid, her expression unreadable. In the two chairs opposite her sat Officer Miller, a veteran cop whose calm demeanor couldn't hide the weariness in his eyes, and Officer Davis, younger, sharper, and radiating a coiled energy. They were waiting.

A knock came at the door—a quick, almost arrogant rap.

"Come in," Dr. Ragsdale called out.

Caleb Anderson entered, a familiar, defiant smirk on his face. He took in the scene, and the smirk faltered, replaced by a flicker of confusion and then defensive annoyance as he saw the two uniformed officers.

"Have a seat, Caleb," Dr. Ragsdale said, her voice devoid of any warmth. She gestured to the empty chair beside Officer Davis.

He hesitated, then slumped into the chair, his eyes darting between the principal and the cops. "What's this about? Did I miss another detention?" he asked, trying to sound bored.

"We're well past detention, Caleb," Dr. Ragsdale stated coldly. "We're here to discuss what happened to Bob Scott this morning in the C-wing hallway."

Caleb’s attempt at nonchalance hardened into a sneer. "I have no idea what you're talking about."

"Don't lie in my office," Dr. Ragsdale shot back, her voice like ice. "And especially don't lie to them. We have the entire incident on security footage. We have you, Caleb, assaulting him and shoving him into his locker."

Panic flared in Caleb’s eyes, quickly masked by a surge of belligerent anger. "He deserved it! Him and that freak cousin of his, they've had it in for me all year! Always staring at me, whispering about me! It was just a prank to teach him a lesson! He's fine!"

Officer Miller, who had been silent until now, leaned forward slightly. His voice was unnervingly calm. "He's not fine, son. We just got off the phone with the hospital liaison. The boy has a severe leg injury. A permanent one."

Dr. Ragsdale drove the point home. "The doctors have confirmed that because of the hours he was trapped, he will have a progressive disability. He will eventually need a wheelchair. You did that, Caleb."

The color drained from Caleb’s face. The reality of the situation was finally crashing through his wall of arrogance. "No... I just... I just shoved him. It was a joke. I didn't mean for..."

"I think we've heard enough," Officer Miller said, sharing a brief, decisive glance with his partner. He pushed himself to his feet, his calm demeanor replaced by an undeniable authority. "Caleb Anderson, on your feet. Now."

Caleb stared at him, his mouth half-open, his mind unable to process the command. "What?"

"Stand up," Officer Davis repeated, rising smoothly from his own chair. "Turn around and place your hands behind your back."

"Wait, no! You can't!" Caleb stammered, a note of genuine terror in his voice as he was pulled to his feet. "It was a misunderstanding! I'm sorry!"

"It's too late for sorry," Officer Miller said grimly as Officer Davis secured Caleb's wrists behind him. The sharp, metallic click of the handcuffs echoed in the small office. "Caleb Anderson, you are under arrest for aggravated assault and felonious restraint. You have the right to remain silent. Anything you say can and will be used against you in a court of law."

As Officer Miller continued reading him his rights, the officers guided the now silent, trembling Caleb out of the office. The door clicked shut, leaving Dr. Ragsdale alone in the sudden, heavy silence. She took no satisfaction from the scene, only a deep, profound sorrow for the lives that had been shattered in her hallways that day.

### Part 6: The Perp Walk

The journey from Dr. Ragsdale’s office to the main entrance of the school was the longest walk of Caleb Anderson’s life. With his hands cuffed securely behind his back, he was led by Officer Miller and Officer Davis through the crowded lobby during the busiest part of a class change.

The usual roar of high school chatter died down as he passed. Students stopped talking and simply stared. These weren't looks of idle curiosity; they were looks of cold, hard judgment. The story of what had happened to Bob Scott had spread through the school like wildfire. Everyone knew. They saw Caleb not as a misunderstood troublemaker, but as the person who had put a well-liked, disabled freshman in the hospital. The whispers that followed him down the hall were not sympathetic.

"That's him," a sophomore girl muttered to her friend. "The one who put Bob in the hospital."

Caleb kept his head down, his face burning with a shame so intense it felt like a physical fever. His earlier bravado had evaporated, leaving only the humiliating reality of his situation.

When they reached the main lobby, a marked Bluewater Police car was waiting at the curb just outside the glass doors. Officer Davis opened the rear door, and Officer Miller firmly guided the now-compliant Caleb into the back seat. The door slammed shut with a heavy, final thud.

From her office window, Dr. Ragsdale watched the police car pull away, her expression grim. There was no triumph in this, only a profound sadness for all the lives that had been shattered. Her duty, however, was not yet over. She turned from the window, sat down at her desk, and picked up the phone. She dialed the number for the county school superintendent's office.

"Yes, this is Rachel Ragsdale at Bluewater High School," she said, her voice steady and professional. "I need to report a serious student incident resulting in a felony arrest on school grounds."

## Chapter 2: The Hospital

### Part 1: The Long Wait

The controlled chaos of the children's hospital emergency room swirled around them, but the Scott family was an island of tense, focused silence. Bob lay on a gurney in a curtained-off bay, his face pale with pain and shock, his mangled legs covered by a thin white sheet.

An emergency physician, Dr. Evans, stood speaking in low, urgent tones to Diane and David. "I need to be very direct with you," he said, his expression grave. "The X-rays show severe fractures, but the primary issue is the time delay. The pressure from being trapped for hours caused extensive and, I'm afraid, irreversible nerve and tissue damage." He took a deep breath. "Had Bob been brought here immediately, we would be looking at a full recovery. Because of the delay... the damage is catastrophic. He will not be able to put weight on either of his legs again and will require a wheelchair for the rest of his life."

Diane let out a choked sob, and David wrapped an arm around her, his own face a mask of disbelief and rage.

"Our surgical team is prepping a room for him now," Dr. Evans continued gently. "The priority is an emergency operation to save as much of his legs as they can."

Beside the gurney, Emily sat on a stool, her hand gripping Bob's tightly. Their own quiet, frantic conversation was happening in the Heart-Hum. He squeezed her hand and managed a series of faint but furious grunts. *It was Caleb. Caleb did this.*

Emily's expression hardened. She pulled out her mPhone, her thumb shaking as she brought up a text message from a classmate. She held the screen so Bob could see it. It read:

OMG Emily they just took Caleb Anderson out of the office in handcuffs

A sliver of dark, terrible satisfaction passed between them just as two orderlies came through the curtain. "It's time," they said gently. After a final, tearful moment, Bob was wheeled away, leaving his family alone to begin the long, agonizing wait.

### Part 2: Emily Explains Everything

Hours later, in a sterile, quiet waiting room, the adrenaline had faded, leaving only the dull ache of fear and grief. Diane finally turned to her niece. "Emily, honey," she said softly, "Who is Caleb? Why would he do this to Bob?"

Through a complex and angry series of grunts, gestures, and sharp chirps, the whole story came out. Emily, using the Heart-Hum, explained everything. She told them about the repeated harassment, his refusal to take no for an answer, and finally, she described the scene in the weight room. She made the sound for "weights," then pointed to an imaginary Bob and mimicked the motion of a bench press.

David's head shot up. "Wait," he said, looking at Diane for confirmation. "Did she just say...?"

Diane, equally stunned, stared at her niece. **"You did WHAT with Bob? Bench press? WHAT!?"**

Emily gave a single, firm, almost proud nod. *Yes, I bench pressed my cousin.*

The message was clear. Apparently, Caleb didn't get the hint. He thought he could get to Emily by hurting Bob.

Emily then pulled out her mPhone and showed both David and Diane the text message she had received earlier from her classmate about seeing Caleb being led away in handcuffs. They stared at the screen, finally understanding the full, bizarre, and tragic sequence of events that had led them to this waiting room.

### Part 3: Waking Up

The first thing Bob was aware of was a sound. A slow, steady, rhythmic beeping that seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere at once. It was a sound that was trying to tell him he was alive.

He forced his heavy eyelids open. The world was a blurry, unfocused smear of white light and fuzzy shapes. A dull, throbbing ache pulsed from his leg, a deep and distant pain that was being held at bay by a warm, heavy fog that filled his mind. He tried to speak, to ask where he was, but all that came out was a weak, dry croak.

"Hey... hey, he's waking up." The voice was his mother Diane's, thick with exhaustion and worry.

A shape leaned over him. It was Emily. Her face was pale, her eyes red-rimmed and huge. She made a soft, questioning sound in the Heart-Hum, a sound so full of pain and love it almost hurt to hear. *Are you here? Are you with me?*

He tried to respond, to make the familiar, comforting hum that meant *I'm here*, but the mask made it impossible. His mouth couldn't move, and the steady push of air prevented any sound. He could only look into her eyes and weakly squeeze her hand.

The world slowly came into focus. He was in a small room filled with machines he didn't recognize. An IV was taped to the back of his hand, and he could feel the uncomfortable presence of a tube in his nose. This was the Intensive Care Unit.

His family was a constant, rotating vigil at his bedside. He would drift in and out of a medicated haze, and every time he woke, one of them was there—Diane, David, or Emily, their faces a mixture of love and profound sadness.

By that evening, a doctor came in after reviewing a series of charts. "He's stable," the doctor said to Diane and David. "His vitals are strong. The surgery went as well as could be expected. We can move him out of the ICU and to a private recovery room on the pediatric wing." As the nurses prepared him for the transfer, one of them gently removed the restrictive CPAP mask, replacing it with a small, clear oxygen tube that rested just under his nose. The moment his mouth was free, Emily let out a shuddering sigh of pure relief.

The transfer was a disorienting journey in his hospital bed, the ceiling tiles sliding by overhead. The new room was quieter, the beeping less insistent. It felt less like a crisis and more like a long, quiet battle had just begun. Emily was there, her hand gripping his, her presence the only anchor in his strange new reality. He was out of immediate danger, but his long, painful road to recovery was only just beginning. As they settled him in, Emily leaned close. He looked at her and managed the faintest, weakest hum. *I'm here.* It was the most beautiful sound she had ever heard.

### Part 4: The Daily Vigil

The days in the pediatric recovery room fell into a quiet, monotonous rhythm. David, having moved his entire work-from-home setup to the small hospital visitor's table, filled the daytime hours with the soft clicking of his keyboard. His steady, calm presence was a comfort, but Bob's days were largely spent watching the clock, his mind counting down the minutes until school let out.

He had Emily's schedule memorized. Dismissal at 2:30 PM. A short walk across the street to the Transit Center. The 2:45 PM Green Line bus. By 3:15 PM, his anticipation would be a palpable, buzzing energy.

Right on schedule, David's phone buzzed with a text from Emily: Here. At the front desk.

"Alright, buddy, be right back," David said, closing his laptop. "The eagle has landed." He gave his son a reassuring pat on the shoulder before leaving the room to escort his niece up from the lobby.

A few minutes later, the door pushed open, and Emily was there, dropping her backpack with a familiar thud. The entire energy of the room shifted. She went straight to Bob's bedside, and they immediately performed their now-practiced hospital ritual—each placing a hand over the other's heart, sharing a deep, resonant hum that spoke volumes.

As Emily settled into the recliner, David turned on the local news. They sat together, a temporary, strange kind of family normal. As the news turned to the weather report, the meteorologist pointed to a swirling mass on the map.

"...and folks, we're keeping a very close eye on a system moving in from the west," she said. "Still a few days out, so it's too early to say for sure, but there is the potential for some significant winter weather toward the end of the week. We'll have more on that on the 5 o'clock news."

David and Emily exchanged a brief, worried glance. A bad snowstorm was no longer just an inconvenience; it was now a serious complication in their already complicated lives. But for now, they pushed the thought aside. Emily was here, and for the next few hours, their small family was whole again in the quiet of the hospital room.

### Part 5: The Storm Arrives

The day started like any other in their new hospital routine. Emily caught the 2:45 PM Green Line bus, arrived at the hospital around 3:15 PM, and was escorted up to Bob's room by David. The weather reports had been consistent all day: a major snowstorm was coming, but it wasn't expected to arrive until well after midnight.

Inside, the atmosphere was one of watchful waiting. The sky outside was a heavy, slate-grey, but no snow had yet begun to fall. They settled into their familiar rhythm: David on his laptop, Emily in a chair she pulled up to the hospital bed, her hand holding Bob's. They had the local news on the television, and soon, the weather report came on.

"Okay, folks, this is an updated 'Storm Watch' alert," the meteorologist said, standing in front of a swirling map. "The models are all in agreement now. The leading edge of this storm system is projected to arrive well after midnight tonight, but it is going to be a big one."

He pointed to a graphic showing potential accumulation totals. "We are looking at sustained, heavy snowfall through the overnight hours and all day tomorrow. Accumulations for Bluewater and the surrounding plateau could easily top **two feet** by this time tomorrow evening. I can't stress this enough: if you don't have to be on the roads tomorrow, please stay home."

David, Emily, and Bob all looked at each other.

"Well," David said, breaking the silence. "Looks like we'll be making it home just in time tonight. But I don't think we'll be able to get back here tomorrow."

A low, worried hum passed from Bob to Emily. The thought of her not being able to visit, of being separated by a wall of snow for a whole day or more, was a new and unwelcome anxiety. She squeezed his hand reassuringly, but she felt the same dread coiling in her own stomach.

For now, they were together. But they all knew that the storm, and a long separation, was coming.

As evening approached, David and Emily made their usual trip down to the cafeteria, getting their food to-go so they could eat with Bob when his dinner tray arrived. As they settled in to eat, the first few flakes of snow began to drift lazily past the window.

Within moments, the gentle flurry became a fierce, swirling blizzard. The world outside vanished into a wall of white. On the television, a breaking news banner flashed across the screen with reports of sudden, treacherous road conditions and multiple accidents. Everyone knew the storm was coming, but no one expected it to arrive so early and with such ferocity.

David looked at the raging blizzard outside, then at the news reports of wrecks already happening. He made a decision. "That's it," he said firmly. "We're not going anywhere tonight. It's not safe."

Just then, a young nurse in her twenties came in to do a routine check on Bob's vitals. "The roads look pretty bad out there," she commented.

"That's what I was just saying," David replied. "It doesn't look like my niece and I will be able to get home tonight."

The nurse's expression became concerned. "Well, sir, you're welcome to stay, but I have to tell you, hospital policy prohibits overnight visitors who are minors."

"I understand," David said calmly. "I am both Emily's uncle and her legal guardian, and given the emergency weather conditions, I'm more than willing to sign a waiver or any other document required for her to stay here with me."

The nurse checked Bob's vitals—he was recovering as well as could be expected—and nodded. "Okay. Let me go get my supervisor."

She returned a few minutes later with the head nurse for the floor. After the situation was explained—that Emily was a minor who couldn't get home safely and that her legal guardian was present and willing to take full responsibility—the arrangements were made. The waiver was signed.

David called Diane, who was safe at home. "Hey, honey. The storm hit early. We're snowed in... Yes, we're fine. The hospital is letting Emily stay with me... Yep, signed the waiver. You stay safe and warm. We'll see you when the roads are clear."

As the staff brought in extra blankets, Bob's excitement was impossible to hide. He let out a happy chirp in the Heart-Hum; Emily was staying the night! But his excitement was immediately followed by a low, sad moan as he looked from his hospital bed to the recliner a few feet away.

Emily heard it and responded with her own soft, sad hum. Bob desperately wanted her to be in the bed with him, to snuggle up like they always did. She wanted it too. But they both understood it wasn't safe for his legs while he recovered. He was happy she was here, but sad she couldn't be right *here*. David took the sofa by the window, and Emily settled into the recliner for the long night ahead.

### Part 6: The Longest Night

At 7:00 PM, the daytime staff rotated out, replaced by the quiet, focused energy of the night shift. By 9:00 PM, the family had settled in for their first unexpected night together. David made his bed on the sofa by the window while Emily curled up in the recliner. The chair was closer to Bob's bed, but it still wasn't close enough for the deep, grounding comfort they craved. They both hated it, but they understood. Doctor's orders were doctor's orders.

Bob, used to the routine, drifted into a light sleep. David, too, from his own past hospital stays, dozed on the sofa, aware the night would not be peaceful. But for Emily, this was a new and jarring experience.

At 11:00 PM, the room was flooded with harsh, fluorescent light. Emily jolted awake, her heart hammering as a nurse she didn't recognize entered. Her protective instincts flared, thinking something was wrong.

"It's okay, honey," David whispered from the sofa. "It's just a routine vitals check. They do this every few hours." The nurse offered a kind, apologetic smile as she quietly checked Bob's blood pressure. Just as quickly as they came on, the lights clicked off, plunging the room back into semi-darkness. Emily's adrenaline slowly subsided, leaving her wide awake.

She had just managed to drift off when, at 2:00 AM, the lights blazed on again. This time, Emily was annoyed but prepared. The nurse came in to give Bob his scheduled medication. Bob, now awake, indicated with a soft grunt that he needed to use the bathroom. The nurse, understanding, called for an assistant, and a moment later, two staff members were gently helping a groggy Bob to the private toilet and back into bed. Emily watched the entire process, a profound sadness settling over her at the sheer logistics of his new life.

At 5:00 AM, the lights came on again for the last overnight check. This time, Emily barely stirred, a weary resignation having settled in.

When the lights came on for good at 7:00 AM, it was the morning nurse with Bob's breakfast tray and his next dose of medication. Emily sat up, her body stiff, her eyes bleary from the broken sleep.

"Morning," David said softly from the sofa. "How about we go hunt and gather? I think the cafeteria is serving pancakes."

As they were putting on their shoes, the local news was running a "Storm Watch" update. "For those of you just waking up," the reporter said, "we have accumulations of at least a half a foot of snow, with more expected. All Carroll County schools and government offices are closed. Officials are advising everyone to stay off the roads."

They were officially trapped. David and Emily went down to the hospital cafeteria, got their breakfast to-go, and brought it back to the room. The three of them ate together, watching the blizzard rage outside the window, a tiny, resilient family in their own private snow globe.

### Part 7: Discharge Day

The morning after the third snowed-in night was bright and clear. The main roads were finally passable, and the doctor officially signed Bob's discharge papers. After twelve long days, they were finally going home.

David went to get the car while Diane and Emily helped Bob get packed. A short while later, David was pushing Bob in the wheelchair through the hospital's main doors for the last time, with Emily walking silently beside them.

When they got to the car, David opened the front passenger door.

Emily stopped, a confused and questioning chirp escaping her. She looked at the open front door, then at the empty back seat where she had expected Bob to be. That was their space.

David saw the look on her face and his expression softened. "Honey," he explained gently. "It's just for now. It's much easier to get him in and out of the front seat than to try and maneuver him into the back. There's more room for his legs here."

A wave of frustration and sadness washed over Emily. It was another small, simple thing that Caleb's actions had stolen from them. She hated it, but she looked at Bob's carefully supported legs and understood. With a reluctant, unhappy sigh, she nodded and opened the rear door.

After David carefully helped Bob transfer from the wheelchair into the front passenger seat, he folded up the chair and put it in the trunk. Diane got into the driver's seat, and David, instead of getting in the front with her, slid into the back seat next to Emily. He put a comforting arm around her as she stared at the empty space beside her, then at the back of Bob's headrest. The distance between them felt immense.

As they pulled away from the hospital, a new chapter was beginning. They were going home, but they were leaving another piece of their old normal behind.

### Chapter Notes: The Planetarium Promise

From the very first week of school in September, the highlight of Bob and Emily's academic year was a date circled on the Earth Science syllabus: the annual spring field trip. What made it special this year was the destination. Galax Community College, a 45-minute drive away, had just opened a brand-new, state-of-the-art planetarium the month before. The chance to see the stars in a new way became a beacon for the two astronomy-loving cousins. Their shared, excited chirps in the Heart-Hum whenever Mr. Shaneyfelt mentioned it were a constant, happy feature of his second-period class.

Then came the locker incident in January.

During the first, pain-filled weeks of his recovery, as Bob lay on the sofa in the living room, a new fear took root, one that had nothing to do with his medical prognosis. One afternoon, he got Emily’s attention and, using the Heart-Hum, made a series of sad, questioning moans while gesturing vaguely at the calendar on the wall. He then put his hands together and drew a large dome in the air, finishing with a soft sound like twinkling stars. Emily understood immediately. He was terrified he was going to miss the planetarium.

His fear was confirmed a few days later when his favorite teacher came to visit. Mr. Shaneyfelt sat by the sofa, talking about class, when Bob repeated the sad, worried sounds.

"He's afraid he's going to miss the field trip in April," Diane translated, her voice soft.

Mr. Shaneyfelt looked at Bob, his expression full of genuine compassion. "Bob, you listen to me," he said firmly but kindly. "That trip isn't for months. You focus on healing. If your doctors say you are cleared for travel by then, I promise you, you have a spot on that bus. We'll make it work."

As if on cue, the doctor making rounds entered the room to do a routine check. Overhearing the conversation, he smiled. "The teacher's right, Bob," he said, checking his chart. "From a medical standpoint, there is no reason you won't be stable enough for a simple bus trip by April. You'll be in your wheelchair, but you'll be able to go."

That promise became Bob's new beacon. It was a tangible piece of his old, normal life that he could still hold onto.
