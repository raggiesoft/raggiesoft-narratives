# Book: Summer 2015 

## Chapter 1: A Tale of Two Sanctuaries

### Part 1: The Christiansburg Connection

The humid, heavy heat of a southwestern Virginia summer settled over Bluewater, bringing with it a quiet, lazy rhythm. With the grueling pressure of their freshman year and the stress of the SOLs officially behind them, Bob and Emily finally had time to breathe.

One lazy Tuesday afternoon in late June, the two cousins were lounging in the air-conditioned comfort of the living room. Bob was resting on his designated sofa, his legs propped up on his usual mountain of pillows, while Emily sat on the floor, leaning comfortably against his side.

Emily's mPad chimed with an incoming video call request on InfoLink Messenger. The caller ID read: Madi.

Emily tapped to accept, and the screen filled with the bright, slightly chaotic energy of the Brooks family basement in Christiansburg. Madison was front and center, her face glowing with a happy, relaxed summer tan. Behind her, Bob and Emily could see the rest of Madison's new "pact"—Zoe typing meticulously at a desk, Lauren and Sarah laughing together on a portable bed, and Aubrie Kelly sitting close to Jordan. Jordan's face looked healthier and far more at peace than it had when Madison had described his tense arrival the previous fall.

"Hey, squirt! Hey, Em!" Madison cheered, waving enthusiastically at the camera.

Bob let out a happy, trilling chirp in the Heart-Hum, waving back eagerly. Emily smiled, her fingers flying across her keyboard case to type a quick response. We miss you, Madi. How is the summer job?

"Exhausting, but totally worth it," Madison grinned. She picked up her laptop and walked over to a coffee table, pointing the camera down at a massive roll of architectural blueprints. "Look what Daniel brought home. We've been planning the layout for the basement suite of the new compound all week."

Jordan wheeled into the frame next to Madison, giving the camera a warm, genuine smile. "Hey guys. We're making sure there's a guest room right down the hall for when you two come visit."

Bob and Emily looked at each other, a shared, resonant hum of approval passing between them. They were so incredibly happy for Madison. They had heard firsthand from Madison's calls about the trauma Jordan had survived, and knowing that her older sister had found such a beautiful, protective family to build a life with lifted a huge weight off Emily's shoulders.

"It's going to be huge," Madison continued, tracing a section of the blueprint with her finger. "We're even mapping out where the Alaskan King bed is going to go."

After a few more minutes of sharing summer updates and promising to visit once the compound was built, they ended the call. The living room in Bluewater was quiet again, but it was a comfortable, secure silence. Madison was actively building her permanent sanctuary in Christiansburg. It was time for Bob and Emily to perfect theirs.

### Part 2: The Ironclad Will

The law offices of Sterling & Vance sat in a quiet, professional business park just a few miles from the Bluewater Transit Center. The air conditioning hummed a steady, white-noise rhythm against the oppressive July heat outside.

Inside the heavy mahogany doors of the inner office, the atmosphere was serious but surprisingly warm. Mr. Sterling, a seasoned family law attorney with a kind face and a calm, deliberate manner, sat behind his large desk. Opposite him sat Diane and David.

Emily stood immediately to the left of Bob’s wheelchair. Her massive, ongoing growth spurt was becoming undeniable; at nearly 5'7" and climbing, she towered over her seated cousin. She rested her hand on his shoulder, a constant, physical tether.

"Thank you all for coming in," Mr. Sterling began, folding his hands over a thick manila folder. He looked directly at Emily, his gaze respectful and acknowledging. He didn't treat her like a child or talk over her to her aunt and uncle. "Emily, this meeting is primarily about you and your future. Your aunt and uncle insisted you be here because they want you to be an active participant in this process."

Diane turned in her chair to face her niece. She let out a soft, warm coo. "We love you, sweetie," Diane said gently. "But we want to make sure every step we take is something you actually want. This is your life."

Emily looked at her aunt, then down at Bob. From the depths of her chest, she produced a quick, sharp click of her tongue followed immediately by a soft, warm coo—the Heart-Hum name for Diane. Our champion. Bob looked up and added a low, repeated, two-part grunt directed at David. Our foundation. Mr. Sterling, who had been briefed by Diane on their unique method of communication, simply smiled patiently and waited for them to finish their exchange.

"Your father, Richard, was a remarkable and incredibly forward-thinking man, Emily," Mr. Sterling continued, tapping the folder. "When Diane first brought me the documents he prepared at the hospital, I was genuinely astounded by his thoroughness. He didn't just leave a letter of intent; he built an absolute legal fortress around you."

He opened the folder and slid a few crisp, notarized pages across the desk so Emily and Bob could see them.

"Knowing that your mother's... ideologies... were escalating, your father took definitive action on his deathbed," the lawyer explained. "He didn't just transfer educational and medical decision-making authority to Diane. He had the attending physician, the charge nurse, and the hospital's clinical social worker all sign sworn affidavits testifying to his total mental capacity and clear intent at the exact moment he signed these directives."

Emily stared at her father's signature. It was shaky, a stark contrast to his usual strong handwriting, but it was there. A wave of profound, aching love washed over her. Even as his body was failing, his only thought had been to protect her.

"What does this mean for Karen?" David asked, his voice a low, protective rumble. "We've received some... bizarre letters from her recently. Lots of red ink, fingerprints, and pseudo-legal jargon claiming we've 'kidnapped her property.'"

Mr. Sterling let out a short, dismissive sigh. "Sovereign citizen filings," he said, shaking his head. "They are legally meaningless and entirely frivolous. Normally, a custody battle with a surviving biological parent can be a long, drawn-out nightmare. But because of what Richard did, Karen's path is completely blocked."

He looked back at Emily. "Richard explicitly documented his reasoning for revoking her authority, citing her anti-vaccine stance and her refusal of mainstream medical care as a direct threat to your health. By grounding his decision in mainstream medical consensus, and backing it up with the hospital staff's testimony, he met the highest legal standard for 'the best interest of the child.' No judge in the Commonwealth of Virginia will overturn this."

Emily let out a long, shaky breath. The phantom anxiety that her mother might somehow burst through the doors and drag her back to a life of paranoia and isolation evaporated. The fortress her father had built was holding strong.

"So," Mr. Sterling said, leaning forward. "The documents give Diane and David de facto control, which the school and your doctors have already accepted. But to make it permanent and airtight, we need to formally petition the court for full legal guardianship. And Emily, because of your age, the judge will want to know your preference."

He pushed a fresh yellow legal pad and a pen across the desk toward her. "If you are comfortable doing so, I'd like you to tell me, in your own words, what you want."

Emily didn't take the pen. Instead, she pulled her mPad from her backpack. Her fingers flew across the keyboard case with a fierce, practiced speed. She turned the screen around and pushed it across the heavy mahogany desk for Mr. Sterling to read.

My mother is sick, and her house is not safe. My father knew this. Diane and David are my parents now. Bob is my brother in every way that matters. I want to stay here. I want them to be my legal guardians forever.

Mr. Sterling read the screen, his expression softening with profound professional and personal respect. He nodded slowly.

"Very clear, and very well said, Emily," he affirmed. "I will attach a printed copy of this statement to our petition. We will request a hearing date before the end of the summer."

Bob reached up from his wheelchair and tugged gently on Emily's sleeve. As she looked down, he produced a low, rumbling, steady purr. It was the sound of absolute, grounded peace.

Emily responded by placing her hand over his heart, mirroring the gesture as he placed his hand over hers. Together, they released a deep, resonant, simultaneous hum that vibrated in the quiet office. I love you. Diane wiped a tear from her eye, and David placed a heavy, reassuring hand on his wife's shoulder. The legalities were just paperwork now. The reality had been settled months ago. They were a family, bound not just by blood, but by choice, by protection, and by the ironclad love of a father who had made sure his daughter would finally be safe.

### Part 3: The Bluewater Star Party

Late August brought a break in the stifling humidity, trading the heavy summer air for clear, crisp evenings. For Bob and Emily, the highlight of the summer wasn’t a vacation or a trip to the mall; it was a specific date circled multiple times on the kitchen calendar: the Bluewater Astronomical Society’s annual "Lights Out" Star Party at Centennial Park.

As a reward for their spectacular SOL scores and as a celebration of their final, decisive legal victory over Karen, Diane and David had surprised them with a shared gift. It wasn't a toy; it was a serious, intermediate-level reflecting telescope mounted on a sturdy, adjustable tripod.

At 8:00 PM, the family van pulled into the sprawling grass parking lot at the edge of the town. The park was already dotted with amateur astronomers setting up their equipment.

David opened the back of the van and began unloading the heavy base of the telescope. Bob wheeled himself down the ramp, his eyes wide as he took in the field of lenses and tripods. Emily, now a towering 5'10" and moving with the wiry, athletic grace born from a year of carrying her cousin, walked right beside him.

A flurry of high-pitched, melodic chirps—the Heart-Hum sound for pure, unadulterated joy—erupted from both of them simultaneously. They were entirely in their element.

"Alright, you two," David said, carrying the telescope tube to a flat patch of grass they had claimed. "Let's get this rig calibrated before it gets too dark."

Working together, communicating through low, steady, two-note hums that indicated they were in "work mode," Emily and Bob directed the setup. Bob used an astronomy app on his mPad to find their precise GPS coordinates, while Emily expertly adjusted the tripod legs and balanced the equatorial mount. Diane and David simply stood back, setting up a couple of folding canvas chairs for themselves, perfectly content to let the two teenagers run the show.

At 9:00 PM, a voice echoed over a portable PA system near the park entrance. "Welcome, everyone, to the Bluewater Star Party! In exactly one minute, the town has graciously agreed to shut down the park and perimeter streetlights. Please turn off all white flashlights and use only red-light filters from this point forward."

Bob and Emily looked at each other, their hands finding each other in the dimming twilight.

"Ten... nine... eight..." the crowd began to chant. Bob and Emily didn't use words, but their shared, anticipatory hum vibrated in perfect sync with the countdown.

"...three... two... one!"

Click. The tall, buzzing halogen lamps that lined the park pathways and the adjacent street instantly went dark. The sudden absence of light pollution was breathtaking. A collective gasp rose from the crowd as the night sky seemed to suddenly pop forward, revealing a staggering, glittering canopy of thousands of stars, with the faint, milky band of our galaxy stretching across the center.

Emily let out a soft, reverent coo. Bob squeezed her hand, his eyes shining as he looked upward. The universe was immense, but right here, anchored to each other, they didn't feel small.

They got to work. Bob tapped his mPad, finding the coordinates for Saturn, which was burning brightly in the southern sky. He pointed, and Emily carefully aimed the telescope, peering through the finder-scope to lock it in.

She stepped back and gestured for Bob to look. Bob wheeled his chair forward, but because Saturn was so high in the sky, the eyepiece was angled awkwardly upward. Even with the adjustable tripod, he couldn't quite reach it without straining his back or compromising the rigid posture his healing legs required.

He let out a soft, frustrated sigh, then looked up at Emily. He offered a soft, upward-lilting whimper while lifting his arms slightly. Carry me?

Emily’s face broke into a warm smile. She responded with a trusting, happy coo. Always.

With a practiced, effortless strength, Emily bent down and scooped her 15-year-old cousin into a secure piggy front carry. Bob wrapped his arms around her neck and his legs around her waist. Because she was now nearly a foot taller than him, lifting him brought him perfectly up to her eye level.

Emily stepped up to the telescope, acting as his human step-stool, holding him steady with absolute stillness. Bob leaned his eye against the lens.

He froze. There, floating in the black void of the eyepiece, was a perfect, golden sphere surrounded by crisp, distinct rings. It wasn't a picture in a textbook or a simulation in a planetarium dome. It was real, and it was right there.

A sharp, incredibly loud chirp of pure awe escaped his lips.

Emily smiled against his shoulder, holding him steady as he looked his fill. After a minute, Bob pulled back. He tapped her shoulder and made the specific, sharp thump on his own chest followed by a soft shhh—the name they had given Dr. Ragsdale—and then the soft, rising hum that popped at the end—Mr. Shaneyfelt. They made this possible, he was saying. They protected us, and they showed us the stars.

Emily nodded in profound agreement. She gently set Bob back down into his wheelchair. Then, she knelt beside him.

They didn't need their mPads for this next part. Simultaneously, they both reached out and placed a hand firmly over the other's heart. Looking directly into each other's eyes under the starlight, they created a deep, resonant, simultaneous hum. The vibration passed between them, a perfect, symmetrical exchange of the most sacred phrase in their language.

I love you.

Diane and David watched from their folding chairs a few yards away. Diane wiped a tear from her cheek, leaning her head on her husband's shoulder. They had survived a nightmare of a year, fought a legal war, and endured unimaginable medical trauma. But looking at the two cousins bathed in starlight, sharing a silent language of pure love and boundless curiosity, they knew every second of the fight had been worth it.

### Part 4: The Best News

The morning after the Bluewater Star Party, the living room of the Scott household was buzzing with a light, triumphant energy. The heavy, oppressive cloud of the custody battle had officially dissipated, replaced by the bright, clear reality of their permanent family structure.

Emily, sitting cross-legged on the floor next to Bob’s wheelchair, pulled her mPad onto her lap. She looked up at Diane and David, who were drinking their morning coffee on the sofa. She let out a soft, questioning coo, tilting her head toward the tablet.

"Absolutely," Diane smiled, understanding immediately. "Call her."

Emily tapped the InfoLink Messenger icon and initiated a video call to Madison. After two rings, the screen lit up. Madison appeared, her hair pulled up in a messy bun, looking brightly awake in the Brooks' basement in Christiansburg.

"Morning, Bluewater crew!" Madison beamed, waving at the screen. "How was the Star Party? Did the telescope work?"

Bob leaned into the frame and unleashed a flurry of high-pitched, melodic chirps—the Heart-Hum expression for pure, unadulterated joy. He then made the specific sound for Mr. Shaneyfelt—the rising hum that ended with a soft pop.

"I'll take that as a yes!" Madison laughed. "I'm so glad you guys got to see Saturn."

"The stars were beautiful, Madi," David said, leaning into the camera's view with a wide grin. "But that's actually not the best thing that happened this week. We have some news."

Madison’s expression immediately sharpened, her protective instincts flaring. "What is it? Did court happen? Did the judge..."

Diane nodded, her eyes shining with happy tears. "Judge Harrison signed the final order, honey. It's done. Your dad and I have full, permanent legal guardianship. Karen has been stripped of all medical, educational, and legal decision-making authority."

Madison let out a loud, joyous shriek that made the iPad speakers crackle. "YES! Oh my god, yes!"

Emily’s fingers flew across her keyboard case. She hit the 'Speak' button, and the synthesized voice filled the living room and echoed through the call.

"I am officially a Scott. She can never take me back."

"You were always a Scott in your heart, Em," Madison said, wiping a tear of relief from her own eye. "I am so incredibly happy for you."

"But wait," David interrupted, a distinct, deeply satisfied smirk spreading across his face. "It actually gets better."

Madison raised an eyebrow. "Better than permanent legal victory? How is that even possible?"

"Well," David chuckled, taking a sip of his coffee. "It seems Karen's 'sovereign citizen' phase finally caught up with her in the real world. A couple of days ago, she was pulled over by a county sheriff. Apparently, she had removed the legal license plates from her car and replaced them with homemade cardboard tags that said she was a 'private vessel traveling, not driving.'"

Madison’s jaw dropped. "You've got to be kidding me."

"I wish I was," Diane said, shaking her head. "When the deputy asked for her license and registration, she refused to provide them, rolled her window up, and started citing maritime law. It escalated. She was arrested for driving an unregistered vehicle, driving without a valid license, and resisting arrest."

"Where is she?" Madison asked, completely stunned.

"Sitting in the Carroll County lockup," David stated flatly. "Awaiting a hearing."

"She was given a bail amount," Diane continued, her voice losing its humor and taking on a tone of firm, unyielding boundary-setting. "She used her phone calls. She called me. She called our parents. She even tried calling Aunt Ellen and Uncle Tom up north. She begged for someone to come post her bond."

Madison held her breath. "And?"

"And every single one of us told her no," Diane said, her voice hard as steel. "We refused. All of us. We told her that her actions have consequences, and we are no longer shielding her from them. She is staying right where she is until a judge decides what to do with her."

A profound, heavy silence settled over the video call.

For Emily, the words were a physical release. Her entire life, her mother had been an unpredictable, terrifying force—a monster who could tear down her world at any moment. To hear that the monster was not only legally powerless but literally locked in a cage of her own making... it was the ultimate relief. The air in her lungs felt lighter.

She looked up at Bob. He met her gaze and let out his specific name for her—the sharp, clear chirp followed by a descending, steady hum. My protector. My person. Emily leaned her head against the armrest of his wheelchair, letting out a low, steady purr of absolute contentment.

"Wow," Madison finally breathed from the screen, shaking her head in amazement. "Talk about karma. You guys... it's really over. It's actually over."

"It's over, Madi," Diane confirmed with a warm, peaceful smile. "Now, we just get to live."

### Part 5: The Schedule and the Ride

As July melted into August, the lazy summer days gave way to the inevitable preparations for the Fall 2015 semester. One afternoon, Diane walked into the living room holding two thick envelopes stamped with the Bluewater High School crest.

"Alright, you two," Diane announced, sitting on the edge of the coffee table. "Schedules are here. Let's see what Mr. Henderson managed to put together for your sophomore year."

Emily immediately moved from her spot on the floor to sit beside Bob's wheelchair, leaning over his shoulder so they could look at the papers together. As Diane laid the schedules out side-by-side, Emily’s eyes quickly scanned the blocks of classes. Her brow furrowed, and a sharp, cutting grunt—the Heart-Hum sound for frustration—escaped her throat.

She reached for her mPad, her fingers striking the keyboard with rapid intensity.

We only share three classes. I asked Mr. Henderson to put us together for all of them. I want to be at my little man's side the entire day.

Diane sighed softly, offering a sympathetic smile. "I know you do, sweetie. And Mr. Henderson really tried. But high school scheduling is a giant puzzle. Because you tested into an advanced literature track and Bob needs a specific block for his physical therapy elective, it was impossible to line everything up perfectly."

Bob looked at Emily's distressed face. He understood her fear. The thought of navigating the crowded hallways without her physical presence was daunting, but he also knew they had to adapt. He gently nudged her arm and let out a low, mournful whimper. He hated it too, but he understood the reality of the situation.

"The good news," David said, stepping into the living room and pointing at the bottom of the pages, "is that Dr. Ragsdale made absolutely sure the non-negotiables were met. You both have 'One Lunch' scheduled at the exact same time, and you've been assigned the same shared locker in the B-Wing, right in the middle of your core classes."

Emily let out a slow, reluctant sigh. The thought of being separated for even an hour felt like a vulnerability, but the shared locker and lunch period were their anchors. She looked at Bob, who offered a soft, steady coo of reassurance. They were sad, but they understood. They gave David and Diane a synchronized nod of acceptance.

"Okay, there's one last logistical hurdle we need to cover," Diane said, pulling a yellow form from the envelope. "Because of the permanent changes to Bob's 504 plan and IEP regarding his mobility, the county has officially authorized door-to-door transportation. They want to know if Bob will be riding the Carroll County Public Schools special needs bus. It has a mechanical lift, and it would pick you up right at the end of the driveway."

Bob’s reaction was instantaneous. He stiffened, shaking his head firmly, and let out a short, breathy puff of air—a definitive, non-negotiable No.

He knew exactly what the yellow form meant. The county's rules were strict. The special needs bus was exclusively for students requiring those specific accommodations. Emily would not be allowed to ride it with him. She would be forced to take the regular yellow school bus with the general population, separating them for the entire commute.

He grabbed his own mPad and typed furiously, turning the screen around for his parents to see.

I am not taking the yellow bus. They won't let Em on it. Emily and I are taking the Bluewater Transit bus. The Blue Line.

Emily immediately backed him up. She placed a hand firmly on his shoulder and produced a low, rumbling hum of absolute agreement.

"Are you sure, Bob?" David asked gently, though he already knew the answer.

Yes, Bob typed, his expression fiercely independent. Most of the kids in town ride the city bus anyway. It drops off at the Transit Center right across the street from the lobby. All we have to do is flash our student IDs to the driver and it's free. We ride together. We are not splitting up.

Diane and David exchanged a look of profound pride. Their son and niece had endured a nightmare that had permanently altered their lives, yet here they were, rejecting the county's isolating accommodations in favor of navigating the real world side-by-side.

"The Blue Line it is," Diane said, taking the yellow form and drawing a large 'X' through the county transportation request. "I'll let the school know you'll be handling your own commute like the rest of the town kids."

Bob and Emily shared a look of quiet triumph. They might not share every single class, and they might face new physical challenges, but they were entering their sophomore year exactly the way they wanted to: together, on the city bus, entirely on their own terms.

### Part 6: The Backyard Feast

The final weeks of August brought a subtle shift to the air in Bluewater. The oppressive, mid-summer humidity began to break, leaving behind warm, golden evenings that practically begged to be spent outside. To celebrate the end of a victorious summer and the impending start of their sophomore year, David declared it was time for a proper, all-out backyard cookout.

The Scott family’s backyard was a private, fenced-in haven. Strings of warm yellow bistro lights hung from the branches of a large oak tree, casting a soft glow over the wooden deck. The rhythmic, hypnotic sound of cicadas provided the background music, underscored by the satisfying sizzle of charcoal and marinated chicken from the grill.

Inside the kitchen, Diane was putting the finishing touches on a massive bowl of potato salad. Emily walked in, the screen door sliding shut behind her. At 5'10", she easily reached the high cabinets to pull down a stack of sturdy melamine plates.

"Can you two handle the transport?" Diane asked with a smile, handing Emily the heavy glass bowl of salad.

Emily let out a soft, affirmative coo. She walked over to the threshold of the back door where Bob was waiting in his wheelchair. With a practiced, seamless coordination that required no spoken words, Emily carefully placed the heavy bowl squarely onto Bob’s lap. He immediately wrapped his arms around the cool glass, securing it against his chest.

Emily stepped behind his chair, grasped the handles, and smoothly navigated him over the threshold and out onto the deck. They were the perfect delivery system—Bob as the secure cargo hold, Emily as the engine.

"Perfect timing, delivery crew," David called out, flipping a row of burgers with a pair of silver tongs. "The grill master is almost ready to plate."

They maneuvered to the large patio table. Emily gently lifted the bowl from Bob's lap, setting it next to a platter of grilled corn on the cob and a pitcher of ice-cold lemonade. Bob wheeled himself into his designated spot at the table, while Emily took the chair immediately to his right, her knee comfortably brushing against the wheel of his chair.

When Diane brought out the last of the side dishes, they all gathered around the table. The food was simple, classic summer fare, but it tasted like a feast.

As they ate, the conversation flowed easily. David shared a funny story about a mix-up at his office, and Diane talked about the final school supplies they needed to pick up over the weekend. Between bites of his burger, Bob would occasionally tap the table to get their attention, letting out a series of bright, melodic chirps—the Heart-Hum equivalent of laughter—whenever David delivered a punchline. Emily would echo him with a happy, rumbling hum, her eyes crinkling with genuine joy.

As the sun dipped below the tree line, painting the sky in vibrant shades of bruised purple and fiery orange, a comfortable, contented silence fell over the table.

David leaned back in his chair, wiping his hands on a napkin. He looked around the table at his wife, his son, and the niece who had become his daughter. He thought about the chaos of the previous winter—the horrifying assault, the terrifying hospital stays, the screaming matches with Karen on the front lawn. It felt like a lifetime ago.

He raised his plastic cup of lemonade.

"I want to make a toast," David said, his voice dropping to a soft, reverent timbre.

Diane, Emily, and Bob raised their own cups, their eyes fixed on him.

"To a summer of victories," David said, looking right at Emily. "To winning the battles that mattered." He shifted his gaze to Bob. "To strength, in all its forms." Finally, he looked at his wife. "And to the four of us. The best team I could ever ask for. Here's to a great sophomore year."

Emily and Bob clinked their plastic cups against the adults'. Bob let out a long, low, steady purr of absolute peace. Emily placed her hand gently over his heart, mirroring the gesture as he placed his hand over hers. They shared a brief, resonant hum that vibrated through the warm evening air.

I love you.

It was a simple backyard cookout, with paper napkins and charcoal smoke, but as the stars began to slowly prick through the darkening sky, they knew there was nowhere else in the world they would rather be.

### Part 7: The Last Drop of Summer

The late afternoon sun of August 23rd, 2015, slanted through the upstairs window of the Scott household, casting long, golden geometric shapes across the interlocking foam mats of Bob and Emily’s bedroom. The air conditioning hummed a steady, quiet rhythm, holding the thick, late-summer Virginia humidity at bay.

Tomorrow, the alarm would ring, the Blue Line city bus would arrive at the corner, and their sophomore year at Bluewater High School would officially begin. But today was sacred. It was the final, uninterrupted drop of their summer freedom.

Bob was sitting on the soft foam floor, his back resting comfortably against the side of his bed. His upper-body strength had grown formidable over the summer, allowing him to navigate their matted sanctuary with an easy, sliding grace. Next to him sat Emily, her long legs crossed. Her massive growth spurt had pushed her to a towering 5'10", and the wiry, athletic muscle she had built from her daily routines was unmistakable.

Between them sat two empty canvas backpacks and a neat pile of school supplies.

Emily picked up a package of spiral notebooks and let out a soft, questioning coo. She gestured to her blue backpack, then to his black one, dividing the pile. Bob responded with a low, agreeable grunt, sliding his share of the notebooks and a handful of pens into the front pocket of his bag.

They worked in a comfortable, synchronized silence. As Emily tucked a brand-new dial combination lock into her bag—for the locker they would once again share in the B-Wing—Bob let out a sharp, exasperated click of his tongue. He pointed at the lock and shook his head, a clear expression of disdain for the frustrating hardware.

Emily let out a melodic, rumbling hum of laughter, bumping her shoulder playfully against his. We'll figure it out, her hum said. Just like last year.

Once the backpacks were packed and zipped, the finality of the summer settled over them. There were no looming court dates with Karen. There were no impending hospital visits. For the first time in a very long time, their horizon was completely clear.

Bob reached for his mPad, tapping the screen to wake it up, but Emily gently placed her hand over his. She shook her head, offering a soft, upward-lilting whimper while slightly lifting her arms. Carry you? Let's go outside.

Bob’s eyes lit up. He nodded eagerly, returning a warm, trusting coo, and stretched his arms upward.

Emily stood up, moving with a fluid, confident grace. She bent down and scooped her cousin into a secure piggy front carry. Bob wrapped his arms tightly around her neck and his legs around her waist. Because of her height, he was lifted completely off the ground, his face resting comfortably near her collarbone. The familiar weight of him against her chest was an instant, grounding anchor, settling any lingering nervous energy she had about the upcoming school year.

She carried him out of the room, down the staircase, and through the house to the back deck. Diane and David were already out there, enjoying the evening breeze. David was relaxing in a canvas chair with a paperback book, while Diane was watering a row of potted marigolds.

"Backpacks all set?" Diane asked, looking over her shoulder with a warm smile.

Emily nodded, giving Bob a gentle bounce to adjust her grip. She walked over to the patio table and carefully lowered him into his wheelchair, which David had left parked near the sliding glass door for exactly this purpose.

David set his book down on the table. "You two realize this is the last night you get to stay up late reading about orbital mechanics, right? Tomorrow, it's back to a reasonable bedtime."

Bob let out a series of bright, melodic chirps, a sound of pure joy and anticipation. He wasn't dreading the routine; he was looking forward to it. He was looking forward to Mr. Shaneyfelt's science class, to eating lunch with Emily in the cafeteria, and to riding the city bus together like everyone else.

Emily pulled up a chair right next to his wheel, leaning her elbows on the patio table. She looked at her cousin, noting the healthy color in his cheeks and the relaxed slope of his shoulders. She thought back to the shattered, terrified boy who had been pulled from a dark metal locker just months ago. That boy was gone. In his place sat a resilient, brilliant young man, ready to face the world.

She felt a profound wave of protective love. She reached out and placed her hand over his heart.

Bob immediately mirrored the gesture, placing his hand firmly over hers. They locked eyes as the evening sky above the tree line shifted from bright blue to a deep, bruised violet. Together, they released a deep, resonant, simultaneous hum.

I love you. I love you, too.

The vibration passed through their connected hands, a perfect, symmetrical exchange of strength and devotion. Tomorrow, they would re-enter the crowded hallways of Bluewater High. But tonight, they were just Bob and Emily, perfectly safe and entirely free, soaking in the very last drops of a victorious summer.

### Part 7: The Last Drop of Summer

The late afternoon sun of August 23rd, 2015, slanted through the upstairs window of the Scott household, casting long, golden geometric shapes across the interlocking foam mats of Bob and Emily’s bedroom. The air conditioning hummed a steady, quiet rhythm, holding the thick, late-summer Virginia humidity at bay.

Tomorrow, the alarm would ring, the Blue Line city bus would arrive at the corner, and their sophomore year at Bluewater High School would officially begin. But today was sacred. It was the final, uninterrupted drop of their summer freedom.

The centerpiece of their upgraded sanctuary was impossible to miss. Taking up a significant portion of the room was a brand-new, plush queen-sized bed. Emily’s massive, dramatic growth spurt over the last year had pushed her to a towering 5'11", and Bob had hit his own growth milestone, reaching 4'11". The full-sized bed they had shared during freshman year had simply become too cramped. But because they utterly and absolutely refused to be separated, Diane and David had replaced it with the sprawling queen mattress, ensuring the two teenagers had plenty of space to sleep side-by-side every single night.

Bob was sitting on the soft foam floor, leaning his back comfortably against the sturdy wooden frame of the new bed. His upper-body strength had grown formidable over the summer, allowing him to navigate their matted floor with an easy, sliding grace. Next to him sat Emily, her long legs crossed, the wiry, athletic muscle she had built from her daily carrying routines unmistakable.

Between them sat two empty canvas backpacks and a neat pile of school supplies.

Emily picked up a package of spiral notebooks and let out a soft, questioning coo. She gestured to her blue backpack, then to his black one, dividing the pile. Bob responded with a low, agreeable grunt, sliding his share of the notebooks and a handful of pens into the front pocket of his bag.

They worked in a comfortable, synchronized silence. Emily picked up a freshly printed copy of their convoluted bell schedule and held it up. Bob took one look at the confusing, staggered grid of their classes and let out a sharp, exasperated click of his tongue, shaking his head.

Emily let out a melodic, rumbling hum of laughter, bumping her shoulder playfully against his. We'll figure it out, her hum said. Just like last year.

Once the backpacks were packed and zipped, the finality of the summer settled over them. There were no looming court dates. There were no impending hospital visits. For the first time in a very long time, their horizon was completely clear.

Bob reached for his mPad, tapping the screen to wake it up, but Emily gently placed her hand over his. She shook her head, offering a soft, upward-lilting whimper while slightly lifting her arms. Carry you? Let's go outside.

Bob’s eyes lit up. He nodded eagerly, returning a warm, trusting coo, and stretched his arms upward.

Emily stood up, moving with a fluid, confident grace. She bent down and scooped her cousin into a secure piggy front carry. Bob wrapped his arms tightly around her neck and his legs around her waist. Because she was now exactly a foot taller than him, lifting him brought his head to rest securely and perfectly against her collarbone. The familiar weight of him against her chest was an instant, grounding anchor, settling any lingering nervous energy she had about the upcoming school year.

She carried him out of the room, down the staircase, and through the house to the back deck. Diane and David were already out there, enjoying the evening breeze. David was relaxing in a canvas chair with a paperback book, while Diane was watering a row of potted marigolds.

"Backpacks all set?" Diane asked, looking over her shoulder with a warm smile.

Emily nodded, giving Bob a gentle bounce to adjust her grip. She walked over to the patio table and carefully lowered him into his wheelchair, which David had left parked near the sliding glass door for exactly this purpose.

David set his book down on the table. "You two realize this is the last night you get to stay up late reading about orbital mechanics, right? Tomorrow, it's back to a reasonable bedtime."

Bob let out a series of bright, melodic chirps, a sound of pure joy and anticipation. He wasn't dreading the routine; he was looking forward to it. He was looking forward to Mr. Shaneyfelt's science class, to eating lunch with Emily in the cafeteria, and to riding the city bus together like everyone else.

Emily pulled up a chair right next to his wheel, leaning her elbows on the patio table. She looked at her cousin, noting the healthy color in his cheeks and the relaxed slope of his shoulders. She thought back to the shattered, terrified boy who had been pulled from a dark metal locker just months ago. That boy was gone. In his place sat a resilient, brilliant young man, ready to face the world.

She felt a profound wave of protective love. She reached out and placed her hand over his heart.

Bob immediately mirrored the gesture, placing his hand firmly over hers. They locked eyes as the evening sky above the tree line shifted from bright blue to a deep, bruised violet. Together, they released a deep, resonant, simultaneous hum.

I love you. I love you, too.

The vibration passed through their connected hands, a perfect, symmetrical exchange of strength and devotion. Tomorrow, they would re-enter the crowded hallways of Bluewater High. But tonight, they were just Bob and Emily, perfectly safe and entirely free, soaking in the very last drops of a victorious summer.
