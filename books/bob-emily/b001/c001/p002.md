The hospital room door swung open. Diane came out first, her face a mask of grief. Behind her was Emily, crying her eyes out, her body trembling. In her profound grief, she was carrying her cousin Bob in a piggy front carry, his body facing hers like a parent carrying a young child, his arms laced tightly around her upper back and shoulders. Bob was crying too, his quiet sobs muffled against her.

Karen was pacing the hallway, looking more annoyed than bereaved. "Oh, stop it, both of you," she snapped, seeing their tears. "You're overreacting. Can't you feel the frequencies in this place? They're broadcasting psychotronic grief waves to make everyone compliant."

"Grief waves?" Diane shrieked, her own grief turning to pure rage at her sister's detachment from reality. "He's GONE, Karen! YOUR HUSBAND JUST DIED! DIED!"

The directness of the statement didn't seem to register. Karen's mind, unable to process the simple, tragic truth, simply pivoted to find a different enemy. Her wild eyes landed on her daughter.

"This is YOUR fault!" she screamed, pointing a shaking finger at Emily. "You and your 'real research'! If you had just listened to me, if we had used the colloidal silver, he would be fine! You let them do this to him!"

The accusation, coming on top of the fresh, raw agony of watching her father die, was too much for Emily's already overloaded system. Her grief-stricken crying transformed into something else entirely—a high-pitched, panicked wail of pure terror. Her body went rigid. Her mind shut down. She was in the throes of a full, severe autistic meltdown, unable to form words, only sounds of distress.

"That's it, you're coming home with me right now," Karen demanded, reaching for Emily.

Diane moved, physically blocking her path. "NO!" Diane shouted. Bob, still being carried by Emily, let out a low, protective moan, a sound only his family would understand as a fierce, unyielding "No." He thought the words that he and his cousin couldn't say.

Diane only focused on Emily's needs. She could see her niece was struggling to stand while carrying Bob, but what Emily really needed was to sit down and hold him on her lap as her teddy bear, to process everything. "Emily, honey," Diane said, gently trying to help get Bob unhooked from the carry.

Emily, lost in her meltdown, instinctively swatted her aunt's hand away with a sharp cry of protest. No one understood yet that Bob was not a burden she was carrying; he was the anchor holding her to the ground.

"Karen, please," Diane begged, tears streaming down her face. "Just let her come with us. A cooling-off period."

But Karen wasn't listening. She was now screaming directly into Emily's vacant face, ranting about government plots. The commotion finally drew the attention of the nurse's station, and a call was made to security. Two calm but firm security guards arrived moments later.

"Ma'am, you need to come with us."

"You can't silence me!" Karen raved at them. "This is a false flag! You're crisis actors, here to cover up his murder!"

As the guards escorted a still-ranting Karen down the hall, Diane gently guided the unresponsive Emily, who was still clinging to Bob, to a chair in a quiet corner of the waiting area. She helped her get situated so that Bob was now sitting on her lap. Finally, blessedly, the screaming was over. Emily continued to hold Bob protectively, her face buried in his hair, like a scared child holding their favorite teddy bear as tightly as they could. In the quiet, the long, slow process of coming back to herself could finally begin.
