# Part 4: The Pact in the Back Seat

Diane gently guided the two children out of the hospital and toward the parking garage. Emily, all of fifteen years old and 5'3", still carried her fourteen-year-old, 4'8" cousin in a piggy front carry, his arms laced tightly around her neck.

When they reached the car, Diane unlocked the doors and opened the rear door on the passenger side. Bob lifted his head. He looked at the empty back seat, then back up at Emily's tear-streaked face, and let out a soft, questioning chirp. Emily looked from Bob to the back seat, and a small, plaintive whimper escaped her lips.

Diane's heart ached. She understood their silent language perfectly. The thought of being separated, even by the small space of a car's front and back seat, was unbearable for them. "Of course you two can sit together," she said softly. "That's a great idea. Bob, why don't you hop in first, and then Emily can slide in right next to you."

With her aunt's help, they got settled, Bob immediately curling up against Emily's side, and the long, 90-minute drive down U.S. 221 from Roanoke to Bluewater began.

Throughout the ride, the cousins alternated between quiet, shuddering sobs for the father and uncle they had just lost, and a low, steady chatter in their own private language. It was a complex series of moans, grunts, chirps, and coos—a language they both understood perfectly, and that Diane mostly understood, like someone listening to a familiar foreign language.

In that non-verbal conversation, they were making a pact. First, a series of soft, plaintive moans from Emily, met with reassuring coos from Bob, confirmed they would stick together, no matter what. That meant sleeping in the same room.

"You two can share Madison's room while she's at college," Diane offered from the front seat, interpreting their exchange. "It's all ready for you."

Emily grunted her assent. But then Bob let out a frustrated, questioning grunt. He gestured first towards the floor of the car, then pointed upstairs, and finally made a motion like turning on a shower.

Diane glanced at them in the rearview mirror. "Are you worried about the bathroom, Bob? You're right, the one upstairs is much bigger. Madison's room only has the little powder room downstairs."

Bob chirped affirmatively. He then looked at Emily and made a sound that clearly meant *my room*.

"You want her to stay in your room with you," Diane verbalized, a soft, understanding smile on her face despite her own grief. "Because it's closer to the good bathroom. That's... a very smart and kind idea, Bob."

In the back seat, Emily, protectively holding Bob tightly against her, let out a series of soft chirps and coos, her own non-verbal way of thanking him for his brilliant, compassionate solution. They had a plan. They would be together.
