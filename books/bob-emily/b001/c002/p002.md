After a short wait, the registrar called them back, and Diane, David, Emily, and Bob made their way to an office. They were greeted by a woman with a warm, energetic smile who stood up from behind her desk.

"Hello," she said cheerfully. "You must be the Scotts. I'm Dr. Rachel Ragsdale, the principal. It's a pleasure to meet you all."

"Thank you for seeing us on such short notice, Dr. Ragsdale," Diane said, shaking her hand. "I'm Diane Scott, and this is my husband, David. We're Emily's aunt and uncle, and as of yesterday, the authorized decision-makers on her school profile."

"I saw the update this morning," Dr. Ragsdale said, her expression full of a genuine empathy. "I am so deeply sorry for your family's loss." She then looked directly at the two cousins. "And I am very glad to welcome you both to Bluewater High."

As they all sat down, the gravity of the moment settled differently on the two cousins. For Bob, meetings like this were routine; he had been attending them with his parents his whole life to plan his accommodations. But for Emily, this was the first time anyone from a school had ever asked what she needed. The thought of it, combined with the raw grief and the knowledge that she was starting over as a freshman when she should be a sophomore, was overwhelming.

She let out a low, frustrated moan, a sound of pure discontent in the Heart-Hum.

Diane smiled apologetically at the principal. "I'm sorry, she's... she's very upset about her class placement. She knows she's a year behind where she should be because of her previous school situation."

Bob, sensing her distress, leaned over and gave her hand a reassuring squeeze. He let out a soft, playful coo, bumping her gently with his shoulder. Diane translated his comfort for the adults.

"And he's reminding her that this way, she gets to be with her favorite little man all day." A small, watery smile touched Emily's lips. She was the only person in the world allowed to call him her "little man."

Their private moment passed, and their focus shifted to logistics. Their hands found each other as they began a new, anxious conversation in the Heart-Hum. "Right now," Diane interpreted, "they're talking about lockers. They know freshmen usually have to share, and they're asking if it would be possible for them to share one together."

Dr. Ragsdale, already typing, nodded without a hint of surprise. "Of course. The same-gender locker policy is a guideline, not a law, especially when it facilitates a student's comfort and security. Consider it done."

Bob and Emily looked at each other and let out a simultaneous happy, melodic chirp. Dr. Ragsdale noticed their hands were still tightly clasped.

"I have another idea," the principal said, looking directly at them. "Would the two of you also like to share the same lunch period?"

Their enthusiastic, chirping approval was all the answer she needed.

"Excellent," she said. "Now, let's talk support. I want to start the process for Emily's IEP and 504 plan immediately. We'll also assign a single aide to both of you."

Just then, a knock came at the door, and an older man entered. "And this is the man who will help us put all this in motion," Dr. Ragsdale said. "Everyone, this is Mr. Henderson, your new guidance counselor."

After thanking the principal profusely, Diane, David, Bob, and Emily followed Mr. Henderson out of the office. They left feeling, for the first time, that they had found a place that wouldn't just tolerate their children's needs, but would actively move heaven and earth to meet them.
