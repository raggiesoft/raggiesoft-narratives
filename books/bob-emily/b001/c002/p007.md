# Part 7: A Failure to Communicate

The system that Dr. Ragsdale and Mr. Henderson had so carefully constructed worked beautifully. For weeks, Bob and Emily’s school life settled into a comfortable routine. Their teachers all knew their accommodations, and their classmates quickly grew accustomed to the sight of them typing out answers on their mPads or mPhones.

The system was perfect, until it wasn't.

One Tuesday, Bob’s regular Language Arts teacher was out sick. In his place was a substitute, a stern-faced man who traveled from school to school and had a rigid, old-school approach to classroom management. The school had dutifully emailed him Bob's accommodation requirements, but the substitute hadn’t bothered to read his email that morning.

He was quizzing the class on a reading assignment. "What was the primary motivation of the protagonist in chapter three?" he asked, his eyes scanning the room. He pointed a finger directly at Bob. "You. Mr. Scott."

Bob wasn't fazed. He knew the answer. He confidently pulled his mPad forward, his fingers immediately flying across the keyboard case as he composed his response for the text-to-speech program. His classmates waited patiently; they were used to how Bob operated.

The substitute, however, was not. His face tightened. "Young man, I asked you a question," he said, his voice sharp with impatience. "Are you ignoring me to type on your machine?"

A couple of boys sitting near Bob immediately spoke up. "Sir, he can't talk," one of them, a lanky kid named Kevin, explained. "He's typing the answer for you."

"Yeah," another added. "He uses the mPad to speak."

The substitute’s expression soured. He clearly didn’t like being corrected by students. "I don't care what the process is. When a teacher asks a question, a student answers. I expect a response, not... this."

Bob’s fingers froze over the keyboard. His head snapped up, his eyes flashing with indignation. He let out a low, guttural, angry groan—the sound in the Heart-Hum for "give me a moment, I’m answering you!"

The substitute's face turned red. "Do not talk back to me!" he snapped. "I will not be disrespected in this classroom!"

Just as the tense standoff reached its peak, the loud, jarring bell rang, signaling the end of the period.

A wave of relief and chaotic energy filled the room as students began packing their bags. Bob, his face a mask of cold fury and humiliation, threw his books into his backpack with sharp, angry movements.

"Mr. Scott, you will stay right where you are," the substitute commanded.

Bob didn't even look at him. He slung his backpack over his shoulder and walked straight out of the classroom, refusing to acknowledge the man who had so thoroughly disrespected him.

In the crowded hallway, Kevin and the other boy caught up to him. "Hey, man," Kevin said, "that was totally messed up. We've got your back. If he tries to write you up, we'll all go to Dr. Ragsdale and tell her what really happened."

Bob looked at his classmates and gave a single, sharp, grateful nod. He was angry, but he wasn't alone. He went looking for Emily, because he needed to be carried. He need the grounding feeling of her embrace. They found each other not too long after, and she saw the look in her cousin’s eye.

Emily, now a solid 5'6", was carrying Bob toward the cafeteria for their shared lunch period. The daily task of carrying her cousin was making her remarkably strong, and she moved through the crowded hallways with an easy confidence.

This time, Caleb was more forceful. He didn't just step near them; he planted himself directly in their path, forcing Emily to a halt.

"Hey, Emily. C'mon, just give me a chance," he said, his tone insistent. Then, his eyes flickered to Bob with a dismissive sneer. "Don't you ever put him down? Or is he permanently attached to you?"

Before Emily could even react, Bob returned fire. From his perch in Emily's arms, he made a sharp, clicking sound with his tongue followed by a puff of air—the Heart-Hum equivalent of a dismissive "Tsk!" or calling someone an insignificant pest.

Emily then straightened to her full height. It was in that moment, as she looked down at the boy who was now two inches shorter than her, that Caleb realized for the first time that she wasn't at eye-level anymore. She was taller. A flicker of genuine intimidation, unwelcome and unfamiliar, shot through him. Her response was instant and sharp: a cutting grunt, like a bark, followed by a pointed pushing-away gesture with her hand.

What truly took Caleb by surprise was the second volley from her passenger. Following Emily's clear dismissal, Bob mirrored her anger perfectly. He let out a low, guttural growl of his own and fixed Caleb with a hard, unwavering stare, shaking his head once, sharply. It was no longer a dismissal; it was a clear warning.

The triple-barreled, silent rejection was completely unexpected. Caleb took an involuntary step back, his bravado faltering. A few students nearby who witnessed the exchange snickered. Being rejected was one thing; being insulted and then warned off by both a girl who was taller than you and the cousin she was carrying was a whole new level of public dismissal.

Flustered, Caleb just muttered, "Whatever," and scurried away into the crowd. Emily and Bob shared a look of perfect, annoyed solidarity and continued on their way to lunch.
