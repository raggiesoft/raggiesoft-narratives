# Book: Life Without a Father

## Chapter 1: The Longest Day

### Part 1: The Final Promise

The hospital room was cold and sterile, the only sound the soft, rhythmic beep of the heart monitor. Emily Jenkins, all of fifteen years old, sat in a chair pulled up close to her father's bed. On her lap sat her 14-year-old cousin, Bob, and she held him tightly, her arms wrapped around him like a shield. She was already crying, her tears silent and steady, because she knew, with a certainty that settled deep in her bones, that her father's time was up.

Her mother, Karen, had been asked to wait in the hallway by her aunt Diane after she'd begun ranting at a nurse about how the hospital's hand sanitizer was a government mind-control agent. Now, it was just Emily, Bob, and the two people her father trusted most in the world: his beloved sister-in-law, Diane, and her husband, David Scott. They stood together on the other side of the bed, their presence a quiet, supportive vigil.

What no one in the room knew, except Richard, was that this was not just a tragic death; it was the premature end of an escape plan. He had already begun the quiet, secret process of looking into divorcing Karen, hoping to take Emily and build a new, safer life with the Scotts. Her refusal to learn the Heart-Hum had been the final straw.

Richard Jenkins's eyes fluttered open, finding them both. "Diane... David," he rasped, his voice a ghost of what it had been. "Promise me... look after my girl. Get her away from... from all the toxicity. She deserves a normal life."

Tears streamed down Diane's face as she and David both reached out to take his hand. "We promise, Richard," Diane whispered, her husband nodding firmly beside her, his own eyes full of tears. "We'll take care of your little girl. We promise."

### Part 2: The Hallway War

The hospital room door swung open. Diane came out first, her face a mask of grief. Behind her was Emily, crying her eyes out, her body trembling. In her profound grief, she was carrying her cousin Bob in a piggy front carry, his body facing hers like a parent carrying a young child, his arms laced tightly around her upper back and shoulders. Bob was crying too, his quiet sobs muffled against her.

Karen was pacing the hallway, looking more annoyed than bereaved. "Oh, stop it, both of you," she snapped, seeing their tears. "You're overreacting. Can't you feel the frequencies in this place? They're broadcasting psychotronic grief waves to make everyone compliant."

"Grief waves?" Diane shrieked, her own grief turning to pure rage at her sister's detachment from reality. "He's GONE, Karen! YOUR HUSBAND JUST DIED! DIED!"

The directness of the statement didn't seem to register. Karen's mind, unable to process the simple, tragic truth, simply pivoted to find a different enemy. Her wild eyes landed on her daughter.

"This is YOUR fault!" she screamed, pointing a shaking finger at Emily. "You and your 'real research'! If you had just listened to me, if we had used the colloidal silver, he would be fine! You let them do this to him!"

The accusation, coming on top of the fresh, raw agony of watching her father die, was too much for Emily's already overloaded system. Her grief-stricken crying transformed into something else entirely—a high-pitched, panicked wail of pure terror. Her body went rigid. Her mind shut down. She was in the throes of a full, severe autistic meltdown, unable to form words, only sounds of distress.

"That's it, you're coming home with me right now," Karen demanded, reaching for Emily.

Diane moved, physically blocking her path. "NO!" Diane shouted. Bob, still being carried by Emily, let out a low, protective moan, a sound only his family would understand as a fierce, unyielding "No." He thought the words that he and his cousin couldn't say.

Diane only focused on Emily's needs. She could see her niece was struggling to stand while carrying Bob, but what Emily really needed was to sit down and hold him on her lap as her teddy bear, to process everything. "Emily, honey," Diane said, gently trying to help get Bob unhooked from the carry.

Emily, lost in her meltdown, instinctively swatted her aunt's hand away with a sharp cry of protest. No one understood yet that Bob was not a burden she was carrying; he was the anchor holding her to the ground.

"Karen, please," Diane begged, tears streaming down her face. "Just let her come with us. A cooling-off period."

But Karen wasn't listening. She was now screaming directly into Emily's vacant face, ranting about government plots. The commotion finally drew the attention of the nurse's station, and a call was made to security. Two calm but firm security guards arrived moments later.

"Ma'am, you need to come with us."

"You can't silence me!" Karen raved at them. "This is a false flag! You're crisis actors, here to cover up his murder!"

As the guards escorted a still-ranting Karen down the hall, Diane gently guided the unresponsive Emily, who was still clinging to Bob, to a chair in a quiet corner of the waiting area. She helped her get situated so that Bob was now sitting on her lap. Finally, blessedly, the screaming was over. Emily continued to hold Bob protectively, her face buried in his hair, like a scared child holding their favorite teddy bear as tightly as they could. In the quiet, the long, slow process of coming back to herself could finally begin.

### Part 3: The Choice

In the quiet corner of the hospital waiting area, Diane knelt before the two children, her own heart aching. She was grieving the loss of her brother-in-law, but her own sorrow would have to wait. Emily and Bob needed her to be the strong one. *I will honor your request, Richard,* she thought, making a silent vow. *I will take care of your little girl.*

She gently rubbed Emily's back, speaking in a low, soothing voice. "It's okay now. She's gone. You're safe."

Emily was still holding Bob tightly on her lap, her face buried in his hair like a scared child clinging to their favorite teddy bear. After a long while, her panicked crying began to subside into shuddering, exhausted sobs. She let out a small, shaky grunt. Bob, still in her arms, cooed softly in response. It was their own language, and Diane understood it to mean they were ready.

"Emily, sweetie," Diane began gently. "I need to know what you want. What do you want to do now?"

Emily shook her head, still unable to form words. A kind nurse from the nearby station, who had witnessed the earlier confrontation, approached with a small notepad and a pencil. "Maybe this will help," she offered.

Diane took it gratefully and handed it to Emily. With a trembling hand, Emily wrote two short, powerful sentences on the page and handed the pad back to her aunt.

Diane read the note. The first line was a gut punch: I never want to see my mother again. The second line was a declaration: I want Bob with me. No matter what.

Diane looked at the two of them, huddled together, and her resolve hardened. She understood completely. "Okay, sweetie," she said, her voice full of a promise. "Okay. You can come home with us. With me, and your uncle, and Bob." She brushed a stray tear from Emily's cheek. "You can stay in Madison's room while she's off at college. We'll figure it all out. Together."

A single, profound nod from Emily was all the answer she needed. Their new life was about to begin.

### Part 4: The Pact in the Back Seat

Diane gently guided the two children out of the hospital and toward the parking garage. Emily, all of fifteen years old and 5'3", still carried her fourteen-year-old, 4'8" cousin in a piggy front carry, his arms laced tightly around her neck.

When they reached the car, Diane unlocked the doors and opened the rear door on the passenger side. Bob lifted his head. He looked at the empty back seat, then back up at Emily's tear-streaked face, and let out a soft, questioning chirp. Emily looked from Bob to the back seat, and a small, plaintive whimper escaped her lips.

Diane's heart ached. She understood their silent language perfectly. The thought of being separated, even by the small space of a car's front and back seat, was unbearable for them. "Of course you two can sit together," she said softly. "That's a great idea. Bob, why don't you hop in first, and then Emily can slide in right next to you."

With her aunt's help, they got settled, Bob immediately curling up against Emily's side, and the long, 90-minute drive down U.S. 221 from Roanoke to Bluewater began.

Throughout the ride, the cousins alternated between quiet, shuddering sobs for the father and uncle they had just lost, and a low, steady chatter in their own private language. It was a complex series of moans, grunts, chirps, and coos—a language they both understood perfectly, and that Diane mostly understood, like someone listening to a familiar foreign language.

In that non-verbal conversation, they were making a pact. First, a series of soft, plaintive moans from Emily, met with reassuring coos from Bob, confirmed they would stick together, no matter what. That meant sleeping in the same room.

"You two can share Madison's room while she's at college," Diane offered from the front seat, interpreting their exchange. "It's all ready for you."

Emily grunted her assent. But then Bob let out a frustrated, questioning grunt. He gestured first towards the floor of the car, then pointed upstairs, and finally made a motion like turning on a shower.

Diane glanced at them in the rearview mirror. "Are you worried about the bathroom, Bob? You're right, the one upstairs is much bigger. Madison's room only has the little powder room downstairs."

Bob chirped affirmatively. He then looked at Emily and made a sound that clearly meant *my room*.

"You want her to stay in your room with you," Diane verbalized, a soft, understanding smile on her face despite her own grief. "Because it's closer to the good bathroom. That's... a very smart and kind idea, Bob."

In the back seat, Emily, protectively holding Bob tightly against her, let out a series of soft chirps and coos, her own non-verbal way of thanking him for his brilliant, compassionate solution. They had a plan. They would be together.

### Part 5: The Line is Drawn

As Diane pulled her car into the driveway, her heart sank. Her sister, Karen, was already there, pacing on the front lawn like a caged animal. Diane got out of the car, positioning herself between her sister and the back door where the two grieving children were huddled together.

"What have you done?" Karen shrieked, her eyes wild. "This is what happens! When you let them pump our bodies full of their vaccine poison! They shut down our natural systems! That's what happened to Richard, and it's why these two can't even speak!"

"You're trespassing, Karen," Diane stated, her voice quiet, cold, and utterly devoid of weakness. She took a deliberate step forward, forcing her sister back toward the sidewalk. "You're on my property. No, Emily does not want to speak to you right now. No, you may not speak to her. I don't care if you are her mother. You are my sister, Karen. What happened to you? Where did mom and dad go wrong? When did you go down the rabbit hole of conspiracy after conspiracy?"

She took another step. "Emily and Bob just made a pact, that they want to stay together right now. Your husband DIED, Karen! DIED! Emily's father DIED! Bob's uncle DIED! My brother-in-law DIED! And you don't feel the least bit sad?" She was at the edge of her property now. "I'm going to ask you to leave my property one more time, and if you don't, I will have the Bluewater Police Department make you leave."

Faced with Diane's unshakeable calm, Karen finally faltered. Without another word, her mind likely already spinning a new theory about her sister being a government agent, she turned and scurried away down the street.

Diane took a deep, shuddering breath to compose herself, then opened the back door of the car. Emily let out a soft chirp and looked at Bob. He cooed in response.

"You want to carry him inside, sweetie?" Diane interpreted softly. "Okay. Let's get you both in the house."

As Emily entered the house, still carrying Bob, her uncle David was waiting in the living room. He had heard Karen's screaming from outside and was filled with a quiet pride for his wife's strength. He saw the grief on their faces, and he just knew: Richard was gone.

Emily went straight to the large recliner in the corner of the room and sat down, pulling Bob fully onto her lap. The two adults understood; it was grounding for the two teenagers. Bob and Emily continued their chatter from the car, their grunts and coos a private language.

"They're going over the plan they made in the car," Diane whispered to her husband. "He's explaining why his room is better because of the bigger bathroom. She's saying she doesn't care about the small bed, as long as she's not alone. Not tonight. Not ever." She watched as Bob let out a series of reassuring coos. "And he's promising her he won't leave her side. Even at bedtime."

David looked at the two teenagers, a flicker of fatherly nervousness crossing his face at the thought of them sharing a room, a bed. But then he looked at his wife's face, at the two shattered children, and his nervousness was immediately replaced by a deep, compassionate understanding. He had known for years that Karen was bad news and had always been secretly ready for this day to come.

"You're right," he said to Diane, his voice full of quiet resolve. "They need each other. If they're going to be sharing a room, that little twin bed isn't going to work for long. We should look into getting them a bigger one."

### Part 6: A Shared Sanctuary

After David’s quiet promise of a bigger bed, Diane gently knelt in front of the two cousins still huddled in the recliner. "Okay, you two," she said softly. "Why don't you head upstairs and get Bob's room set up for the night? You can decide where you want to put Emily's things." It was a gentle way of giving the two children a task to focus on, and to give herself and David a moment alone to process the immense tragedy of the day and figure out how to handle the start of school the very next day.

Emily looked down at Bob, who was still nestled securely in her lap. She let out a soft, questioning chirp. May I carry you again?

Bob looked up at her, then toward the stairs, and responded with a happy, affirmative grunt. Yes, and I'll show you the way.

With a practiced ease, Emily stood up, adjusting her hold on Bob as he wrapped his arms and legs around her. She followed his quiet pointing up the stairs and into his bedroom. She gently sat down on his twin bed, perhaps a little harder than she intended, the emotional and physical exhaustion of the day hitting her all at once.

From her lap, Bob let out another soft chirp, looking at the space on the bed beside them. Let's lie down together. Emily responded with a low coo of agreement.

He chirped again, a more practical, questioning sound as he looked at the small bed. Are we going to fit? Emily grunted her approval of the idea to test it.

They shifted and rearranged themselves, finally lying side-by-side. They fit, but just barely. Did Emily grow half an inch since we left the hospital? Bob wondered. He wasn't imagining things; at fifteen years old, Emily's massive growth spurt was just beginning to kick into gear.

She instinctively curled into the big spoon position, holding Bob tightly against her. A new series of happy, contented chirps and coos passed between them as they celebrated the simple victory of fitting on the bed together. Then, their chatter changed, the tone shifting to more somber grunts and moans. They never wanted to see Karen again. And they were glad David was going to look for a bigger bed.

Once they knew they could share the bed, they began the quiet, purposeful task of organizing the room for two. Through a series of chirps, moans, and chattering, they decided where to place the few possessions Emily had. Bob insisted she take a drawer in his dresser for her own.

The last thing they placed was the possession that meant the most to them: a framed picture of the two of them with Richard, taken just a month ago. In the photo, Emily was carrying Bob, her face beaming with pride, and Richard was smiling at the camera, a look of pure, uncomplicated happiness for his daughter and his nephew. It was the last picture ever taken of him. They placed it front and center on their shared dresser.

Looking at her father's kind, normal, loving face, a sudden, powerful thought struck Emily. Vaccines. Her mother's rants. Her father's quiet, steady disagreement. Now that she was free, she could finally get the medical care he would have wanted for her. She needed to get caught up on everything a child was supposed to get.

She chirped at Bob, a new sense of urgency in the sound, wanting to carry him again, downstairs so she could ask for help. He stretched his hands out, agreeing instantly. They headed back down the stairs. Emily knew she needed an adult's help for this next step. And if getting her vaccinations meant her mother cut her off completely? So be it. She was happier here, with her new family, anyway.

### Part 7: A New Plan of Action

When Emily and Bob came back downstairs, Emily still carrying him, she chirped urgently at her aunt and gestured toward her own arm, mimicking a shot.

"You want to get your vaccinations?" Diane asked, understanding immediately. Emily nodded emphatically. "Of course, sweetie," Diane promised. "Absolutely. We will call the doctor first thing tomorrow." Emily chirped her appreciation, her relief palpable.

The three of them—Diane, Emily, and Bob—were emotionally numb, the day's events having finally drained them of all energy. Seeing this, David stood up. "You all need to eat something," he said gently. He returned a few minutes later with simple peanut butter and jelly sandwiches, chips, and apple juice.

After they ate, Emily stood up and once again gathered Bob into her arms. David watched them, his expression one of gentle curiosity. "Emily, honey," he began softly, "can I ask you something? I'm only asking so I can understand better. Why do you need to carry Bob?"

"It helps keep her calm, David," Diane explained, her voice quiet. "It's grounding for her. For Emily, carrying Bob right now is like a frightened child carrying their favorite stuffed animal for protection."

David nodded, his face full of complete understanding. He was fully on board with whatever helped these two special needs teenagers feel safe. The two cousins chirped their quiet thanks for his understanding, then cooed their goodnights to the two adults. It was still early, but they were mentally exhausted.

"Get some sleep, you two," Diane said. "We'll go to Bluewater High School in the morning and try to untangle the mess Karen made of your classes and the fact that you don't have an IEP or a 504 plan."

Emily groaned, the frustration of her situation bubbling up. Diane understood completely. "I know you're upset about being a freshman, sweetie."

Bob chirped from Emily's arms. *At least we can be together.* Emily's groan softened into a sound of agreement. At least they would be together.

She carried Bob into the upstairs bathroom. This was the one time she had to separate from him, and the brief moment of privacy as they each took their turns using the toilet and changing into pajamas brought a fresh wave of anxiety. She was immensely happy to be reunited with Bob once they were both done.

They went to Bob's bedroom and gently closed the door. Bob climbed into his twin bed while Emily turned off the light. She then joined him, immediately becoming the big spoon. As she held him, his suspicions from earlier were confirmed—Emily definitely felt taller than she had at the hospital. They chirped their goodnights to each other, finally finding a sliver of peace at the end of the longest day of their lives.

## Chapter 2: The New Normal

### Part 1: The First Day

The morning after the longest day of their lives was a quiet, somber affair. After a simple breakfast, Diane drove Emily and Bob to Bluewater High School. She walked them past the throngs of students and straight into the main office.

To her immense relief, Diane was pleasantly surprised to discover that she was still listed in the school's system as someone who could make decisions for Emily—a relic from years ago, before Karen's paranoia had completely severed their family ties. She immediately planned to take advantage of it, knowing she had to get the process rolling to set Emily up with the 504 plan and IEP that her mother had always refused her.

While Diane spoke with the school registrar, Bob and Emily sat together in the lobby chairs, holding hands. Their quiet, non-verbal chatter was a comfort in the bustling office. Through a series of soft coos, Emily expressed her immense happiness and relief that Bob was there at her side. He squeezed her hand and responded with a low, reassuring grunt, promising he would stay there, always.

Their chatter then shifted, their sounds becoming more practical as they watched other freshmen being assigned lockers. They both knew freshmen had to share.

Diane, finishing at the counter, turned and saw them. "Are you two talking about lockers?" she asked, already knowing the answer. They both looked at her and nodded. "You want to share one together, don't you?"

They both responded with enthusiastic, hopeful chirps.

"Okay," Diane said with a determined smile. "Let me see if I can talk them into it. No promises, but I'll try."

### Part 2: Meeting with the Principal

After a short wait, the registrar called them back, and Diane, David, Emily, and Bob made their way to an office. They were greeted by a woman with a warm, energetic smile who stood up from behind her desk.

"Hello," she said cheerfully. "You must be the Scotts. I'm Dr. Rachel Ragsdale, the principal. It's a pleasure to meet you all."

"Thank you for seeing us on such short notice, Dr. Ragsdale," Diane said, shaking her hand. "I'm Diane Scott, and this is my husband, David. We're Emily's aunt and uncle, and as of yesterday, the authorized decision-makers on her school profile."

"I saw the update this morning," Dr. Ragsdale said, her expression full of a genuine empathy. "I am so deeply sorry for your family's loss." She then looked directly at the two cousins. "And I am very glad to welcome you both to Bluewater High."

As they all sat down, the gravity of the moment settled differently on the two cousins. For Bob, meetings like this were routine; he had been attending them with his parents his whole life to plan his accommodations. But for Emily, this was the first time anyone from a school had ever asked what she needed. The thought of it, combined with the raw grief and the knowledge that she was starting over as a freshman when she should be a sophomore, was overwhelming.

She let out a low, frustrated moan, a sound of pure discontent in the Heart-Hum.

Diane smiled apologetically at the principal. "I'm sorry, she's... she's very upset about her class placement. She knows she's a year behind where she should be because of her previous school situation."

Bob, sensing her distress, leaned over and gave her hand a reassuring squeeze. He let out a soft, playful coo, bumping her gently with his shoulder. Diane translated his comfort for the adults.

"And he's reminding her that this way, she gets to be with her favorite little man all day." A small, watery smile touched Emily's lips. She was the only person in the world allowed to call him her "little man."

Their private moment passed, and their focus shifted to logistics. Their hands found each other as they began a new, anxious conversation in the Heart-Hum. "Right now," Diane interpreted, "they're talking about lockers. They know freshmen usually have to share, and they're asking if it would be possible for them to share one together."

Dr. Ragsdale, already typing, nodded without a hint of surprise. "Of course. The same-gender locker policy is a guideline, not a law, especially when it facilitates a student's comfort and security. Consider it done."

Bob and Emily looked at each other and let out a simultaneous happy, melodic chirp. Dr. Ragsdale noticed their hands were still tightly clasped.

"I have another idea," the principal said, looking directly at them. "Would the two of you also like to share the same lunch period?"

Their enthusiastic, chirping approval was all the answer she needed.

"Excellent," she said. "Now, let's talk support. I want to start the process for Emily's IEP and 504 plan immediately. We'll also assign a single aide to both of you."

Just then, a knock came at the door, and an older man entered. "And this is the man who will help us put all this in motion," Dr. Ragsdale said. "Everyone, this is Mr. Henderson, your new guidance counselor."

After thanking the principal profusely, Diane, David, Bob, and Emily followed Mr. Henderson out of the office. They left feeling, for the first time, that they had found a place that wouldn't just tolerate their children's needs, but would actively move heaven and earth to meet them.

### Part 3: Planning with the Counselor

Mr. Henderson led the family down the hall to his office, a space much cozier and more cluttered than the principal's. The walls were covered in college pennants, photos of graduating classes, and student artwork.

"Please, have a seat," he said with a kind, grandfatherly smile. "I know this is all happening very fast, with classes starting this very morning, but I promise you, we will get you both settled in." He acknowledged the immense weight of the situation. "What happened to your father," he said, looking at Emily, "was a tragedy. I am so sorry for your loss."

He turned to his computer, his tone becoming gentle but efficient. "Dr. Ragsdale has briefed me. Our top priority is building a supportive schedule for you both right now."

"They have very similar interests," Diane offered. "They both love anything to do with space, so we were hoping for an Earth Science class."

"Perfect," Mr. Henderson said, his fingers clicking on the keyboard. "Mr. Shaneyfelt's Earth Science class it is. It's the only section we offer for freshmen, so you two will be together for that one. Normally we try to give family members separate schedules where possible, but in this case, it can't be helped." He looked over his glasses at them. "And frankly, I think it will be a very good thing for you both."

He pulled up the course syllabus on his screen. "And it looks like you're in for a treat. I see Mr. Shaneyfelt has his annual field trip to the Planetarium at Galax Community College scheduled for the spring. That's always the highlight of the year for his students."

At the mention of the planetarium, Bob and Emily looked at each other, and a simultaneous, excited chirp escaped them—a shared sound of pure, nerdy joy. It was the one thing they were both looking forward to most.

Working together, they quickly built out the rest of their schedules, finding that their duplicate interests in subjects like coding and history made creating similar timetables easy.

Once the schedule was set, Mr. Henderson produced a thick packet of papers for Emily's IEP. "This just gets the process in motion," he explained to Diane and David. "We'll have a full team meeting with her teachers in a couple of weeks."

He then made a quick call on his desk phone. "Ms. Albright? Could you join us for a moment?"

A moment later, the kind-faced woman from Dr. Ragsdale's office stepped in.

"Bob, Emily," Mr. Henderson said with a smile. "This is Ms. Albright. She will be your dedicated aide for the year. She'll have a copy of your schedules and will be your go-to person for anything you need."

Ms. Albright knelt slightly to be closer to Bob's eye-level and then looked up kindly at Emily. "I'm very happy to be working with you both," she said softly.

They had a schedule. They had a plan. And now, they had an ally right there in the school with them. The day felt a little less daunting.

### Part 4: The First Class

As they left the guidance office, Mr. Henderson gave them a reassuring smile. "I'm sending a message ahead to Mr. Shaneyfelt now," he said. "I'll let him know to expect you and to have you seated as lab partners for second period."

Their new aide, Ms. Albright, led them from the office into the main hallway. "Alright, you two, your locker is in the C-Wing. Let's get your morning books put away before the bell rings."

She led them to a locker and handed them a slip of paper with the combination. Bob took it first, studying the numbers before turning the dial on the combination lock. His fingers fumbled slightly, and he let out a low, frustrated grunt. Emily took a turn, her expression equally annoyed. They both hated dial locks. Finally, with a soft click, the locker opened. They looked at each other and shared a sound of mutual, exasperated relief.

They quickly stored the books they wouldn't need and closed the metal door. "Okay," Ms. Albright said, checking the time. "Your Earth Science class is in the B-Wing. We should head over now."

Just as they started walking, a loud, jarring bell rang through the school, signaling the end of the first period. Doors flew open, and the hallways instantly flooded with a chaotic sea of students. Ms. Albright expertly acted as a gentle shield, guiding Bob and Emily through the busiest intersections and keeping a clear path for them.

They arrived at Mr. Shaneyfelt's classroom just as the last of his first-period Oceanography students were filing out. The teacher, a man with a kind face and an energetic air, was erasing the whiteboard.

"Mr. Shaneyfelt?" Ms. Albright said from the doorway. "These are your new students, Bob Scott and Emily Jenkins."

He turned, a welcoming smile on his face. He clearly hadn't had a chance to read the email from guidance yet. "Welcome! It's great to have you both. We've got an open lab table for you right over there."

Bob and Emily both quickly typed on their mPhones and held them up for him to see. The screens read: Nice to meet you.

"It's nice to meet you, too," he said warmly.

Ms. Albright helped them get settled at the lab table before giving them a final, reassuring nod. "I'll be right outside in the hall if you need anything."

Bob and Emily sat together, pulling out their larger mPads and pens, ready for class. Just as they got settled, the second bell rang, signaling the start of the period.

Mr. Shaneyfelt turned to the class. "Good morning, everyone, and welcome to Earth Science," he began, his voice full of genuine passion. "For our first day, I want to talk about what this class really is. It's the story of our world, told through four major scientific fields: Geology, Oceanography, Meteorology, and finally," he said, a twinkle in his eye, "my personal favorite... Astronomy, the study of our planet's place among the stars."

He continued, "And speaking of astronomy, I'm thrilled to be the first to tell you that this spring, we'll be taking our annual field trip to the brand-new Planetarium at Galax Community College. It just opened last month, and I'm told the new projection system is absolutely breathtaking. It's the highlight of the year."

At the mention of the planetarium, Bob and Emily looked at each other, and a simultaneous, loud, and joyous chirp escaped them both—a shared sound of pure, nerdy glee. Their eyes shone with an excitement that, for a moment, outshone all the grief and chaos of the past few days. They were in the right place.

### Part 5: Bus Orientation

On Wednesday, the second day of school, Bluewater High held its first "One Lunch" of the year. During the lunch period, in addition to the usual activities, students were invited across the street to the Bluewater Transit Center for the annual bus orientation.

Driven by curiosity, Bob and Emily finished their lunch quickly and joined the crowd of freshmen gathered around a parked, out-of-service 35-foot bus. Standing near the open doors were Brenda Joyce from Bluewater Transit and Dr. Ragsdale. Nearby, Dr. Ragsdale's daughter, Caroline, sat in her manual wheelchair, flanked by her cousins Daniel and Rowena Ragsdale, and Daniel's best friend, Opal Raymond, Jr., who had all come to offer support.

"Alright everyone," Brenda began cheerfully. "Welcome! We're here to show you how easy it is to use your transit system. The most important thing to know," she said, holding up a sample ID card, "is that you don't have to pay bus fare. You just show your valid county public school ID to the bus operator when you board, and that's your ticket to ride."

Bob and Emily, intrigued, used their mPhones to type out a question for Brenda about the schedule for their Blue Line route. As Brenda answered them, Dr. Ragsdale added with a warm smile, "And I can tell you from experience, they are very reliable. My daughter Caroline and I ride the bus to and from school every day."

Brenda then gestured to the bus ramp. "Now, for those of you who might use a wheelchair, or have family members who do, one of your fellow students, senior Caroline Ragsdale, has kindly volunteered to demonstrate the proper way to board."

Caroline wheeled herself to the bottom of the ramp. "It feels weird at first," she explained to the students watching, "but if you roll on backward, it makes it much easier to get into the securement space without having to do a three-point turn." Caleb Anderson was nowhere to be seen. The display of raw power had done its job: it had put a healthy fear of Emily Jenkins into him. It hadn't, however, made him get the hint. In his mind, her rejection had nothing to do with her unbreakable bond with Bob; he just needed to find another way to win his "prize." But for today, at least, the weight room was a peaceful, Caleb-free zone.
