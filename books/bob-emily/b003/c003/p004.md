The final click of the computer mouse echoed loudly in the quiet of Computer Lab B. On Emily’s screen, the secure testing portal of the Quantum OS 7 Enterprise machine flashed a stark, grey message: Assessment Submitted.

A moment later, Ms. Albright unlocked the drawer at the proctor's desk and walked over, handing them back their powered-down devices. The instant Emily’s mPad booted up, her fingers flew across the keyboard case. She wheeled around in her chair to face the aide.

"Did I pass?" the synthesized voice asked, the digital tone failing to mask the desperate urgency behind it. "What is my grade? Can you check the computer?"

For Emily, this wasn't just a test; it was a validation. After years of her mother’s paranoid, disjointed "homeschooling," she had spent the last five months fighting tooth and nail to prove she wasn't broken. She wanted the numerical proof right now.

Ms. Albright offered a gentle, sympathetic smile. "I'm so sorry, Emily. That's not how the state handles SOLs. The tests are encrypted and sent directly to the Virginia Department of Education in Richmond for scoring. We won't know the results for a few weeks."

Emily’s shoulders slumped. A low, vibrating moan of pure frustration escaped her throat. She had poured her heart and soul into that secure browser, and walking away without knowing if she had succeeded felt like leaving a race before crossing the finish line.

Bob, who had just powered on his own mPad, wheeled his chair forward until their armrests bumped. He reached out and tapped her knee, letting out a soft, melodic coo. You did your best. I know you passed.

Emily looked down at her cousin, the frustration slowly bleeding out of her posture. She understood the bureaucracy of it, even if she hated it. She let out a soft sigh, gave a small nod of acceptance to Ms. Albright, and stood up to move behind Bob's wheelchair to push him out into the hallway. The waiting game had begun, but she wouldn't let it ruin the day.
