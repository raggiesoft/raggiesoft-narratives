# Part 6: The Graduation Pact

Three days after the school year ended, Diane and David drove the cousins back to Bluewater High for one final meeting. The hallways were eerily quiet, stripped of their posters and echoing with the sound of floor buffers.

They sat in Mr. Henderson’s office to review their final transcripts and finalize their IEP and 504 plans for the fall.

"Well," Mr. Henderson said, beaming as he looked at the printouts on his desk. "The SOL scores came back yesterday. I am thrilled to report that you both passed Earth Science and Algebra I with flying colors. In fact, Emily, your Earth Science score was one of the highest in the freshman class."

Emily’s face lit up with a radiant, deeply satisfied smile. She had done it. She had proven her mother wrong.

"Which brings me to my next point," Mr. Henderson continued, leaning forward. "Emily, your final grades are spectacular. Given your chronological age—you'll be turning sixteen soon—and your demonstrated academic ability, we could talk about credit recovery over the summer. With a little extra work, we could easily accelerate you. You could skip your sophomore year and enter the fall as a Junior, right where you 'should' be for your age."

Diane and David looked at Emily, their expressions supportive, ready to back whatever choice she made.

Emily didn't even hesitate. The smile vanished from her face, replaced by a look of absolute, unyielding resolve. She grabbed her mPad, her fingers striking the keys with emphatic force. She turned the volume all the way up and pressed speak.

"No." The single, synthesized word rang out in the quiet office.

She typed again, faster this time, and held the screen up for Mr. Henderson to read.

I don't care about my chronological age. I don't care where the state thinks I 'should' be. Bob and I started this together. We survived this year together. I am going to be a Sophomore. I am staying in his class, and we are going to graduate together.

Bob, sitting beside her in his wheelchair, let out a soft, emotionally thick hum. He reached out and grabbed her hand, squeezing it tightly. She squeezed back, their fingers interlocking.

Mr. Henderson looked at the two of them, their hands clasped, their bond an unbreakable, physical force in the room. He slowly smiled and closed the file containing the credit recovery paperwork.

"A 'super sophomore' it is, then," he said warmly. "I will officially schedule you both for the 10th grade. Together."

A dual, harmonious chirp of pure joy filled the guidance office. The hardest year of their lives was officially over, and the future—a future they would face entirely side-by-side—was waiting.
