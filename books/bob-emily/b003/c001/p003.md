The first day home from the hospital was a lesson in harsh new realities. Bob, exhausted and in a haze of pain medication, was carefully settled onto the living room sofa, his injured leg propped up on a mountain of pillows. This central, ground-floor location was now his entire world.

As evening approached, Diane came in with a 5-gallon bucket of warm water and an armful of towels. "Okay, buddy," she said gently. "Time to get you cleaned up for the night."

Emily was immediately at her side, ready to help. She instinctively moved to help Bob sit up, her body already preparing to lift him.

"Whoa, honey, wait," David said, placing a firm but gentle hand on her shoulder. "Remember what the doctor said. No carrying him for any reason until we get the okay from the physical therapist. It's not safe for his leg yet."

A low, frustrated sound escaped Emily's throat. She hated this. She hated not being the one to provide the physical support he needed. Bob, hearing her distress, let out a soft whimper of his own. They both knew it was necessary, but it felt wrong.

The sponge bath was a clumsy, public, and deeply intimate affair in the middle of the living room. With no privacy, Diane and David worked as a gentle, clinical team. Emily's role was relegated to handing her aunt a fresh washcloth and making sure the clean pajamas she’d laid out on the sofa cushion were ready. She watched every movement, her frustration at being sidelined a palpable force in the room.

Once the sponge bath was over, Emily immediately propped Bob’s head back up in the pillow that she placed on her lap.

Later, as it grew dark outside, David came over. "Emily, honey," he said softly. "It's time for bed. He needs to be able to stretch out properly, and you need some real sleep, too."

A low, protesting moan rumbled in Emily's chest. She hated this. She hated not being able to stay right there, to be the one he woke up next to. It felt like begging for five more minutes of a game she knew was already over.

With a deep sigh of resignation, she carefully lifted Bob's head, replacing her own lap with a stack of pillows before sliding off the sofa. She looked at him sleeping, then turned and curled up on the other sofa, the one perpendicular to his.

It was her new post. From here, she could watch over him all night. The few feet of empty air and the corner of a coffee table that separated them felt like a vast, cruel canyon, but she would keep her vigil. The new normal, a reality of being together but apart, had begun.
