# Part 5: The Schedule and the Ride

As July melted into August, the lazy summer days gave way to the inevitable preparations for the Fall 2015 semester. One afternoon, Diane walked into the living room holding two thick envelopes stamped with the Bluewater High School crest.

"Alright, you two," Diane announced, sitting on the edge of the coffee table. "Schedules are here. Let's see what Mr. Henderson managed to put together for your sophomore year."

Emily immediately moved from her spot on the floor to sit beside Bob's wheelchair, leaning over his shoulder so they could look at the papers together. As Diane laid the schedules out side-by-side, Emily’s eyes quickly scanned the blocks of classes. Her brow furrowed, and a sharp, cutting grunt—the Heart-Hum sound for frustration—escaped her throat.

She reached for her mPad, her fingers striking the keyboard with rapid intensity.

We only share three classes. I asked Mr. Henderson to put us together for all of them. I want to be at my little man's side the entire day.

Diane sighed softly, offering a sympathetic smile. "I know you do, sweetie. And Mr. Henderson really tried. But high school scheduling is a giant puzzle. Because you tested into an advanced literature track and Bob needs a specific block for his physical therapy elective, it was impossible to line everything up perfectly."

Bob looked at Emily's distressed face. He understood her fear. The thought of navigating the crowded hallways without her physical presence was daunting, but he also knew they had to adapt. He gently nudged her arm and let out a low, mournful whimper. He hated it too, but he understood the reality of the situation.

"The good news," David said, stepping into the living room and pointing at the bottom of the pages, "is that Dr. Ragsdale made absolutely sure the non-negotiables were met. You both have 'One Lunch' scheduled at the exact same time, and you've been assigned the same shared locker in the B-Wing, right in the middle of your core classes."

Emily let out a slow, reluctant sigh. The thought of being separated for even an hour felt like a vulnerability, but the shared locker and lunch period were their anchors. She looked at Bob, who offered a soft, steady coo of reassurance. They were sad, but they understood. They gave David and Diane a synchronized nod of acceptance.

"Okay, there's one last logistical hurdle we need to cover," Diane said, pulling a yellow form from the envelope. "Because of the permanent changes to Bob's 504 plan and IEP regarding his mobility, the county has officially authorized door-to-door transportation. They want to know if Bob will be riding the Carroll County Public Schools special needs bus. It has a mechanical lift, and it would pick you up right at the end of the driveway."

Bob’s reaction was instantaneous. He stiffened, shaking his head firmly, and let out a short, breathy puff of air—a definitive, non-negotiable No.

He knew exactly what the yellow form meant. The county's rules were strict. The special needs bus was exclusively for students requiring those specific accommodations. Emily would not be allowed to ride it with him. She would be forced to take the regular yellow school bus with the general population, separating them for the entire commute.

He grabbed his own mPad and typed furiously, turning the screen around for his parents to see.

I am not taking the yellow bus. They won't let Em on it. Emily and I are taking the Bluewater Transit bus. The Blue Line.

Emily immediately backed him up. She placed a hand firmly on his shoulder and produced a low, rumbling hum of absolute agreement.

"Are you sure, Bob?" David asked gently, though he already knew the answer.

Yes, Bob typed, his expression fiercely independent. Most of the kids in town ride the city bus anyway. It drops off at the Transit Center right across the street from the lobby. All we have to do is flash our student IDs to the driver and it's free. We ride together. We are not splitting up.

Diane and David exchanged a look of profound pride. Their son and niece had endured a nightmare that had permanently altered their lives, yet here they were, rejecting the county's isolating accommodations in favor of navigating the real world side-by-side.

"The Blue Line it is," Diane said, taking the yellow form and drawing a large 'X' through the county transportation request. "I'll let the school know you'll be handling your own commute like the rest of the town kids."

Bob and Emily shared a look of quiet triumph. They might not share every single class, and they might face new physical challenges, but they were entering their sophomore year exactly the way they wanted to: together, on the city bus, entirely on their own terms.
