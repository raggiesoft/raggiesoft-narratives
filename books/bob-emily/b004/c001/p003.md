Late August brought a break in the stifling humidity, trading the heavy summer air for clear, crisp evenings. For Bob and Emily, the highlight of the summer wasn’t a vacation or a trip to the mall; it was a specific date circled multiple times on the kitchen calendar: the Bluewater Astronomical Society’s annual "Lights Out" Star Party at Centennial Park.

As a reward for their spectacular SOL scores and as a celebration of their final, decisive legal victory over Karen, Diane and David had surprised them with a shared gift. It wasn't a toy; it was a serious, intermediate-level reflecting telescope mounted on a sturdy, adjustable tripod.

At 8:00 PM, the family van pulled into the sprawling grass parking lot at the edge of the town. The park was already dotted with amateur astronomers setting up their equipment.

David opened the back of the van and began unloading the heavy base of the telescope. Bob wheeled himself down the ramp, his eyes wide as he took in the field of lenses and tripods. Emily, now a towering 5'10" and moving with the wiry, athletic grace born from a year of carrying her cousin, walked right beside him.

A flurry of high-pitched, melodic chirps—the Heart-Hum sound for pure, unadulterated joy—erupted from both of them simultaneously. They were entirely in their element.

"Alright, you two," David said, carrying the telescope tube to a flat patch of grass they had claimed. "Let's get this rig calibrated before it gets too dark."

Working together, communicating through low, steady, two-note hums that indicated they were in "work mode," Emily and Bob directed the setup. Bob used an astronomy app on his mPad to find their precise GPS coordinates, while Emily expertly adjusted the tripod legs and balanced the equatorial mount. Diane and David simply stood back, setting up a couple of folding canvas chairs for themselves, perfectly content to let the two teenagers run the show.

At 9:00 PM, a voice echoed over a portable PA system near the park entrance. "Welcome, everyone, to the Bluewater Star Party! In exactly one minute, the town has graciously agreed to shut down the park and perimeter streetlights. Please turn off all white flashlights and use only red-light filters from this point forward."

Bob and Emily looked at each other, their hands finding each other in the dimming twilight.

"Ten... nine... eight..." the crowd began to chant. Bob and Emily didn't use words, but their shared, anticipatory hum vibrated in perfect sync with the countdown.

"...three... two... one!"

Click. The tall, buzzing halogen lamps that lined the park pathways and the adjacent street instantly went dark. The sudden absence of light pollution was breathtaking. A collective gasp rose from the crowd as the night sky seemed to suddenly pop forward, revealing a staggering, glittering canopy of thousands of stars, with the faint, milky band of our galaxy stretching across the center.

Emily let out a soft, reverent coo. Bob squeezed her hand, his eyes shining as he looked upward. The universe was immense, but right here, anchored to each other, they didn't feel small.

They got to work. Bob tapped his mPad, finding the coordinates for Saturn, which was burning brightly in the southern sky. He pointed, and Emily carefully aimed the telescope, peering through the finder-scope to lock it in.

She stepped back and gestured for Bob to look. Bob wheeled his chair forward, but because Saturn was so high in the sky, the eyepiece was angled awkwardly upward. Even with the adjustable tripod, he couldn't quite reach it without straining his back or compromising the rigid posture his healing legs required.

He let out a soft, frustrated sigh, then looked up at Emily. He offered a soft, upward-lilting whimper while lifting his arms slightly. Carry me?

Emily’s face broke into a warm smile. She responded with a trusting, happy coo. Always.

With a practiced, effortless strength, Emily bent down and scooped her 15-year-old cousin into a secure piggy front carry. Bob wrapped his arms around her neck and his legs around her waist. Because she was now nearly a foot taller than him, lifting him brought him perfectly up to her eye level.

Emily stepped up to the telescope, acting as his human step-stool, holding him steady with absolute stillness. Bob leaned his eye against the lens.

He froze. There, floating in the black void of the eyepiece, was a perfect, golden sphere surrounded by crisp, distinct rings. It wasn't a picture in a textbook or a simulation in a planetarium dome. It was real, and it was right there.

A sharp, incredibly loud chirp of pure awe escaped his lips.

Emily smiled against his shoulder, holding him steady as he looked his fill. After a minute, Bob pulled back. He tapped her shoulder and made the specific, sharp thump on his own chest followed by a soft shhh—the name they had given Dr. Ragsdale—and then the soft, rising hum that popped at the end—Mr. Shaneyfelt. They made this possible, he was saying. They protected us, and they showed us the stars.

Emily nodded in profound agreement. She gently set Bob back down into his wheelchair. Then, she knelt beside him.

They didn't need their mPads for this next part. Simultaneously, they both reached out and placed a hand firmly over the other's heart. Looking directly into each other's eyes under the starlight, they created a deep, resonant, simultaneous hum. The vibration passed between them, a perfect, symmetrical exchange of the most sacred phrase in their language.

I love you.

Diane and David watched from their folding chairs a few yards away. Diane wiped a tear from her cheek, leaning her head on her husband's shoulder. They had survived a nightmare of a year, fought a legal war, and endured unimaginable medical trauma. But looking at the two cousins bathed in starlight, sharing a silent language of pure love and boundless curiosity, they knew every second of the fight had been worth it.
