# Part 6: The Backyard Feast

The final weeks of August brought a subtle shift to the air in Bluewater. The oppressive, mid-summer humidity began to break, leaving behind warm, golden evenings that practically begged to be spent outside. To celebrate the end of a victorious summer and the impending start of their sophomore year, David declared it was time for a proper, all-out backyard cookout.

The Scott family’s backyard was a private, fenced-in haven. Strings of warm yellow bistro lights hung from the branches of a large oak tree, casting a soft glow over the wooden deck. The rhythmic, hypnotic sound of cicadas provided the background music, underscored by the satisfying sizzle of charcoal and marinated chicken from the grill.

Inside the kitchen, Diane was putting the finishing touches on a massive bowl of potato salad. Emily walked in, the screen door sliding shut behind her. At 5'10", she easily reached the high cabinets to pull down a stack of sturdy melamine plates.

"Can you two handle the transport?" Diane asked with a smile, handing Emily the heavy glass bowl of salad.

Emily let out a soft, affirmative coo. She walked over to the threshold of the back door where Bob was waiting in his wheelchair. With a practiced, seamless coordination that required no spoken words, Emily carefully placed the heavy bowl squarely onto Bob’s lap. He immediately wrapped his arms around the cool glass, securing it against his chest.

Emily stepped behind his chair, grasped the handles, and smoothly navigated him over the threshold and out onto the deck. They were the perfect delivery system—Bob as the secure cargo hold, Emily as the engine.

"Perfect timing, delivery crew," David called out, flipping a row of burgers with a pair of silver tongs. "The grill master is almost ready to plate."

They maneuvered to the large patio table. Emily gently lifted the bowl from Bob's lap, setting it next to a platter of grilled corn on the cob and a pitcher of ice-cold lemonade. Bob wheeled himself into his designated spot at the table, while Emily took the chair immediately to his right, her knee comfortably brushing against the wheel of his chair.

When Diane brought out the last of the side dishes, they all gathered around the table. The food was simple, classic summer fare, but it tasted like a feast.

As they ate, the conversation flowed easily. David shared a funny story about a mix-up at his office, and Diane talked about the final school supplies they needed to pick up over the weekend. Between bites of his burger, Bob would occasionally tap the table to get their attention, letting out a series of bright, melodic chirps—the Heart-Hum equivalent of laughter—whenever David delivered a punchline. Emily would echo him with a happy, rumbling hum, her eyes crinkling with genuine joy.

As the sun dipped below the tree line, painting the sky in vibrant shades of bruised purple and fiery orange, a comfortable, contented silence fell over the table.

David leaned back in his chair, wiping his hands on a napkin. He looked around the table at his wife, his son, and the niece who had become his daughter. He thought about the chaos of the previous winter—the horrifying assault, the terrifying hospital stays, the screaming matches with Karen on the front lawn. It felt like a lifetime ago.

He raised his plastic cup of lemonade.

"I want to make a toast," David said, his voice dropping to a soft, reverent timbre.

Diane, Emily, and Bob raised their own cups, their eyes fixed on him.

"To a summer of victories," David said, looking right at Emily. "To winning the battles that mattered." He shifted his gaze to Bob. "To strength, in all its forms." Finally, he looked at his wife. "And to the four of us. The best team I could ever ask for. Here's to a great sophomore year."

Emily and Bob clinked their plastic cups against the adults'. Bob let out a long, low, steady purr of absolute peace. Emily placed her hand gently over his heart, mirroring the gesture as he placed his hand over hers. They shared a brief, resonant hum that vibrated through the warm evening air.

I love you.

It was a simple backyard cookout, with paper napkins and charcoal smoke, but as the stars began to slowly prick through the darkening sky, they knew there was nowhere else in the world they would rather be.
