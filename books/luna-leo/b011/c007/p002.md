# Part 2: A House Full of Joy

By December 27th, the house on Daniel Maloney Drive had been joyfully overrun for four full days. The presence of Olivia's Canadian family—her **Tante Isabelle** and **Oncle Mark**, and her boisterous teenage cousins **Luc, Chloé, Mason, and Sophie**—had transformed the quiet sanctuary into a bustling hub of laughter, conversation, and a happy mix of French and English.

The initial shyness had long since evaporated. After the grand success of the Réveillon and a wonderful, shared Christmas Day, a comfortable, chaotic routine had settled in.

On this particular afternoon, the living room was a perfect picture of the blended families. Stella and Tante Isabelle were on the sofa, deep in conversation over cups of coffee. Arthur and Oncle Mark were engaged in a quiet but intense chess match at the dining room table.

The younger generation had taken over the floor space. Luna and Chloé were huddled in a corner, looking at a fashion magazine, Luna pointing out designs she liked while Chloé chattered away in a mix of French and English. Olivia, Luc, and Mason were in the middle of a loud, competitive video game, their shouts and laughter filling the room.

Leo, positioned in his wheelchair near the center of the action, was taking it all in. The constant noise and activity would normally be overwhelming, but after several days, he had acclimatized to the happy chaos of Olivia's family. He felt a sense of calm, a quiet joy in being a part of this larger, loving circle. He had his new **Quantum Communicator** on his lap, and he would occasionally type a comment or a joke, his clear voice adding to the cheerful din. He was not just an observer; he was a participant, his new voice allowing him to fully engage with the wonderful, noisy, and loving "Canadian Invasion" that had so completely and wonderfully taken over his home.

The local evening news was playing quietly on the television in the background. Suddenly, a news anchor's voice cut through the noise with an update on a local court case. The word "trial" was all it took. For Leo, it was a trigger. The image of a courthouse, the thought of having to face Delores again, of having to tell his story to strangers—it sent a jolt of ice-cold fear through him. His fragile sense of peace shattered. He flinched, his hands instinctively dropping from his communicator to grip the arms of his wheelchair, his breathing suddenly shallow.

The shift was almost imperceptible to anyone else in the noisy room, but Olivia, who was sitting on the floor near him, sensed it instantly. Her head whipped around, her eyes immediately finding his. She saw the panic, the distant, terrified look that meant he was no longer in the room with them, but back in the horror of his ordeal.

Without a word, she abandoned the video game and moved to his side, kneeling so she was at his eye level. She gently placed her hand over his, her touch firm and grounding. "Hey," she whispered, her voice for him alone. "I'm right here, *mon chum*. You're safe. It's just noise on the TV. It's not now. It's not here. You're home."

Leo's focus slowly shifted from the television to her face. He leaned into her presence, her calm voice and steady touch a lifeline pulling him back from the edge of a panic attack.

From across the room, Tante Isabelle noticed the quiet, intense moment between them. She had been about to call out to Olivia to ask a question, but she stopped herself. She had learned over the past few days that when Leo looked like that, and Olivia went to him, they were to be left alone. She watched for a moment, seeing the tension slowly leave Leo's shoulders as he focused on her niece. She then gently caught her son Luc's eye as he was about to shout something about the video game and gave a slight, subtle shake of her head. Luc, understanding instantly, quieted down.

This was the biggest thing Olivia's extended family had come to understand and respect: Leo did not want to be separated from Olivia. Even in this glorious, chaotic happiness, his fears were still present, a quiet undercurrent to his joy. They had learned that Olivia was his anchor, and when he needed her, the rest of the world had to wait. They knew that to talk to Olivia, they had to come to her, and to approach gently, with respect for the silent, powerful work she was doing. It was a lesson in the language of the fortress, and they had all, to their credit, become fluent.
