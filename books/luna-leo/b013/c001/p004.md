# Part 4: The Tape

November 2005 1091 Daniel Maloney Drive - Living Room

The living room had been transformed into a makeshift recording studio. Stella had hung heavy blankets over the windows to dampen the street noise. Leo, maneuvering his manual wheelchair with precise, short strokes, adjusted the tripod of Stella’s high-end digital camera.

"Angle is good," Leo signaled, tapping his Quantum Communicator which sat on the coffee table. *"LIGHTING IS NEUTRAL."*

Luna stood in the center of the room. She wasn't wearing her marching band uniform or a t-shirt. She was dressed in concert black—a simple, elegant blouse and slacks. She held the Orpheus 680 at her side, the silver gleaming against the dark fabric.

"Okay," Stella said from behind the camera. "One take. Just play the music. Don't worry about the lens."

Olivia Walker Porter sat quietly on the sofa, holding a stopwatch. "Whenever you're ready, Lu."

Luna lifted the flute. She closed her eyes for a second, visualizing the opening notes of Debussy’s *Syrinx*. It wasn't just a song; it was a story about a god chasing a nymph who turns into reeds. It had to sound lonely. It had to sound haunting.

She took a breath—deep, diaphragmatic, invisible.

She played.

The opening descending chromatic line floated into the room. On a student flute, these notes often sounded breathy or thin. On the Orpheus 680, they were rich, dark, and velvety. The solid silver body vibrated under her fingers.

She moved through the piece. The middle section required immense breath control—long, languid phrases that looped and swirled without a pause for air. Luna didn't gasp. She didn't clip the notes. She used the strength she had built marching across football fields with a saxophone to fuel the delicate flute line.

Then came the ending. The piece descends into the bottom octave of the instrument, requiring the player to project the lowest notes with perfect intonation.

Luna reached the final phrase. She pressed the keys for the Low B—a note that didn't even exist on standard student flutes.

The B-Footjoint did its work. The note resonated with a deep, woody timbre, fading away into absolute silence like a ghost disappearing into the fog.

She held her position for three seconds, letting the silence hang.

Leo didn't clap. He looked at the camera’s LCD screen. He checked the audio levels.

"PERFECT," he typed. "NO CLIPPING. PURE SIGNAL."

"That was it," Olivia whispered. "That was the one."

Luna lowered the flute, letting out a long, shaky breath. "I didn't crash on the low B. It actually spoke."

"It didn't just speak," Stella said, stopping the recording. "It sang. Dr. Aris is going to lose his mind."
