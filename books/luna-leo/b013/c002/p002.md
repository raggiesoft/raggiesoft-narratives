# Part 2: The Hospitality Standard

## 04:50 PM The University Inn - Lower Level Entrance

The glass sliding doors hissed shut behind them, sealing out the hum of the station concourse. The transition was instant: the acoustic brightness of the terrazzo tunnel was replaced by the hushed, carpeted silence of the hotel’s lower lobby.

Leo, having won the drag race against the moving walkway, was waiting by the elevator bank, spinning slow, bored circles in his titanium manual chair.

Arthur and Eleanor caught up, stepping off the walkway with Stella. Arthur was moving slower now, the long day of travel catching up to him, but his eyes were sharp, taking in the polished brass fixtures and the BSU-branded artwork on the walls.

"Fancy," Arthur noted. "This is student housing?"

"It’s a teaching hotel," Stella corrected. "It’s where the Hospitality majors learn to be managers. Which means they are graded on service."

Leo pressed the elevator call button. It was mounted lower than usual—perfectly positioned for his hand height.

Ding.

The center doors opened. It wasn't a cramped service car; it was a freight-sized passenger elevator with mirrored walls and a rear door that would open onto the lobby side.

"Capacity: 3,500 pounds," Olivia read the plaque as they filed in. "We all fit."

They rode up one floor. The sensation was smooth, hydraulic, and expensive.

## The Lobby Level

The rear doors opened. They emerged into the Main Lobby.

It was impressive. A massive stone fireplace dominated the center of the room (a nod to the New England setting), surrounded by wingback chairs. The air smelled of woodsmoke and pine. Through the floor-to-ceiling windows, they could see the dark, churning Atlantic Ocean and the lights of the station plaza.

But Leo wasn't looking at the view. He was looking at the Front Desk.

Usually, hotel desks were high walls—fortresses designed to keep guests out. If you were in a wheelchair, you stared at the wood paneling while the clerk talked over your head to the standing person next to you.

Here, the entire desk was a sweeping curve of multi-level granite. A massive section in the center was dropped to 30 inches—wheelchair height.

Leo rolled straight to it.

A young woman in a sharp blazer stood behind the computer. Her nametag read: JESSICA - Junior / Hospitality Management.

"Good evening," Jessica said, smiling directly at Leo, not at Stella. "Checking in?"

Leo tapped his Quantum Communicator.

"PORTER PARTY. TWO ROOMS."

"Welcome, Mr. Porter," Jessica said, typing quickly. "We have you in the Chancellor Wing. It’s our Universal Design showcase."

Stella stepped up beside him, handing over her credit card. "We need the rooms close. As close as possible."

"I have you in 101 and 103," Jessica confirmed. "They are side-by-side. Room 101 is the Executive Double Suite—two queen beds and a sleeper sofa in the parlor. Room 103 is a King Accessible."

"Does 101 have a roll-in shower?" Stella asked. "We need it for the suite, too."

"Absolutely," Jessica nodded. "Zero-threshold showers, grab bars, and we pre-set the shower heads to the lower position. And..." She leaned over the counter slightly. "I activated the vibrating pillow alarms for the doorbells in case of emergency."

Arthur leaned on his cane, listening. He looked at Eleanor.

"Vibrating pillow alarms?" Arthur whispered. "I didn't even know that was a thing."

"It's thorough," Eleanor whispered back, looking pleased. "They thought of everything."

Jessica handed two key packets to Leo. He took them.

"THANK YOU," he typed. "I LIKE THE DESK HEIGHT."

Jessica beamed. "It’s standard BSU code. Eye level is respect level. Enjoy your stay."

## The Hallway

They moved down the wide corridor of the Chancellor Wing. The carpet was low-pile (easy rolling). The sconces were bright.

They reached Room 101. Leo tapped the key card against the sensor. The heavy door had an automatic opener button. He hit it with his elbow, and the door swung inward silently.

He rolled in.

The room was massive. It wasn't just a hotel room; it was a staging area. There was plenty of turning radius between the two queen beds. The bathroom door was a wide sliding barn door, not a hinged one that got in the way.

"This works," Stella said, dropping the bags. "We take this one. Grandpa, Grandma, you're right next door in 103."

Arthur stood in the doorway of 101, looking at the setup. He saw Leo spin a 360 in the middle of the room without hitting a single piece of furniture.

"I've stayed in five-star hotels that weren't this well thought out," Arthur observed. "You were right, Stella. This place... it wants you here."

"That's the vibe I'm getting," Stella agreed.

Leo rolled to the window. He pulled back the curtain. He could see the glowing white canopy of the train station and the dark ocean beyond. He was six hundred miles from home, but he didn't feel lost. He felt arrived.

He tapped his screen.

"UNPACK THE SUITCASE. I AM HUNGRY."

"Room service?" Olivia suggested, flopping onto one of the queen beds. "I saw 'Braxton Clam Chowder' on the menu in the elevator."

"Two bowls," Luna said. "And a side of fries."

"Make it six bowls," Arthur said, moving toward his own room to freshen up. "We'll eat in here. A family dinner in the new world."
