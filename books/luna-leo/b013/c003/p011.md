# Part 11: The Vessel

## December 31, 2005 Braxton City - The Arts District 10:00 AM

They didn't leave on the 30th. They stayed to handle the business of death with the same efficiency Arthur had taught them.

Stella found a local funeral home in the city—an old, respectful establishment that didn't try to upsell her on caskets or services. They handled the cremation quickly and with dignity.

But Stella wasn't going to put Arthur Bennett in a standard catalog urn. He wasn't a "Catalog" man. He was a "Custom Order" man.

She led the family to a studio in the **Braxton Arts District**, a reclaimed industrial block near the river. The sign on the door read: **BRAXTON STONE & CERAMIC**.

Inside, the air smelled of clay dust and wet stone. The artist, a BSU MFA graduate named Elias (no relation to the land seller), wiped his hands on a rag as they entered.

"I need a vessel," Stella told him. "Not a vase. A cornerstone."

She described Arthur. The car dealer who hated tricks. The patriarch who bought the land for the Fortress. The man who loved the ocean and solid foundations.

"He liked things heavy," Stella explained. "He liked things that didn't rattle."

Leo looked around the studio. He saw blocks of raw granite and marble. He tapped his **Quantum Communicator**.

"HE WAS LIKE THE MOUNTAIN," Leo typed. "HE NEEDS STONE."

"Granite," the artist nodded. "I have a block of Rhode Island Blue Granite. It's quarried locally. It’s extremely hard, very dense. It polishes to a deep grey-blue."

"Show me," Stella said.

The artist brought out the rough block. It was heavy, cold, and solid.

"Turn it," Stella commissioned. "Hand-turned. Polished smooth. And I want a band of **Polished Chrome** around the rim."

"Chrome?" the artist asked.

"He sold cars for thirty-five years," Stella smiled sadly. "He loved the chrome bumpers on the '78 Sentinel. It needs to shine."

**The Inscription:** Stella wrote it down on a notepad.

**ARTHUR JAMES BENNETT** 1926 – 2005 "There’s Only One Choice."

"Can you have it ready in three days?" Stella asked. "We're taking him home on the train."

The artist looked at the block of stone, then at the family. "I'll put it on the lathe today. It will be ready."

## January 4, 2006 The Studio

They returned to pick it up.

The urn sat on the workbench. It was magnificent. The granite was polished to a glass-like finish, the blue-grey flecks shimmering in the light. The band of chrome around the lid was mirror-bright. It looked less like a funerary object and more like a trophy or a piece of fine automotive engineering.

Eleanor stepped forward. She ran her hand over the cool stone.

"Oh, Arthur," she whispered. "You would have loved this. It's solid."

Stella picked it up. It was heavy—fifteen pounds of stone and ash. It felt substantial. It felt permanent.

"Thank you," Stella said to the artist, handing him a check.

She placed the urn into a velvet-lined carrying case.

"Okay, Grandpa," she whispered. "We're packed. We have the deed. We have the plan. Let's go catch the train."

Leo watched Stella secure the case. He knew what was inside. But looking at the polished stone, he didn't feel fear. He felt respect.

"HE IS READY TO TRAVEL," Leo typed.

"First Class," Olivia agreed.

They walked out of the studio, carrying their patriarch into the winter sun, ready for the final ride south.
