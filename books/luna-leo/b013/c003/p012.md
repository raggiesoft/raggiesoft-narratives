# Part 12: The Final Passenger

## January 5, 2006 Braxton Central Terminal - Level 1 (The Deep) 08:00 AM

The Fortress stood on the platform, waiting for **ARTS Coastal Service 95**—the southbound train to Newport News.

They looked different than they had when they arrived a week ago. They were tired, grieving, and dressed in black. But they also looked stronger. They stood a little straighter. They weren't just visitors anymore; they were residents returning to the old country to settle affairs.

Stella held the velvet-lined case containing the heavy granite urn. She held it close to her chest, like a child.

"Train approaching," the automated voice echoed through the glass cathedral.

The silver Amfleet train glided in. The conductor—a different one from the trip up—stepped out and saw Leo. He immediately deployed the bridge plate.

"Morning," the conductor said, tipping his cap. "Heading south?"

"Newport News," Stella said. "We have reservations in Business Class."

They rolled on. The Business Class car was quiet. They found their spot: the four-person conference table at the rear of the car.

Stella placed the case gently in the center of the table. She unzipped the top, revealing the polished blue granite and the gleaming chrome rim.

She sat down. Olivia sat next to her. Leo parked his manual chair in the open bay. Luna took the seat across from Stella.

There was one empty seat left at the table—the one next to Luna.

Stella reached into her bag. She pulled out a packet of **paper tickets**. (They didn't have the Bull Pass yet; they were still civilians paying full fare).

She placed **six tickets** on the table.

1.  Stella

2.  Luna

3.  Leo (Accessible Space)

4.  Olivia

5.  Eleanor (seated across the aisle)

6.  **Arthur**

The conductor came by to scan them. He took the tickets, punching them one by one.

Punch. Punch. Punch. Punch. Punch.

He reached for the sixth ticket—the one sitting in front of the urn. He paused. He looked at the granite vessel. He looked at the name engraved on the side: **ARTHUR JAMES BENNETT**.

He didn't ask a stupid question. He didn't say it was against the rules. He understood.

He picked up the ticket. He punched it.

Click.

He placed the ticket gently on top of the urn.

"Welcome aboard, Mr. Bennett," the conductor said softly. "Ride in peace, sir."

He nodded to Stella and moved down the aisle.

Leo watched the ticket sitting on the chrome rim. He tapped his **Quantum Communicator**.

"HE HAS A TICKET. HE IS A PASSENGER."

"He's the VIP," Stella whispered, wiping a tear. "He paid for the ride. It's only right he gets a seat."

Eleanor, sitting across the aisle with her knitting, looked at the urn. "He always liked a window seat," she said. "Make sure he can see the ocean when we cross the Connecticut river."

"I will, Grandma," Luna promised, turning the urn slightly so the "face" of the inscription looked out the window.

The train lurched. The electric locomotive whined. They pulled out of the Glass Cathedral, accelerating south.

For thirteen hours, they rode. They watched the snow-covered hills of New England fade into the industrial grey of New Jersey, then the flat pine forests of Virginia.

They drank coffee. They ate sandwiches. They didn't talk much. The presence of the urn in the center of the table was a heavy, grounding force. It wasn't macabre; it was comforting. He was right there with them, anchoring the center of the Fortress one last time.

## 09:30 PM Newport News Station (NPN)

The train slowed, the diesel engine (which had been swapped back in D.C.) rumbling beneath the floor.

"Now arriving... Newport News."

They gathered their bags. Stella picked up the urn.

They stepped off the train. The air in Virginia was cold, but damp—a different cold than Braxton. It smelled of mud and the James River.

The conductor deployed the **Mobile Lift**.

Leo rolled onto the lift. He looked back at the train. He looked at the urn in Stella’s arms.

He realized that the next time he got on a train, it would be a one-way trip. He wouldn't be coming back here. This wasn't home anymore. It was just a house they had to empty.

The lift shuddered down to the low platform.

Stella walked to the **Holt Sentinel** parked in the long-term lot. She unlocked the doors.

She placed the urn in the front passenger seat and buckled the seatbelt around it.

"Okay, Grandpa," she said, starting the engine. "One last drive to Daniel Maloney Drive. Let's go finish the job."

They drove out of the station, the headlights cutting through the Virginia night, heading home to pack up the past so they could finally live in the future.
