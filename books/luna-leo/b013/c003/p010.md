# Part 10: The Quiet Departure

## December 30, 2005 The University Inn - Front Desk 07:30 AM

Stella took the elevator down to the lobby alone. She was dressed in clean clothes, her hair brushed. She didn't look like a woman in crisis; she looked like a woman with business to conclude.

The lobby was quiet. The morning shift was just starting. **Jessica**, the Hospitality student who had checked them in, was at the desk, organizing key cards.

Stella walked up to the counter.

"Good morning, Ms. Porter," Jessica smiled. "How is everything? Did the storm keep you up?"

"The room was perfect, Jessica," Stella said softly. "But I need to speak with the General Manager. Is Mr. Vance in?"

Jessica saw something in Stella’s eyes—a stillness that wasn't there yesterday. Her smile faded into professional concern. "He is in his office. Is there a problem with the suite?"

"No problem," Stella said. "But my grandfather, Arthur Bennett... he passed away in his sleep early this morning."

Jessica froze for a split second, the shock registering, but her training kicked in immediately. She didn't gasp or make a scene. She lowered her voice.

"I am so, so sorry, Ms. Porter."

"It was peaceful," Stella assured her. "It was expected. He was ready. But we need to handle the next steps quietly. I don't want sirens. I don't want a spectacle in the lobby. I need a doctor to pronounce, and I need the funeral home to come via the service entrance."

"Understood completely," Jessica said. She picked up the phone. "I will get Mr. Vance right now. We will handle everything."

## 08:15 AM Room 103

The hotel manager, Mr. Vance (a seasoned industry veteran teaching the practicum), arrived at the room with a quiet knock. He was accompanied by the University Medical Center's on-call physician, who had come through the Concourse Tunnel to avoid the snow.

There was no chaos. The doctor entered, confirmed what they already knew—natural causes, heart failure, peaceful—and signed the death certificate on the desk.

"He is released," the doctor said gently.

Stella handed Mr. Vance the card of the local funeral home she had already called.

"They are on their way," Stella said.

"We will bring them up via the service elevator," Mr. Vance promised. "We’ll clear the hallway. No other guests will see. Take all the time you need."

## 09:00 AM The Service Corridor

The funeral home staff arrived with a gurney covered in a respectful velvet quilt, not a plastic sheet.

The family gathered in the hallway to walk him out. Eleanor kissed Arthur’s forehead one last time before they covered him.

"Go on, Arthur," she whispered. "You scout the road ahead."

They walked the gurney to the service elevator at the end of the hall. It was a wide, clean, private path.

Leo rolled behind them. He didn't cry. He watched the process. It was efficient. It was respectful. It was exactly how Arthur would have wanted to leave a hotel—without causing a fuss at the front desk.

As the elevator doors closed, taking Arthur down to the waiting hearse in the loading dock (out of sight of the plaza), Stella let out a long breath.

"He's clear," she said.

She turned to Mr. Vance. "We'll need the room for a few more days. We aren't leaving until he's ready to travel."

"The room is yours as long as you need it," Mr. Vance said. "And Ms. Porter? Housekeeping will not disturb you. I've taken the room offline in the system."

"Thank you," Stella said. "He liked this hotel. He said it was well-run."

Mr. Vance bowed his head slightly. "That is the highest compliment we could receive. I will pass it on to the students."

They walked back to the suite. The sun was blazing outside, melting the snow on the Esplanade. The city was waking up, the trains were running, and the Fortress was standing strong, one member lighter, but infinitely heavier in spirit.

Arthur Bennett had checked out. But he had left them the keys to the kingdom.
