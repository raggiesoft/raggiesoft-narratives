# Part 5: The Final Crossing

## July 26, 2006 Warwick Boulevard (US-60) - Southbound 05:10 AM

The ride down Warwick Boulevard had been smooth. The **CVTA** bus was warm and nearly empty, save for a few shipyard workers dozing in their uniforms.

But as the bus passed 40th Street, the landscape changed. The suburban manicured lawns of the university district gave way to the gritty, industrial concrete of downtown Newport News.

"Next stop, 33rd Street," the driver called out. "Train station."

Stella looked out the window. She saw the station—a brick building on the *left* side of the road. The bus was in the right lane.

"He's dropping us across the street," Olivia whispered, realizing the geometry.

The bus pulled over. It didn't pull into a bay. It pulled onto the shoulder of the highway.

The driver kneeled the bus. The ramp deployed—not onto a sidewalk, but onto a patch of wet, overgrown grass and gravel on the side of the road.

"Watch your step," the driver said cheerfully.

They unloaded. Leo’s manual chair landed with a thud on the uneven ground. Stella helped Eleanor down. They grabbed the luggage and the velvet case with Arthur’s urn.

The bus doors closed. The bus roared away, merging back into traffic, leaving them standing in the weeds on the side of a four-lane highway in the pre-dawn darkness.

## The Obstacle Course

They stood on the westbound shoulder. Across the four lanes of **US-60** lay the train station.

Between them and safety was the **Eastbound** lanes—currently a river of headlights. Thousands of shipyard workers were racing to make the 6:00 AM shift change. They were driving fast, aggressive, and tired.

There was no crosswalk. There was no traffic light. There was no pedestrian signal. There wasn't even a streetlamp on this side of the road.

"This is insane," Luna hissed, clutching her flute case. "We have to cross *that*?"

Stella looked at the traffic. Cars were doing 50 MPH in a 35 zone.

"We have to time it," Stella said, her voice tight. "We wait for a gap in the shipyard traffic. We run to the median turn lane. Then we cross the westbound side."

Leo sat in his chair, his wheels sinking slightly into the mud. He looked at the cars whizzing by, inches from his face. He looked at the station across the street—so close, yet separated by a river of steel.

He thought about **Iron Point**, where the bus stopped at his driveway and the sidewalk went straight to the platform.

He tapped his **MagnaByte Scribe**.

*"HOSTILE ARCHITECTURE,"* he typed. *"THIS ROAD WANTS US DEAD."*

"Not today," Olivia said, gripping his handles tight. "I've got you. When Stella says go, we fly."

They waited. The stream of cars was relentless. A pickup truck roared past, blasting its horn, the Doppler effect screaming in their ears.

Finally, a gap. A red light a mile upstream had caught the pack.

"GO!" Stella shouted.

They moved.

Olivia pushed Leo hard, the tires bumping over the asphalt lip. They sprinted across the two Eastbound lanes.

"Median!" Stella yelled.

They hit the center turn lane—the "suicide lane." They paused, breathless, standing on the painted yellow lines while cars zoomed past on both sides.

"Clear!" Luna shouted, looking West.

The westbound side was empty. They dashed across the final two lanes.

Olivia hit the curb cut of the station driveway at speed, popping Leo’s front casters up so he wouldn't crash. They rolled onto the concrete of the station parking lot.

They stopped. They were breathing hard. They were safe.

Stella looked back at the road. The gap had closed. A fresh wave of traffic was thundering past the spot where they had been standing seconds ago.

"We made it," Eleanor breathed, adjusting her grip on her purse. "Good lord, Arthur hated that intersection. Now I remember why."

Leo looked at the station. It was ugly, industrial, and fenced in. But there was a train waiting.

*"LAST OBSTACLE CLEARED,"* he typed.

"Let's get on the train," Stella said, shaking with adrenaline. "I never want to cross a street without a signal ever again."

They walked toward the platform, leaving the hostile asphalt of Virginia behind for good.
