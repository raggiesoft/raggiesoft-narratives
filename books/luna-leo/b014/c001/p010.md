# Part 111: The Missing Frequency

## August 29, 2006 (Tuesday) Braxton Conservatory - Room 304 04:00 PM

The door clicked shut, sealing them inside the vault. The double-drywall and Green Glue acoustic treatment worked perfectly. The roar of the hallway—trumpets, scales, laughter—vanished, replaced by a heavy, studio-quality silence.

Leo rolled his Titan X-Treme into the corner. He parked at the small table Stella had placed there for him. He opened his laptop to work on his Physics lab report.

Luna stood in the center of the room. She assembled her Orpheus 680. The silver caught the overhead halogen lights.

"Okay," Luna said, adjusting her stand. "I need to run the Taffanel & Gaubert scales. It's boring, but I need the finger speed for the Mendelssohn."

She started.

Da-da-da-da-da-da-da-da...

It was fast. It was fluid. The tone was liquid silver.

Leo tried to focus on his vectors. Force = Mass x Acceleration.

But he couldn't.

His brain locked onto the sound. He didn't hear "Music"; he heard Data. He heard a sine wave oscillating at specific frequencies.

Frequency: High. Texture: Smooth. Structure: Floating.

He frowned. He stopped typing.

Luna played a long, ascending run. It hung in the air, beautiful but unsupported.

Leo tapped his finger on the table. Tap... tap... tap...

He was subdividing the beat. He was looking for the grid.

Luna finished the phrase. She lowered the flute. "My timing felt loose in the third measure."

Leo spun his chair around. He looked at her.

"IT IS FLOATING," he typed on his Scribe. "YOU HAVE NO ANCHOR."

"I know," Luna sighed. "I'm rushing. I need a metronome."

She reached for her bag to get her digital click track.

"NO," Leo typed rapidly. "DIGITAL IS COLD. YOU NEED PHYSICS."

He looked at his hands. He remembered the feeling of holding mallets in high school. He remembered the specific, sharp impact of plastic on metal.

He looked at the room. It was too small for a drumline snare (too loud). It was too small for a marimba (too big).

He needed something Metallic. Something Precise. Something that occupied the frequency range above the flute but provided the rhythmic grid below it.

He closed his eyes. He imagined the sound. Ping. Ping. Ping. Clean. Sharp. Steel.

He opened his eyes.

"I NEED A GLOCKENSPIEL," he typed.

Luna blinked. "Bells? Like... orchestra bells?"

"YES. ALUMINUM OR STEEL. HARD MALLETS."

He rolled closer to her music stand.

"THE FLUTE IS WATER," Leo typed, his fingers flying. "IT FLOWS. BUT WATER NEEDS A CHANNEL. I CAN BUILD THE CHANNEL WITH BELLS."

Luna pictured it. The bright, percussive attack of the bells marking the downbeat, while the flute wove the melody around it. It wasn't just accompaniment; it was a structural lattice.

"You want to play?" Luna asked, a smile spreading across her face. "In here? With me?"

"I CANNOT JUST LISTEN," Leo typed. "I AM PART OF THE SIGNAL. I NEED HARDWARE."

"Portable hardware," Luna noted, looking at the door. "You have to carry it on the train."

Leo nodded. He was already searching "Portable Bell Kit" on his browser. He found the Vanguard VK-900. It came in a backpack case. It weighed 15 pounds. It clamped to a table.

"I CAN CARRY IT," he typed. "I WILL ENGINEER A RACK FOR THE CHAIR."

Luna laughed. "You really are an engineer. You're designing a mount for an instrument you haven't even bought yet."

"LOGISTICS FIRST," Leo typed. "THEN MUSIC."

"Okay," Luna said. "We tell Stella tonight. We get the bells. And then..." She raised her flute. "We make some noise."

Leo tapped the table. Ping. Ping.

He could already hear it. The missing frequency was found.
