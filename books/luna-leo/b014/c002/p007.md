# Part 7: The Station Assault

## December 16, 2006 Iron Point Station - The Iron Kettle 09:30 AM

The morning after the "Sanctuary Protocol" was invoked, the Fortress wasn't hiding. They were fortifying.

They had taken over the large corner table in **The Iron Kettle**.

- **The Fortress:** Stella, Olivia, Luna, Leo.

- **The Refugees:** Amanda and Sarah.

- **The Law:** **Judge Silas Miller** (in a heavy wool coat) and **Officer Dave Miller** (in full uniform, starting his shift).

They were eating breakfast sandwiches and drinking coffee milk. The mood was tense but controlled.

Sarah was checking her new phone (the one Stella gave her). "He landed," she whispered. "My old phone—which Leo killed—got a voicemail notification on the carrier log before we cut the line. He’s in the state."

"Let him be in the state," Judge Miller said, dipping a cruller in his coffee. "He doesn't know where the house is exactly, and he can't get past the gate."

Leo sat in his **Titan X-Treme**, watching the window. He was scanning every car that pulled into the station lot.

He saw a generic grey rental sedan pull in aggressively. It parked in a "No Parking" zone near the bus loop.

A man stepped out. He was tall, wearing a rumpled trench coat and looking furious. He was scanning the area.

He looked at the station building. Through the large plate glass window, he saw the group. He saw **Sarah**.

Leo tapped his **Quantum Communicator**.

*"INTRUDER ALERT,"* the robotic voice cut through the chatter. *"TARGET ACQUIRED."*

Sarah looked up. She saw the man coming toward the door. She went pale.

"It's him," she choked out.

## The Breach

The bells above the door jingled violently as Robert Halloway threw it open. A gust of cold wind followed him in.

He didn't look at the menu. He didn't look at the police officer. He looked straight at his daughter.

"Sarah!" he barked. "Get your things. We're leaving."

The coffee shop went silent. **Sal DiMarco** stopped wiping the counter.

Sarah stood up, shaking. "No, Dad. I'm not going."

Robert marched over to the table. He ignored Stella, who had risen to her feet. He ignored Leo.

"I am not playing games with you," Robert shouted, his face turning red. "You turned off your phone. You ran away to a cult. You are clearly having an episode. I am taking you home to get you help."

"She isn't having an episode," Stella said, stepping in his path. "She is having breakfast. Back off."

"Get out of my way," Robert sneered at Stella. "This is a family matter. You have no right to interfere with my daughter."

"She's an adult," Officer Miller said from his seat. He hadn't stood up yet. He wanted to see what the guy would do. "And she stated she doesn't want to go."

Robert finally looked at the cop. He dismissed him.

"She's incompetent," Robert snapped. "She doesn't know what she wants. I'm her father."

He reached past Stella. He grabbed Sarah’s upper arm. Hard.

"Ow!" Sarah cried out, trying to pull away.

"You are coming with me right now," Robert growled, yanking her toward the door. He dragged her a step, her shoes squeaking on the linoleum.

## The Response

That was the mistake.

Officer Miller moved. He didn't stand up slowly; he launched.

"HEY!" Miller shouted.

Robert ignored him, pulling Sarah harder.

Miller crossed the distance in two strides. He grabbed Robert’s wrist—the one holding Sarah—and applied a pressure point lock that forced the hand to open.

"Let her go!" Miller commanded.

Robert spun around, swinging his free arm wildly to shove the cop away. His hand connected with Miller’s chest.

## The Miranda Warning

It was over in three seconds.

Miller swept Robert’s leg, driving him face-first into the linoleum floor. He put a knee in the small of his back.

"You are under arrest!" Miller shouted, pulling his cuffs.

"Get off me!" Robert screamed, struggling. "I'm her father! You can't arrest me! She's sick!"

*CLICK-CLICK.*

The handcuffs locked. Miller hauled him up.

"You are under arrest for **Domestic Assault** and **Assault on a Law Enforcement Officer**," Miller recited, spinning Robert around.

Robert looked wild. He looked at the table.

"You people are crazy!" he yelled. "I'm filing a motion! I'll have your badges! I'll sue all of you!"

He looked at the older man sitting calmly at the table, finishing his donut.

"And you!" Robert shouted at Judge Miller. "You're a witness! You saw them kidnap her!"

Judge Silas Miller stood up slowly. He wiped a crumb from his coat. He walked over to Robert.

"Mr. Halloway," the Judge said, his voice deep and terrifyingly calm. "You are threatening to file a motion?"

"Damn right I am!" Robert spat. "In Probate Court! Today!"

"Well," Silas said, leaning in close. "That’s going to be difficult. Because I *am* the Probate Court."

Robert froze. "What?"

"I am **Judge Silas Miller**," he announced. "And since you just assaulted a resident of my town and a police officer in my presence, I can tell you right now how your motion is going to go."

Officer Miller hauled Robert Halloway to his feet. Robert’s expensive trench coat was rumpled, his face pressed against the linoleum just seconds before. He was red-faced, sputtering, his eyes darting around the donut shop as if looking for an ally. He found none.

"You're making a mistake!" Robert shouted, spit flying. "She's my daughter! I have rights!"

"You have the right to remain silent," Officer Miller said, his voice calm, practiced, and absolute. He spun Robert toward the door, gripping his arm firmly.

"Anything you say can and will be used against you in a court of law," Miller continued, marching him past the stunned customers who were watching over their coffee.

"I'm suing all of you!" Robert screamed, looking back at Judge Miller. "You heard me! This is a conspiracy!"

"You have the right to an attorney," Miller recited, ignoring the outburst. "If you cannot afford an attorney, one will be provided for you. Do you understand the rights I have just read to you?"

"I don't need a public defender!" Robert yelled as Miller pushed him out the door into the cold December air. "I have money! I have—"

"And you!" Robert shouted at Judge Miller. "You're a witness! You saw them kidnap her!"

Judge Silas Miller stood up slowly. He wiped a crumb from his coat. He walked over to Robert.

"Mr. Halloway," the Judge said, his voice deep and terrifyingly calm. "You are threatening to file a motion?"

"Damn right I am!" Robert spat. "In Probate Court! Today!"

"Well," Silas said, leaning in close. "That’s going to be difficult. Because I *am* the Probate Court."

Robert froze. "What?"

"I am **Judge Silas Miller**," he announced. "And since you just assaulted a resident of my town and a police officer in my presence, I can tell you right now how your motion is going to go."

He looked at Officer Miller.

"Book him, Dave. No bail until Monday arraignment. I want him to think about his parenting choices in a cell."

"Yes, Your Honor," Miller said, frog-marching Robert toward the door.

Sarah stood there, rubbing her bruised arm. She watched her father—the monster who had controlled her life for eighteen years—being dragged out of a donut shop by the local police.

## The Remand

Judge Miller followed them out to the curb. He watched Officer Miller shove Robert into the back of the cruiser. The barrier slammed shut. The lock clicked.

Robert pressed his face against the glass, still shouting.

Judge Miller leaned down to the window.

"Mr. Halloway," the Judge said, his voice audible through the glass. "You mentioned bail earlier. Let me be clear."

He pointed to the courthouse down the street.

"It is Saturday. My court is closed until Monday at 9:00 AM. Given the flight risk you pose and the violent nature of the assault on a police officer... I am **Denying Bail**."

Robert’s eyes went wide. He pounded on the glass.

"You sit in the town lockup until Monday morning arraignment," Judge Miller said coldly. "Enjoy the hospitality of Rhode Island."

He slapped the roof of the cruiser.

"Take him away, Dave."

The cruiser pulled out, lights flashing briefly, taking the monster to a cage.

## The Aftermath

Inside the shop, the silence was heavy.

Sarah stood frozen near the table, rubbing her bruised arm. She was shaking so violently her teeth were chattering. She was staring at the empty parking spot where her father’s rental car still sat, abandoned.

Stella stepped forward to hold her, but Leo moved first.

He rolled his **Titan X-Treme** directly in front of Sarah. He blocked her view of the window. He blocked her view of the past.

He reached out. He took her hand. His grip was firm.

Sarah looked down at him. She saw Leo. The boy who had been kidnapped. The boy who had been voiceless.

Leo tapped his **Quantum Communicator**.

*"HE IS GONE,"* the robotic voice said softly. *"THE BADGE STOPPED HIM."*

Sarah squeezed his hand. "He... he looked so mad. He's never been arrested. He always wins."

*"NOT HERE,"* Leo typed. *"IN IRON POINT, THE BULLIES LOSE."*

Sarah’s legs gave out. She sank to the floor, right next to Leo’s chair. She buried her face in his armrest, sobbing.

"I'm sorry," she gasped. "I brought him here. I ruined breakfast."

Leo put his hand on Sarah’s head. He stroked her hair, awkward but gentle.

*"YOU DID NOT BRING HIM,"* Leo typed. *"HE HUNTED YOU. BUT THE FORTRESS WALLS HELD."*

Judge Miller walked back inside. He looked down at Sarah. He didn't look like a terrifying judge anymore; he looked like a grandfather.

"Sarah," the Judge said gently. "Listen to me. I am going to issue a **Temporary Restraining Order** immediately. He will be arraigned Monday morning. One of the conditions of his release—if I grant it—will be that he must leave the state of Rhode Island immediately and have zero contact with you. If he texts you, if he calls you, if he sends a carrier pigeon... he goes to jail for six months. Minimum."

Sarah looked up, wiping her eyes. "Really?"

"Really," the Judge promised. "You are a resident of my town. And we protect our own."

Leo looked at the window. The cruiser was gone. The monster was gone.

He tapped his Scribe one last time.

*"YOU ARE A GHOST NOW,"* he typed. *"HE CANNOT SEE YOU."*

"I'm a ghost," Sarah whispered, a small, shaky smile breaking through the tears. "I like that."

"Come on," Stella said, helping Sarah stand up. "Let's go home. I think we all need a mental health day."

They walked out of the shop, leaving the cold coffee and the half-eaten donuts behind, stepping into the crisp winter air as a family that had fought a war before 10:00 AM and won.
