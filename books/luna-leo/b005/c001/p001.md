# Northwood High Cafeteria – Lunch Period, Fall 1999 (From Luna and Leo Porter’s POV)

The Northwood High cafeteria during 5th period lunch was a symphony of controlled chaos, a noisy, echoing cavern filled with the clatter of trays and the roar of hundreds of teenage conversations. Fourteen-year-old Luna Porter, leaning slightly on the armrest of her twin brother Leo’s Quantum XT wheelchair for support as they scanned for a relatively calm table, felt a familiar wave of freshman overwhelm. Leo, beside her, his own Quantum Communicator XT resting on his tray, observed the scene with his usual quiet intensity.

They'd heard the upperclassmen's "Unofficial Orientation" earlier that morning. Mark and that girl with the pink hair had spun wild tales about the cafeteria supervisor, Mrs. DeMarco: her encyclopedic memory for pranks, her fanatical defense of the mystery meat, and her alleged fluency in every language known to humankind. Luna had exchanged a skeptical look with Leo. Every language? Really? Leo had typed back a succinct: HIGHLY IMPROBABLE. They’d figured most of it was just freshman hazing, designed to terrify.

They found a table, and as they settled in with their lukewarm pizza, the relative peace was shattered.

"PENIS!"

The shout, loud and arrogant, came from a few tables over. Luna nearly choked on her milk.

"Seriously?" she muttered to Leo, her eyes wide. "First day?"

Leo was already typing, his expression a mixture of disbelief and grim anticipation: JOSH RILEY. OF COURSE. THIS WILL BE... EDUCATIONAL.

They watched as a formidable-looking woman – that must be Mrs. DeMarco, Luna realized with a jolt – began to walk with slow, deliberate steps towards Josh Riley’s table. The cafeteria hummed, then quieted. Josh, still smirking, was clearly oblivious to the doom approaching.

Then, another sound erupted, even more surprising. A girl at a nearby table – Luna thought she’d seen her earlier with the other boy in a wheelchair, Alex Miller – was on her feet, her voice sharp and surprisingly fierce, unleashing a torrent of what sounded like… French? But a fiery, rapid-fire French Luna had never heard in her school classes.

"TABAROUETTE, RILEY!" the girl spat, and continued with a string of passionate, clearly insulting words.

"Whoa," Luna breathed to Leo. "What language is that? She’s furious!"

Leo typed: FRENCH. I THINK.

Josh Riley looked utterly dumbfounded.

Just as Luna was thinking, Well, so much for Mrs. DeMarco knowing every language, a new voice cut through the air, cool and precise.

"Mademoiselle Mason."

It was Mrs. DeMarco. And she was speaking French. But not the fiery, earthy French the girl had used. This was… different. Perfect. Like something from a movie set in Paris.

Luna’s jaw physically dropped. She stared at Leo, who looked equally stunned. No… way. The upperclassmen… they hadn’t been exaggerating?

Mrs. DeMarco addressed the girl, Chloe, in flawless Parisian French, gently but firmly admonishing her language while acknowledging her indignation. Chloe, looking both chagrined and deeply respectful, replied in more formal French.

Leo typed, his own usual wit momentarily deserting him: SHE... SHE REALLY SPEAKS FRENCH. TWO KINDS.

Then, Mrs. DeMarco turned her icy gaze on Josh Riley, switching to perfect English. As she began to catalogue his offenses, Luna leaned closer to Leo, whispering, "Okay, I officially believe everything those seniors said. This is terrifying. And amazing."

But the linguistic acrobatics weren't over. Just as Josh was wilting, some other sophomore idiot, Kevin Schmidt, muttered a snide comment in truly terrible German.

Mrs. DeMarco’s head snapped towards him. "Entschuldigung?" she inquired, and then proceeded to verbally dismantle him in fluent, crisp, and utterly fluent German.

Luna felt a shiver go down her spine. "German too?" she whispered to Leo. "Is there any language she doesn't know?"

Leo typed, a new level of profound awe in his synthesized voice: MRS. DEMARCO IS NOT A CAFETERIA LADY. SHE IS A LINGUISTIC SUPERWEAPON. AND WE ARE ALL POTENTIAL TARGETS. NOTE TO SELF: DO NOT ATTEMPT SARCASM IN SWAHILI.

The sentencing of both Josh and Kevin to a week of trash duty felt almost anticlimactic after the linguistic fireworks. But then, Mrs. DeMarco did something that made Luna’s respect for her solidify into something akin to hero-worship. She turned back to Chloe Mason and the boy in the wheelchair, Alex Miller, and spoke to Chloe softly, again in that beautiful Parisian French, "Tout va bien maintenant, mes enfants?" (Is everything alright now, my children?)

Chloe replied, her gratitude evident. Alex Miller then used his own AAC device, and its synthesized English voice filled the small pocket of quiet: THANK YOU, MRS. DEMARCO. YOU ARE… AMAZING. TRULY. CHLOE IS A VERY GOOD FRIEND.

Mrs. DeMarco actually smiled at him, a tiny, genuine smile. And then, she addressed the entire cafeteria, asking if anyone else wanted trash duty. The silence was absolute.

Luna let out a breath she hadn’t realized she was holding. "Okay," she whispered to Leo, who was using his Quantum Communicator XT with a grin. "Mrs. DeMarco is officially my new hero. Did you see that? French and German? Josh and Kevin looked like they were going to wet their pants!"

Leo typed, his own amusement evident: SHE IS VERY… EFFECTIVE. AND CHLOE MASON… VERY BRAVE. LIKE YOU, LU. GOOD TO SEE BULLIES GET WHAT THEY DESERVE. THE TRASH DUTY PART IS FUNNY. HOPE THEY HAVE TO SORT RECYCLING.

Luna snickered. "Totally. Maybe Mrs. DeMarco will make them do it in French."

She watched Chloe Mason and Alex Miller, who were now actually starting to eat their lunch, a visible sense of safety settled around them. That Chloe girl had been fierce. And Alex, with his quiet presence and his own talker, seemed… interesting. Like Leo, but different.

"You know, Leo," Luna said thoughtfully, "that Chloe Mason… she's alright. Really alright."

Leo Porter nodded, a thoughtful expression on his face as he observed the other pair. YES. SHE IS.

The first day of freshman year, and they had already witnessed a legend in action. Northwood High was going to be an adventure, and clearly, the cafeteria was its most unpredictable and educational stage.


It was a quiet Saturday evening, a few weeks after they'd moved in and registered for school. The initial chaos had subsided into a tentative routine. The twins were sitting together on the living room floor, perhaps looking through school books or simply enjoying the quiet. Stella, feeling a rare moment of relaxation and perhaps a touch of nostalgia, decided it was time for something different.

She went to the back of a closet and pulled out some equipment Luna and Leo had never seen before: a sleek turntable and a boxy amplifier, components she'd held onto from her own high school days but hadn't set up yet in the new house. She also retrieved a cardboard box filled with vinyl LPs. Intrigued by the unfamiliar objects, the twins watched as Stella carefully found a spot for the turntable and amplifier on a side table near the main entertainment center, connecting them to the existing stereo receiver and speakers. What *was* all that stuff?

Stella selected one of the black discs – removing the Polaris Superhits compilation album carefully from its sleeve – and placed it onto the flat rotating part of the main machine. She fiddled with an arm-like piece, lowering the tiny point onto the very beginning of the disc, which then began to spin. Suddenly, music filled the room – the unmistakable opening piano glissando of "Starlight Princess."

Luna and Leo exchanged baffled looks. How was music coming from that spinning black circle? Before they could formulate a question, however, their attention was diverted by an even more surprising sight. Stella, seemingly forgetting they were there, had closed her eyes and started to dance. The usually composed, ultra-responsible Stella melted away, replaced by someone looser, more joyful. She spun, her arms gesturing dramatically, completely lost in the rhythm generated by the mysterious machine.

The twins stared, momentarily torn between the bizarre spectacle of Stella dancing and the technological puzzle spinning on the side table. Leo tilted his head, pointing tentatively first at Stella, then questioningly at the turntable. Luna, equally bewildered, finally used her AAC device (which Stella had recently acquired for them), typing rapidly. The synthesized voice broke the spell: *"What is that machine? How does it make music?"*

Hearing the electronic voice, Stella froze mid-spin, her eyes flying open. She saw Luna and Leo staring, not just at her, but with intense curiosity at the record player. A deep blush instantly flooded Stella’s face.

"Oh!" she stammered, quickly lifting the needle off the record, plunging the room into sudden silence. "I... uh... didn't realize..." She looked from their confused faces to the turntable. "Right. You guys have probably never seen one of these, huh? This is... well, this is a record player."

Seeing their continued fascination, Stella's embarrassment faded, replaced by a teacherly enthusiasm. "Come here," she beckoned, kneeling beside the turntable. She carefully lifted the record slightly. "See these tiny little grooves? They wiggle back and forth in patterns." She pointed to the needle on the tone arm. "This tiny point, called a stylus or needle, sits in the groove. As the record spins, the needle wiggles, and those wiggles get turned into electrical signals, which the amplifier makes loud enough for the speakers to turn back into music."

She then opened the cardboard box she'd retrieved earlier. "This is vinyl," she explained, carefully sliding out another record, this one featuring Gloria Day. "It's how people mostly listened to music before CDs basically took over everything. Feels like ancient history now, doesn't it?" She showed them a few more – the The Brothers Shine, Elements of Soul, and the iconic cover of Fleetwood Mac's Whispers – pointing out the large, colorful album art.

Luna and Leo were completely captivated. Leo leaned forward in his chair, tracing the grooves on the POLARIS record with his finger (careful not to touch the playing surface after a gentle caution from Stella). Luna asked questions, sometimes verbally, sometimes through her device, about how the needle stayed in the groove and why the records were so big. They examined the album covers, so different from the small CD booklets they were used to. For the rest of the evening, the mystery machine held their full attention, a fascinating window into their sister's past and a whole different way of experiencing music. That night, exploring Stella's collection, sparked something new in them. It was the beginning of a shared, lifelong love for the sounds and stories of classic rock, forever linked to this memory of discovery and connection in Stella's living room.


Inspired by the twins' burgeoning interest in music, sparked by the record player discovery, Stella decided to explore it further before the summer school session began. One Saturday afternoon, she took them to a local music store, the kind filled with the gleam of brass, the smell of wood polish, and the potential for noise.

"Just looking around," Stella announced as they entered, letting the twins absorb the atmosphere. Luna, naturally drawn to the wind instruments, gravitated towards the saxophones. A store employee kindly let her hold a gleaming tenor sax. Though heavy, the moment she figured out how to hold it and managed to produce a tentative, breathy note through the mouthpiece (without the reed properly set, of course), her eyes lit up with an intensity Stella hadn't seen before. "This one," Luna declared quietly but firmly. "I want to learn this one."

Meanwhile, Leo had wheeled over to the percussion section. His attention was caught by the xylophones and marimbas. He reached out, tapping a wooden bar with a mallet provided by another helpful employee, a clear, resonant tone filling the air. He tried another, then another, a look of focused concentration on his face. The employee, noticing his interest and his wheelchair, came over. "You know," he said kindly, "these stands," gesturing to the metal frame holding the bars, "are usually separate pieces. We often modify them for players who need to sit. It wouldn't be hard to adjust one so you could play comfortably from your chair." Leo looked up at Stella, his eyes wide with possibility, then back at the instrument, nodding emphatically.

Right there in the store, the twins' excitement was palpable. "Lessons? Can we take lessons, Stella? Please?" Luna begged. Leo used his AAC device to echo the sentiment with equal fervor.

Stella, thrilled by their enthusiasm, turned to the helpful employee near the front counter. "Do you guys offer lessons here? Or know good instructors for tenor sax and xylophone?"

The employee smiled. "We sure do. Let me check the schedule." He consulted a binder behind the counter. "Okay... looks like Mr. Thompson, he's fantastic with woodwinds, has an opening tomorrow afternoon. And... wow, lucky break, Mrs. Gable, our percussion teacher, has an opening at the exact same time. Makes transportation easier!"

"Tomorrow?" Stella asked, surprised but pleased. "That's perfect!" She looked at the twins, who were practically bouncing. "Alright then," she declared, turning back to the employee. "Sign them up! And we'll need to purchase a tenor sax and purchase that xylophone – the one you said could be modified – plus whatever accessories they need to get started. Oh, and two music stands, please."

They left the store not just with newfound musical ambitions, but with instruments in hand (or carefully packed for transport), lesson times secured for the very next day, and a shared sense of excitement for this new adventure.

(The connection to Mr. Thompson being the band director and inviting them to join would likely happen after a few lessons, as established previously)


Just because Luna and Leo were safely settled in Stella's house didn't mean the challenges were over. The peace was shattered a few weeks after the move when the doorbell rang insistently. Peeking through the peephole, Stella's heart sank – it was her parents. She had explicitly told them they were not welcome after the forced extraction. Remembering the lawyer's advice to avoid confrontation if possible, Stella held firm and simply did not answer the door. She quietly instructed the twins, who had tensed up at the sound of the bell, to do the same and stay away from the windows. Her parents eventually yelled a few things through the door about wanting to see *their* children and how Stella didn't know what she was doing, before finally leaving.

This happened several more times over the following weeks – unannounced appearances, demands shouted through the door, sometimes lingering on the porch or even peering into windows. Each incident was stressful, upsetting the twins and reinforcing Stella's need to protect their newfound stability. Recognizing this harassment couldn't continue, Stella, again with her lawyer's help (and funded by the grandparents' trust), went back to court to obtain a Temporary Protective Order (TPO). It felt like another battle, another layer of formality separating her from the people who should have been family, but it was necessary for peace and stability.

Simultaneously, Stella made sure to visit the twins' new high school, providing the administration with copies of the guardianship and conservatorship orders. She explicitly updated all emergency contact information and access lists, ensuring that under no circumstances were her parents to be contacted or allowed access to the twins or their school records. It was another difficult but essential step in creating a secure bubble around Luna and Leo.

News trickled back to Stella indirectly over the following months – her parents had divorced. Apparently, the stress of the court case and losing custody had fractured their already weak relationship, leading to bitter recriminations where each blamed the other for the family falling apart. Neither, Stella learned with a weary sigh, seemed capable of acknowledging their own choices and neglect – likely fueled by years of heavy drinking and denial – as the root cause. The twins, when they heard snippets of this news, showed little reaction beyond a quiet acceptance. They had no desire to reconnect with the parents who had failed them so profoundly. Stella felt the same. The emotional ties had been frayed for years, and the recent battles had severed them completely.

Eventually, understanding the finality of the situation and wanting to ensure long-term peace, Stella pursued and obtained a permanent restraining order. This comprehensive court order legally prohibited her parents from contacting, harassing, or coming within a specified distance of not only Luna and Leo, but also Stella herself. The order explicitly covered their home, Northwood High School, and Stella’s workplace, making any attempt to approach these locations a violation subject to immediate arrest. The credible threat of facing Officer Whitehead at the school or other law enforcement, and the very real possibility of jail time, finally proved to be the one consequence her parents truly feared. This fear compelled their compliance; they didn't dare test the boundaries again, going so far as to alter their driving routes to avoid even passing near the high school. The chapter with their parents was definitively, legally, and necessarily closed.

With the toxic influence of her parents legally removed, Stella turned her attention to another fractured family connection. Her maternal grandparents, **Arthur and Eleanor Bennett**, who had provided the crucial, discreet financial support via the trust fund, had been largely cut off from seeing the children years ago due to their daughter's (the twins' mother's) resentment or control. Now, as legal guardian, Stella reached out to them. She explained the situation, the court orders, and her desire for Luna and Leo to have positive, supportive family members in their lives. As a gesture of gratitude for their unwavering, albeit hidden, support, Stella worked with her lawyer to formally facilitate the restoration of **Arthur and Eleanor’s** grandparental visitation rights, ensuring everything was legally sound and respected the twins' comfort levels. The reconnection was tentative at first, but filled with quiet joy. **Arthur and Eleanor**, relieved and grateful, became regular fixtures at the periphery of the twins' lives. They attended every home football game, sitting proudly in the stands, their eyes fixed on Luna and Leo during the halftime show. They drove hours to attend marching band competitions, cheering enthusiastically from the audience. They respected Stella's role as guardian implicitly, offering quiet advice and gentle guidance based on their own life experience when Stella sought it, but always careful never to overstep her authority as the twins' primary decision-maker. Understanding the immense responsibility Stella carried, they readily agreed when Stella asked them to serve as official backup contacts. Stella formally listed them as alternate emergency contacts with Northwood High and completed the necessary paperwork authorizing them to make emergency medical decisions for Luna and Leo should she ever be unreachable. This provided Stella with a vital safety net, knowing there were other trusted adults legally empowered to act in the twins' best interest if needed. **Arthur and Eleanor** were simply happy to witness their grandchildren thriving in the safe, nurturing environment Stella had created, an environment their own financial foresight had helped make possible. It was a quiet mending, a rebuilding of family ties on a healthier foundation.


With the legal and safety issues settled, Stella focused again on ensuring the twins had the best tools for communication beyond the basic typing they'd mastered. She arranged for updated assessments with speech and occupational therapists now that she was their guardian. Based on recommendations, she learned about and navigated the process of acquiring modern (for the late 90s/early 2000s) Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) devices – perhaps dedicated units with synthesized speech or dynamic symbol displays. These were crucial for their success at school and interacting outside the home, offering capabilities like voice output that the MagnaByte Opus lacked. The twins began the process of learning these new systems, mastering new interfaces and vocabulary sets. While these devices opened new doors, Stella likely noticed that their fluency and comfort weren't quite the same as when they used the MagnaByte Opus for pure text composition. The muscle memory and emotional connection to their original digital voice remained strong.


Perhaps it was watching them painstakingly program a phrase into their new AAC device that Luna could have typed in seconds on MagnaSuite. Or maybe Leo, trying to explain a complex idea, gestured towards the corner of their shared workspace in the living room, mimicking typing on an invisible keyboard, his AAC device momentarily feeling inadequate for the speed of his thoughts. It might have been Luna explicitly saying, "Remember how easy it was on the MagnaByte?" during a moment of frustration with a newer technology. These small moments, these echoes of their past, resonated with Stella. She saw that the MagnaByte Opus wasn't just a memory; it represented a time of peak competence and comfort for them. The seed planted by their history began to sprout into a concrete idea in Stella's mind, now potentially feasible thanks to her steady job.


Starting high school, Luna and Leo carried the weight of years of academic neglect. Despite Stella's diligent work setting up IEPs and arranging accommodations like shared lockers, early dismissal, and quiet library time, the initial months were challenging. There were significant gaps in their foundational knowledge, and the pace of high school work was demanding. Simply being in a supportive environment wasn't an instant fix; catching up required immense effort from everyone.  

But the combination of factors began to yield results. The stability of Stella’s apartment provided a secure base they'd never had. Stella herself was a constant source of encouragement, helping with homework, advocating fiercely for their needs at school, and ensuring they consistently had the right tools – including their essential AAC devices and Leo's properly functioning wheelchair. The school's special education department worked closely with Stella, implementing the IEPs effectively.  

Most importantly, Luna and Leo tackled the challenge together. Motivated by the chance to finally learn and succeed, they poured their energy into their studies. Luna used her AAC device to participate more fully in class discussions and structure her thoughts for essays. Leo, equipped with his device and supported by teachers who understood how to facilitate his participation, demonstrated a sharp intellect previously masked by communication barriers. They spent hours in the library, helping each other review notes and complete assignments.  

Slowly at first, then with gathering momentum, they began to bridge the gaps. Confusion gave way to comprehension, and missed concepts clicked into place. By the end of their freshman year, the transformation was remarkable. Luna and Leo weren't just "caught up"; they were excelling. Their grades placed them near the top of their classes. Teachers who had initially worried about their significant disadvantages now praised their diligence, insight, and rapid progress. Having been starved of educational opportunity for so long, they thrived when finally given the chance, proving that they hadn't just been left behind – they had been held back. Now, academically, they were soaring far ahead of where anyone, except perhaps Stella, had thought possible just one year earlier


One afternoon, sometime after the MagnaByte Opus machines had become the established homework hub, another vinyl exploration session was underway. Luna and Leo were taking turns choosing albums, their confidence growing with each successful placement of the needle. Stella watched, enjoying their shared enthusiasm. Leo, browsing through the box, pulled out an album with a bizarrely elaborate cover designed exactly like a folded, multi-page local newspaper: *The The Dunmoor Gazette*. It was Greensleeve's *Dense as a Stone*.

"What's this one?" Luna asked, leaning over Leo's shoulder to look at the dense print and parody articles filling the cover.

"Ah, that's a weird one," Stella chuckled. "It's supposed to be a concept album based on a poem by a fictional kid, and The Modsle cover is a fake newspaper about him. Pretty complicated." As she spoke, she carefully placed the record on the turntable and lowered the needle, letting the album's intricate, folk-rock instrumental opening fill the room.

Leo unfolded the large cover carefully, spreading the "newspaper" out on the floor. Both twins stared at the columns of text, the fake advertisements, the crossword puzzle. It looked intimidatingly dense. The complex music swirled around them as they focused on the printed words.

"Try reading some of it," Stella encouraged gently, remembering their past struggles but also seeing the spark of curiosity in their eyes, now amplified by the accompanying music.

Hesitantly at first, Luna started sounding out a headline aloud. It was slow going, and Stella knelt beside her, quietly helping her sound out trickier words. Leo pointed to a picture caption, his eyes following the words intently. He then used his AAC device to type out a key word from the caption, looking to Stella for confirmation. "That's right!" Stella affirmed, pointing to the word on the page. Then, something seemed to click. Luna continued reading aloud with increasing confidence as Stella offered quiet prompts, while Leo pointed to sections and then typed related words or short summaries on his device, sharing triumphant looks with Stella when she confirmed his understanding. They stumbled occasionally, but the context of the parody newspaper, combined perhaps with the relaxed atmosphere, the engaging music, and Stella's patient help, seemed to unlock something. They weren't just decoding words; they were *reading* complex, varied text together. It felt warm, connected – a true family bonding moment wrapped in classic rock and shared effort.

After several minutes engrossed in the fictional *The Dunmoor Gazette*, the music still playing softly, Luna looked up at Stella, her eyes shining. "We can read this!" she exclaimed, a note of genuine surprise and triumph in her voice.

Leo nodded vigorously, a huge grin spreading across his face. He quickly typed on his AAC device: *"Read newspaper! Easy!"*

It was a breakthrough moment. After years of academic challenges and the frustrations of inaccessible tools, here they were, comfortably reading the dense, satirical text of a classic rock album cover. The excitement was palpable.

"Hey," Luna said suddenly, looking at the unfolded cover, "this is like... a whole story. Can we do our English book report on *this*?"

Leo immediately agreed, tapping enthusiastically on his device.

Stella considered it. It was unconventional, certainly, but incredibly fitting given their journey. "You know what? That's a fantastic idea," she said. "Let's ask your English teacher. I bet she'll appreciate the creativity."

As it turned out, their English teacher loved the idea, praising their initiative and unique approach. The Greensleeve album cover didn't just provide another musical discovery; it became an unexpected bridge to a new level of literacy and academic confidence for Luna and Leo.


It was a Tuesday evening in late 1999. Rain pattered against the Quantum OS of their Virginia Beach home. Inside, the air was thick with the quiet tension of homework frustration. Luna and Leo were huddled around the family's beige tower PC set up on its desk in the living room, its monitor displaying the busy interface of Quantum WritePad 97 running on Quantum OS 98. They were supposed to be typing up a short report on the Jamestown settlement for their history class.

Luna had control of the mouse; her brow furrowed in concentration as she tried to navigate the drop-down menus. "Okay, I just want to indent the paragraph," she muttered, more to herself than Leo. She clicked 'Format,' then 'Paragraph,' and a complex dialog box popped up with multiple tabs and options for spacing, indents, and line breaks. Her eyes scanned the options, overwhelmed. "Which one? Left? Special? First line? By how much?" She remembered MagnaSuite being so much simpler – wasn't there just a key command or a single menu option? She clicked tentatively, and the entire paragraph suddenly jumped halfway across the page. "Augh! No!"

Leo, watching intently, shifted restlessly in his wheelchair. He hated this computer. The mouse pointer felt jumpy and imprecise under his hand when he tried it. Even the keyboard felt wrong – soft and mushy compared to the distinct, springy click of the MagnaByte keys he remembered. That satisfying tactile feedback was gone, replaced by uncertainty with each key press. Added to that, the sheer number of icons and menus on the screen felt like visual noise. He much preferred the predictable grid and clear, simple menus of the old MagnaByte Opus desktop. Here, everything felt buried under layers of clicks and unsatisfying key presses. He saw Luna struggling and reached for his AAC device resting on the desk beside the keyboard. Slowly, he typed out a message: *"Too confusing. Keyboard feels bad."*

Luna glanced at his screen and sighed, slumping back in the chair. "Tell me about it. I can't find anything. It took me ten minutes just to figure out how to change the font, and I *still* don't know how to save this properly. Where does it even *go* when you save?" She gestured vaguely at the screen. "It keeps asking about folders and directories. And this keyboard *is* mushy."

Stella walked in from the kitchen, drying her hands on a towel. She saw their tense postures and the half-finished, oddly formatted document on the screen. "Everything okay over here?" she asked gently.

Luna threw her hands up in exasperation. "No! This computer is impossible! We're trying to do this report, but WritePad is just... weird. And the keyboard feels sticky or something. I wish we still had that old computer from elementary school. The MagnaByte one."

Leo nodded vigorously, pointing towards Luna and then tapping his chest emphatically. He then used his device again: *"Want MagnaSuite. Easy. Good keyboard."*

"MagnaSuite... good keyboard..." Stella echoed softly, looking at their genuinely distressed faces. She remembered them mentioning the software before, but hearing them complain about the physical feel of the keyboard too – it added another dimension to their discomfort. It wasn't just the confusing software; the very tools felt wrong in their hands. Seeing their struggle firsthand, hearing the specific name of the program and the mention of the keyboard – it hit differently. She saw past the typical homework grumbling; this was a real barrier, fueled by the unfamiliarity clashing with their autistic need for predictable systems and comfortable sensory input. They weren't just confused; they were deeply uncomfortable and anxious using this machine.

"I know this one's different," Stella said soothingly, trying to push down her own frustration on their behalf. "Let's see if we can figure out this indenting thing together for now, okay?"

They managed to finish the report eventually, with Stella patiently guiding them through Word's quirks, but the relief on their faces when they finally closed the program was palpable. The frustration of the process lingered, solidifying Stella's quiet resolve to find a better way for them – a way that felt right, both on screen and under their fingertips. *Maybe just finding a different keyboard could help in the meantime,* she mused silently as she cleared the dinner plates later that evening. *Something with proper springs, that satisfying click they remembered. Weren't those old COMPUBLUE keyboards, the KeyMech things, still around somewhere? They used the standard connector, didn't they? Perhaps that could ease the typing part while she started the much harder search for the complete MagnaByte Opus systems she now knew they truly needed.*


Finding a COMPUBLUE KeyMech keyboard, as Stella had considered, provided some tangible relief for homework sessions involving the family PC. The twins appreciated the familiar, clicky feel under their fingertips, a definite improvement over the PC's standard membrane keyboard. Typing itself became less of a sensory frustration. However, this interim fix only highlighted the remaining, larger problem: navigating the confusing Quantum OS 98 operating system and the complex menus of Quantum Suite 97 was still a significant hurdle compared to the intuitive simplicity they remembered. Stella saw that while the keyboard helped, the core need for a completely familiar *environment* – the one tied intrinsically to their autistic need for predictability and their first positive computing experiences – remained unmet.

Witnessing this solidified her decision. It wasn't enough to just fix the keyboard; she needed to recreate the setup where they truly thrived. Recognizing this wasn't just nostalgia, but about accommodating a genuine need tied to their neurotype, and knowing her steady job provided the means, Stella became determined to find not one, but two functional MagnaByte Opus computers – one for each twin – and a compatible MagnaByte GraphiPrint printer, despite the difficulty of sourcing such vintage hardware in the year 2000.

It took several weeks of dedicated searching through online forums, classifieds, and countless InfoLink Trade listings. Finding one working MagnaByte Opus was tricky enough, but Stella was determined to find *two* – one for each twin to work simultaneously – plus a compatible printer. Eventually, her persistence paid off. She secured two fully functional MagnaByte Opus units, complete with monitors and drives, from different sellers. In a separate auction, she snagged a MagnaByte GraphiPrint II printer, the noisy but reliable workhorse often paired with the Opus. As a final piece, she tracked down a simple parallel port A/B switch box, allowing both computers to share the single printer.

The arrival day felt like Christmas, though the twins didn't know it yet. Several large, plain cardboard boxes arrived while Luna and Leo were at school; Stella had arranged to work from home that day specifically to receive the packages. She wrestled the heavy boxes inside, stacking them in the living room near the existing computer desk, but she resisted the urge to unpack them. She also left the boxes containing a new, larger rectangular table and two extra office chairs unopened. She wanted the twins to be there for the entire reveal and setup.

When Luna and Leo came home that afternoon, walking past the familiar single desk holding the Quantum OS 98 machine, Stella met them with a grin. "Got a surprise for you guys," she said, gesturing towards the pile of unopened boxes taking up a significant portion of the living room floor.

"More boxes?" Luna asked, looking curious. Leo wheeled closer, examining them. They looked like furniture boxes, maybe some electronics, but nothing obviously special.

"Not just boxes," Stella said, her excitement building. "Help me open them. Let's start with these flat ones."

Luna stood up, her own instrument forgotten, her face pale with worry. As Dave carefully took hold of Leo's wheelchair handles, Officer Whitehead arrived, nodding curtly to Stella. "Got my key," he said quietly, already pulling it out.

"Inside," Stella directed Dave, gesturing towards the locked access door near concessions that led into the school building. "Lobby."

Officer Whitehead quickly unlocked the door, holding it open. Stella put an arm around Luna. "Come on, Lu. With me."

Together, the small group moved quickly off the sideline and through the door, leaving the roar of the stadium behind. Officer Whitehead secured the door again. They navigated swiftly down the relatively quiet hallway – past the closed doors of the gymnasium, past the darkened cafeteria entrance where tables were stacked neatly against the wall – and continued straight towards the main lobby.

The lobby was blessedly empty and dim compared to the field. Officer Whitehead gestured towards a set of padded benches near the main entrance. Dave parked Leo's chair nearby. "Need anything else, Stella?" Dave asked quietly.

"No, thank you, Dave. We've got it," Stella replied gratefully. Dave nodded and headed back towards the field access door. Officer Whitehead stood back respectfully, giving the family space but remaining present as a calm, supportive authority figure.

Leo was still rocking slightly, his breathing ragged, hands covering his ears now against the phantom noise. Stella sat on the bench beside his chair, not crowding him, just being present. Luna sank onto the bench on his other side, resting a gentle hand on his arm, her own anxiety palpable but overridden by her need to support him. Officer Whitehead might offer a quiet, "Take your time, folks," or simply stand watchfully.

Slowly, gradually, in the quiet and dim light of the lobby, away from the overwhelming sensory input of the field, Leo's rocking lessened. His breathing deepened, becoming less ragged. He eventually lowered his hands from his ears, blinking, looking around the familiar lobby space as if reorienting himself. The peak of the meltdown had passed.

He looked down at his lap, his expression shifting from overwhelmed panic to deep sadness. He reached for his AAC device, which Luna quietly retrieved from his backpack for him. He slowly typed: *"Messed up song. Bad notes. Hurt."* He vaguely gestured towards his leg where the discomfort had flared.

Stella leaned closer, her voice soft. "Oh, honey. I know. It's okay about the notes. Everyone makes mistakes sometimes, especially during a performance with all those lights and people. And I'm sorry you were hurting too. That's a lot to handle all at once."

"Yeah, Leo," Luna added quickly, her voice thick with emotion but earnest. "The song doesn't matter! Really. It's just one part of one song. What's important is that you're okay now."

Leo looked from Luna to Stella, absorbing their reassurance. He gave a small, weary nod. The disappointment and lingering discomfort were still there, but their immediate acceptance and dismissal of the mistake seemed to help anchor him. Officer Whitehead offered a small, understanding smile from a distance. They would sit there for a few more minutes, gathering themselves, safe in the quiet lobby under Stella's care and the officer's watchful eye.

A short while later, after the band had marched off the field and the crowd noise swelled again for the start of the third quarter, the lobby door opened quietly. Mr. Thompson entered, carrying Luna's tenor saxophone, now safely secured in its case. He had noticed it left behind near the Pit area during the emergency exit and, knowing it was Luna's personal instrument purchased by Stella, had made sure one of the reliable Pit Crew parents carefully brought it off the field and cased it up once the performance concluded.

He approached the small group on the benches, his expression showing concern but also calm understanding. Officer Whitehead gave him a brief nod.

"Stella? Luna? Leo?" Mr. Thompson said softly. "Just wanted to check in. How are we doing?"

Stella looked up, managing a small, appreciative smile. "We're okay, Mr. T. Just needed a quiet minute. Leo's feeling much better now."

Mr. Thompson looked at Leo kindly. "Rough moment out there, huh, Leo? Don't you worry about those notes one bit. Happens to the best of us under pressure. The important thing is you're alright." Leo looked at his director and gave another small nod, seeming to appreciate the direct reassurance.

Mr. Thompson then placed the saxophone case gently on the bench beside Luna. "Figured you'd be wanting this back, Luna. Handled beautifully." Luna's eyes immediately fixed on the case, relief washing over her face, visibly relaxing her posture. That saxophone – *her* saxophone, the first truly significant thing that felt like it belonged just to her, bought by Stella – was safe. She reached out almost reverently, her fingers tracing the outline of the case. "Thank you, Mr. Thompson," she whispered, the words thick with emotion. Having it back, knowing it was cared for even amidst the emergency, felt like regaining a crucial piece of herself. Mr. Thompson smiled warmly, understanding the instrument's importance went beyond just the music. "Alright," he said, glancing between them. "Take all the time you need here. No rush to get back outside unless you feel up to it. Officer Whitehead and I will make sure you're undisturbed. Just let us know if you need anything at all." With another reassuring nod, he stepped back to confer quietly with Officer Whitehead near the hallway entrance, giving the family space while ensuring they weren't alone.
