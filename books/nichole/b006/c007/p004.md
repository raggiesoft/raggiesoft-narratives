# Part 4: The Bull Run

## Date: July 6, 2003 Time: 10:20 AM Location: Narraganset Esplanade Station (Level C - The Concourse)

Patrick and Nichole took the massive, glass elevator down from the sunlit plaza to the underground concourse.

When the elevator doors slid open on Level C, they were hit with a wall of sound and the overwhelming smell of frying food.

The concourse wasn’t just a ticket hall; it was a subterranean mall. A long row of fast-food stalls—burger joints, taco stands, and coffee shops—lined the main corridor, bustling with students grabbing mid-morning fuel. It was loud, chaotic, and completely flat.

Patrick pushed the Titan AeroMotion past the food court, his eyes scanning the architectural layout.

"Okay, this is great for lunch," Patrick said, steering around a group of students clustered near a bronze statue of a bull. "But what happens in February? Uncle Mark said Rhode Island gets Nor'easters. Snow drifts. Ice. If we're commuters, how do we get from the ferry to a classroom half a mile away without you turning into a popsicle?"

Nichole frowned. It was the one flaw in the plan. Virginia got cold, but it didn't get buried. Snow meant stuck wheels. Ice meant sliding.

As they reached the far end of the concourse, the crowd thinned out, funneling toward a massive set of double-wide automatic doors.

Above the doors was a large, illuminated green sign: THE BULL RUN: CAMPUS PEDESTRIAN NETWORK.

Next to the doors was a massive, color-coded map.

Patrick rolled Nichole up to the map. He stared at the colorful lines branching out from the station. The lines didn't represent bus routes. They represented underground tunnels connecting every single academic building, library, and dining hall on the entire BSU campus.

Patrick’s jaw dropped. He looked at the map, then at the doors, and then down at Nichole.

"Nikki," Patrick whispered, his engineering brain completely overloading. "They built a grid. Under the ground."

Nichole looked at the map. She tapped her screen. "NO. SNOW."

"No snow," Patrick confirmed, a massive grin breaking across his face. "No rain. No ice. It's a completely climate-controlled loop. You could roll from the ferry dock to a creative writing class in a blizzard wearing shorts."

He didn't wait. He pushed her toward the entrance.

The automatic doors slid open, and they rolled into The Bull Run.

It wasn't a dark, claustrophobic utility tunnel. It was twenty feet wide, brightly lit with recessed lighting, and paved in smooth, sealed concrete that offered absolutely zero resistance to Nichole’s wheels. The air was cool and filtered.

It felt like an airport terminal without the stress.

"Look at the ceiling," Patrick said, pointing up.

Hanging from the ceiling were street signs. They weren't just walking blindly; the tunnels were named after the surface streets directly above them. They were currently rolling down *Sub-Commerce Ave*.

Nichole looked at the wall. Every fifty feet, there was a sleek, modern distance marker bolted to the concrete.

\<-- Station Plaza: 500 ft (152 m) Library Commons: 1,200 ft (365 m) --\>

Patrick chuckled, tapping the sign. "Imperial and metric. They really did think of everything. It's universal design in every sense of the word."

Nichole made a soft, continuous *hummm* of pure satisfaction as they rolled.

Putney had offered a single dorm room with an empty space where her brother should be. Raritan State had offered massive outdoor distances.

BSU offered a subterranean highway built for wheels, protected from the elements, and mapped out to the exact meter.

"Next stop, the library," Patrick said, picking up the pace.

Nichole didn't just feel accommodated here. She felt prioritized. For the first time on the tour, the world wasn't just tolerating the Machine. It was built for it.
