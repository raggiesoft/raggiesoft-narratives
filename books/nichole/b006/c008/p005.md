# Part 6: The Visitor

## Date: September 28, 2003 Time: 2:15 PM Location: The Driveway, 95 West Main Road, Portsmouth, Rhode Island

The Sunday afternoon was quiet. Outside, the autumn air was turning crisp. Inside the master bathroom of the Bennett Wing, the atmosphere was purely mechanical.

"Okay, no water today," Uncle Mark said, standing by the massive white hydrotherapy tub. "Just a dry run for the hardware. I want to see how the ergonomics hold up."

Patrick parked the Titan AeroMotion parallel to the tub. He locked the wheel brakes.

"Side door open," Patrick announced. He unlatched the heavy, sealed acrylic door. It swung wide, revealing a molded bench that looked more like a loveseat than a bathtub.

Nichole watched him. She wasn't wearing a swimsuit; she was in her regular Sunday clothes—jeans and a Virginia Beach t-shirt.

"Ready, Nikki?" Patrick asked.

Nichole blinked twice. *Yes.*

Patrick didn't have to deadlift her. He didn't have to brace his knees against the porcelain rim. He simply put one arm under her legs, one behind her back, and slid her sideways off the chair cushion and directly onto the wide tub bench.

She landed softly against the contoured backrest.

"Okay," Patrick said, breathing easy. He stepped through the open door and sat down right next to her.

He fit perfectly. The bench was designed for two.

Patrick put his left arm around Nichole’s shoulders, drawing her close. Without the water, the tub was just a secure, high-walled chamber. Nichole let out a long, slow breath. She leaned her weight into Patrick, resting her head against his shoulder. Her spasticity, which usually kept her arms rigid and her neck tense, seemed to melt into the acrylic support.

For the first time all day, her hands stopped shaking.

Mark stood in the doorway, his arms crossed. He watched the tension drain out of his niece's face. In the three weeks since they had arrived in Rhode Island, she had been happy, excited, and determined. But right now? Sitting inside this fiberglass fortress, anchored against her twin brother?

She looked at peace.

"Well," Mark said quietly, feeling a lump rise in his throat. "I'd say the engineering is sound."

Nichole closed her eyes and smiled.

*Knock. Knock. Knock.*

The heavy thud of the front door echoed down the main hallway.

Patrick looked up, surprised. "We expecting company? The rest of the family isn't due up until next week."

Mark looked at his watch. 2:15 PM.

His expression instantly changed. The jovial, caring uncle vanished. His eyes went flat, and his jaw set.

"Transfer back," Mark said, his voice clipped and serious. "Get her in the chair. Go to the front window. Keep the lights off. Do not open the door."

Patrick didn't ask questions. He felt the shift in the room's energy. He slid Nichole back into the AeroMotion, strapped her chest harness, and wheeled her down the hall into the main living room.

Through the front window, they saw a sleek, heavy luxury sedan with tinted windows and New York plates parked in the driveway. A man in a tailored suit stepped out, carrying a leather attaché case.

Before the man could ring the bell again, the front door opened.

Uncle Mark stepped out onto the porch. He held a ceramic coffee mug. He didn't look surprised. He looked bored.

"Mr. Reynolds!" the man called out, putting on a dazzling smile. "A pleasure to finally meet you in person. I'm David Chen. From Global Oceanographic Solutions."

Patrick cracked the window open an inch.

"I know who you are, 'David'," Mark said. He didn't invite the man up the ramp. He leaned against the doorframe, taking a slow sip of coffee. "Or at least, I know the cover name your handlers assigned you. And I know who signs your paychecks. It isn't an oceanography firm."

The man stopped at the bottom of the ramp. His smile didn't falter, but his eyes hardened. "We’re just admirers of your work, Mark. Specifically, your recent paper on biological background noise filtering."

"That paper is public domain," Mark said flatly. "You can download it at the library."

"True," the man said, taking a step up the concrete ramp. "But we’re interested in the data you *didn't* publish. The removal of the mechanical artifacts. The Block III propulsion signatures."

"That's a proprietary algorithm," Mark said, his voice dropping to a low, dangerous rumble. "Owned by the United States Navy. Classified TS/SCI."

"Proprietary is just a word for 'underpaid,'" the man said smoothly. He patted his attaché case. "My clients in the East are looking to modernize their own fleet. They have a noise problem with their new reactors. They create a distinct... rattle. We believe you have the solution."

He reached into his jacket pocket and pulled out a thick envelope.

"Fifty thousand," he whispered. "Just for a consultation. A thumb drive with the source code gets you ten times that. Retiring in the Caribbean sounds better than Portsmouth, doesn't it?"

Mark looked at the envelope. He looked at the spy.

Mark took another sip of coffee.

"You know, 'David,'" Mark said conversationally. "You guys always make the same mistake. You think because I'm a contractor, I'm a mercenary. You think because I don't wear a uniform, I don't have a flag."

Mark set his coffee mug down on the porch railing.

"By the way," Mark added, checking his watch. "You're trespassing on a residence occupied by a holder of Top Secret clearance. Soliciting classified defense information is a federal felony under the Espionage Act. Title 18, Section 793."

The man laughed, shaking his head. "Please. It’s just two men talking. There’s no wire. No witnesses."

"I don't need a wire," Mark grinned, pointing over the man’s shoulder toward the street. "I have a landscaper."

The man turned around.

A nondescript green landscaping van with "Newport Tree Service" painted on the side—which had been parked down the street for twenty minutes—suddenly roared to life. The side doors slid open. Four men in FBI windbreakers and tactical gear stepped out, weapons drawn but low.

A second car—an unmarked, standard-issue government sedan—screeched into the driveway, blocking the luxury car in.

"FBI! Show me your hands! Do not move!"

The man’s face went white. He dropped the attaché case.

Mark didn't flinch. "Special Agent Miller!" Mark called out to the lead FBI agent. "He offered fifty grand for the Block III spectral keys. It's in the envelope. Make sure you book him for attempted bribery, too."

Agent Miller nodded to Mark as his team cuffed the man against the hood of the luxury car. "Thanks for the tip-off, Mr. Reynolds. We've been tracking this guy's alias since Boston. We appreciate you keeping him talking."

"Always happy to help Uncle Sam take out the trash," Mark said, picking up his coffee mug.

As the FBI shoved the handcuffed spy into the back of the unmarked sedan and began searching his vehicle, Mark turned around and looked at the window where Patrick and Nichole were watching, wide-eyed.

Mark winked. He walked back inside the house, locking the door behind him.

Patrick turned the wheelchair around as Mark walked down the hallway.

"Uncle Mark," Patrick stammered. "You just... the FBI... was that a spy?"

Mark stopped. He looked at the twins. The playful uncle vibe was gone, replaced by the steely resolve of a man who knew exactly where the monsters lived.

"That," Mark said quietly, "was a bad actor. He thought he could buy the keys to the castle."

Nichole looked at her uncle. Five minutes ago, she was resting her head on Patrick's shoulder in the bathtub. Now, federal agents were arresting a foreign operative in her front yard.

She tapped her screen.

"YOU. SET. A. TRAP."

"I knew he was coming three days ago," Mark admitted. "I called the Providence Field Office on Thursday. We arranged the meet. Never let a threat get close to your perimeter without a plan."

He looked at the mini-apartment door.

"That's lesson one of the Gap Year, kids," Mark said, gesturing to their apartment. "Protect the Machine. At all costs. Nobody gets in unless they're cleared."

He pointed a thumb upstairs.

"I'm going back to work. Red light is still on."

Mark turned and vanished up the stairs to his SCIF.

Patrick looked out the window. The FBI was towing the luxury car away. The driveway was empty again.

"He's not just a consultant," Patrick whispered to Nichole.

Nichole shook her head. She clicked her mouse, her new voice filled with absolute awe.

"HE. IS. THE. FIREWALL."
