# Part 2: The Portsmouth Project

## Date: August 12, 2003 Time: 7:00 PM Location: The Kitchen, 1850 Delaney Street, Virginia Beach

The phone on the kitchen wall rang. Ellen picked it up, hearing the distinctive *beep* of a three-way conference call connection.

"We closed, El! The deed is recorded!" Uncle Mark’s booming voice filled the receiver.

"I'm on the line too! He actually did it!" Aunt Carol chimed in from New Jersey, her voice buzzing with excitement.

Ellen put the phone on speaker and set it on the kitchen table, where Tom, Patrick, and Nichole were eating dinner.

"You bought it?" Tom asked, a huge smile breaking across his face.

"Free and clear," Mark confirmed from three hundred miles away. "I’m standing in the living room right now. 95 West Main Road. Portsmouth, Rhode Island. And the best part? Zero steps at the front door."

Nichole made a high-pitched, excited *keening* sound. *Zero steps.*

"And it's on West Main," Mark continued proudly. "The OSTA Route 60 runs right past the driveway, seven days a week. It’s a straight shot to the ferry dock."

"Tell them about the layout, Mark," Carol urged.

"Right," Mark said. "It’s a big 1960s brick build. Three levels. I'm taking the second floor, so I'm out of your hair. The first floor is all common living space, complete with a new roll-in shower off the main hallway for quick mornings. But the real magic is the master wing."

Mark lowered his voice, adopting the tone of a project manager.

"That wing is going to be the 'Bennett Mini-Apartment.' I know you guys have your hearts set on that Foundry Lofts place in Braxton next year—the one on top of the Blue Line. This is your practice run. The wing has its own bedroom, a small living room, a study area for your desks, and its own kitchenette. You get to practice living totally independent, but if anything goes wrong, your landlord is right upstairs."

Patrick stopped chewing. A kitchenette. A study. He looked at Nichole. It wasn't just a room; it was a simulation of their future.

"And the bathroom inside the mini-apartment?" Mark continued. "Total gut job. We're ripping the doors out to thirty-six inches minimum. And we're installing a custom walk-in hydrotherapy tub. Sealed side door, Pat. You roll her in, shut the door, and fill it up. The bench is double-wide. Enough room for two, so you can both soak the CP out of your muscles."

Nichole closed her eyes, letting out a soft, continuous *hummm*. The tub was her sanctuary. A place where the twin nervous systems could reset.

"Now, the lease," Mark said, shifting to business. "My lawyer is drawing it up. I’m the landlord; the twins are the tenants. The rent will be set at exactly one dollar per person, per year. We sign it the second you drop them off. It creates a paper trail for 'consideration,' establishing legal domicile to start the clock for Rhode Island in-state tuition."

"That is... incredibly generous," Tom said, stunned.

"And," Mark interrupted, his voice dropping into a conspiratorial whisper. "I told the contractor to leave a structural gap in the main hallway. Between the basement, the first floor, and the second floor. I don't know what the market looks like yet, but there has to be such a thing as a residential elevator. I'm going to research it. Because Nikki should be able to get to every inch of this house."

Nichole’s eyes went wide. She tapped her screen. "ELEVATOR?"

"Someday, Nikki," Mark laughed. "For now, I'm staying month-to-month at my attic apartment in Newport. By September first, the dust will be cleared and the Portsmouth house will be fully operational."

"It's perfect," Ellen said, wiping her eyes.

"It gets better, El," Carol chimed in, her voice softening. "Mark invited me. The house has a massive, unfinished basement. Dry, high ceilings. He told me I could lease it for a dollar a year, too."

The kitchen went silent.

"Come home, El," Mark said softly through the speaker. "You and Tom. Carol’s in. I want the clan back. The twins get the first floor, but they're gonna need a village. And we all grew up on this island. Why are we spread out all over the East Coast?"

Ellen looked at Tom. The empty nest was looming over them. Virginia Beach was fine, but it wasn't *home*.

Tom looked at the phone. He thought of his own mother, still living in Newport, whose health was starting a slow, painful decline. The guilt of being ten hours away had been eating at him for months.

Tom reached across the table and took Ellen's hand. He squeezed it tight.

"My mom's not doing so great, Mark," Tom said, his voice thick with emotion. "I need to be closer to her."

"Then it's settled," Mark said, his voice regaining its boom. "The family reunites. One dollar a head."

Nichole looked at her parents. She tapped her screen.

"DO. IT."

"We're in," Tom said, smiling at his daughter. "Let's get the twins up there for September. We'll pack the Virginia house and follow right behind them."

"Copy that," Mark said triumphantly. "West Main Road is waiting for you."
