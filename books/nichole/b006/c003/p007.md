# Part 7: The Magic Carpet

## Location: The Sciences Quad to Webb University Center Time: 2:30 PM

"Back down the ramp, folks!" Megan chirped. "Watch your step, and watch your wheels."

They exited the Mills Godwin Building the same way they came in, retracing their path down the long concrete switchback. Patrick controlled the descent carefully, using his body weight to keep the heavy chair from picking up too much speed.

Once they hit the sidewalk, Megan took a sharp left.

"We are now entering the Engineering and Science corridor," she narrated, walking backwards with practiced ease.

To their left sat the low, domed roof of the Planetarium.

"That’s the Planetarium," Megan pointed. "Great for astronomy classes, and they do laser light shows on Friday nights. It’s a cheap date."

Next to it was the Chemistry Building—an older brick structure with fume hoods visible in the windows.

To their right loomed the Engineering Systems Building. It was utilitarian, blocky, and serious.

"Civil and Environmental Engineering over there," Megan noted. "They build the bridges."

But Patrick wasn't looking at the brick buildings. His eyes were locked on what was rising above them.

Running straight through the heart of the campus, cutting between the buildings like a spine, was a massive, elevated concrete track. It sat on thick pillars, about twenty feet in the air.

"And this," Megan said, stopping dramatically in the shadow of the guideway, "is the future."

## The Maglev

They arrived at the construction site for the Webb University Center Station. The station itself was a shell of concrete and steel, still surrounded by orange construction fencing and "DANGER: HARD HAT AREA" signs. But the track was continuous.

"This is the VDU Maglev," Megan announced, her voice echoing slightly off the concrete pillars. "We are the first university in the country—actually, the first in the world—to have a privately funded student transportation system running on magnetic levitation."

Patrick stopped the chair. He looked up.

"Induction motors," Patrick whispered, geeking out. "Zero friction. No wheels. Just magnets."

"Exactly!" Megan said, hearing him. "It connects the deepest part of campus—Powhatan Avenue—all the way to the 43rd Street corridor. It runs silently, it runs smoothly, and it’s fast."

She pointed to the station platform above them, accessible by a dedicated elevator shaft that was currently wrapped in white construction sheathing.

"This station right here serves the Student Union. So, imagine this: You finish your Chem lab," she pointed to the Chemistry building, "you walk—or roll—right here, you hop on the elevator, and ninety seconds later, you are floating to your dorm or the parking garage."

"When?" Tom asked, shading his eyes to look at the track.

"They're running final tests now," Megan said confidently. "Grand Opening is scheduled for this Fall Semester. So if you guys enroll for next year, you’ll be the inaugural class of commuters."

## The Vision

Nichole stared at the track.

For a walking student, the Maglev was a cool novelty. A way to save ten minutes of walking in the rain.

For her, it was a revolution.

The Norfolk sidewalks were old. They were buckled by tree roots. They had cracks that jarred her spine and set off her spasms. Getting across a campus this big by pushing was a physical marathon for Patrick and an endurance test for her.

But up there? On the rail?

Smooth.

She imagined gliding above the chaos. No curbs. No puddles. No cars cutting them off. Just a magnetic wave carrying her from Class A to Class B.

"It's perfect," Patrick murmured, analyzing the engineering. "It solves the transit problem. We could get an apartment near the Powhatan station, ride the Maglev to the center of campus, and never have to load the van."

"It's like the monorail at a theme park," Ellen marveled. "But for school."

Megan beamed. "It really is. VDU is putting itself on the map with this. We aren't just a state school; we're an innovation hub."

Patrick looked at Nichole. He pointed to the concrete track soaring above them.

"That's our ride, Nikki," he said. "That's the Magic Carpet."

Nichole looked at the station. She could see herself there. Autumn. Leaves falling. Rolling onto the train. Going to get coffee.

She tapped her armrest.

FAST. TRACK.

"Yeah," Patrick grinned. "Fall 2003. We'll be riding the magnet."
