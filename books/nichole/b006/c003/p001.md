# Part 1: The Thirteen-Hour Problem

## Date: June 18, 2003 Time: 10:00 AM Location: 1850 Delaney Street, Virginia Beach, VA

The Fortress was quiet.

After the chaotic retreat from the mountains yesterday, the house on Delaney Street felt like a decompression chamber. The familiar hum of the central air, the smell of their own coffee, and the sight of the specially modified hallway gave them space to breathe.

But the kitchen table was a war zone.

Ellen had the Putney College prospectus spread out next to a large road atlas. The color-coded spreadsheet was there, too, with Roosevelt Rehabilitation Center crossed out in ink so thick it had torn the paper.

"Okay," Ellen said, rubbing her temples. "Virginia is officially a bust. The private business schools are predatory, the state rehab center is a prison, and the community colleges don't have dorms. That leaves the out-of-state list."

Tom stood at the counter, refilling his coffee mug. "Putney College. The open house is next Saturday."

Ellen traced a line on the map with her finger. "Vermont. I’ve been looking at the route. If we want to avoid the massive toll bridges and the metropolitan gridlock of D.C. and New York, we have to swing west. It’s going to be a thirteen-hour drive. Minimum."

Nichole was parked at the table, her tray elevated to clear the edge. She stared at the map. Thirteen hours in the van. The thought made her tailbone ache in sympathy.

"It's a specialized campus, though," Ellen said, trying to inject optimism into her voice as she tapped the glossy brochure. "It's built for accessibility. It says right here: 'Integrated Personal Care Management.'"

"But what does that mean?" Patrick asked. He was sitting next to Nichole, dissecting an orange. "Are they going to do the same thing Roosevelt did? Separate the genders and push the 'independent living' stuff where they force you to use a random aide?"

"It's possible," Ellen sighed. "It's the standard model for these specialty colleges. There's another one down in Florida—Sunstate College—and a big state school out in Ohio that has underground tunnels so students never have to push through the snow. But they all use that same attendant-care model. They think the family is a crutch."

"We're not a crutch," Patrick said, his voice hard. He placed a slice of orange on Nichole’s tray. "We're a machine."

Nichole looked at the orange slice. She didn't eat it. She looked at the brochure for Putney.

Ohio. Vermont. Florida. Thirteen hours in a van just to go to another segregated campus where strangers would touch her. Strangers who didn't know her signals. Strangers who didn't know that she needed her left leg elevated exactly 15 degrees to stop the spasms.

She only trusted Patrick. He was the only one who knew the code.

She reached for her VocaLink. The screen glowed blue in the morning light.

TIRED.

"I know, honey," Ellen said gently. "We'll get a good week of rest here before we pack up for the crusade to Vermont."

Nichole shook her head. That wasn't it.

NO. MORE. LABS.

Patrick stopped peeling the orange. He looked at the screen. "Labs?"

Nichole clicked her tongue—a sharp, frustrated sound. She tapped the keyboard again.

WANT. NORMAL.

"Normal," Patrick repeated. He looked out the sliding glass door at the backyard. The grass needed mowing. It was a normal suburban chore.

"We've been looking at this backwards," Patrick said, turning to his parents. "We keep looking for schools that cater to disabilities, thinking they'll understand us. But they don't. They treat her like a lab experiment. They have rigid protocols and liability lawyers who are terrified of lawsuits."

"So what do we do?" Tom asked, leaning against the counter. "Cancel Vermont?"

"No, we go to Vermont," Patrick said. "We give it a shot. Putney claims they are different. We test that hypothesis. But if it's just another version of what we saw yesterday... then we look at a normal school."

"What normal school?" Ellen asked. "Most campuses are a nightmare for access."

"A massive, regular old state school," Patrick said. "One that's so big, they don't have the time to micromanage our living arrangements. One that's close to home. Like Virginia Dominion."

## Virginia Dominion University (VDU)

It was the local giant. Located in Norfolk, a mere twenty-minute drive from their driveway. It was a sprawling, urban campus with Division I sports, rowdy fraternities, and a massive English department. It wasn't "special." It was the definition of average, chaotic college life.

"VDU?" Ellen asked, her maternal anxiety spiking. "Patrick, their dorms are traditional. Shared bathrooms, tiny rooms, no elevators in half the freshman housing..."

"We wouldn't live in the dorms," Patrick realized, the idea locking into place. "We're eighteen. We could rent an apartment right off campus. Just the two of us."

Nichole’s head snapped up.

"Think about it," Patrick continued, speaking faster now. "We wouldn't have to ask a Dean's permission to share a room. We wouldn't have to deal with aides, or curfews, or single-gender hallways. We pay rent. We go to class. We come home. Just... normal students."

"And the infrastructure is changing," Tom added, nodding slowly. "They're building that Maglev system. It’s supposed to open this fall."

Patrick’s eyes lit up. "Exactly. It’s a magnetic levitation train. It runs on an elevated track right through the center of campus. It connects the Engineering Building, the Library, and the Dorms. No bumps. No traffic. You just roll onto the platform, ride the magnet, and roll off."

"It's perfect engineering," Patrick said, looking at Nichole. "It's smooth. It's fast. It solves the cross-campus transit problem without needing a specialized van service."

Nichole pictured it. Floating above the campus on a magnetic wave. Zipping from English Lit to the Student Union without fighting curbs or potholes.

An apartment. With Patrick. Riding a futuristic train to class. No nurses. No "levels." No "intake forms." Just going to a football game on Saturday. Just rolling into English 101 and handing in a paper without someone congratulating her for holding the pen.

She tapped her armrest rapidly. Yes. Yes. Yes.

NORMAL. GIRL.

Tom smiled, taking a sip of his coffee. He looked at the map of Vermont, then at the siblings sitting at the table.

"Okay," Tom said. "That is a hell of a Plan B. An apartment near VDU and a season pass for the Maglev."

He walked over and tapped the Putney brochure.

"But we still have the reservation in Brattleboro. Let's see how Vermont goes first. If Putney tries to separate you two, or if they try to assign a stranger to manage Nichole... we come home, and we start looking at lease agreements on Colley Avenue."

Nichole settled back in her chair. For the first time since the tour began, she didn't feel like a problem to be solved. She felt like a girl with options.

She looked at the Putney brochure.

Okay, Vermont, she thought. You have one chance. Beat "Normal."
