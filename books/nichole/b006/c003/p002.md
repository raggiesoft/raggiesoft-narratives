# Part 2: Home Field Advantage

## Date: June 19, 2003 Time: 1:00 PM Location: En route to Virginia Dominion University, Norfolk, VA

The drive to Virginia Dominion University didn't require a map. It didn't require a navigation spreadsheet. It was written into the Bennetts' muscle memory.

They merged off I-264 at Exit 11B, the familiar concrete ramp depositing them onto Brambleton Avenue.

"This feels weird," Patrick commented from the back seat, adjusting his sunglasses. "We're going on a college tour, but we're ten minutes from home."

"That's the point," Tom said, cruising past the sparkling water of The Hague.

They crossed the bridge near the NOAA building, its white facade reflecting the harsh summer sun. Brambleton curved sharply to the right, wrapping around the massive brick complex of Tidewater General Hospital.

"If you get sick," Ellen noted, pointing at the ER entrance, "you are literally three miles from your doctor."

The road straightened out and became Hampton Boulevard. The vibe shifted instantly as they entered Ghent. The speed limit dropped to a polite 30 MPH. They drove down the two-lane road, separated by a double yellow line, passing the stately, older apartment buildings with their ivy-covered brick and wrought-iron balconies.

"I remember this," Nichole thought. This was the route to summer computer camp. It wasn't a scary drive into the unknown; it was just Tuesday.

They dipped under the railroad underpass—the unofficial gateway to the university district. The road widened, the lanes expanding as the city gave way to the campus.

From 25th Street to 41st Street, the landscape changed. Fast food joints, bookstores, and students on bicycles replaced the historic apartments.

"Here we go," Tom said.

He made a sharp right onto 43rd Street.

To their right loomed the 43rd Street Parking Garage, a massive concrete hive. To their left was the crown jewel of the campus expansion: The Vanguard Convocation Center. It was a gleaming modern arena, glass and steel flashing in the sun, hosting everything from basketball games to graduation ceremonies.

Tom pulled the van into the garage entrance. The clearance bar read 8' 2".

"Plenty of room," Tom noted. He swiped his credit card and parked on the ground level, right next to the elevator.

## The Arrival

Patrick unloaded Nichole. The air in the parking garage was hot and smelled of car exhaust and river water—the signature scent of Norfolk in June.

"Okay," Patrick said, locking her footplates. "Let's go see if 'Normal' works."

They rolled out of the garage and crossed the street toward the Welcome Center, which was nestled into the side of the Vanguard Arena.

The sidewalk was wide. The curb cuts were flush—perfectly graded. Patrick didn't have to pop the front casters; the chair glided up the ramp without a bump.

"Smooth," Patrick murmured.

They weren't the only ones there. A small crowd had gathered near the glass doors of the Welcome Center. There were parents fanning themselves with brochures, and teenagers looking awkwardly at their shoes.

A young woman in a bright blue polo shirt with "VDU AMBASSADOR" embroidered on the chest spotted them. She didn't look at the wheelchair with panic. She didn't look for an aide. She just smiled and waved her clipboard.

"Welcome to VDU!" she chirped, holding the door open for a family ahead of them. "Tour starts in five minutes! Come on in to the AC!"

Patrick looked at Nichole.

"No intake form," he whispered.

Nichole tapped her armrest.

GOOD. START.

They joined the group, rolling into the cool air of the lobby, just another set of prospective students checking out the local giant.
