# Part 2: The Commuter Special

## Date: June 16, 2003 Time: 10:30 AM Location: Atlantic Coast Polytechnic (Greenwich Road)

The drive from Glenrock Mall to Greenwich Road took exactly five minutes.

Tom pulled the van into an office park shaded by old pine trees. It was clean, quiet, and corporate. They parked in front of a six-story glass and concrete building that looked exactly like the accounting firm Tom used for his taxes.

"Atlantic Coast Poly," Patrick read the directory plaque near the entrance. "Floors five and six."

Inside, the first-floor lobby was deserted. There was no front desk, no student union, and no campus map. There was just a directory board and a bank of two elevators.

Patrick pushed the Titan AeroMotion into the left elevator. It was a tight fit with all four of them, but the ride up was smooth. The doors opened on the fifth floor to a bustling, well-lit reception area. Students were walking around, but they weren't wearing the standard college uniform of pajama pants and oversized hoodies. They were wearing business casual. They carried briefcases and leather portfolios.

They were met immediately by the Director of Admissions, a woman named Ms. Carter. She shook Tom and Ellen's hands, then stepped around the desk to shake Patrick’s hand and directly address Nichole.

"Nichole, Patrick, welcome," Ms. Carter said warmly. "We're so glad you're here. Let's head back to my office."

Patrick steered the chair down the carpeted hallway. Already, this felt different than TechStream. Ms. Carter didn't look at Tom’s watch. She didn't try to hand them a free laptop.

Inside her office, Ms. Carter sat down and pulled up their transcripts on her computer.

"Excellent grades," she noted, looking at Nichole. "And your writing samples are fantastic. Now, Atlantic Coast Poly is a little different than a traditional school. We are SCHEV certified to operate, and we run on a five-week accelerated term. You take two classes at a time, hyper-focused, and then move on. You can get your Associate's degree, or even an accelerated Bachelor's, in just two and a half years."

"That sounds intense," Ellen said. "How does the school handle accommodations? Do you have a Disability Services Office?"

"We don't have a dedicated standalone office," Ms. Carter admitted frankly. "Because of our size, it falls under the Student Services umbrella. But we are extremely proactive. For instance, knowing Nichole uses a wheelchair, our scheduler would ensure that all of her classes for the term are on the same floor—say, the sixth floor. That way, Patrick, you wouldn't have to wait for the elevator between periods to get her to her next class on time."

Nichole looked at Patrick. It was a thoughtful accommodation. It wasn't predatory. It was practical.

"What about tuition?" Tom asked, his internal calculator always running.

Ms. Carter handed them a printed sheet. "We are a private institution, so it is an investment. The Bachelor's program runs about forty thousand dollars for the full two and a half years."

Tom's eyebrows shot up. "Forty? Community College of Hampton Roads is a fraction of that. Even Virginia Dominion University is cheaper per semester for in-state students."

"True," Ms. Carter nodded, unoffended. "But VDU will take you four to five years. We get you into the workforce in half the time. We are career-focused."

Patrick picked up the brochure. He flipped to the back, looking at the fine print.

"Accredited by the ACICS," Patrick read. He looked up. "Is that the same accreditation as VDU?"

Ms. Carter hesitated for a microsecond. "It is a national accreditation, yes. VDU uses a regional accrediting body. They are different systems."

"So, if we did two years here and wanted to transfer to Virginia Dominion for a Master's degree... would the credits transfer?" Patrick asked.

"Regional colleges like VDU typically do not accept nationally accredited credits," Ms. Carter said gently.

Patrick put the brochure down. It was the same trap as TechStream, just wearing a nicer suit.

Patrick walked over to the office window, looking down at the Greenwich Road traffic five stories below. Nichole rolled up next to him, looking out at the parking lot.

"Where are the dorms?" Patrick asked, looking out at the asphalt. "And the library?"

"Oh, we're a commuter campus," Ms. Carter said. "Most of our students work full-time. We don't have residence halls. You live close enough to commute daily, right? Patrick, you have your license?"

"I do," Patrick said. He looked down at Nichole.

They had been sharing a bedroom for eighteen years. They had been fighting the school system for twelve. They were tired of the grind.

They didn't want a "commuter campus." They didn't want to drive to an office building, take an elevator, sit in a room for four hours, and drive home to their childhood beds. They wanted independence. They wanted a quad. They wanted late-night pizza in a dorm room and the terrifying, wonderful freedom of living away from Mom and Dad.

They wanted the *authentic* experience.

Nichole tapped Patrick's arm. He looked down. She gave a small, definitive shake of her head.

"It's a really great program, Ms. Carter," Patrick said, turning back to the room. "And we appreciate the thought you put into the schedule. But... we want the four-year experience. We want to live on campus."

Ms. Carter smiled, understanding immediately. "You want to be college kids."

"Yeah," Patrick grinned. "We do."

"Well, you can't get that on the sixth floor of an office building," Ms. Carter laughed, standing up to shake their hands again. "I wish you both the absolute best of luck."

As they took the elevator back down to the empty first-floor lobby, Tom folded up the ACP brochure.

"Respectful," Tom summarized as the elevator hummed. "But way too expensive for credits that don't transfer."

"And no campus," Patrick added, pushing the Titan out into the lobby. "Cross it off the list."

Ellen pulled out her color-coded spreadsheet as they walked back to the van. "Well. That leaves the big ones out of town. Roosevelt Rehab in Fishersville, and Putney College up in Vermont."

Nichole tapped her VocaLink screen.

"ROAD. TRIP."
