# Part 7: The Intersection

## Location: Parking Lot, Roosevelt Rehabilitation Center Time: 1:55 PM

Patrick finished locking down the wheelchair tie-downs in the back of the van. His hands were shaking slightly—not from palsy, but from the sheer, vibrating rage of being told his future was scooping potatoes.

He sat down on the bench seat next to Nichole and slammed the sliding door shut, sealing them inside the safety of the family vehicle.

In the front seat, Ellen was already dialing. She held the phone up, her finger hovering over the "Speaker" button.

"I need a sanity check," Ellen murmured. "I need to know if I'm the crazy one, or if that man was actually as horrible as I think he was."

The line rang twice.

"Hello?" A warm, familiar voice filled the van.

"Sherry?" Ellen said, her voice tight. "It's Ellen. I'm putting you on speaker. Tom and the kids are here."

"Ellen!" Sherry’s voice brightened instantly. "Where are you? Did you survive the 'Roosevelt Experience'?"

Tom put the van in gear. He pulled out of the parking spot, driving past the sign that read BIRDSALL-HOOVER BUILDING: MEDICAL REHABILITATION.

"We survived," Ellen said, watching the brick fortress slide past the window. "But... Sherry, it was a disaster. They didn't look at their transcripts. They didn't look at the portfolio. They looked at the wheelchair and offered Nichole a job as a Door Greeter at ValuMart."

"Excuse me?" Sherry’s voice dropped an octave. "A greeter? For the girl who wrote that sci-fi novella last Christmas?"

"And Patrick," Ellen continued, her voice trembling with anger. "Food Service. Scoping potatoes in a cafeteria. Because they said his 'keyboard stamina' might be an issue."

"Oh, hell no," Sherry snapped. "I've seen that boy type. He types faster with one hand than I do with two. That is insulting. That is... that is state-sanctioned ableism, is what that is."

Tom turned the van onto Route 358, heading away from the complex. The road was straight, lined with the same functional, uninspiring architecture as the center.

"They wanted to separate them, Sherry," Ellen said, looking back at the twins. "Strict segregation. No visitation. They called their relationship a 'dependency' that needed to be broken."

"You get out of there," Sherry commanded. "You get out of there right now."

"We're leaving," Tom said, his eyes fixed on the traffic light ahead.

They were approaching the intersection with US-250. This was the decision point.

Tom clicked his turn signal. Left.

To the left lay Mule Academy Road. That road led to Interstate 64 East. It led back to Afton Mountain, back to the humidity of Tidewater, back to the safety of their living room where they could lick their wounds and give up on the road trip.

Left was retreat. Left was going home defeated.

"Tom, where are you going?" Sherry asked through the speakerphone.

"Heading toward I-64," Tom said heavily. "I think we just need to get them home."

"Don't you dare," Sherry said. "You are twenty minutes away from me. You are not dragging those kids back home feeling like garbage. You are coming to Annex. I have a guest room, I have a ramp, and I just pulled a cherry pie out of the oven."

Tom looked at the blinking green arrow for the left turn.

"Sherry, we don't want to impose—" Ellen started.

"Ellen Bennett, if you drive past me, I will never forgive you," Sherry interrupted. "Those kids need to see a friendly face. They need to remember that not everyone in this valley is a bureaucratic moron. Come decompress. Sit on the porch. breathe."

Tom stared at the intersection. The left turn signal clicked. *Tick-tock. Tick-tock.* Go home. Give up.

Then, he looked in the rearview mirror. He saw Patrick and Nichole. They looked exhausted. They looked small. If he took them home now, this failure would sit on them for weeks.

"Pie?" Tom asked.

"Warm," Sherry promised. "And ice cream."

Tom’s hand moved to the stalk. He flicked the signal up.

Right.

"We're coming," Tom said.

He swung the heavy van Right onto US-250 West.

They turned their backs on Mule Academy Road. They turned their backs on the retreat to Norfolk.

The van accelerated, heading toward Staunton, toward the junction with I-81 North, and toward the sanctuary of Annex.

"Good," Sherry said, hearing the engine rev. "Drive safe. I'll put the coffee on."

The line clicked dead.

Ellen let out a long breath she seemed to have been holding since noon. She looked at Tom.

"Thank you," she whispered.

Tom nodded. "We aren't going home on a loss. We regroup. Then we hit Vermont."

In the back seat, Nichole watched the mountains get closer. They weren't leaving the fight. They were just moving to a better base of operations.

She tapped her armrest.

*PIE. IS. GOOD.*

Patrick smiled—a real smile this time. "Yeah. Pie is good strategy."
