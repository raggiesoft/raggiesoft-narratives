# Part 1: The Valley of Hope

## Date: June 17, 2003 Time: 11:30 AM Location: Interstate 64 West, Afton Mountain

The drive was a seduction.

As Tom piloted the van west away from the flat, humid congestion of the Tidewater area, the landscape began to heave and roll. They climbed Afton Mountain, the engine humming a deep baritone as they ascended into the blue haze of the Appalachians.

Then came the crest.

As they tipped over the summit and began the long, steep descent into the Shenandoah Valley, the view was breathtaking. It was a physical release—like a belt being loosened. The humidity of the coast was gone, replaced by a massive bowl of emerald green that stretched out before them, dotted with silos that glinted like silver pins and framed by the rolling spine of the Blue Ridge.

They flew past Exit 96, the Delphine Avenue sign signaling the edge of Waynesboro. The air rushing through the vents felt different here—lighter, crisp, smelling of pine needles and damp earth rather than salt and swamp.

"Look at that," Ellen said, taking a deep breath as they passed the US-340 interchange. "It feels... healthy here. Clean. Like you can actually think."

Nichole looked out the window as the valley floor opened up. She saw the cows grazing on the hillsides at impossible angles. She saw the shadow of clouds drifting across the fields. It looked like a painting.

For a moment, passing the inviting signs for Waynesboro, she let herself believe the seduction. She let herself believe that the "Rehabilitation Center" nestled in this valley would be like the landscape: open, peaceful, and free.

She imagined a campus where she could sit under a tree and write poetry about the mountains. She imagined Patrick pushing her down a country lane, the wheels crunching on gravel, no concrete walls in sight.

Then, the green signage appeared on the right. Exit 91. Fishersville.

Tom clicked the blinker. The van slowed, shedding the freedom of the open highway, and turned onto the off-ramp toward Route 285. The seduction was over. The reality was waiting at the bottom of the hill.
