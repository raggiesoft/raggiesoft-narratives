# Part 13: The Morning Report

## Location: The Living Room / Kitchen, 168 Hermitage Road Time: 8:45 AM

The sun was already high, cutting through the sheer curtains and warming the dust motes dancing in the air.

Sherry came down the stairs first, tying the sash of her robe. She moved quietly, intending to check on the "refugees," but stopped at the bottom step.

The sofa bed was a tangle of flannel sheets and limbs. Patrick and Nichole were still deeply asleep, their breathing heavy and syncopated. They looked exhausted—dark circles visible even in sleep, faces slack with the kind of fatigue that comes from emotional wringers.

But Barnaby was awake.

The orange cat was sitting tall on the armrest of the sofa, his tail wrapped neatly around his paws. He looked like a gargoyle guarding a cathedral.

When he saw Sherry, he didn't jump down for food. He looked her dead in the eye and let out a specific sound.

*Mrrr-ow-row.*

It wasn't a "feed me" meow. It was low, guttural, and serious. It was the feline equivalent of an Incident Report.

Sherry walked over, keeping her voice to a whisper. "Rough shift, Barnaby?"

*Mrrrp.* He blinked slowly, then looked down at Nichole’s sleeping form. He nudged her hair with his nose, then looked back at Sherry.

"I hear you," Sherry whispered, scratching him behind the ears. "She cried, didn't she?"

Barnaby purred, a short, confirming rumble, then finally hopped down from his post. His watch was over.

Tom and Ellen came down a moment later, carrying their overnight bags. They stopped next to Sherry, looking at their children.

"They look wiped out," Ellen whispered, noting the grip Patrick still had on the back of Nichole’s t-shirt.

"Barnaby gave me the report," Sherry said softly, nodding toward the cat who was now heading to the kitchen. "Sounds like it was a three-hanky night. He stayed on her head until dawn."

Ellen looked at the cat, then at her daughter. "Good kitty," she murmured.

Sherry clapped her hands together—softly, but with purpose. "Right. Emotional hangovers require grease and carbohydrates. I'm starting the bacon. Don't you wake them until you smell the coffee."

## The Reboot

It took another twenty minutes for the smell of frying hickory-smoked bacon to penetrate the fortress of the sofa bed.

Patrick stirred first. He groaned, the stiffness in his left leg announcing itself immediately. He blinked open his eyes, disoriented by the sun and the unfamiliar ceiling.

*Farm. Sherry’s. Safe.*

He looked at Nichole. She was awake, but barely. Her eyes were puffy, and she was staring at the ceiling with the glazed look of someone whose brain was still buffering.

"Morning," Patrick croaked. His voice was gravel.

Nichole blinked slowly. She tapped his arm once. *Coffee.*

"Yeah," Patrick agreed, sitting up and running a hand through his messy hair. "Coffee. Then food. Then we get out of here."

The logistics of the morning were slower than usual. Patrick swung his legs out of bed, testing his balance. He grabbed the wheelchair from where it was parked and brought it to the edge of the sofa.

"Pit stop first," Patrick said.

He scooped Nichole up—the lift felt heavier this morning, his own muscles fatigued from the stress—and carried her into the small powder room.

They closed the door. It was a tight squeeze.

"Cold water," Patrick muttered, turning on the tap.

He splashed cold water on his face, then wet a washcloth for Nichole. She pressed the cool fabric against her swollen eyes, sighing. They changed back into yesterday's clothes—wrinkled jeans and t-shirts that smelled like the van—moving with the silent, sluggish coordination of two people sharing a single, tired brain.

Patrick brushed her hair, trying to smooth out the bedhead with his fingers since they didn't have a brush.

"Good enough for government work," Patrick muttered, looking at their reflection in the small mirror. They looked like they’d been in a fight. Which, in a way, they had.

## The Fueling Station

When they emerged from the bathroom, the kitchen table was transformed.

Sherry wasn't messing around. There was a platter of scrambled eggs, a mountain of bacon, a bowl of grits with cheese melting on top, and a stack of toast thick enough to stop a door.

"There they are," Sherry boomed, turning from the stove with a coffee pot in hand. "Sit. Eat. No talking until the caffeine hits the bloodstream."

Patrick rolled Nichole to the table. He sat down heavily.

Tom passed the juice. Ellen passed the bacon.

Barnaby, having finished his own breakfast, jumped onto the windowsill. He watched them eat, blinking slowly in the sun, satisfied that his charges were refueling for the next leg of the journey.

"Eat up," Sherry commanded, dropping a slab of butter into Nichole’s grits. "You've got a drive to Virginia Beach, a turnaround, and then a crusade to Jersey. You can't fight the world on an empty stomach."

Nichole took a bite of the cheesy grits. They were warm, salty, and comforting. She looked at Patrick. He was already halfway through a piece of toast, color returning to his cheeks.

She tapped the table.

*GOOD.*

"Yeah," Patrick mumbled, mouth full. "This works."
