# Part 12: The Night Watch

## Location: The Living Room, 168 Hermitage Road Time: 3:14 AM

The vibration woke him first.

It wasn't a spasm. It wasn't the rhythmic kick of Nichole’s legs fighting a dream. It was a high-frequency trembling, like a hummingbird trapped in the flannel sheets.

Patrick opened his eyes. The farmhouse was pitch black, save for a single beam of moonlight cutting across the floorboards. The silence was absolute—no cars, no sirens, just the settling of the old house.

He shifted his arm, the one draped over her chest. Her t-shirt was damp.

"Nikki?" he whispered.

She didn't answer. She just curled tighter into a ball, burying her face into the crook of his neck. A soft, hitching sound escaped her throat—a wet, ragged gasp for air.

She was crying. Not the angry tears of the afternoon, but the deep, hollow sobbing of 3:00 AM, when the defenses are down and the monsters come out.

Patrick knew exactly which monsters were in the room.

He didn't ask "What's wrong?" He knew. She was replaying the tape. She was seeing the plastic rails of the hospital bed. She was hearing Mr. Swayne’s voice reducing her complex, brilliant mind to a "Door Greeter." She was feeling the crushing weight of a world that looked at her and saw only a broken object to be managed.

"I know," Patrick breathed into her hair. "I know."

He tightened his grip. He wrapped both arms around her, pulling her so close that the buttons of his oversized shirt pressed into her shoulder. He applied the "Vice Grip"—maximum deep pressure. He tried to squeeze the fear out of her, tried to use his own body as a physical barrier between her and the memory of that brick fortress.

"You are not a greeter," Patrick whispered fiercely. "You are a writer. You are the smartest person I know. They are small, Nikki. They are so small."

Nichole sobbed harder, her body shaking against his chest. It hurt so much. It hurt to fight this hard just to be seen as human.

Then, the third member of the sleep pile woke up. Barnaby.

The orange tabby had been sleeping near their feet. He felt the distress. To a cat, the change in pheromones and the shaking of the bed were a call to action.

He stood up, stretched, and walked up the length of the sofa bed, navigating the tangle of limbs in the dark.

He found Nichole’s head.

He didn't hesitate. He stepped onto the pillow. He lowered his face to hers.

*Rough. Wet. Rough.*

Barnaby licked the tears off her cheek with a sandpaper tongue. It was startling, abrasive, and incredibly grounding.

Then, he made his move. He stepped down from the pillow and curled his body directly against the top of her head, pressing his fur against her forehead. He draped one heavy orange paw over her ear, as if trying to block out the sound of Mr. Swayne’s voice.

*Purrrrrrrrrrrrr.*

He cranked the engine to maximum volume. The sound vibrated through her skull. It was a demand: *Stop thinking. Feel this. I am here. You are warm. You are safe.*

Nichole froze. The combination of Patrick’s crushing hug and the cat’s insistent, vibrating weight on her head broke the loop.

She took a shuddering breath. She reached up with her good hand, finding the cat’s fur. Barnaby pushed his head into her hand, purring louder.

Patrick felt her muscles begin to unlock.

"See?" Patrick whispered. "Barnaby thinks Swayne is an idiot, too."

Nichole let out a weak, watery huff of laughter against his neck.

She tapped Patrick’s chest three times. *I'm okay.*

"We're not going back to the potato factory," Patrick promised, shifting his leg to anchor her more securely. "We're going to the mountains. We're going to find the people who read books."

Nichole nodded. She closed her eyes, listening to the dual rhythm of Patrick’s heart and the cat’s purr. The monsters retreated back into the shadows, unable to breach the fortress of the sofa bed.

She drifted back to sleep, held tight by her brother and guarded by a farm cat who took his job very seriously.
