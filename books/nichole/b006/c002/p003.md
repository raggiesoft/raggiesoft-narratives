# Part 3: The Intake

## Location: Admissions Office, Birdsall-Hoover Building Time: 1:15 PM

The interior of the Birdsall-Hoover Building smelled of lemon antiseptic and old paper. The air was frigid, the kind of aggressive air conditioning used to preserve government documents.

They were ushered into a small office just off the lobby. Mr. Swayne sat behind a metal desk that was almost completely bare, save for a stapler and a stack of intake forms.

"Alright," Swayne said, clicking a ballpoint pen. He looked at Tom and Ellen, then shifted his gaze to Nichole. He offered her a practiced, pitying smile.

"So, we are looking at a Summer Term intake for Nichole. Assessing for Vocational Rehabilitation and Life Skills." He began checking boxes on his form without asking any questions. "And Patrick is here for... emotional support?"

Patrick stiffened. "I'm here to enroll."

Swayne’s pen stopped. He looked up, confused. He scanned Patrick up and down—the standing posture, the broad shoulders, the lack of visible medical equipment (other than the slightly stiff posture from his cerebral palsy affecting his left leg).

"Enroll?" Swayne frowned. "Mr. Bennett, Roosevelt is a rehabilitation facility for individuals with *significant* barriers to employment. Usually, siblings without... major deficits... attend traditional universities."

"I have Cerebral Palsy," Patrick said, his voice hard. "I have hemiplegia on the left side. And we are a team. We are applying for the Business and Information Technology track. Together."

Swayne blinked. He looked at the parents. "Is he... classified?"

"He has an IEP," Ellen said sharply. "And he is fully qualified for your services. They are attending together, or not at all."

Swayne sighed, the sound of a bureaucrat having his routine disrupted. "Very well. Two intakes. But be aware, Patrick, that given your high level of function, your housing placement will be completely different from your sister's. You don't need the medical wing."

He stood up, grabbing his keys.

"Let's begin the tour. We'll start with the residential sector."

## The Transfer of Power

They walked back out into the lobby. Waiting for them was a young woman in a blue polo shirt with the DARS logo. She looked nice, eager, and completely untrained in disability etiquette.

"This is Sarah," Swayne introduced. "She’s one of our Mobility Assistants."

Sarah stepped forward, smiling at Patrick.

"Hi there," she said brightly. "I can take the chair for you so you can relax on the tour."

She didn't look at Nichole. She didn't ask the person in the chair. She reached out her hands toward the push handles, assuming Patrick would relinquish control like a tired parent handing over a stroller.

Patrick didn't move. His hands clamped down on the black rubber grips like vices.

"No," Patrick said.

Sarah paused, her hands hovering in the air. "Oh, it's really no trouble! That's what I'm here for. To assist the clients."

"She isn't luggage," Patrick said, his voice dropping to a low, dangerous register. "And I don't need to relax. I steer."

Nichole watched the exchange. She felt a flash of panic at the thought of this stranger pushing her—moving too fast, hitting bumps she didn't see, parking her in a corner while the adults talked. She tapped her armrest twice. *Hold the line.*

Swayne cleared his throat, his smile tightening. He turned his gaze away from Patrick, looking directly at Tom and Ellen. He assumed, naturally, that the parents would step in. In his world, parents were the authority figures who corrected "difficult" siblings and smoothed things over with the administration.

"Mr. and Mrs. Bennett," Swayne said, his voice dripping with professional concern. "We find that tours are often more effective when family members are free to observe and ask questions, rather than being burdened with care duties. Perhaps you could..."

He gestured vaguely, inviting them to order their son to stand down.

Tom didn't blink. He had his hands in his pockets, looking at a framed photo of the 1998 Wheelchair Basketball team on the wall. He didn't even look at Swayne.

"Patrick's driving," Tom said simply.

Swayne blinked. "I understand, but—"

"You don't understand," Ellen interrupted. Her voice was soft, Southern, and utterly immovable. "Nichole is the captain of that ship. Patrick is the navigator. We're just the cargo."

She smiled sweetly at Sarah. "You can put your hands down, honey. If you touch that chair, he might bite."

Sarah quickly retracted her hands, looking uncertainly at her boss.

Swayne stood there for a moment, processing the failure of the hierarchy. He wasn't dealing with a overwhelmed family looking for relief. He was dealing with a phalanx.

"Very well," Swayne said, his tone cooling significantly. "If you prefer."

He turned on his heel, his rubber-soled shoes squeaking on the linoleum.

"Right this way," Swayne said, walking briskly toward the double doors. "We'll start with the Physical Therapy wing."

Patrick leaned down to Nichole's ear as he pushed her forward, following the administrator at his own pace.

"Cargo," Patrick whispered. "I like that."

Nichole tapped her armrest.

*GOOD. MOM.*

They rolled through the doors, not as a client and a case file, but as a unit.

## The Elevation

Swayne pursed his lips but gestured toward the back of the lobby. "Fine. Follow me to Rothrock Hall."

The architecture of the building was confusing. The lobby they were standing in was essentially a sunken level. The main corridor of the facility was visible directly ahead of them, but it was elevated exactly three feet higher—separated by a short flight of three terrazzo steps.

"Stairs," Patrick noted, looking at the obstacle.

"The elevator is right there," Swayne said, pointing to a standard metal elevator door set into the wall next to the stairs.

Patrick pushed the button. The doors slid open. It was a standard, full-sized elevator that also served the basement level.

Patrick rolled Nichole inside. Tom and Ellen squeezed in behind them.

"We'll meet you at the top," Swayne called out.

The doors closed, sealing them in.

Swayne and Sarah simply walked up the three steps.

Inside the elevator, Patrick pressed the button for the Main Level. The machinery hummed. The car rose slowly, traveling a vertical distance of only thirty-six inches. It felt ridiculous—waiting for the doors to close, the slow lurch upward, the wait for the doors to open—just to traverse a distance that Swayne had walked in two seconds.

*Ding.*

The doors opened. They exited straight out the back door of the elevator car, emerging onto the upper level.

Swayne was already there, checking his watch.

"This way," Swayne said, turning to the left.

They followed him down the corridor. The "Admissions" feel of the lobby was gone. This hallway was wide, quiet, and lined with heavy wooden handrails. The air smelled different here—less like paper, more like rubbing alcohol and cafeteria food.

A sign on the wall read: ROTHROCK HALL.

"This is where Nichole would live," Swayne said, his keys jingling as he stopped in front of a set of heavy double doors. "As I mentioned... it is a secure unit."

Nichole looked at the doors. They didn't look like they were designed to keep the world out. They looked like they were designed to keep the "patients" in.
