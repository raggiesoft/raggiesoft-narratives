# Part 14: The Pivot

## Location: 168 Hermitage Road to Virginia Beach Time: 10:00 AM

The goodbye was short, loud, and full of carbohydrates.

Sherry packed them a "road ration"—a tin of leftover biscuits and four apples from her tree. She stood by the open driver's side door, leaning in to look at Tom.

"You drive safe," Sherry ordered. "And don't you dare stop at that brick prison on the way back."

"Not a chance," Tom promised.

Sherry walked around to the passenger side where the lift was stowed. She reached through the open window and squeezed Patrick’s hand.

"You're a good brother, Patrick," she said fiercely. "But don't forget you're a student, too. You find a place that teaches you, not just accommodates her."

Patrick nodded, his throat tight. "Yes, ma'am."

She looked at Nichole. "Write the book, kid. Send me the first copy."

Tom put the van in gear. As they pulled out of the gravel driveway, Patrick looked back. Sherry was standing on the porch, flanked by Buster and Scout, waving a dishrag like a battle flag.

## The Rearview Mirror

The drive east on I-64 felt different than the drive west.

Yesterday, the mountains had been a mystery. Today, they were a known quantity. They held beauty, but they also held the Birdsall-Hoover Building.

As they passed Exit 91, everyone in the van went silent.

Patrick looked out the window. He could see the exit sign for Fishersville. He could picture the road winding past the Mule Academy, past the school board, and into the parking lot of the potato factory.

He felt a phantom tightening in his chest—the memory of Swayne’s voice, the smell of the floor wax, the sight of the hospital bed rails.

Then, the exit blurred past. They kept moving. 65 miles per hour. 70.

"Gone," Patrick whispered.

"Good riddance," Ellen said from the front seat, not even looking at the exit.

## Home Base Location: The Bennett Home, Virginia Beach Time: 1:30 PM

Pulling into their own driveway felt like docking a ship after a storm.

The house wasn't a farmhouse, and it wasn't a dormitory. It was The Fortress. It was modified, hacked, and engineered specifically for them.

Patrick unloaded Nichole. As soon as they entered the living room, the smell hit them—not floor wax, not hay, but their smell. Books, electronics, and familiar laundry detergent.

"Shower," Patrick announced. "Real shower."

The sponge bath at Sherry’s had been a necessity, but it hadn't washed away the feeling of the institution.

They went to their bathroom. It was a chaotic sanctuary. The roll-in shower was huge, equipped with a sturdy bench and a handheld sprayer with industrial water pressure. There were grab bars exactly where they needed them, not where a code book said they should be.

Patrick helped her transfer. The water hit them—hot and strong. They washed off the sweat of the drive, the smell of the farm, and the lingering, greasy film of Mr. Swayne’s condescension.

## The Dream Scenario Time: 4:00 PM

Clean, fed, and wearing fresh clothes, they sat on the King-sized bed in their room.

The packing had begun. Suitcases were open on the floor. Winter coats were being pulled from the back of the closet—because Vermont in June was an unknown variable, and the Bennetts prepared for everything.

But on the bed, spread out between them, was the Putney College prospectus.

It was heavy stock paper. Glossy. Expensive. And it spoke a completely different language than the Roosevelt pamphlets.

Patrick traced the text on page four.

## "The Center for Academic Support"

- Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

- Accessible Campus Infrastructure

- Integrated Housing Options

- Personal Care Attendant (PCA) Management Training

"Look at that," Patrick said, his eyes widening. "Management Training. Roosevelt wanted to be the caregivers. Putney wants to teach us how to manage the care."

It was a subtle distinction, but it changed everything. It shifted the power dynamic from "Patient/Nurse" to "Manager/Staff."

Nichole leaned in, her wet hair smelling of their strawberry shampoo. She scanned the housing section.

"Residence Halls are designed to foster community while respecting individual needs. Suites are fully accessible."

There was no mention of "medical wings." No mention of "privilege levels." No mention of "curfews for liability reasons."

"It says 'Community,'" Nichole thought. "Not 'Rehabilitation.'"

She looked at the photos. The students weren't wearing scrubs. They were wearing flannel shirts and combat boots. They were sitting in circles on the grass. Some had blue hair. Some were in wheelchairs. Some were reading under trees.

They didn't look managed. They looked free.

"This is it," Patrick said, reading the section on the Computer Science program. "Small class sizes. Focus on individual projects. No potato scoops."

He looked at Nichole. The skepticism of the morning was gone, replaced by the fervent belief of the convert. They had seen Hell, and now they were looking at the map to Heaven.

"This place gets it," Patrick said. "They actually get it. We can just be students here."

Nichole looked at the brochure. It felt heavy with promise. It felt like the place where she could write her novel without anyone asking about her "limitations."

She tapped her armrest.

THE. HOLY. GRAIL.

"Yeah," Patrick said, tossing the brochure onto the pile of sweaters to be packed. "We leave Wednesday. We go to Jersey. Then we go get our acceptance letter."

He stood up and grabbed his duffel bag, moving with a renewed energy.

"Pack heavy, Nikki. We aren't coming back until we win."
