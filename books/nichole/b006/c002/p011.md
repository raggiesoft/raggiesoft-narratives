# Part 10: The War Council

## Location: The Kitchen / Living Room, 168 Hermitage Road Time: 6:30 PM

Dinner was a restoration ritual.

Aunt Sherry didn't believe in "light suppers." She believed in caloric reinforcement. The massive oak table was groaning under the weight of the spread: skillet-fried chicken with a batter so crisp it shattered when you bit it, pole beans simmered with ham hocks, mountains of mashed potatoes with a butter-yellow well in the center, and a basket of buttermilk biscuits that were still steaming.

"Eat," Sherry commanded, piling a third piece of chicken onto Patrick’s plate. "You're a growing boy, and you spent half the day fighting the state government. You need fuel."

Patrick didn't argue. He ate with the ravenous, focused intensity of a teenager who had skipped lunch. Nichole, usually a picky eater due to swallowing difficulties when tired, found herself matching his pace, energized by the safety of the room.

As the plates were cleared and the decaf coffee was poured, the conversation turned to logistics.

"Now," Sherry said, wiping the table. "Sleeping arrangements. The guest room is upstairs. That’s for you, Tom and Ellen. It’s got the quilt your mama made."

She turned to the twins.

"Obviously, you two aren't doing stairs. And I'm not separating you."

She pointed to the massive, overstuffed floral sofa in the living room.

"That’s a sleeper sofa. Queen mattress. I've already put the flannel sheets on it. You two take the ground floor."

Patrick looked at the sofa. It was soft, low, and directly in the center of the house. "Thanks, Aunt Sherry. We... we appreciate not being split up."

"I know how the machine works," Sherry winked. "But we have a logistical issue. The bathroom."

The farmhouse had a half-bath downstairs—a "powder room" built in the 1920s. It was narrow. The door frame was maybe twenty-four inches wide.

Sherry looked at the massive, purple Titan AeroMotion. Then she looked at the tiny door.

"That chair isn't fitting through there," Sherry noted, frowning. "Not in a million years. Do we need to bring in a kitchen chair? Maybe slide her over?"

Tom didn't answer. He just took a sip of his coffee and watched his son.

Patrick stood up. He didn't look at the door frame. He didn't ask for a kitchen chair. He just stepped in front of Nichole.

"Ready?" Patrick murmured.

## The Carry

To an outsider like Sherry, the logistics seemed impossible. Lifting a ninety-pound teenager with dead-weight legs usually required equipment, slide boards, or two people.

But for the Bennetts, this wasn't a medical procedure. It was a hug.

Nichole didn't hesitate. She leaned forward, wrapping her arms around Patrick’s neck, locking her fingers in the "Safety Grip." She buried her face in his shoulder.

Patrick bent his knees, bracing his good leg against the hardwood floor. He hooked his arms under her knees and behind her back—the "Fireman's Cradle."

"Up," he whispered.

He lifted her easily. To him, she wasn't a weight; she was part of his own center of gravity.

Sherry’s eyes went wide. She watched as Patrick navigated the narrow hallway sideways, pivoting his hips to clear the doorframe without bumping Nichole’s knees. He moved with a practiced, fluid grace that made the heavy lift look like a dance step.

"Lord have mercy," Sherry whispered to Ellen. "He just... scooped her up."

"He's her legs," Ellen said softly. "Always has been."

Inside the small bathroom, Patrick lowered her onto the closed lid of the toilet first, steadying her while he caught his breath. His shirt was damp with sweat—not from the effort, but from the humidity—but he was smiling.

"Field expedient," Patrick joked.

They managed the routine in the cramped space—washing up at the small sink, brushing teeth—with the silent, efficient choreography of two people who had been a single unit for eighteen years.

When they came back out—Patrick carrying her back to the sofa—Sherry was waiting with two giant, faded t-shirts.

"We didn't plan for sleepwear," Sherry said softly, handing them over. "These were my husband's. They're huge. They'll work as nightgowns."

Patrick gently deposited Nichole onto the pull-out sofa bed. She sank into the flannel sheets, wearing the oversized shirt, looking tired but completely safe.

## The Strategy Session

Once everyone was settled—Tom and Ellen with coffee, the twins resting on the pull-out bed—Ellen dumped her purse onto the coffee table.

She pulled out the Roosevelt Rehabilitation Center brochures.

She laid them side-by-side with the reality they had just lived.

"Look at this," Tom said, pointing to a photo of smiling students in a brightly lit computer lab. "It says 'State of the Art Technology Training.' But the guy told us Patrick would be scooping potatoes."

"And this," Ellen pointed to a photo of a dorm room that looked suspiciously wide and sunny. "It doesn't show the hospital beds. It doesn't show the rails. It doesn't mention that siblings are banned from seeing each other."

"It's a brochure for a lie," Patrick said from the sofa, adjusting the pillow behind Nichole’s head. "They sell 'Independence,' but they deliver 'Compliance.'"

Ellen swept the Roosevelt papers off the table and into the trash can.

"Gone," she said.

Then, she pulled out the thick, matte-finish folder for Putney College.

The mood in the room shifted. This wasn't a tri-fold pamphlet; it was a prospectus.

"Okay," Tom said, putting on his reading glasses. "Let's look at the logistics. We can't fail this one."

"The Open House is Saturday, June 28th," Ellen read. "That gives us a week."

"We need to go home first," Patrick said. "We have no clothes. We need winter gear. Vermont is cold, even in June, right? And I need to grab the backup charger."

"Agreed," Tom said. "So, here is the flight plan."

He took a napkin and drew a map.

1.  Sunday (Tomorrow): Drive back to Virginia Beach. Regroup. Laundry. Re-pack the van for a long-haul expedition.

2.  Wednesday: Drive North. Destination: Highland Park, New Jersey.

    - "We stay with Aunt Carol," Ellen said. "She's dying to see you guys. And it breaks up the drive."

3.  Thursday: Highland Park to Brattleboro, Vermont.

    - "We have a reservation at the Yankee Traveler Motor Lodge in Brattleboro," Tom noted. "It's right down the road from Putney. Accessible room, two queen beds."

4.  Friday: Scout the area. Rest.

5.  Saturday: Putney College Open House.

"Carol is excited," Sherry added, sipping her tea. "She called me right after you guys got here. She wants to show you her garden in Highland Park. And Patrick, she wants to thank you in person for that website."

Nichole looked at the Putney brochure lying on the table.

The cover showed a path winding through the woods. It was paved. It was wide. And the student on it was alone, looking at a book, not being herded by a nurse.

It looked like freedom.

"It's a lot of driving," Patrick murmured, rubbing his sore leg. "Thirteen hours total."

"Is it worth it?" Sherry asked.

Nichole looked at the trash can where the Roosevelt brochure lay crumpled among the chicken bones. She thought about the "Door Greeter" vest.

She looked at Patrick.

*WORTH. IT.*

"Yeah," Patrick said, pulling the quilt up over them. "We go to Jersey. Then we go get the Holy Grail."

"Then it's settled," Tom said, slapping the table. "We rest tonight. Tomorrow, we retreat to VB. Wednesday, the crusade begins."
