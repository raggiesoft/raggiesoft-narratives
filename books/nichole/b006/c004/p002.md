# Part 2: The Delaware Trap

## The Ferry Decision Date: July 1, 2003 Time: 10:45 AM Location: US-113 Northbound, approaching Georgetown, Delaware

The Liberty Trekker was cruising north on US-113, a four-lane divided highway cutting through the heart of Sussex County. To their left and right were endless flat fields of soybeans and corn.

"Approaching Georgetown," Tom announced, scanning the road signs. "Decision point in two miles."

They were coming up on the intersection with US-9. A right turn here would take them east to Lewes and the Cape May-Lewes Ferry. Staying straight would keep them on the asphalt track toward Milford and the Delaware Memorial Bridge.

"Okay, check the math," Tom said, glancing at the dashboard clock. It read 10:45 AM.

Ellen flipped the page in the AAA TourBook. "The ferry schedule says boats depart every fifty minutes in the summer. But the rule is you have to be in line twenty minutes before departure or you lose your spot."

"It's twenty minutes from here to the terminal on Route 9," Tom calculated. "That puts us at the gate at 11:05. The next boat is probably 11:15 or 11:20."

"That is cutting it way too close," Ellen said, shaking her head. "If we hit one red light in Georgetown, or if there's beach traffic on Route 9, we miss that boat."

Patrick looked up. "And if we miss it?"

"Then we sit in a parking lot for an hour waiting for the noon boat," Tom said. "Then it's an eighty-five minute crossing. Then it's another fifteen to unload. We wouldn't hit the Jersey Turnpike until... what? 2:30?"

Patrick looked out the window. He saw a line of cars in the right lane, blinkers on, turning east onto US-9. They had surfboards on the roof and towels in the windows. They were on vacation time.

The Bennetts were on mission time.

"Too much friction," Patrick said. "If we stay on wheels, we control the schedule."

"Agreed," Tom said. "We aren't rolling the dice on a boat ride."

The traffic light at the intersection turned green. The cars in the right lane peeled off toward the coast.

Tom kept the van in the left lane. The engine hummed as they accelerated through the intersection, leaving the ferry route behind.

"Staying on 113," Tom confirmed. "We merge with Route 1 in Milford, then it's a straight shot to the Bridge."

Nichole watched the sign for Lewes / Ferry disappear in the side mirror. She didn't mind. The ferry bathrooms were tiny, and the boat rocked too much for her spasms.

She tapped her screen.

*BRIDGE. FASTER.*

"That's my girl," Tom grinned, checking his speedometer. "We'll be eating burgers in Jersey by 1:00."

## Time: 10:30 AM Location: Dover, Delaware

The drive up the Eastern Shore of Maryland (US-113) was a blur of chicken trucks, flat cornfields, and speed traps in towns like Pocomoke City.

But as they crossed into Delaware, the game changed.

"Okay, decision point," Tom said, looking at the road signs. "Do we stay on 13 or pay the ransom for Route 1?"

In 2003, Delaware Route 1 (SR-1) was the "new" way. It was a sleek, limited-access toll road designed to bypass the traffic lights of the local towns.

"Take the toll," Ellen said. "We need speed."

Tom merged onto SR-1. The road smoothed out instantly. They picked up speed, cruising past the Dover Air Force Base, seeing the tails of massive military transport planes sticking up over the trees.

"This is nice," Patrick noted, watching the odometer tick up. "We're making good time."

"Don't get too comfortable," Tom warned. "We still have the Canal."

## The Bottleneck

## Time: 11:45 AM Location: The C&D Canal, St. Georges, DE

The smooth sailing of SR-1 hit reality at the Chesapeake & Delaware Canal.

In the summer of 2003, the "Relief Route" was tantalizingly close to completion, but a critical piece was missing: the new bridge.

Traffic slowed to a crawl as the highway funneled down.

"Look at that," Patrick said, pointing out the window.

Rising out of the water to their right was a massive, unfinished structure—concrete pylons towering hundreds of feet into the air, with cables dangling like spiderwebs. It was the future cable-stayed bridge that would eventually carry the highway over the canal.

But today, it was just a construction site.

"We have to take the old bridge," Tom grumbled, following the detour signs.

They were diverted onto the St. Georges Bridge. It was a tied-arch steel bridge built in the 1940s. It was narrow, steep, and terrifyingly high. The lanes felt like they were barely wide enough for the van, let alone the tractor-trailer squeezing past them in the opposite direction.

Nichole gripped her armrests. She hated narrow bridges. The metal truss flashed by the window, chopping the sunlight into strobe-light fragments.

"Steady," Tom whispered, keeping the van centered in the tight lane.

They crested the top, getting a dizzying view of the canal far below, and then descended back to solid ground.

"We made it," Tom exhaled as they merged back onto the wider highway toward New Castle.

"Next stop, the Jersey line," Patrick said, checking his watch. "We're definitely beating the D.C. traffic."

Ahead of them, the twin spans of the Delaware Memorial Bridge loomed on the horizon—the steel archway that marked the point of no return. Once they crossed that water, they were in the Northeast.

Nichole tapped her screen.

*BYE. VIRGINIA.*

"Yep," Patrick smiled, settling back into his seat. "Hello, New Jersey."
