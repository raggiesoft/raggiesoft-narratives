# Part 3: The Diner Debrief

## Date: July 5, 2003 Time: 1:15 PM Location: The Edison Diner, US-1 South, New Jersey

The chrome diner was packed, smelling of coffee, Taylor pork roll, and griddle grease. The Bennetts and Aunt Carol were tucked into a large, accessible corner booth.

Nichole was finishing a plate of silver-dollar pancakes. She was in good spirits. The tour had gone well. The guide was polite. The campus was massive, flat, and paved in glorious, smooth concrete.

"I loved the buses," Patrick said, mapping out the routes on a diner napkin with a red pen. "They run every ten minutes. Hydraulic lifts on the back doors. They connect the whole university. It's a completely closed loop."

"Except for the dorms," Ellen reminded him gently. "Brian said it himself. Single-occupancy medical beds only. No co-habitation."

Patrick dropped his pen. He looked at the napkin. The perfect transit system, ruined by state housing codes.

Carol put her coffee mug down. Her eyes lit up with a sudden, fierce energy.

"Screw the dorms," Carol said.

Tom raised an eyebrow. "Excuse me?"

"You don't need the dorms," Carol said, leaning over the table. "Look, Raritan State waives the housing requirement for local commuters. Right?"

"Sure," Tom nodded. "If you live within a certain radius. But we live three hundred miles away."

"You do," Carol said. "I don't. Change their address to my house. They become New Jersey residents. Boom. Commuter status. They bypass the housing department entirely."

Patrick looked up, his engineering brain immediately running the numbers. "Wait. We live with you in Highland Park? The Number 810 bus goes right down Raritan Avenue. It crosses the bridge and drops off at the train station. We transfer from the transit bus to the campus bus right there."

Nichole made an excited, high-pitched vocalization. She tapped her screen. "AUNT. CAROL. HOUSE."

"Exactly!" Carol beamed at Nichole.

"Hold on, hold on," Tom said, holding his hands up to stop the runaway train. "Carol, I love you. But your house has four concrete steps up to the porch, and a bathroom the size of a phone booth. It's a historic home. We can't make it accessible without gutting the whole first floor."

"So I sell it," Carol shrugged, as if selling a house she'd owned for twenty years was as simple as ordering a second cup of coffee. "I've been thinking about downsizing anyway. I sell the Highland Park house, I buy a ground-floor condo in New Brunswick near the station, or somewhere on a better bus line. We get a place with a roll-in shower. Problem solved."

The table went quiet. The magnitude of the offer hung in the air, heavy and warm.

Nichole looked at her aunt. Her eyes filled with tears—happy ones this time. She reached across the table, pressing her hand against Carol's.

"Carol..." Ellen said, her voice choked with emotion. "You can't sell your home for us."

"Why not?" Carol asked stubbornly. "Family is family."

"Because of the logistics," Patrick said softly.

Everyone looked at Patrick. He was staring at the napkin.

"I looked at the schedule on the wall at the bus shelter during the tour," Patrick said. "The 810 bus only runs once an hour. On Sundays, it stops at 5:00 PM. If the lift is broken, or the bus is full, we miss class. The campus buses are great, but the public transit to get to them is a bottleneck."

Patrick looked at his aunt, his eyes full of deep gratitude.

"It's an amazing offer, Aunt Carol," Patrick said. "Seriously. Thank you. But we can't let you uproot your whole life for a transit system that only runs once an hour."

Nichole sighed. The logic was inescapable. She knew Patrick was right. The New Jersey plan was a beautiful dream, but it was just too heavy a lift.

Carol looked disappointed, but she nodded slowly. "The 810 is notoriously awful," she admitted. "And the lifts are always broken."

Tom reached across the table and squeezed Carol’s hand. "Thank you. For everything."

"So," Carol said, clearing her throat to push past the emotion of the moment. "What's the plan, then? If Putney is out, and Raritan State is out. What's left?"

Patrick looked at Nichole. He put his arm around the back of her wheelchair.

"We go home," Patrick said. "We go back to Norfolk and Virginia Beach. We have options right in our backyard."

He flipped the napkin over, using his red pen to draw a crude map of the Hampton Roads area.

"Look," Patrick said, pointing with the pen tip. "We have the Community College of Hampton Roads. We have Virginia Dominion University in Norfolk. If we want to look across the water on the Peninsula, there's Peninsula State University in Newport News, or even the Royal College of Virginia up in Williamsburg."

"Williamsburg?" Ellen asked. "That's an hour's drive away on a good traffic day."

"I looked at the transit maps before we left," Patrick said, his eyes lighting up with that same transit-nerd energy he’d had on the Raritan bus. "Hampton Roads has an interconnected bus grid. Even from Williamsburg. You can take the local bus to Newport News, transfer to the express bus over the bridge-tunnel to Norfolk, and catch the local bus straight back to our neighborhood in Virginia Beach."

"That would take five hours," Tom pointed out.

"But the *track* exists," Patrick emphasized. "If there's an emergency, or a long weekend, or if Nikki is just feeling homesick and you guys can't pick us up... we just roll onto the bus. It's a closed loop all the way to our front door."

Nichole looked at the red ink on the napkin. She could picture it. A long bus ride didn't scare her. A long bus ride meant she could listen to her music and watch the world go by, knowing that at the end of the line was her own bed. It was the ultimate safety net.

"I'll get an apartment near whichever campus we pick," Patrick continued. "A ground floor unit. I'll sign the lease. I'll be the landlord. I'll be the RA. I'll be the cafeteria chef. We don't need their permission."

Nichole looked up at him. She smiled. A real, genuine smile.

"Just us?" Tom asked.

"Just us," Patrick confirmed. "No dorm rules. No housing administrators. We build the machine ourselves."

Nichole tapped her tray. "HOME."

"Home it is," Tom said, raising his coffee mug. "To Virginia."

They clinked their mugs and glasses together over the plate of half-eaten pancakes. The Misfit Tour was officially over. They hadn't found the perfect college, but they had found the answer. They didn't need a sanctuary built by someone else. They were going to build their own.
