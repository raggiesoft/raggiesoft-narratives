# Part 5: The Ocean State Arrival

## Date: July 5, 2003 Time: 8:45 PM Location: The Bayside Hotel, Newport, Rhode Island

The drive up I-95 from New Jersey was a blur of exhaustion and sudden, renewed adrenaline. They crossed the state line from Connecticut into Rhode Island as the sun was setting, the sky bruising a deep purple over the Atlantic.

"Okay, look sharp," Tom said, checking the overhead signs. "We want Exit 4."

He signaled, guiding the heavy van onto the off-ramp marked I-895 NORTH. The green reflective sign listed the destinations clearly: Iron Point, Liberty, Newport.

"North?" Patrick muttered from the back, looking at the compass on his watch. "We're driving East toward the ocean."

"Don't ask," Tom laughed. "It’s a Rhode Island thing."

They merged onto the highway. For the first few miles, it felt massive—a six-lane superhighway with wide shoulders that felt just like the Connecticut Turnpike they had left behind. But as they crossed the US-1 overpass, the character of the road shifted instantly. The third lane vanished, the concrete barrier disappeared, and they were suddenly on a narrower, four-lane parkway with a grassy median.

"Welcome to the funnel," Tom noted as the traffic condensed.

Then, the water appeared. They hit the Jamestown Bridge, a high, sweeping concrete span crossing the West Passage of the Bay. To their right, running parallel to them, stood the skeleton of the original bridge—a narrow, terrifying steel truss that looked like it belonged in a museum of dangerous engineering.

"See that grate over there?" Tom pointed to the old bridge. "Back in the day, that was the final exam. To get your Rhode Island driver’s license, all you had to do was show the instructor you could drive across that steel grate without having a heart attack. If you didn't pass out, you passed."

They crossed the island of Jamestown and approached the toll plaza for the main event: The Newport Pell Bridge.

Tom rolled down the window and handed the attendant two dollars. As the van climbed the massive suspension bridge, Patrick looked out the window. He spotted a massive concrete pier jutting out of the water near the anchorage—a lonely, unused support structure.

"Twin spans," Patrick realized aloud, recognizing the engineering scar. "They were supposed to build two of these.".

"Budget cuts," Tom said, cresting the top of the bridge.

The highway descended into Newport, but instead of a grand entrance, it ended abruptly. The interstate designation terminated at a traffic light at Admiral Kalbfus Road—the "Highway to Nowhere".

"End of the line," Tom said, braking for the red light.

He made a sharp left onto Admiral Kalbfus, then a right onto Broadway, transitioning instantly from highway speeds to the tight, colonial grid of the city. A few blocks down, a large brick complex loomed on their left.

"Look, kids," Ellen said softly, pointing out the window. "Newport Hospital."

Nichole looked at the building. It wasn't just a hospital; it was the starting line.

"April 8th, 1985," Tom recited the date like a holy verse. "That's where the Bennett Machine came online."

They continued down Broadway, winding their way toward the water until they reached Long Wharf. By the time Tom pulled the van into the parking lot of the Bayside Hotel, it was fully dark. The air didn't smell like the humid asphalt of New Jersey anymore; it smelled of salt, cold harbor water, and fried clams.

Standing under the portico, waving enthusiastically, was a tall, broad-shouldered man with a greying beard.

"The Virginians have returned!" Uncle Mark bellowed as the van doors opened.

The reunion was immediate and loud. Mark hadn't seen the twins since they were 9 years old, but there was no awkward distance. When Patrick lowered the lift, Mark walked right up to the ramp. He didn't hover. He didn't use a "disability voice."

"Look at this rig!" Mark grinned, slapping the heavy aluminum frame of the Titan AeroMotion as Nichole rolled off the lift. "They didn't have hot rods like this in ‘94. You look great, Nikki."

Nichole beamed. She hit a pre-programmed button on the VocaLink. "HI. UNCLE. MARK."

"Hi yourself, kiddo," Mark smiled, leaning down to give her a gentle side-hug, mindful of the heavy *VocaLink mount* clamped to her frame. He looked up at Patrick, who was securing the van. "And you got big! You look like you could bench press me."

"I lift wheelchairs for a living, Uncle Mark," Patrick laughed, shaking his hand.

Mark had come through. The hotel room was on the ground floor, fully accessible, with a massive roll-in shower and plenty of space between the beds. On the table sat three large, greasy boxes from a local Newport pizzeria.

"Dig in," Mark ordered, grabbing a slice. "Best pie on the island."

While Ellen and Tom caught up with Mark at the table, discussing rental rates and the logistics of finding a zero-step ranch house in the area, Patrick and Nichole were in their element.

Patrick had hijacked the hotel room’s complimentary dial-up internet connection. He had his heavy laptop open on the bed, connected to the phone jack, the BTA (Braxton Transit Authority) website glowing on the screen.

Nichole sat parked next to the bed, a slice of pepperoni pizza on her lap tray, watching the screen intently.

"Okay, look at this, Nikki," Patrick said, pointing to the screen with a slice of pizza in his other hand. "This is tomorrow's target. The Narraganset Esplanade Station. It's the main transit hub for the Braxton State campus."

He clicked on a photo gallery on the BTA website. An image loaded, line by slow line.

"This is the Sky Platform," Patrick said, his voice dropping into the hushed, reverent tone he reserved for high-quality infrastructure.

Nichole leaned forward. The picture showed a massive, open-air train platform. It was a side-platform setup with two heavy rail tracks running down the middle. One track for northbound, one for southbound. In the background, looming over the bay like a steel giant, was the massive suspension span of the Newport Bridge.

"If you stand on the platform and look one way, you see the water and the bridge," Patrick explained. "If you turn around, you're looking directly down at the university."

Nichole made a soft, impressed *whistle* sound.

"It gets better," Patrick grinned. "It's shared track. BTA Regional Rail uses it, but so does the ARTS Northeast Regional line. It's all high-level boarding. Flat, roll-on, roll-off."

Nichole tapped her screen. "WHERE. DOES. IT. GO?"

"Next stop northbound is Braxton Central," Patrick said. "Uncle Mark says it's massive. Like the Berlin Hauptbahnhof. Glass and steel. From there, you can connect to BTA Metros, local buses, the ARTS Acela, or the OSTA Route 14 straight back to Providence. It's a massive arterial vein, Nikki. And BSU sits right on top of it."

He looked at his sister. Her eyes were reflecting the glow of the laptop screen. She wasn't looking at a trap. She was looking at a launchpad.

"Tomorrow morning, 9:00 AM," Patrick said, closing the laptop. "We take the OSTA bus to Perrotti Park. We board the Newport Flyer ferry. The boat drops us off right at the base of this station. We take the elevator up to the Sky Platform just to see the view. And then... we tour the campus."

Nichole smiled. It was the easiest, most unburdened smile of the entire week.

"Just us," she tapped on the VocaLink.

"Just us," Patrick agreed. "The commuters."

From the table, Uncle Mark raised his beer. "To the Ocean State," he toasted.

"To the Ocean State," Tom echoed, clinking his own bottle.

Nichole reached for her cup of soda on the tray. Patrick helped her lift it. They didn't say the words, but the pact was sealed. The Misfit Tour was over. The BSU experiment was about to begin.
