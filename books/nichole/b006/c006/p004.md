# Part 4: The Ocean State Option

## Date: July 5, 2003 Time: 1:35 PM Location: The Edison Diner, US-1 South, New Jersey

The table was quiet for a moment, the Virginia plan solidifying in the diner air. Patrick was already folding up his napkin map, and Tom was reaching for the check. The tour was over.

"Wait," Carol said, putting her hand over Tom’s wallet. "Don't pay yet."

Tom paused. "Carol, we love the enthusiasm, but we've made the call. No more touring."

"I know, I know. No dorms, no administrators," Carol said rapidly, her eyes darting between Ellen and Patrick. "But we forgot about our brother."

Ellen blinked. "Mark? In Newport? Carol, he lives in a converted attic on the third floor of a Victorian. It has two flights of stairs. It is the literal opposite of accessible."

"I know that," Carol said, waving her hand dismissively. "But I talked to him last week. His lease is up at the end of August. He hates those stairs. He’s been looking to move to a ground-floor apartment anyway, but he said the rent in Newport is too high for a single guy. He told me he’d love to find roommates to split a bigger place. A three-bedroom, zero-step ranch."

Patrick stopped folding the napkin. He looked up. "Aunt Carol, you're suggesting we move in with Uncle Mark?"

"Why not?" Carol beamed. "He loves you guys. And he lives just across the bay from Braxton State University. The big one. BSU."

"I've never heard of it," Tom admitted.

"It's public. It's huge. And it sits right on the water in Braxton, directly west of Newport," Carol explained. "Mark said the whole campus is a 'Universal Design' project. Flat as a pancake. Automatic doors everywhere. No cars allowed on the main quad."

Ellen looked at Tom. "We haven't seen Mark since Christmas of ‘99. And the kids were born in Newport. We moved away when they were barely two years old."

Nichole felt a strange spark of electricity run up her spine. She had no memories of Rhode Island, but the idea of it carried a mythic weight. It was where her story started.

"Okay, but what's the transit situation?" Patrick asked, his inner engineer taking over. He wasn't swayed by nostalgia; he needed logistics. "If we get a new place with Uncle Mark in Newport, how do we get west across the bay to Braxton? There's no way I'm driving across the Newport Bridge in the winter."

Carol grinned. "You don't need a van. You take a boat."

"A boat," Patrick deadpanned.

"The Newport Flyer," Carol said triumphantly. "It's an easy ride on the OSTA bus to Gateway Center. You roll one block over to Perrotti Park, and you get on the Braxton Transit Authority harbor ferry. It goes straight west across Narragansett Bay and drops you off directly at the Narraganset Esplanade Station, right in the middle of the BSU campus. The gangways are hydraulic. It's perfectly level."

Patrick stared at his aunt. A local bus to a ferry to a campus. A completely public, accessible infrastructure loop.

Nichole’s eyes were wide. A ferry. She pictured the ocean. She pictured the wind. She pictured a campus where the doors actually opened.

She slammed her hand on the table, making the silverware jump.

Everyone looked at her. Nichole didn't hesitate. She jammed her finger onto the VocaLink.

"GO."

"Nikki, hold on," Tom said gently. "We're exhausted. We were just talking about home."

Nichole shook her head fiercely. She looked at Patrick. She made a high-pitched, insistent *keening* sound and pointed north. She wanted to see where she was born. She wanted to ride the boat.

"GO. RHODE. ISLAND."

Patrick looked at his sister's face. The exhaustion of Putney and Raritan was completely gone, replaced by a fierce, blazing curiosity.

"Okay," Patrick said, holding up a hand to calm the table. "I'm in. But we need ground rules. This is not another Open House. If we tour Braxton State, we do it our way."

"Which means what?" Ellen asked.

"No housing office," Patrick said firmly. "We don't even look at the dorms. We don't ask for permission. We tour the campus as commuters. If we like it, we find an apartment with Uncle Mark, establish residency, and bypass the whole system. We operate as free agents."

"Commuter status only," Tom agreed, nodding. "I like it. Zero bureaucracy."

"I'll call him," Ellen said, already pulling the heavy cellular phone out of her purse.

She dialed the number. The table waited in tense silence, listening to the ambient noise of the diner.

"Mark? Hey, it's Ellen," she said. A pause. "No, everything's fine! Actually, we're in New Jersey with Carol... Yes, all of us. The twins too."

Ellen smiled, holding the phone slightly away from her ear so the table could hear Mark’s booming, excited voice on the other end.

*"El! Put them on speaker! Where are you guys?"* "Listen, Mark," Ellen continued. "We had a change of plans. How would you feel about some houseguests tonight? The kids want to tour Braxton State."

*"Are you kidding me?!"* Uncle Mark’s voice was loud enough to be heard over the diner clatter. *"Get up here! We'll put the twins up in a ground-floor hotel for the night, and tomorrow we can start looking at rental listings. Tell Patrick and Nichole I'm ordering the good pizza!"*

Ellen hung up, smiling at the table. "Well. The Ocean State is waiting."

Nichole let out a loud, joyous laugh—a bubbling, throaty sound that made a few patrons in the nearby booths turn and smile. She grabbed Patrick’s hand and squeezed it hard.

"Alright," Patrick grinned, putting his napkin map in his pocket. "Next stop, Narragansett Bay. Let's go see the water."
