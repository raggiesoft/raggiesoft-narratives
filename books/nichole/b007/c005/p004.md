# Part 4: The All-Clear

## Date: July 16, 2004 Time: 11:45 PM Location: The Living Room, Unit 404

The hammering against the glass finally stopped.

The roar of the wind, which had been a constant companion for six hours, faded into a low, distant whistle. The silence that followed was heavy and wet.

Patrick walked to the window. He pressed his forehead against the cool pane.

"Water's receding," he reported. "The drains are finally catching up. I can see the tops of the fire hydrants again."

Nichole checked her screen. The wall of red alerts on the transit dashboard blinked, then refreshed.

SYSTEM UPDATE: THE BRAXTON LINK: FLOODGATES RETRACTING AT 04:30. PASSENGER SERVICE RESUMES: 05:00. BTA SURFACE ROUTES: RESTORED ON DELAYED SCHEDULE. OSTA REGIONAL RAIL: INSPECTING TRACKS. SERVICE EXPECTED 06:00.

She let out a breath. The siege was over. The city had taken a beating, but the grid was rebooting.

She looked at the digital clock on the microwave. The numbers were steady. The lights had flickered—a few heart-stopping blinks around 8:00 PM—but the current had held. Their fortress had kept them warm, dry, and powered while the world outside drowned.

She looked at Patrick. He was staring down at the wet pavement of Foundry Square, probably calculating the flow rate of the storm drains.

She tapped her armrest.

"NO. TUNNELS. TOMORROW."

Patrick turned around. "No tunnels?"

Nichole shook her head. She loved the subway. She loved the "Link." It was their secret highway. But after being locked inside a brick box for twelve hours while a storm raged, she felt a desperate need for open sky. She wanted to see the sun, even if it was shining on puddles.

"TOPSIDE. ONLY. I. NEED. AIR."

Patrick smiled. He walked over to the sofa where she was still nested under the blanket.

"Copy that," he said softly. "Operation Fresh Air. We'll roll down to the riverwalk. Check the damage. See if the fishing pier is still there."

He sat down on the edge of the sofa. He reached out and pulled her into a hug—tight, grounding, and fierce.

He held her for a long time, his chin resting on top of her head. It wasn't just a hug for comfort; it was a victory lap.

"We did it, Nikki," he whispered into her hair. "First major crisis on our own. No parents. No RAs. Just us and the brick walls. And we didn't even lose the internet."

Nichole squeezed him back, burying her face in his chest. They had survived a subtropical storm without a single phone call to Portsmouth for help.
