# Part 8: The Bridge Fund

## Date: April 15, 2004 Time: 4:30 PM Location: The Kitchen, 95 West Main Road, Portsmouth, Rhode Island

The mail arrived at 4:00 PM. By 4:30 PM, the kitchen table at the Fortress was covered in confetti, and Uncle Mark was pouring sparkling cider into everyone's glasses.

Sitting in the center of the table were not two, but **three** heavy envelopes.

Two were navy blue, embossed with the silver Bull seal of **Braxton State University - Office of Admissions**.

The third was stark white, bearing the **crimson shield and silver staff** of the **University Medical Center - College of Health Professions**.

"The Triple Crown," Tom announced, his voice thick with pride as he held up the first two blue folders. "Patrick into the College of Engineering. Nichole into the College of Arts and Sciences, Department of Technical Communication. It’s official. You are Braxton Bulls."

Nichole sat at the end of the table. Her Apex ConvertiBook was detached from her wheelchair tray and resting on the table in front of her. She clicked her Quantum OptiMouse.

**"BENNETT. DYNAMICS. IS. OPEN. FOR. BUSINESS."**

The family cheered, the sound echoing through the wide hallways of the Fortress.

"But wait," Patrick said, holding up his hand. He reached for the third envelope—the white one with the crimson shield.

He opened it slowly. Inside wasn't just a letter; it was a clinical rotation schedule and a heavy patch that read **STUDENT NURSE**.

"Accepted," Patrick read, a grin spreading across his face. "School of Practical Nursing. Accelerated LPN Track. Clinicals start in September at the Trauma Center."

He looked at Nichole.

"I'm not just an engineer, Nikki. I'm officially a nursing student."

He tapped the acceptance letter.

"This is the last piece of the firewall. Sarah Mitchell gave us the waiver to live off-campus, but this... this stops them from forcing us to hire an Agency. If the Dean of Students asks for our 'Professional Care Plan,' I don't have to show them a contract with a home health company. I just show them my badge."

Nichole looked at the patch. It was the final piece of armor. **"MY. MEDIC."**

"Okay, the fun part is over," Patrick said, placing the patch on top of his engineering acceptance. He pulled out a thick binder and set it on the table. He looked at his father. "Now we talk logistics. The Foundry Lofts. We need to secure the unit now, before the summer rush. What did the property manager say the rent is for the two-bedroom accessible unit?"

Tom opened his ledger. "You picked a premium location, Pat. Downtown grid, direct underground subway access, secured building. In 2004, that goes for a premium. The rent is \$2,250 a month."

Aunt Carol let out a low whistle. "Ouch. That's Boston prices."

"It is," Tom agreed. "But look at the offset. If Patrick wasn't doing the nursing program, the university might insist we hire a certified Home Health Aide to minimize their liability. That would cost us fifty thousand dollars a year. We are spending \$27,000 on rent to save \$50,000 on care. The ROI is positive."

He flipped to a page highlighted in yellow.

"Which brings us to the IRS tax code."

"We have \$122,000 sitting in Grandma's 529 College Savings Plans," Tom explained. "But under the law, those funds are locked. We cannot withdraw a single penny for off-campus housing until you two are officially enrolled at least half-time. That doesn't happen until you register for classes in August."

"So we can't use the 529 money to sign the lease today," Patrick realized, his engineering brain seeing the gap in the bridge. "If we wait until August, the accessible unit will be gone."

"Exactly," Tom smiled. "Which is why your Grandmother Helen was a genius. She didn't just leave you a college fund. She left you a bridge fund."

Tom pulled out the passbook for the separate, traditional cash savings account Helen had left them.

**Balance: \$23,497.61.**

"The landlord at The Foundry requires a standard security deposit of one month's rent, plus the first month up front," Tom calculated, tapping his calculator. "That's \$4,500 just to get the keys. But to lock it down against other students, we need leverage."

"We prepay," Patrick said.

"We prepay," Tom nodded. "We walk into that leasing office, and we drop \$22,500 on the table. We pay the security deposit and the first **nine months** of rent in absolute cash. It locks the apartment down instantly, proves you are premium tenants, and covers your housing all the way through next May."

Nichole looked at the passbook on the table.

Her grandmother hadn't just left them money; she had left them **timing**. The cash account was the exact key they needed to unlock the system before the official gears of the university started turning.

"And the 529 plans?" Ellen asked.

"Once August hits, the 529s unlock," Patrick said, the master plan clicking into place. "We use that money to buy the BSU Platinum Meal Plan directly from the university. That covers our food, so we never have to grocery shop in the winter. We use the rest for tuition, books, and the sophomore year rent."

Patrick looked at Nichole.

"Nine months of rent, paid in full," Patrick said. "With nine hundred bucks left over for pizza. We don't just have an apartment, Nikki. We own the grid for the entire freshman year."

Nichole felt a wave of absolute security wash over her. No loans. No begging for waivers. No hoping the bus ran on time. And a brother who was officially her nurse.

She tapped her screen.

**"CALL. THE. LANDLORD."**

"Done," Tom said, closing the ledger with a satisfying thud. "Tomorrow morning, we buy the Fortress."
