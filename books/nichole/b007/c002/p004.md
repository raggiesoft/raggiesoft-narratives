# Part 4: The Liability Lie

## Date: January 22, 2004 Time: 1:00 PM Location: Office of Access & Equity, BSU Student Union

Patrick stared at the paper in his hand. **OFF-CAMPUS MEDICAL HARDSHIP WAIVER.**

It was a simple form. Standard font. A single signature at the bottom. It had taken Sarah Mitchell less than five minutes to type up, print, and sign.

Patrick felt a strange, hot pressure building in his chest. It wasn't relief anymore. It was confusion.

He looked up at Sarah.

"Can I ask you a question?" Patrick asked, his voice tight. "Off the record?"

"Sure," Sarah said, leaning back in her chair.

"We toured a specialty college in Vermont last summer. Putney College," Patrick said. "Their whole campus is supposed to be designed specifically for students with disabilities. But when we asked to stay together, they told us that federal safeguarding laws required them to separate us. They called me a 'liability.' They made it sound like their hands were tied by the government."

He held up the waiver Sarah had just signed.

"But you just handed us this. You cited the exact same federal laws—the ADA and the Rehabilitation Act—and you used them to keep us together. So who is lying? The specialty disability college? Or you?"

Sarah sighed. She took off her glasses and looked at the pink receipt from the Nursing School that was still sitting on the corner of her desk.

"Nobody is lying, Patrick," Sarah said gently. "Putney wasn't wrong. They were just... risk-averse."

She picked up the LPN application receipt and held it up.

"If you were just a brother—an untrained, eighteen-year-old family member performing intimate care and medical transfers in a dorm room—you *are* a liability. Statistically speaking, that is a lawsuit waiting to happen. If you drop her, or if there is an accusation of abuse, the college has zero oversight. We can't fire a brother. We can't revoke a brother's license."

Patrick stiffened.

"But," Sarah said, tapping the receipt with her finger, "you aren't just a brother anymore. You just applied to the School of Nursing."

She leaned forward, her expression shifting from administrator to ally.

"This is the difference, Patrick. Putney saw a sibling. I see a prospective Licensed Practical Nurse."

Patrick looked at the receipt.

"When you become an LPN," Sarah explained, "you stop being a 'family caregiver' in the eyes of the law. You become a **Licensed Healthcare Professional**. You answer to the State Board of Nursing. You carry a license number. You have a legal and ethical code of conduct."

She gestured to the waiver in his hand.

"I can sign that waiver because you are professionalizing the care. If something goes wrong, it's not just a family accident; it's a clinical issue. By getting that license, you are taking the liability *off* the university and putting it on your own license."

Patrick felt the realization hit him like a physical weight.

Putney hadn't just been being mean. They had been terrified of the lack of accountability. But by applying for the LPN program, Patrick had essentially created his own insurance policy.

"So the license is the shield," Patrick whispered.

"The license is everything," Sarah confirmed. "It turns you from a 'Risk' into an 'Asset.' Putney didn't think you would go that far. They underestimated your commitment."

She smiled.

"But we don't underestimate Bulls. You identified the legal gap, and you filled it with a credential. That is exactly the kind of problem-solving we want at this university."

Nichole reached for her mouse. She had been listening intently.

She clicked on a folder on her desktop that she had named **ARCHIVE**. She found the file for **Putney College**.

She dragged it to the Recycle Bin.

**"DELETE,"** her synthetic voice announced.

Patrick looked at the screen. He let out a short, sharp laugh.

"Yeah," Patrick said, carefully folding the BSU waiver and placing it in his internal jacket pocket, right next to his heart. "Delete."

He looked at Sarah. "Thank you for explaining the math."

"It's what we do here," Sarah smiled, putting her glasses back on. "Now get out of here. You have a lease to sign. And good luck with the NCLEX."

Patrick grabbed the handles of the Titan AeroMotion. He didn't just push the chair; he propelled it with a new, fierce energy.

They rolled out of the office and back into the tunnel.

"You were right, Nikki," Patrick whispered as they merged into the student traffic of The Bull Run. "They didn't think we'd do the work. They didn't think I'd become a nurse just to stay with you."

Nichole didn't look back. She looked forward, toward the subway station, toward the city, and toward the medical center where her brother would earn his rank.

**"FORWARD,"** she tapped. **"THE. MACHINE. MOVES. FORWARD."**
