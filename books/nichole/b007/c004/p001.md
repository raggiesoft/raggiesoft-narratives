# Part 1: The War Room

## Date: May 15, 2004 Time: 8:55 AM Location: The Command Center, Unit 404

The second bedroom of The Foundry Lofts looked like NASA Mission Control.

Patrick sat at his desk, his fingers hovering over the keyboard. On his screen, the BSU Student Portal (Banner Web) was loaded, displaying the login screen.

Nichole was parked next to him under her custom cutout. Her Apex ConvertiBook was docked, and she had a second window open on her screen: The Official U.S. Naval Observatory Master Clock.

"Registration opens at 9:00:00 AM," Patrick said, his voice tense. "The server is going to crash. Every freshman in the state is trying to get the 'Good English Professor.' We have to be faster than the server."

Nichole tapped her screen.

"REFRESH. ON. MY. MARK."

They had the Course Reference Numbers (CRNs) written on a whiteboard on the wall, prioritized by "Scarcity."

- ENG 101 - Sec 04 (MWF 9:00 AM): The "Good Professor" (Prof. Harrison). High demand.

- PSY 101 - Sec 02 (MWF 11:00 AM): The large lecture hall. Easy to get into, but they needed the specific accessible seating block.

- HIS 110 - Sec 01 (TuTh 10:00 AM): Western Civ.

- MATH 095 - Sec 03 (TuTh 1:00 PM): Pre-Algebra.

Patrick looked at the math entry. He frowned.

"I still think you should have tested out of 095," Patrick muttered. "You scored a near-perfect on the placement exam. You could be in Calculus."

Nichole shook her head.

The placement test had indeed flagged her for advanced math. But Patrick... Patrick had tested into MATH 095: Developmental Pre-Algebra.

If Nichole took Calculus, she would be in a different building, at a different time. Patrick would be alone in Pre-Algebra, struggling with equations he hated, while worrying about where she was. She would be alone in a Calculus class with a stranger assigned to take notes for her.

She wasn't going to let that happen.

She tapped her screen.

"NO. MAN. LEFT. BEHIND."

She had intentionally waived her placement results to drop down to his level. She wasn't just his sister; she was his tutor.

"I. WILL. TEACH. YOU."

"Yeah, well, try not to laugh when I fail to solve for X," Patrick said, sweating slightly. "Math is my kryptonite."

Time: 8:59:50 AM

"Ten seconds," Patrick warned.

He had two browser tabs open—one logged in as him, one logged in as her.

"5... 4... 3... 2... 1..."

"MARK."

Patrick hit F5 on both keyboards simultaneously.

The screens flashed white. The little loading icon spun.

Spin. Spin. Spin.

"COME. ON."

The page loaded. ADD/DROP CLASSES.

Patrick’s fingers flew. He didn't search for classes. He typed the CRN codes directly into the "Quick Add" boxes.

10452\. 10889. 11230. 10045.

He hit SUBMIT for Nichole first. Status: Registered.

He switched to his tab. He typed the same numbers. He hit SUBMIT.

Status: Registered.

Patrick slumped back in his chair, exhaling a massive breath.

"We're in," he said. "Section 04 for English. Section 03 for Math. Same room. Same time."

Nichole looked at the screen. The schedule was locked.

- Monday/Wednesday/Friday:

  - 09:00 AM - 09:50 AM: English 101 (Prof. Harrison)

  - 11:00 AM - 11:50 AM: Psychology 101 (Prof. Silva)

- Tuesday/Thursday:

  - 10:00 AM - 11:15 AM: Western Civ (Prof. Dubois)

  - 01:00 PM - 02:15 PM: Pre-Algebra (Prof. Miller)

It was a perfect "Block Schedule." No early mornings (before 9 AM). No late nights. And plenty of gaps for "Maintenance" (lunch, bathroom, rest).

Patrick looked at the Math class again.

"You realize you're going to be bored out of your mind in Pre-Algebra, right?"

Nichole grinned. She typed quickly.

"EASY. A. FOR. ME."

She paused, then added:

"AND. I. GET. TO. WATCH. YOU. SWEAT."

Patrick laughed, tossing a pen at her. "You're evil."

"I. AM. EFFICIENT."

Patrick laughed at the "Efficient" comment, but the laughter faded quickly into something warmer. He looked at the screen, then at the girl sitting in the chair next to him.

She had just voluntarily tanked her own academic placement—choosing to sit through a semester of basic algebra she could do in her sleep—just to make sure he didn't have to face his worst subject alone.

She called it efficiency. He knew what it really was. It was loyalty.

"Come here, you evil genius," Patrick murmured.

He leaned over the armrest of his chair. He didn't just pat her on the shoulder. He wrapped both arms around her, pulling her upper body toward him.

Nichole melted into the hug. She buried her face in the crook of his neck, breathing in the scent of his deodorant and the faint metallic smell of the computer tower humming under the desk.

He squeezed her tight—tight enough to register as deep pressure input, tight enough to make her feel the solid reality of his presence.

"Thank you," Patrick whispered into her hair. "For the math. For the plan. For everything."

Nichole closed her eyes. She felt his arms anchoring her. This was the fuel. The wieners and the apartment and the subway pass were all logistics. This—this absolute, crushing, protective love—was the energy source.

She squeezed him back as hard as her spastic muscles would allow, pressing her cheek against his shoulder.

She didn't need to type "I love you." The hug said it all.

They stayed like that for a long moment, the registration screen glowing blue in the background, two soldiers in the war against the world, taking a minute to just be brother and sister.

Patrick finally pulled back, grinning. He wiped a smudge of dust off her cheek.

"Alright, Math Tutor," Patrick said, spinning his chair back to his desk. "We have four months until classes start. Teach me how to solve for X, and maybe I'll make you another Fluffernutter."

Nichole smirked. She tapped her screen.

"DEAL."

Patrick pulled back from the hug, but he didn't let go of her shoulders. He looked at her—really looked at her.

For eighteen years, the world had told him that caring for her was a burden. That it was a sacrifice. That he was "losing his childhood."

But looking at the registration screen—seeing that she had deliberately chosen to sit through a remedial math class just to protect him from feeling stupid—he knew the truth.

She wasn't his burden. She was his shield.

He brushed a stray lock of hair behind her ear, his thumb resting gently on her cheekbone.

"Hey, Nikki?" Patrick whispered, his voice thick.

Nichole blinked, looking up at him with those wide, intelligent eyes.

"I love you," Patrick said.

It wasn't a casual "love ya" tossed over a shoulder. It was heavy. It was the kind of "I love you" that meant I would walk through fire for you. I would carry you until my back breaks. I would never, ever leave you behind.

Nichole felt a warmth bloom in her chest that had nothing to do with the summer heat.

She didn't nod. She didn't smile. She reached for her mouse.

She needed the words to be precise. She navigated to her CORE VOCABULARY page. She found the pronouns. She found the verbs.

She clicked.

"I. LOVE. YOU. TOO."

The synthetic voice was flat and robotic, but in the quiet of the loft, it sounded like a choir.

Patrick smiled—a real, crooked, teary-eyed smile. He leaned forward and kissed her forehead.

"Okay," Patrick sniffed, clearing his throat and turning back to his monitors. "Enough mushy stuff. We have a semester to plan."

Nichole watched him type. She tapped her screen one last time, just for herself.

"FOREVER."

## Date: May 15, 2004 Time: 9:30 AM Location: The Command Center, Unit 404

The words "I. LOVE. YOU. TOO." and "FOREVER." were still lingering in the air, heavier and more permanent than the concrete walls of the loft.

In that silence, a silent treaty was signed.

Patrick looked at the semester schedule on his screen. Between the engineering labs, the shared Gen-Ed classes, the grocery runs, the laundry cycles, the bathroom transfers, the shower routines, and the nightly "shutdown sequence," there were no white spaces left in the calendar.

And looking at it, he realized he didn't want there to be.

The world expected him to be a "normal" college guy. To go to mixers at the Student Union. To flirt with girls in the cafeteria. To bring someone home on a Friday night.

But Patrick played the scenario out in his engineer's brain.

- Step 1: Bring girl home.

- Step 2: Explain why his sister sleeps in his bed.

- Step 3: Interrupt the date to carry his sister to the toilet.

- Step 4: Watch the girl look at them with that mix of horror and pity.

He shook his head. He had no interest in auditioning for someone else's approval. He had a full-time job. He was the CEO, the Chief Engineer, and the Head of Security for Bennett Dynamics. The position was filled.

"You know," Patrick said, closing the browser tab for the 'Freshman Mixer' event that had popped up. "We're going to be pretty busy this fall. I don't think I'm going to have time for the social circuit."

Nichole looked at him. She knew exactly what he was saying.

She thought about the last twenty-four hours. She thought about the shower—Patrick standing there naked, washing her back with zero hesitation. She thought about the sofa—lying across his lap, his hand on her hip, feeling safer than she had ever felt in her life.

If she brought a boyfriend into this equation, he would demand boundaries. He would tell her it was "weird" to cuddle with her brother. He would demand that she hire a stranger to help her in the bathroom so Patrick "didn't have to."

He would try to "fix" them.

Or worse: he might try to be the hero.

A cold chill ran down her spine as she imagined a well-meaning boyfriend trying to "take over" Patrick’s duties. She imagined a stranger trying to lift her without knowing her balance points, dropping her on the floor. She imagined someone trying to handle her spasticity with force instead of leverage, bruising her legs. She imagined someone forgetting to check for pressure sores because they were "too shy," leaving her to develop an ulcer that could put her in the hospital.

He would try to "fix" them. And in doing so, he would break her.

No romantic suitor would ever understand that her intimacy with Patrick wasn't sexual—it was survival. It was a level of trust that a boyfriend of three weeks—or three years—could never earn. Patrick didn't just love her; he was calibrated to her.

She didn't want a boyfriend. She wanted her brother.

She looked at the empty third chair in the room—a hypothetical space for an outsider.

She reached for her mouse.

"JUST. US."

Patrick looked at the screen. He nodded, a look of profound relief washing over his face. The pressure to perform "normalcy" was gone.

"Just us," Patrick agreed. "The Closed Loop."

He reached out and fist-bumped her gently.

"No dates. No drama. No explaining ourselves to strangers. We focus on the degree, and we focus on the Machine."

Nichole smiled. It wasn't a tragic vow of celibacy. It was a declaration of independence. They had everything they needed right here in Unit 404.

She tapped her screen one last time to seal the deal.

"BENNETT. PARTY. OF. TWO."

Patrick grinned, spinning his chair back to the monitors.

"Party of two, confirmed. Now... let's look up the syllabus for that Pre-Algebra class. If I have to solve for X, I'm going to need a head start."
