# Part 3: The Edge of the Grid

## Date: April 18, 2004 Time: 12:15 PM Location: Narragansett Esplanade, Pier 2

After decimating the wieners and coffee milk at Buddy’s, Patrick and Nichole rolled back toward the waterfront to digest. The "New York System" meat sauce was sitting heavy, but in a good way—the specific heaviness of a mission accomplished.

They stopped at the railing of the Narragansett Esplanade, the massive concrete boardwalk that ran along the bay, separating the city skyline from the water.

Below them, the water was churning.

A sleek, white-and-gold catamaran was backing into Pier 2. It was significantly larger than the nimble BTA ferries they had taken from Portsmouth. Its engines rumbled with a deep, throaty diesel growl that vibrated through the railing.

On the side of the hull, in bold naval lettering, was the logo:

RIFTBA (Rhode Island Ferry, Turnpike, and Bridge Authority)

"Rift-Ba," Patrick pronounced, reading the acronym. "The Heavy Iron."

Nichole looked at the vessel. It wasn't a commuter bus on water. It was a ship.

"Check the destination board," Patrick pointed.

The digital matrix sign on the stern read: BLOCK ISLAND - OLD HARBOR.

"That's the State run," Patrick explained, his obsession with logistics kicking in. "BTA handles the bay—Newport, Providence, the calm water stuff. But RIFTBA? They handle the bridges and the open ocean. That boat goes twelve miles out into the Atlantic."

He traced the route in the air.

"They run three lines," Patrick listed, downloading the mental schematic.

1.  The Mainline (Point Judith): The big car ferries. The lifeline for trucks and fuel.

2.  The Tourist Run (Newport): Fast ferry from Perrotti Park.

3.  The City Link (Braxton): This one right here. Passenger-only high-speed to the island.

Nichole watched the passengers disembarking. They looked different than the commuters on the subway. They were wearing windbreakers, carrying heavy backpacks and fishing gear. They looked wind-blown. They had just crossed open water.

She looked at the horizon. Beyond the breakwater, the Atlantic Ocean stretched out, gray and endless.

The BTA subway and the tunnels were their "Fortress"—a controlled, climate-controlled, predictable grid. RIFTBA was the "Frontier"—the wild, uncontrollable variable of the open sea.

She wasn't ready for the ocean yet. She liked the tunnels.

She tapped her armrest.

"COOL. BOAT. BUT. WE. STAY. UNDERGROUND."

Patrick laughed. "Agreed. No sea legs for us today. The subway doesn't have 6-foot swells."

He turned the Titan AeroMotion around, facing back toward the city skyline and the entrance to the Narragansett Esplanade Station.

"Recon complete?" Patrick asked. "We have the apartment, the pharmacy, the hardware store, the wieners, and we know where the edge of the map is."

Nichole nodded. She felt full—of food, of information, and of confidence. The city wasn't a terrifying labyrinth anymore. It was just a series of systems, and they had the manual.

"MISSION. COMPLETE. LET'S. GO. HOME."

"Copy that," Patrick said.

They rolled toward the glass elevators of the subway station. They descended into the cool, fluorescent safety of the Blue Line, leaving the wind and the ocean behind.

They were going back to The Foundry. Back to the loft. Back to their space.

For the first time in their lives, "going home" didn't mean going to their parents' house. It meant going to the place where they made the rules.
