# Part 4: The Winter Test

## Date: December 24, 2003 Time: 7:00 PM Location: The Living Room, 95 West Main Road, Portsmouth, Rhode Island

The Nor'easter hit Aquidneck Island just as the sun went down on Christmas Eve.

It wasn't a gentle holiday dusting; it was a brutal, howling blizzard. The wind shrieked off the Narragansett Bay, dumping heavy, wet snow at a rate of two inches per hour. Through the living room's picture window, the custom plexiglass bus shelter Uncle Mark had built was already half-buried in a drift.

Patrick walked into the living room, holding a mug of hot cider. He looked out the window, then down at the local news ticker running across the bottom of the television screen.

"Grid is officially down," Patrick announced to the room. "The Newport Pell Bridge is closed to high-profile vehicles, and OSTA just suspended all bus service. Route 60 is dead until the plows get through."

In the armchair near the fireplace, Grandma Helen adjusted her glasses. "Snowed in," she smiled, not looking the least bit upset. "Just like the old days."

She didn't feel trapped. Just an hour ago, she had taken the newly installed hydraulic elevator from her first-floor bedroom down to the basement to inspect the renovations for Tom and Ellen's new apartment. The house was her grid, and it was fully operational.

Nichole was parked near the Christmas tree, the colorful lights reflecting off the silver chassis of her Apex ConvertiBook.

She heard Patrick’s report about the dead transit lines. A year ago, the thought of being snowed in with a wheelchair would have triggered a spike of claustrophobic anxiety. Snow meant isolation. Snow meant the world shrank to the size of a single room.

Tonight, Nichole just smiled. She didn't look out the window at the blizzard. She looked at her screen.

She wasn't coding in Quantum Studio. She had a high-resolution PDF open. It was the official architectural map of *The Braxton Link*—the massive subterranean pedestrian network that ran beneath the entire city of Downtown Braxton.

But Nichole had zoomed in on one specific section: *The Bull Run*, the dedicated sub-network that connected every single building of Braxton State University directly to the Narragansett Esplanade Station.

Patrick walked over and leaned on the back of her chair, looking at the screen.

"Studying the escape routes?" Patrick asked softly.

Nichole pointed to a massive intersection labeled "The Pit"—the sunken amphitheater where *Sub-University Avenue* met *Sub-College Avenue*.

She tapped her screen.

"NEXT. YEAR. SNOW. DOES. NOT. MATTER."

Patrick followed the blue lines of the map with his finger. "You're right," he whispered. "Look at this connectivity. The Link covers the whole city, but The Bull Run covers our whole world. Every single academic building, from the main library to the maintenance sheds, has a direct, climate-controlled connection."

It was the ultimate realization of the "Mole" lifestyle. In nine months, a blizzard like the one currently burying their front yard would be nothing more than a scenic background through a glass window. They could wake up at The Foundry Lofts, take their apartment elevator down to the basement to enter the city grid, and ride the Metro straight to the campus.

From the Narragansett Esplanade Station concourse, they would enter *The Bull Run* and walk all the way to their classrooms without ever putting on a winter coat.

Nichole leaned her head back against the headrest, closing her eyes. She pictured the wide, smooth concrete tunnels. She pictured the warmth. The safety. The absolute freedom to move regardless of what the weather gods threw at them.

She made a soft, contented *hummm* in her throat.

"Dinner is served!" Aunt Carol called from the kitchen, wiping her hands on an apron. "Feast of the Seven Fishes! Everybody to the table!"

"Come on, Nikki," Patrick said, unlocking the brakes of the Titan AeroMotion. "Let's go eat. The grid will be there in August."

Patrick pushed her toward the dining room. As they rolled, the heavy winter wind battered the brick walls of the Fortress, but inside, the Bennett Machine was safe, warm, and dreaming of the future.

## Date: December 24, 2003 Time: 7:54 PM Location: 95 West Main Road, Portsmouth, Rhode Island

The dining room table was a scene of holiday perfection—empty wine glasses, plates scraped clean of Aunt Carol’s feast, and the warm glow of the Christmas tree in the adjacent room.

Then, with a sharp *POP*, the entire house plunged into absolute darkness.

The silence that followed lasted exactly four seconds.

Then came the roar. Without the ambient hum of the refrigerator or the television, the sheer volume of the Nor'easter slamming against the brick walls of the Fortress was deafening.

"Nobody move!" Uncle Mark’s voice boomed through the dark. "Standby for auto-switch!"

*Ka-CHUNK.* A deep, vibrating thrum resonated from the backyard, shaking the floorboards. The lights flickered back on, but they were dimmer. In the hallways, red-tinted emergency fixtures hummed to life.

"Commercial backup generator," Mark explained, sitting back down as the room relaxed. "Fueled by the main propane tank. The grid prioritizes the SCIF on the second floor, the medical equipment, and the furnace. We have rudimentary heating and basic lighting. But the heavy draw items are dead. That means no electric stove, no washing machine, and no elevator."

Aunt Carol looked at Grandma Helen, a flicker of worry in her eyes. "Mom, your bedroom is on the first floor, right?"

"Right down the hall, dear," Helen smiled, completely unfazed in her silver wheelchair. "I took the elevator down from the basement thirty minutes ago. Mark’s architecture saves the day again."

"Well," Tom said, looking at his watch. "Since the TV is dead and the dishes have to wait for the dishwasher, I say we call it a successful Christmas Eve."

Patrick unlocked Nichole’s brakes. "Agreed. Let's go hunker down."

Patrick pushed Nichole down the dim hallway into the Bennett Wing. Inside their mini-apartment, the baseboard heaters were clicking, pushing warm air into the room to fight off the freezing temperatures outside. The red glow of the emergency lights cast long shadows across the room.

If they were back at Morrison Middle School, a power outage would have been a crisis. If they were at Putney College, trapped in separate dormitories, Nichole would have been terrified, waiting in the dark for a stranger to come check on her.

But here, the system was fully redundant.

Patrick efficiently managed the transfer, lifting her from the purple Titan AeroMotion and settling her directly into the center of their massive king-sized bed.

He helped her out of her heavy holiday sweater and into her softest pajamas. He didn't have to rush, and he didn't have to worry about the cold.

Patrick changed into his own sleep clothes, tossed his heavy plastic ankle brace into the corner, and climbed into the bed next to her. He pulled the heavy winter duvet up to their chins.

Nichole immediately shifted her weight. She rolled onto her side, pressing her back flush against his chest.

Patrick wrapped his left arm around her waist, locking her in close. He draped his heavier leg over hers, anchoring her spastic muscles to prevent them from kicking in the night.

Nichole let out a long, shuddering breath of pure relief.

She listened to the wind howling against the bedroom window. The blizzard was trying to get in. The cold was trying to seep through the glass.

But it couldn't reach her.

She was surrounded by a wall of body heat. Patrick was like a human furnace. He didn't just tolerate sleeping in the same bed; he preferred it. He was naturally protective, and holding her kept his own mind from racing.

Nichole reached down and placed her hand over his where it rested on her waist. She tapped his knuckles twice.

*Thank you.*

"Merry Christmas, Nikki," Patrick whispered into her hair, his breath warm against her neck. "We're safe."

Nichole closed her eyes, the rhythmic thrum of the backup generator vibrating through the mattress, and fell into the deepest, safest sleep of her life.
