# Part 7: The Transit Chase

## Date: October 12, 2004 Time: 6:00 AM Location: Foundry Square Station (Level B1 - The Link)

They left the apartment at 0600 sharp. Patrick locked the heavy steel door of Unit 404 and they took the residential elevator down to the basement level.

They rolled out into The Braxton Link, the subterranean pedestrian highway that connected their building directly to the subway. At this hour, the tunnels were quiet, populated only by early-shift janitors and the hum of ventilation fans.

They reached the Foundry Square Station fare gates.

Patrick pulled out his lanyard. He held his Bull Pass—the BSU student ID hard-coded as a Zone 15 Unlimited LinkPass —against the reader.

BEEP. The gate swung open.

He did the same for Nichole, tapping her card against the sensor. They rolled through without breaking stride.

They took the elevator down to the Blue Line platform. A train was waiting, its doors open, the cars bathed in cool fluorescent light.

They rolled onboard.

"Three stops," Patrick muttered, locking Nichole’s brakes.

The doors hissed shut. The train accelerated into the dark tunnel.

Stop 1: State Center

Stop 2: Market East

Stop 3: Narragansett Esplanade

The ride took six minutes. They stood in silence, the rhythmic thrum-whoosh of the train replacing conversation. Nichole sat with her head back, her eyes closed, conserving energy. Every vibration of the train track sent a tiny phantom sting through her hip, but she didn't groan. She just breathed.

## Time: 6:15 AM Location: Narragansett Esplanade Station (Ferry Terminal)

They exited the subway at the end of the line and took the glass elevator up to the Plaza Level.

The sun was just beginning to crack the horizon, painting the sky in bruises of purple and orange. The air on the waterfront was cold and damp.

They rolled straight to the ferry slip. The Newport Flyer, the BTA’s sleek catamaran harbor ferry, was already boarding.

Patrick tapped their Bull Passes again at the turnstile—seamless, prepaid access.

He pushed Nichole onto the hydraulic gangway. The tide was high, making the ramp perfectly flat. He parked her in the sheltered main cabin, away from the wind.

"Twenty-eight minutes," Patrick said, checking his watch as the engines rumbled to life.

The boat cast off. As they cut across Narragansett Bay, Patrick didn't look at the view. He watched Nichole’s face, looking for the tell-tale grimace of pain.

She was stoic. She was holding the line.

## Time: 6:45 AM Location: Perrotti Park, Newport

The ferry docked at Perrotti Park right on schedule. Patrick pushed her down the ramp and onto the familiar cobblestones of the Newport waterfront.

"One block," Patrick said. "We hit the Gateway and grab the first thing smoking."

They hurried to the Gateway Center, the main bus hub for the island. It was buzzing with the morning rush.

Patrick scanned the digital departure board. The Gateway served the entire of OSTA’s Newport network:

- 60 Providence via West Main Road

- 60 Providence via East Main Road

- 63 Purple Line (Shopping Centers)

- 62 Red Line (Newport Square)

- 64 (Rhode Island State University / Kingston ARTS Station)

- 67 Yellow Line (Newport Mansions)

"There," Patrick pointed.

A massive white-and-blue bus marked 60 PROVIDENCE was idling in Bay 1.

"Go, go, go," Patrick urged, breaking into a jog.

They reached the bus just as the driver was checking his mirrors. Patrick waved. The driver nodded and hit the switch to deploy the ramp.

Patrick pushed Nichole onboard.

The Bull Pass didn't work here—BTA and OSTA were "frenemies" who didn't share fare data. But Patrick was prepared.

He reached into his pocket and pulled out their laminated OSTA Monthly Passes. He flashed them to the driver.

"Newport Hospital," Patrick said, breathless.

"You got it," the driver said. "Broadway and Friendship. Two stops."

Patrick locked the wheelchair down. He sat in the jump seat next to her, his knee pressing against her wheel.

"Almost there, Nikki," he whispered. "We're in the final sector."

The bus roared out of the station, turning onto Broadway. They were ten minutes early. The Machine was running hot, but it was running on time.
