# Part 5: The Downgrade (Day Release)

## **Date:** Tuesday, February 14, 2006 (Valentine's Day) **Time:** 10:00 AM **Location:** The Living Room, Unit 404

The calendar on the wall had a big red circle around **FEB 14**.

It wasn't for Valentine's Day. Neither of them had dates. It was for **"Day Release."**

Dr. Evans had given the order over the phone yesterday: *“Soft tissue healing is sufficient. We need to start loading the joints. Switch to Night Splinting only.”*

Patrick stood in the living room. The wheelchair was parked in the center of the rug. It looked different.

He had spent the morning modifying it.

- **Removed:** The long, elevating leg rests (ELRs) that made the chair look like a recliner.

- **Installed:** The standard, 70-degree swing-away footrests.

The chair was back to its "Active" configuration.

"Okay, Nikki," Patrick said, walking into the bedroom. "Happy Independence Day."

Nichole was lying in bed, still strapped into the **Blue Foam Immobilizers**. She looked at the ceiling, then at him. She didn't look excited. She looked nervous.

For six weeks, those braces had held her knees perfectly straight. She had forgotten how to hold them herself.

"I know," Patrick said, sitting on the edge of the mattress. "It feels safe in there. But if we don't take them off, your quads are going to turn into soup. We need gravity."

He reached for the Velcro straps. *Riiip. Riiip.*

He slid the foam troughs out.

Her legs lay on the sheets, pale and skinny. The scars were pink lines now, not angry red.

"Okay," Patrick said. "I'm going to lift you. Your knees are going to want to buckle because they're weak. Don't fight it. Just let them bend."

He slid his arms under her—the familiar "scoop" transfer.

"One. Two. Up."

He lifted her.

As soon as gravity hit her, Nichole gasped. Her lower legs, no longer supported by the foam or the locking mechanism of her spasticity, swung freely. They felt heavy and loose, like pendulums.

Patrick pivoted. He lowered her into the wheelchair.

## The First Bend

This was the moment of truth.

Usually, when sitting, her tight hamstrings would pull her pelvis forward, making her slide.

Patrick lowered her hips into the back of the seat. He guided her feet onto the footplates.

He knelt down to check the angles.

- **Hips:** 90 degrees. Back against the upholstery.

- **Knees:** 90 degrees. Bent naturally over the edge of the seat.

- **Feet:** Flat on the plastic plates.

He waited for the slide. He waited for the spasm to kick in and push her out of the chair.

It didn't happen.

The hamstrings, lengthened by the surgeon’s knife, surrendered. They allowed the bend.

Nichole gripped the armrests. She felt... distinct. She wasn't fighting the chair. She was just *sitting*.

Patrick looked up at her, grinning.

"Look at that," he whispered. "Ninety-ninety-ninety. The Golden Ratio."

He grabbed the Letter Board from the side table and held it up.

"Verdict?"

Nichole took a shaky breath. She looked at her knees. They looked small and knobby without the foam, but they were bent.

She tapped the board.

**W - E - I - R - D**

She paused, moving her finger to the green tape.

**P - A - I - N ?**

She hesitated. There was a dull ache behind her knees—the feeling of the tendons being stretched in a new way—but it wasn't the sharp, tearing pain of before.

She tapped **N - O**.

Then she spelled:

**L - O - O - S – E**

"Loose is good," Patrick said, standing up and locking the footplates in place. "Loose means we won. But now comes the hard part."

He pointed to the blue foam braces lying on the bed.

"You have to wear those every single night."

Nichole glared at the braces. She hated them. She tapped the board violently.

**F - O - R - E - V - E - R ?**

Patrick shook his head.

"No. Not forever. Just until the muscle memory resets. Your hamstrings have spent twenty years thinking 'short' is normal. We have to teach them that 'long' is the new normal."

He held up a finger.

"Dr. Evans said **one year**. If we keep them straight every night for a year, the brain will finally stop fighting us. Then we can burn them."

Nichole looked at the braces. *One year.*

365 nights.

It was a long time. But it wasn't forever. It was a lease, not a mortgage.

She looked at Patrick. She held up one finger. *One year.*

She tapped **O - K**.

"Good," Patrick said. "Now, since you are officially 'Day Release,' we have a new exercise."

He walked behind the chair and unlocked the brakes.

"We are going to the kitchen. And you are going to sit at the table. Not the recliner. The actual table."

He pushed her.

The chair moved differently now. Without the long leg rests sticking out five feet in front, the turning radius was tight. He spun her 360 degrees in the hallway just to show off.

"Look at that cornering!" Patrick laughed. "We're a sports car again. No more bus turns."

Nichole smiled. She felt light. She felt fast.

She looked down at her "new legs." They were weak. They couldn't walk. They couldn't run.

But for the first time in twenty years, they weren't fighting her.

She tapped the armrest with her finger. *Let's go.*



Date: September 12, 2006 (Junior Year) Location: Dean of Engineering’s Conference Room, Braxton State University Present: Dr. Aristhorne (Engineering Dept. Head), Dr. Vance (English Dept. Head), Patrick, and Nichole.

The table was covered in schematics.

On one side sat Dr. Aristhorne, a man who believed if you couldn't build it out of concrete, it wasn't real engineering. On the other sat Dr. Vance, who believed if you couldn't deconstruct the narrative, it wasn't real writing.

Patrick stood at the whiteboard. Nichole was parked at the head of the table, her *ConvertiBook* connected to the room’s projector.

"So," Dr. Aristhorne grunted, looking at the file. "You want to combine ECE-401 and ENG-490. Two different colleges. Two different grading rubrics. Into one project."

"Yes, sir," Patrick said calmly.

"It's highly irregular," Dr. Vance added, adjusting her glasses. "Usually, technical writing students document software or policy. Hardware documentation is... messy."

Patrick looked at Nichole. She tapped her screen.

PROJECTOR TEXT: "IN THE REAL WORLD, HARDWARE WITHOUT DOCUMENTATION IS A PAPERWEIGHT. DOCUMENTATION WITHOUT HARDWARE IS FICTION."

Dr. Vance suppressed a smile. "She has a point."

"The device," Patrick continued, pointing to the schematic, "is the Bennett Haptic Interface. It translates my sister's involuntary muscle spasms into digital data. It tells me she is hurting before she can even type the word 'Pain'."

He paused, letting the weight of that sink in.

"But the engineering is useless if the caregiver doesn't know how to interpret the data. If I build this and get hit by a bus tomorrow, who explains it to the next caregiver?"

He gestured to Nichole.

"The Engineer solves the problem," Patrick said. "The Writer ensures the solution survives the Engineer."

Dr. Aristhorne looked at the schematic. He saw the complex circuit design. He saw the ambitious coding requirements for the signal processing.

"And you're building a working prototype?" Aristhorne asked. "Not just a simulation?"

"I'm retrofitting her chair this semester," Patrick said. "We will have live telemetry data by December."

"And you, Ms. Bennett," Dr. Vance asked. "You are writing the manual?"

Nichole typed rapidly.

PROJECTOR TEXT: "I AM THE BETA TESTER AND THE LEAD WRITER. I AM THE ONLY PERSON QUALIFIED TO TRANSLATE THE MACHINE'S LANGUAGE INTO HUMAN LANGUAGE."

Dr. Aristhorne looked at Dr. Vance.

"If they pull this off," Aristhorne muttered, "it's patentable."

"If they pull this off," Vance replied, "it's publishable."

Aristhorne picked up his pen. He signed the waiver form.

"Approved," Aristhorne said. "But God help you both if the prototype fails. If the hardware doesn't work, there's nothing to write about, and you both fail."

Patrick looked at Nichole. They shared a look of absolute, terrifying confidence.

"It will work," Patrick said.
