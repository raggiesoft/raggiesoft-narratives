# Part 5: The Discharge

## **Date:** Tuesday, December 27, 2005 **Time:** 6:00 PM **Location:** Phase II Recovery (Step-Down Unit), Bed 12

The late afternoon light had long since vanished, leaving the hospital window a square of pitch-black darkness reflecting the room’s fluorescent lights. The snow had stopped falling, but the roads below were slick and wet, shining under the streetlamps.

Dr. Evans had kept Nichole two hours longer than the standard protocol.

It wasn't a surgical complication. It was **Orthostatic Hypotension**. Around 4:00 PM, when they had tried to sit her up for the first time, her blood pressure had bottomed out (90/50), and she had gone pale. The combination of the anesthesia, the Morphine, and the sudden shift in gravity was too much for her system.

Dr. Evans had ordered: *"Lay her flat. Fluids wide open. Try again in two hours."*

Now, at 18:00, the second attempt was successful. Her BP was stable at 110/70. She was sitting up, chewing on ice chips, and the oral Hydrocodone was holding the line against the fire in her hamstrings.

Sarah walked in with a thick packet of paperwork and a pair of scissors.

"Green light from Dr. Evans," Sarah announced, smiling tiredly. "BP is holding steady. Discharge orders are signed. You guys are parolees."

Patrick stood up from his command post in the corner. He didn't just grab his bag. He walked to the bedside to oversee the **"De-tethering Protocol."**

"Okay, Nichole," Sarah said. "I'm going to take this IV out. Deep breath."

She snipped the tape. She pulled the catheter.

Patrick watched the site. He watched for the hematoma.

"Hold that," Sarah said, handing him the gauze.

Patrick pressed down firmly on the vein. He held it for a full two minutes—ignoring the urge to rush. He peeked. The bleeding had stopped. He applied the Band-Aid himself.

"Hemostasis achieved," Patrick murmured.

## The Chair Prep

Next came the logistics.

Patrick rolled Nichole’s personal manual wheelchair into the room. It wasn't in its standard configuration. He had modified it specifically for this night.

- **Leg Rests:** He had installed **Elevating Leg Rests (ELRs)**—long, padded troughs that could be raised to horizontal.

- **Cushion:** He had swapped her standard gel cushion for a **Roho Air Cushion** to distribute pressure better during the bumpy ride.

"Okay, Sarah," Patrick said. "I need one assist for a Slide Board transfer. Her knees are locked at zero degrees. We can't do a pivot."

"Got it," Sarah said. She grabbed the smooth plastic transfer board from the wall hook.

Patrick lowered the hospital bed until it was slightly higher than the wheelchair seat (gravity assist). He removed the armrest of the wheelchair.

He slid the board under Nichole’s hip.

"Okay, Nikki," Patrick said, leaning close to her ear. "We're going to slide. Do not try to help. Do not try to bend. You are a statue."

Nichole gripped the chest of his hoodie. Her eyes were wide, the Vicodin making her pupils pinpricks, but she nodded.

"One. Two. Slide."

Patrick controlled her hips. Sarah guided her rigid, heavy legs.

She slid across the board like a piece of cargo.

Patrick caught her hips as she hit the wheelchair seat. He immediately adjusted her position, ensuring her sacrum was all the way back to prevent slumping.

"Legs coming up," Sarah said.

She lifted the blue foam-encased legs and placed them gently onto the elevated calf pads. They stuck straight out, like two pylons.

Patrick checked the alignment.

- **Hips:** 90 degrees.

- **Knees:** 0 degrees (Straight).

- **Ankles:** Neutral.

"Perfect alignment," Patrick judged. "Good transfer."

## The Paperwork

Sarah handed Patrick the discharge folder.

"Okay, Patrick. You know the drill. Watch for fever over 101. Ice 20 on, 20 off. Keep the legs elevated."

She paused, looking at the standard discharge sheet that usually said *'Do Not Drive or Operate Heavy Machinery for 24 Hours.'* She glanced at Nichole’s wheelchair and gave a small, knowing smile.

"I'm guessing the 'No Driving' rule is a moot point," Sarah said, "but technically, I have to say it. No making major life decisions while on the narcotics."

She handed him a second, smaller envelope.

"And here is the hard copy prescription for the breakthrough meds. Percocet 5/325. Dr. Evans wrote for thirty tabs."

Patrick took the envelope. He didn't just stuff it in his pocket. He opened the binder. He punched holes in the envelope and clipped it securely into the **"Pharmacy"** tab.

"Received," Patrick said. "Is the outpatient pharmacy in the lobby still open? It's after six."

"They're open until eight," Sarah confirmed. "Ground floor, past the gift shop. Much easier than stopping somewhere in this weather."

"Agreed," Patrick said. "We'll fill it on the extraction route. Minimize the stops."

He extended a hand to Sarah.

"Thanks for the assist with the blood pressure scare, Sarah. You run a good floor."

Sarah shook his hand firmly. "You're a good nurse, Patrick. She's lucky to have you. Drive safe. The roads are wet out there."

## The Exit

Patrick grabbed the push handles.

"Okay, Boss," he whispered. "Let's blow this joint."

He pushed her out of the room, past the nurses' station where the night shift was just clocking in.

They took the elevator down to the ground floor. The doors opened to the lobby, which smelled of floor wax and old coffee.

As they rolled toward the automatic sliding doors, the cold December air hit them. It was biting, sharp, and smelled of wet pavement.

Patrick stopped for a second to zip up Nichole’s jacket all the way to her chin. He pulled a thick wool blanket from his bag and tucked it tightly around her exposed toes sticking out of the casts.

"Phase One complete," Patrick said, checking the time. *6:00 PM.*

Normally, this would be the time they headed to the Narragansett Esplanade Station to catch the Blue Line. Three stops to Foundry Square. Easy.

But not tonight. Tonight, the Metro was forbidden. The vibration, the gap between the train and platform, the crowds—too much risk.

"No train tonight," Patrick said, leaning down. "Executive transport only. Now for the transfer from hell."

He pushed her through the doors and into the concrete echo of the parking garage, aiming for the van.
