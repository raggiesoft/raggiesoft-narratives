# Part 1: Engine Failure

## Date: February 10, 2005 (Freshman Year) Time: 6:15 AM Location: The Master Suite, Unit 404, The Foundry Lofts

The alarm on the bedside table hummed its soft, gradual wake-up tone.

Patrick opened his eyes, and the room violently tilted to the left.

It wasn't a gentle dizziness; it was a sickening, hydraulic lurch, as if the entire Foundry building had just slid off its foundation.

He squeezed his eyes shut, gripping the sheets. *Earthquake?*

No. The floor wasn't moving. *He* was moving.

His head throbbed with a dull, pressurized ache behind his eyes, and his throat felt like he had swallowed a handful of sand.

Then came the cold.

Despite the heavy winter duvet and the body heat of his sister pressed against his side, Patrick was freezing. A violent, teeth-chattering shudder ripped through his body, shaking the mattress.

*Flu,* his brain registered sluggishly. *Bad flu.*

He instinctively reached out to check on Nichole. His hand brushed her bare arm.

To him, her skin felt like ice. To her, his hand felt like a branding iron.

Nichole stirred immediately. She turned her head on the pillow. Even in the grey pre-dawn light, she saw it.

Patrick’s face was pale, slick with a sheen of sweat, yet he was shivering. His eyes were glassy and unfocused.

She made a soft, distressed sound. *Ungh?*

"I'm good," Patrick croaked. His voice was a wrecked whisper, a sound that scraped against the air. "Just... just a cold."

He tried to sit up. The room spun again, a kaleidoscope of grey shadows. He planted his hand on the nightstand to steady himself, knocking over a bottle of water. It clattered to the floor.

He ignored it.

*The Oath,* he told himself. *I promised. No days off. The Machine does not get sick.*

"Transfer time," Patrick wheezed, throwing the heavy duvet back.

The exposure to the 72-degree room air hit his fever-spiked skin like a blast from an airlock. He gasped, his teeth clicking together uncontrollably.

He stood up. His knees buckled, soft as rubber, but he locked them out. He grabbed the edge of the mattress for support.

He walked around the foot of the bed to Nichole’s side. He moved like a marionette with tangled strings—stiff, jerky, and uncoordinated.

"Shower," Patrick mumbled, his eyes unable to focus on her face. "Let's get you... let's get you clean for class."

Nichole stared at him. She was wide awake now. She saw the tremor in his hands—a fine, high-frequency vibration. She felt the heat radiating off his body from two feet away.

She shook her head against the pillow. *No.*

"I got you," Patrick insisted, his voice taking on a desperate, stubborn edge. "I can do it. Arms up, Nikki."

He bent down. He slid his left arm under her knees and his right arm behind her back.

He engaged his core. He lifted.

Usually, the deadlift was effortless. Patrick was nineteen, broad-shouldered, and conditioned by years of transfers.

But today, the virus had drained his glycogen. As he pulled her weight up from the mattress, his vision grayed out. The periphery of the room dissolved into static.

He swayed.

He took a step back to catch his balance, but his heel caught on the edge of the rug.

For one terrifying second, gravity won. He started to tip backward.

*Don't drop her. Don't drop her.*

He twisted his body, throwing his weight to the side. He slammed his shoulder hard into the doorframe of the bathroom, using the wall to arrest the fall.

He groaned, the impact jarring his aching bones.

He hadn't dropped her. He was still holding her tight against his chest, her bare skin pressed against his sweating neck. But he was leaning heavily against the wall, sliding down an inch, gasping for air.

Nichole wasn't scared for herself. She was terrified for *him*.

She could feel his heart hammering against her ribs like a trapped bird—fast, erratic, and pounding. He was burning up. He was shaking so hard her own teeth were rattling.

And he was *still* trying to push off the wall to get to the shower.

"Almost... there," Patrick panted, his eyes squeezed shut against the dizziness.

Nichole slammed her hand against his chest. *STOP.*

She made a loud, angry vocalization. *AAH!*

She threw her weight backward, shifting their combined center of gravity back toward the bed. She forced him to sit down.

Patrick collapsed onto the edge of the mattress, still holding her. He sat there, head hanging, chest heaving, defeated by his own biology.

"I can do it," he whispered, tears of frustration mixing with the sweat on his face. "I swore, Nikki. I swore I wouldn't leave you dirty."

Nichole reached up. She placed her cool hand on his burning forehead.

She pushed his head back until he looked at her.

She shook her head firmly. *No.*

She pointed to the pillows. *Down.*

"But the shower..."

She pointed to the bed again. It wasn't a suggestion. It was an order from the Boss.

She wiggled out of his arms, sliding onto the mattress. She pulled the heavy duvet up, covering herself. Then she patted the empty space next to her.

Patrick looked at the pillow. It looked like the only thing in the world that mattered.

"Just for a minute," Patrick slurred, his eyes rolling back. "Just... a quick recharge."

He fell back against the pillows. He didn't even pull his legs up. He was out before his head hit the foam.

## Date: February 10, 2005 (Freshman Year) Time: 10:15 AM Location: The Master Suite, Unit 404, The Foundry Lofts

Patrick woke up to a clicking sound.

*Click. Click. Click.*

He cracked one eye open. Nichole was sitting up in bed next to him, propped up against the headboard. She had dragged her Apex ConvertiBook onto her lap from the bedside swing-arm.

She was typing.

Patrick tried to sit up. "Class," he rasped. "Western Civ..."

He looked at the screen. Nichole had a browser window open. It was the BSU Faculty Email directory.

TO: Professor Dubois FROM: Nichole Bennett SUBJECT: ABSENCE - MEDICAL EMERGENCY

PROFESSOR,

PATRICK AND I WILL NOT BE IN CLASS TODAY. MY CARE TEAM IS DOWN. THE ENGINE HAS OVERHEATED.

WE WILL GET THE NOTES FROM THE TA. DO NOT WORRY. WE ARE SAFE.

\- NICHOLE

Patrick read the email. *My care team is down.*

"I failed," Patrick whispered, closing his eyes. "I broke the streak."

Nichole stopped typing. She reached over and tapped his arm. She hit the 'Speak' button.

"YOU. ARE. HUMAN. NOT. A. ROBOT."

"I'm supposed to be the machine," Patrick mumbled.

"THE. MACHINE. NEEDS. MAINTENANCE," Nichole typed. "YOU. MAINTAIN. ME. TODAY. I. MAINTAIN. YOU."

She pointed to the nightstand.

There, sitting in a neat row, were three items she had managed to organize while he slept:

1.  A bottle of water (she had dragged it from his backpack).

2.  The bottle of Ibuprofen (she couldn't open it, but she had put it there as a signal).

3.  Her mouse.

She tapped the mouse.

"OPEN. THE. BOTTLE. THEN. SLEEP."

Patrick looked at the bottle. He looked at her. She wasn't helpless. She had emailed the professor. She had triaged the situation. She had recognized that *he* was the patient today.

He sat up, his hands shaking violently. He managed to pop the cap on the medicine. He took three. He drank the water, the cool liquid soothing his raw throat.

He slumped back, but his eyes stayed on her. He saw the way she was shifting her weight. He knew the signs.

"What about you?" Patrick asked, his voice thick with worry. He looked at her bare shoulders; she was still following the Zero-Friction protocol. "Bathroom? Food?"

He tried to push himself up again. "I can get you to the toilet..."

Nichole shook her head. She pointed to the bed. *Stay.*

"No," Patrick insisted, his stubbornness fighting through the fever fog. "I promised. No days off. I can do it."

He threw the covers back. The cold air hit his fever-spiked skin like a physical blow, sending a fresh wave of shudders through his frame.

He stood up. His knees buckled slightly, but he locked them. He walked around the bed to Nichole’s side. He moved like a zombie—mechanical, stiff, and slow.

"Arms up, Nikki," he wheezed. "Let's get you... let's get you clean."

Nichole stared at him. She saw the tremor in his hands. She felt the heat radiating off his body from two feet away.

She shook her head again. *No.*

"I got you," Patrick insisted, his voice taking on a desperate, frantic edge. "I can do it. Arms up."

He leaned down. He slid his arms under her knees and back.

He lifted.

Usually, the deadlift was effortless. Patrick was strong. But today, the virus had sapped his glycogen. As he pulled her weight up, his vision grayed out. Black spots danced in his peripheral vision.

He swayed.

He took a step back to catch his balance, his heel catching on the rug. For one terrifying second, gravity won. He started to tip backward.

*Don't drop her. Don't drop her.*

He slammed his shoulder into the doorframe, using the wall to arrest the fall. He groaned, the impact jarring his aching bones.

He hadn't dropped her. He was still holding her tight against his chest. But he was leaning against the wall, gasping for air, sweat dripping off his nose onto her shoulder.

Nichole wasn't scared for herself. She was terrified for *him*.

She could feel his heart hammering against her ribs like a trapped bird. He was burning up. He was shaking so hard her teeth were rattling.

And he was *still* trying to take a step toward the bathroom.

"Almost... there," Patrick panted, pushing off the wall.

Nichole slammed her hand against his chest. *STOP.*

She made a loud, angry vocalization. *AAH!*

She threw her weight backward, shifting the center of gravity back toward the bed. She forced him to sit down.

Patrick collapsed onto the edge of the mattress, still holding her. He sat there, head hanging, chest heaving.

"I can do it," he whispered, tears of frustration pricking his eyes. "I swore, Nikki. I swore I wouldn't leave you dirty."

Nichole reached up. She placed her cool hand on his burning forehead.

She looked him in the eye. She shook her head firmly.

She pointed to the bed. *Down.*

"But the shower..."

She pointed to the bed again. It wasn't a suggestion. It was an order from the Boss.

She wiggled out of his arms, sliding onto the mattress. She pulled the heavy duvet up over him.

She typed quickly on her screen.

"YOU. CANNOT. STAND. IF. YOU. DROP. ME. WE. BOTH. BREAK."

Patrick flinched. She was right. He was dizzy just sitting up. If he tried to deadlift her again, he might pass out mid-transfer.

"But you can't just... wait," Patrick argued, his voice weak. "It's going to be hours. You need to go."

Nichole looked at the bathroom door. She looked at her wheelchair parked by the bed. She calculated the distances.

She looked back at Patrick.

"I. AM. AN. ENGINEER," she typed. "I. WILL. SOLVE. IT."

"How?"

"SLEEP. ASSET. THAT. IS. AN. ORDER."

Patrick looked at her determination. He didn't have the strength to fight her, and the Ibuprofen was starting to pull him under.

"Okay," Patrick whispered. "Just... wake me if it goes wrong."

He closed his eyes. The Bennett Machine didn't break. It just switched command.

## Time: 10:30 AM Location: The Master Suite

Patrick was out cold. The Advil had knocked the edge off the fever, but his breathing was heavy and congested. He hadn't moved in twenty minutes.

Nichole sat up in bed. She looked at the clock.

10:30 AM.

Her bladder was full.

In the previous version of her life—before the Loft, before the "Zero Friction" sleep protocol—this would have been a crisis. She would have had to wake him. She would have had to watch him stumble, risking a fall, just to help her with a zipper or a transfer.

But she looked down at herself. She was naked.

She looked at the bed. The height was perfectly calibrated to her wheelchair seat height—a decision Patrick had insisted upon during the move-in.

She looked at her Titan AeroMotion. It was parked right next to the bed, brakes locked.

She didn't need to wake him. She just needed to be careful.

## The Transfer

She scooted to the edge of the mattress. Her spasticity was manageable because she was warm and loose from the sleep.

She reached out and grabbed the armrest of her wheelchair.

*Pivot.*

She didn't have the core strength to stand, but she had the upper body strength to slide. She used the "depression transfer" technique Patrick had helped her practice for emergencies. She pushed down on the mattress, lifting her hips, and swung her body across the gap.

She landed in the seat. It wasn't graceful—she was crooked—but she was in.

She grabbed the push rim. She pulled herself upright.

She looked at the seat cushion. It was her ROHO air cushion. If she had an accident on it, it was washable rubber. But she wanted to be clean.

She reached for the clean towel Patrick had left on the nightstand (part of the "morning prep" he had failed to complete). She tucked it under her hips.

## The Bathroom

She unlocked the brakes. She rolled silently out of the bedroom and into the master bath.

The door was wide—36 inches. She glided through.

She approached the toilet. It was a comfort-height model with a heavy-duty grab bar mounted on the wall next to it.

*Transfer Two.*

She locked the brakes. She grabbed the bar. She slid from the chair to the toilet seat.

She sat there for a moment, breathing hard. She had done it. No help. No lifting.

She took care of business.

## The Hardware Failure

This was the hard part.

Usually, Patrick wiped her. Her range of motion was limited; reaching behind her back was difficult and often triggered a spasm in her shoulder.

She reached for the toilet paper. She managed to grab a bunch, but her fine motor skills made folding it impossible. She just had a wad of paper.

She tried to reach. She strained, her arm shaking. She managed a partial wipe, but it wasn't thorough. She dropped the paper in the bowl.

She tried again.

It wasn't enough. She knew she wasn't perfectly clean. The realization stung—a sharp reminder of her physical reality. Without Patrick, the job was messy.

She looked at the standard toilet seat.

*I need water,* she thought. *I need a machine to do what my hands can't.*

She grabbed the towel she had brought. She patted herself dry as best she could, but she knew she would have to rely on the towel as a barrier to keep the wheelchair cushion clean.

It wasn't dignified, but it was functional.

## The Return

She reversed the process.

Toilet to Wheelchair. (She landed on the towel, ensuring any residual moisture didn't touch her cushion).

She flushed the toilet with her elbow.

She rolled back to the bedside. Patrick was still asleep, mumbling something about "thermodynamics" in his fever dream.

She locked the brakes.

She transferred back onto the bed.

She didn't put pajamas on. That was too much work, and frankly, unnecessary. The duvet was warm.

She pulled the covers up. She scooted close to him, pressing her cool skin against his fever-hot arm to help regulate his temperature.

She reached for her Apex ConvertiBook on the swing arm. She opened the document she had been typing.

STATUS REPORT: 1. TOILETING: COMPLETE (SOLO). 2. HYGIENE: SUB-OPTIMAL. MANUAL WIPE FAILED. 3. PATIENT (PATRICK): STABLE.

She looked at him. He woke up, blinking groggily. He looked at the empty water bottle he had drained earlier.

"Bathroom?" he croaked, trying to sit up. "Nikki... do you need..."

Nichole shook her head. She pointed to the screen.

"ALREADY. DONE."

Patrick stared at her. "You... you went by yourself?"

"I. USED. THE. TOWEL. BUT. IT. WAS. HARD."

She clicked the mouse again, typing a new line.

"HANDS. CANNOT. REACH. WE. NEED. AN. UPGRADE."

Patrick squinted at the screen, confused. "Upgrade?"

"I. NEED. A. BIDET," she typed. "IF. I. HAD. WATER. JETS. I. WOULD. NOT. NEED. HANDS."

Patrick let out a long breath, his head falling back onto the pillow. He processed the logic through the fog of his fever. She had managed the transfer, but the hygiene was the bottleneck.

"Bidet," Patrick whispered. "Washlet... yeah. That makes sense."

He looked at her with profound respect. She hadn't just survived the gap in care; she had analyzed it and proposed an engineering solution.

"I'll order one," he rasped. "Soon as I can see straight."

"GOOD," Nichole typed. "NOW. SLEEP. OR. I. WILL. SEDATE. YOU."

Patrick smiled weakly. "Yes, ma'am."

He closed his eyes. The Bennett Machine hadn't just survived the fever; it had identified a design flaw and scheduled a patch.

Chapter 4: The License

Part 1: The Status Update

Date: September 15, 2005

Time: 9:02 AM

Location: The Command Center (Second Bedroom), Unit 404, The Foundry Lofts

The air in the second bedroom was stale. Patrick hadn't opened a window in forty-eight hours.

He sat at his L-shaped desk, his leg bouncing in a rapid, nervous rhythm that vibrated through the floorboards. On the screen of his desktop computer, the web browser was open to the National Council of State Boards of Nursing portal.

STATUS: PENDING

It had been two days since he sat for the NCLEX-PN. He had answered the minimum number of questions—85 items—before the computer shut off.

To a nursing student, the "shut off at 85" meant one of two things:

1\. You were so brilliant the algorithm knew you passed.

2\. You were so dangerous the algorithm knew you failed.

There was no middle ground.

"Stop refreshing," Nichole typed.

She was parked next to him at her station, working on a technical writing assignment for her Documentation Design class. But she wasn't working; she was watching him.

Patrick took his hand off the mouse. He wiped his sweaty palm on his jeans.

"If I failed," Patrick muttered, staring at the screen, "I have to wait forty-five days to retest. That pushes the license to November. That leaves us exposed for two months."

Nichole reached out and tapped his bouncing knee. She clicked her mouse.

"YOU. DID. NOT. FAIL."

"I don't know, Nikki. The pharmacology questions were brutal. And that prioritized triage scenario? I might have killed the hypothetical patient."

"YOU. KNOW. THE. CODE," Nichole insisted. "YOU. LIVED. THE. BOOK."

Patrick looked at her. She was right. He hadn't just studied the textbooks; he had been running clinical simulations in this apartment for nineteen years.

He looked back at the screen. The auto-refresh timer counted down.

3... 2... 1...

The page blinked. It reloaded.

Patrick leaned in, his breath hitching in his throat.

EXAM: NCLEX-PN

DATE: 09/13/2005

STATUS: DELIVERY SUCCESSFUL

RESULT: PASS

He froze. He read it again. PASS.

Then, he scrolled down to the license verification section.

RHODE ISLAND DEPARTMENT OF HEALTH

LICENSEE: PATRICK BENNETT

LICENSE TYPE: LICENSED PRACTICAL NURSE

LICENSE NUMBER: LPN-\[XXXXXXXX\]

STATUS: ACTIVE

Patrick let out a sound that was half-laugh, half-sob. He slumped back in his chair, the tension snapping like a cut wire.

"I got it," he whispered. "It's active. I'm licensed."

Nichole let out a high-pitched squeal of victory. She slammed her hand on her lap tray.

"MY. MEDIC."

Patrick stood up. He walked to the printer. He hit Print.

The machine whirred. A moment later, a warm sheet of paper slid out. It was just a temporary verification, a piece of internet printout, but to Patrick, it looked like a shield of Valyrian steel.

He picked it up. He looked at Nichole.

"Do you know what this is?" Patrick asked, his voice shaking slightly.

He wasn't holding a job offer. He wasn't holding a pay raise.

"This is a restraining order," Patrick said fiercely. "For every Housing Director, every Dean, and every social worker who ever looked at me and said I wasn't 'qualified.'"

He walked over to her. He knelt down, holding the paper up so she could see the word ACTIVE.

"They can't touch us now, Nikki. If they try to separate us, I'm not a brother complaining. I'm a nurse filing a clinical objection."

Nichole looked at the paper. She saw the license number.

She remembered Dr. Aris at Putney College calling him a liability. She remembered the intake coordinator at Roosevelt calling him a barrier to independence.

She looked at her brother. He had gone to engineering school all day, then gone to the hospital and emptied bedpans and scrubbed wounds all night, just to get this piece of paper.

He had professionalized his love so that no one could ever delegitimize it again.

She reached out and took the paper. She held it to her chest.

She clicked her mouse.

"PROMOTED."

Patrick grinned, wiping his eyes. "Yeah. Promoted."

He stood up and grabbed his backpack. He pulled out the patch he had bought at the bookstore three months ago, waiting for this moment.

It was a simple white patch with blue lettering.

PATRICK BENNETT, LPN

He placed it on the desk.

"Bennett Dynamics has a Medical Division," Patrick announced. "And we are officially open for business."
