# Part 2: The Vow

Patrick gently pulled her shirt back down. He picked up a fresh pillow and wedged it between her side and the armrest of the chair to splint the ribs.

Then, he didn't stand up. He sank down to his knees on the carpet, putting himself at her eye level.

He took both of her hands in his. His grip was firm—the "Anchor Grip" they used when her spasticity was bad.

"I promised you under the tree," Patrick said, his voice cracking. "But I'm saying it again here. In the light."

Nichole looked at him. His eyes were red-rimmed. He hadn't slept. She knew he had spent the night sitting in the hallway outside her bedroom door, listening to make sure she kept breathing.

"I let the gap get too big," Patrick said. "I let the crowd get between us. I thought... I thought the 'system' would work. I thought the teachers would stop them. I thought people would see you."

He shook his head, a dark laugh escaping his lips.

"They didn't see you. They don't look down."

Nichole squeezed his hands. She tapped her index finger against his palm. *TALL. WORLD.*

"Yeah," Patrick whispered. "It's a tall world. And they are blind."

He leaned forward, pressing his forehead against their joined hands.

"So here is the new rule," Patrick stated. "The Two-Second Rule. From now on, I am never more than two seconds away from you. Not in a crowd. Not in a classroom. Not in a parking lot."

He looked up, locking eyes with her. The intensity in his gaze was terrifying and comforting all at once. It was the look of a soldier guarding a perimeter.

"And no more 'privacy' nonsense," Patrick said, his voice dropping to a hard, clinical register. "If you go to the bathroom, I'm not waiting in the hall. I'm coming in. I'm locking the door. And I'm watching you."

He tightened his grip on her hands.

"I don't care if it's awkward. I don't care if people think it's weird. If you seize in a stall, you can't scream. If you slip, you can't catch yourself. The only warning I get is the look on your face. So I am keeping my eyes on your face."

Nichole looked at him. She thought about the terror of being under the bleachers, unable to vocalize, unable to signal. She realized that modesty was a dangerously high price to pay for safety. She didn't want privacy. She wanted to survive.

She nodded slowly. *Agreed.*

"If the aisle is too narrow," Patrick continued, remembering the tour of the high school gym, "then I make the aisle wider. I don't care who yells. You are never going to be on the floor again, Nikki. I will carry you before I let you touch the ground. You are not debris. You are the most important thing in the world."

Nichole felt the tears finally spill over. She looked at the bruises on her arms. She felt the ache in her ribs. She felt the terrifying fragility of her own skeleton.

She realized she couldn't do this alone. She couldn't navigate the stampede of life—the colleges, the cities, the crowds—without a shield.

She freed one hand. She reached out and touched Patrick’s face. She traced the line of his jaw.

She didn't need the machine. She mouthed the word, her lips forming the shape clearly.

*PROMISE.*

"I promise," Patrick choked out. "The Bennett Machine. Closed loop. Eyes on. Just us."

Nichole nodded. She leaned forward, wincing as her ribs protested, and rested her forehead against his.

They stayed like that for a long time, kneeling on the carpet in the quiet house, while the world outside rushed on without them. The bruises would heal, turning yellow and green before fading. But the psychological architecture had shifted permanently. The Fortress wasn't a building anymore. It was Patrick.
