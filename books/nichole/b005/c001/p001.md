# Part 1: The Breach

## Date: June 14, 2003 Location: Northwood High School Gymnasium, Virginia Beach, VA Event: Class of 2003 Commencement

The air in the gymnasium was thick enough to chew. It smelled of floor wax, corsage flowers, and the nervous sweat of four hundred teenagers.

The Northwood High School gymnasium was draped in Purple and Gold. Banners hung from the rafters, celebrating the "Home of the Lions."

The band, seated on the far risers, raised their instruments. The conductor brought his baton down.

The Music: *Pomp and Circumstance, March No. 1.*

The heavy double doors at the rear of the gym swung open. The Class of 2003 began their slow, dignified march.

Patrick Bennett stood tall in his purple gown. His gold tassel bobbed against his cheek. His hands were firmly gripped on the push handles of the *Titan AeroMotion*.

In front of him, Nichole sat perfectly still. She was wearing the matching purple gown, her gold honor cords draped carefully over her lap to keep them from tangling in the wheels. She couldn't look behind her to see him, but she tapped her armrest once. *Here we go.*

They moved in perfect sync with the person in front of them. Step, pause. Step, pause.

It was orderly. It was solemn. It was the moment they had fought for.

"Looking good, Bennett," a guy whispered from the line next to them.

Patrick smiled. "You too, man."

They reached the halfway point of the gym floor. The parents in the bleachers were standing, craning their necks, cameras flashing like lightning bugs.

Then, a low roar started from the back of the gym.

It wasn't applause. It was the sound of a barrier failing.

At the main entrance—the doors designated for *Spectators Only*—a security guard was arguing with a large man in a polo shirt. The man shouted something about "saving seats." He shoved past the guard.

That was the signal.

Like a dam breaking, the crowd outside realized the doors were unguarded. Hundreds of parents, siblings, and grandparents—desperate to claim the limited bleacher seating—surged forward.

"Hey!" a teacher shouted, waving his arms. "Wait! The graduates aren't seated!"

Nobody listened.

The mob burst through the doors. They weren't looking at the students in purple gowns. They were looking at the empty spots on the wooden benches. They ran.

The orderly lines of graduates began to buckle.

"Patrick?" Nichole’s head jerked to the side. She sensed the vibration of the stampede through the floor before she heard it.

"Hold on," Patrick said, his grip tightening on the rubber handles. "Just keep moving forward."

But the wave was too fast.

A group of parents running for the lower bleachers cut directly through the procession. They slammed into the line of students.

"Move!" someone shouted.

Patrick planted his feet, trying to act as a brake, trying to shield the wheelchair from the impact.

But then, a second wave hit them from the right—people sprinting for the center aisle.

A heavy shoulder slammed into Patrick’s back. He stumbled forward, his chest hitting the back of Nichole’s headrest.

"Watch it!" Patrick roared.

Another body slammed into him from the side, spinning him around.

His hands slipped off the push handles.

"Nikki!"

He reached out, his fingers brushing the purple fabric of her gown.

But the crowd swarmed into the gap. A wall of strangers—adults, teenagers, people carrying coolers and stadium seats—filled the space between them.

The purple and gold ocean swallowed the wheelchair whole.

And the band played on.
