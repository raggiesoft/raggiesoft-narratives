# Part 3: The Offer

## Date: July 15, 2002, Time: 11:30 AM, Location: The Bennett Family Van (Driving home from SSA Office)

The van was quiet, the only sound the hum of the tires on the asphalt. The air conditioning was blasting, fighting off the July heat.

In the back, Nichole was out of her chair. She had asked to be moved to the bench seat. She was leaning against Patrick, her head resting on his shoulder, her arm draped across his chest. It was her standard travel position—anchored, safe, and close.

Patrick had his arm around her, his hand resting on her arm. He could feel the tension leaving her body, the rigid spasticity melting away now that the threat of the "Keeper" was gone.

He looked out the window at the passing strip malls. His mind was racing.

He was thinking about the caseworker’s words. *Personal care aides... things insurance won't touch.*

In the front seat, the silence finally broke.

"We did the right thing, El," Tom said, gripping the steering wheel a little tighter than necessary. "We couldn't sign that paper. We couldn't let them put a cap on her life."

"I know," Ellen sighed, staring out the passenger window. "I know we did. But... that woman wasn't wrong about the math, Tom."

"We have good insurance," Tom countered. "The policy through the firm is solid."

"It covers *medical*," Ellen said, her nurse's pragmatism cutting through the optimism. "It covers surgeries and hospital stays. It doesn't cover *custodial* care. It doesn't pay for someone to come in and help her shower or get dressed."

She turned to look at him. "Private home health aides cost twenty dollars an hour. Minimum. If she needs help just four hours a day... Tom, that’s almost two thousand dollars a month. That’s a second mortgage."

Tom winced. "We'll figure it out. I can pick up extra consulting work. We can dip into the savings if we have to."

"And the equipment?" Ellen pressed gently. "Her new chair was eight thousand dollars. The VocaLink repairs are five hundred a pop. Without the Medicaid waiver... that's all out of pocket now. If we burn through the savings just keeping her clean, what’s left for college?"

Patrick listened from the back. He heard the fear in his mother's voice. He heard the weight of the world landing on his father's shoulders.

They had just walked away from the money that would pay for a stranger to come into their house and bathe his sister. A stranger who wouldn't know her signals. A stranger who might handle her like a doll.

He looked down at Nichole. She was almost asleep, trusting him completely to hold her upright as the van turned a corner.

She didn't want a stranger. She wanted him. She slept in his bed every night because the dark terrified her, and his presence was the only thing that kept the monsters away. She let him carry her because his arms were the only ones that felt safe.

The realization hit him with the force of a physical blow. *I am the aide.*

He cleared his throat.

"Mom? Dad?"

Tom looked in the rearview mirror, his face instantly softening as he tried to hide the stress. "Yeah, Pat?"

"So... if we don't take the SSI, that means we don't get the Medicaid waiver for the personal care attendant, right?"

"That's right," Ellen said, turning to look back at them. "We'll figure it out. We always do. Insurance covers some home health, but it's limited."

"We don't need a stranger," Patrick said. His voice was steady. "I can do it."

Nichole opened her eyes. She didn't move her head from his shoulder. She just listened.

"Do what, honey?" Ellen asked.

"Be her aide," Patrick said. "I can do everything. I can take care of her."

Tom glanced at Ellen, then back at the mirror. "Patrick, that's... that's a lot. We're talking about bathing, toileting, dressing. It's heavy work. And it's... personal."

"I know," Patrick said. "But she trusts me. She doesn't trust anyone else. And we want to go to college together, right? If I'm her caregiver, we can just... go. We don't have to wait for an agency to assign someone. We don't have to worry if the aide shows up."

He looked at Nichole.

"What do you think, Nikki? Would you be okay with me doing... everything? The bathroom stuff? The shower?"

Nichole lifted her head. She looked at him. She saw the seriousness in his eyes. He wasn't offering out of pity. He was offering because he wanted to protect her dignity. He knew that with him, it wouldn't be clinical. It would just be life.

She smiled. A real, genuine smile.

She reached for her travel keyboard, which was resting on her lap.

"YES."

She typed again.

"BETTER. YOU. THAN. STRANGERS."

She leaned back into him, snuggling closer, sealing the deal with a squeeze of his arm.

Tom let out a long breath. He looked at Ellen. He saw the relief in her eyes—the financial panic replaced by a sudden, profound gratitude for the bond their children shared.

"It makes sense," Ellen whispered. "They're already inseparable. And he's strong enough."

"Okay," Tom said, his voice thick with emotion. "Okay. If you're both on board... then we do it."

"But we do it right," Ellen added, her nurse-mode kicking in. "We aren't just going to throw you in the deep end, Patrick. As soon as we get home, we start training. Dad and I will teach you everything. How to do the transfers safely so you don't hurt your back. How to manage the shower chair. How to help her in the bathroom without making it awkward."

"I'm ready," Patrick said. "I want to learn."

"We start tonight," Tom said firmly. "Bath time. You watch, then you help. By the time school starts, you'll be a pro."

Patrick tightened his arm around his sister. "Did you hear that, Nikki? You're stuck with me."

Nichole closed her eyes again, a profound sense of peace washing over her. She wasn't going to have a keeper. She wasn't going to have a poverty limit. She was going to have her brother.

And that was exactly what she had wanted all along.
