# Part 2: The Poverty Trap

## Date: July 15, 2002 Time: 10:00 AM Location: Social Security Administration Office, Virginia Beach, VA

The office smelled of stale coffee and despair. It was a windowless room filled with rows of plastic chairs bolted to the floor, occupied by people clutching numbered tickets.

Patrick sat next to Nichole. He had parked her Titan AeroMotion at the end of the row. He was reading a car magazine, but his leg was bouncing—a sign that he didn't like the vibe of the room.

"Number 42," a voice called out.

Tom stood up, holding a folder of medical records. Ellen grabbed her purse. Patrick stood up and grabbed the push handles.

They walked to Cubicle 4.

The caseworker, Ms. Perkins, was a woman who looked like she had been eroded by paperwork. She didn't look up as they approached; she just gestured to the chairs.

"Application for Supplemental Security Income," Ms. Perkins droned, reading the file. "Claimant is Nichole Bennett. Seventeen years old. Turning eighteen in April."

She finally looked up. She saw the wheelchair. She saw the headrest. She saw the heavy VocaLink 3000 mounted to the tray.

Her eyes glazed over with a look the Bennetts knew well: *The Pity Stare.*

"Well," Ms. Perkins sighed, opening a new form. "This should be straightforward. Based on the medical evidence of Cerebral Palsy and Autism, she meets the Blue Book listing for total disability. Once she turns eighteen, she is eligible for the maximum federal benefit."

She slid a paper across the desk toward Tom.

"We just need to set up the Representative Payee," she said.

Tom frowned. "Representative Payee?"

"The person who manages the money," Ms. Perkins explained, clicking her pen. "Obviously, the claimant cannot manage her own finances. Since she is non-verbal and requires total physical care, the Administration will require a designated payee to receive the checks on her behalf. Usually a parent."

Nichole felt a cold spike in her chest. *Obviously.*

Because she couldn't walk to the bank, this woman assumed she couldn't count.

"She manages her own money," Ellen said sharply. "She has an allowance. She budgets for her own computer software."

Ms. Perkins gave a tight, condescending smile. "Mrs. Bennett, buying a video game is different than managing rent and utilities. The SSA requires a Capability Determination. Given her condition..." She gestured vaguely at Nichole's body. "...we would likely require a formal Guardianship and Conservatorship to prevent fraud."

The words hung in the air like a threat.

*Guardianship.*

Patrick stopped bouncing his leg. He sat up very straight.

"Guardianship?" Tom repeated, his voice dropping to a low, dangerous register. "You want us to go to court and strip her of her civil rights?"

"It’s for her protection," Ms. Perkins insisted. "A Conservatorship ensures that a responsible adult makes legal and financial decisions. If she turns eighteen without one, she is legally an adult. She could sign a bad contract. She could be taken advantage of."

"She is an adult," Patrick interrupted. "Or she will be. And she's smarter than I am."

Ms. Perkins looked at Patrick, then back at the parents. "Mr. and Mrs. Bennett, please understand. If she is on SSI, she is subject to the asset limit. She cannot have more than \$2,000 in her name at any time. She cannot own a home. She cannot have a savings account. A Conservator ensures she stays compliant so she doesn't lose her benefits."

Nichole’s mind raced. *Two thousand dollars.*

That was the limit?

She looked at the Apex laptop she wanted to buy for college. It cost \$2,500.

If she went on SSI, she couldn't save for it. She couldn't save for an apartment. She couldn't save for the Bennett Machine.

She would be trapped in a cycle of poverty, forced to spend every dollar she got just to stay poor enough to get the next check. And to do it, she had to let her parents sign away her right to vote, marry, or sign a lease.

It wasn't a safety net. It was a cage.

She reached for her device. The heavy grey plastic of the VocaLink was under her hand.

She didn't look at Ms. Perkins. She looked at her father.

She tapped the screen.

"NO."

The mechanical voice of "Digital Dan" cut through the office noise.

Ms. Perkins jumped. "Oh. She... uses a machine."

"She speaks," Tom corrected. "And she said no."

"I. CAN. COUNT," Nichole typed, her finger hitting the keys with deliberate force. "I. DO. NOT. WANT. A. KEEPER."

"It's free money, dear," Ms. Perkins said slowly, speaking to Nichole like a toddler. "It helps your parents take care of you."

"We don't need the government's help to take care of her," Tom said, standing up. "And we certainly don't need the government telling her she can't save money for her future."

"Mr. Bennett, be reasonable," Ms. Perkins huffed. "She is totally dependent. How will she survive without income? She can't work."

Patrick stood up. He stepped behind the Titan AeroMotion. He looked at the caseworker with a look of absolute, icy resolve.

"She has a job," Patrick said. "She's the Boss. And I'm the muscle."

He unlocked the brakes.

"We're leaving," Ellen said, grabbing her purse. "We are not signing a Guardianship. And we are not taking the SSI."

"You're making a mistake!" Ms. Perkins called after them. "You're turning down guaranteed support!"

Tom held the door open for his family. He looked back at the bureaucrat.

"It's not support," Tom said. "It's a ceiling. And my daughter doesn't have a ceiling."

They walked out into the bright Virginia sun.

Nichole took a deep breath. She felt lighter. They had just walked away from a monthly check, but they had kept something much more valuable.

Her signature.

She looked at her parents. She tapped her screen.

"THANK. YOU."

"Don't thank us," Tom said, loosening his tie. "Just promise me one thing."

"WHAT?"

"When you make your first million," Tom grinned, "you hire a good accountant."

Nichole smiled.

"DEAL."
