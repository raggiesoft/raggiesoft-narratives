# Part 1: The Area of Rescue

Date: April 12, 2002 (Spring of 11th Grade)

Time: 10:15 AM

Location: Northwood High School, Second Floor Academic Wing ("The Blue Mile")

The lesson on the Reconstruction Era was dragging. Mr. Henderson was midway through explaining the Compromise of 1877, his voice a steady drone against the hum of the classroom ventilation.

Patrick sat at his desk, spinning his pen. To his right, parked in the designated accessible cutout, Nichole was typing notes on her VocaLink. Her aide, a quiet woman named Mrs. Lipton, had excused herself to the faculty restroom five minutes ago, leaving the twins in their standard formation: side-by-side.

Then, the air changed.

It wasn't a sound. It was a scent. It hit Patrick’s nose sharp and sudden—a heavy, acrid tang like a pencil eraser being held against a belt sander. It wasn't the wood-smoke smell of a campfire; it was chemical. It smelled of ozone and melting rubber.

Patrick stopped spinning his pen. He looked up at the rectangular HVAC vent in the drop ceiling. There was nothing to see—no grey haze, no curling wisps—but the smell was pouring out of it, invisible and choking.

"Mr. Henderson?" Patrick interrupted, his voice cutting through the lecture. "Do you smell that?"

Mr. Henderson paused, sniffing the air. He frowned. "I... yes. It smells like burning rubber."

He walked toward the door to check the hallway. "Probably just the shop class downstairs working on tires. Everyone sit tight."

He reached for the door handle.

EEEEEEEEEEEEEEEEEEEEEEE!

The alarm didn't ramp up; it detonated. The system installed at Northwood didn't use the mechanical vibratory horns of their middle school. This was solid-state electronic efficiency. It was a piercing, high-frequency shriek that felt less like a sound and more like a needle being driven into the eardrum.

And then came the lights.

FLASH-FLASH... FLASH... FLASH-FLASH.

Every twenty feet along the "Blue Mile," the rectangular plastic strobe units exploded with white light. Because the system was older technology, the strobes were unsynchronized. They fired in a chaotic, random stutter—a strobing, psychedelic assault that turned the classroom into a disjointed stop-motion nightmare.

Nichole’s reaction was instantaneous.

The high-pitched whine was a physical knife in her head, but the unsynchronized lights destroyed her equilibrium. The room wasn't just loud; it was glitching. Her hands flew up, clamping over her ears. Her legs locked into violent, rigid extension against the footplates of the Titan AeroMotion Z-5. She squeezed her eyes shut, but the light was so bright it pulsed red through her eyelids.  

"Fire drill! Single file!" Mr. Henderson shouted over the shriek, grabbing his emergency clipboard. He looked at the empty chair where Mrs. Lipton should have been. "Bennett! You have your sister?"

"I got her!" Patrick yelled.

He abandoned his books. He kicked his chair back and grabbed the push handles of the Titan.

The smell was getting stronger now—a thick, invisible heaviness that coated the back of the throat. Patrick pushed Nichole out of the classroom and into the hallway.

The "Blue Mile" was a river of students moving toward the exits. The unsynchronized strobes made the movement look jerky and robotic. Patrick pushed hard, weaving the heavy chair through the crowd, aiming for Stairway A—the critical entry point near the elevator alcove.  

They reached the landing. The single Summit Vector elevator stood closed.

Patrick hit the call button. The light didn't illuminate. The elevator had automatically recalled to the ground floor or locked out, as per fire code.

"It's dead!" Patrick yelled.

Mr. Henderson appeared beside them, ushering the last of the history students into the stairwell. He pointed to a recessed corner of the landing, marked with a blue sign: AREA OF RESCUE ASSISTANCE.

"Park her there!" Henderson shouted, pointing to the spot directly under a flashing strobe. "That’s the protocol! We evacuate, and I tell the Fire Department where she is!"

Patrick looked at the spot. It was a dead end.

He looked at Nichole. She wasn't just scared; she was disintegrating. She was rocking back and forth in the chair, her body rigid with spasticity, her face buried in her knees to hide from the assault of the lights.

The smell of burning rubber was intense now. It was stinging Patrick’s eyes. Even though the air looked clear, his lungs felt heavy.

Leave her here?

The memory of the charred ruins of Princess Anne High School flashed through his mind. The library roof caved in. The total destruction. If this wasn't a drill—if that smell was a boiler about to blow or a wall full of burning wires—waiting for a fireman who might not come for twenty minutes was a death sentence.  

"No," Patrick said.

"Patrick!" Henderson barked, trying to be heard over the electronic scream. "Leave the chair! You cannot take it down the stairs! It’s a liability! Wait for the professionals!"

"I'm not waiting," Patrick snapped.

He spun the wheelchair around, shielding Nichole from the strobe with his own body. He dropped to one knee on the linoleum.

"Nikki! Eyes on me!"

She didn't move. She was in full sensory lockdown.

Patrick reached out and grabbed her wrists, pulling her hands away from her ears. Her eyes snapped open, wide and terrified behind her glasses.

"Code Black," Patrick shouted. "Arms up!"

Nichole saw his face. She saw the set of his jaw. She recognized the command.

She unlocked her death grip on the armrests. She reached out, wrapping her arms around his neck in a desperate vice grip.

Patrick leaned in. He slid his left arm under her knees, his right arm behind her back, locking his hands together to form a cradle. He ignored the burning in his bad leg. He fired his quads and stood up.

He lifted her dead weight out of the custom seat. As soon as she was in the air, Nichole buried her face in his neck, pressing her ear against his shoulder to muffle the shriek of the alarm.

The VocaLink 3000 sat silent on the empty wheelchair tray. The purple Titan frame was left behind, parked exactly where the school wanted the girl to be.

"Patrick, stop!" Henderson grabbed at his arm. "You are violating safety protocol!"

Patrick turned. He looked at the teacher, then at the invisible, choking scent filling the air.

"She goes where I go," Patrick said.

He turned his back on the teacher and stepped into the flow of traffic at the top of the stairs, carrying his sister down into the crush.
