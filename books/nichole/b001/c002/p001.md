# Origin Story: Project Alpha

## Date: June 15, 1996 Age: 10 Years Old Location: The Garage / Kitchen, 1850 Delaney Street

The crisis started, as most innovation does, with a failure of the existing technology.

Ten-year-old Nichole was sitting in the bathtub. She was trying to tell her mom that the water was too hot. She reached for her laminated paper communication chart—the one the speech therapist had given them.

But her hand was wet. The water dripped onto the paper. The lamination peeled. The ink ran. The chart dissolved into a soggy mess.

Nichole screamed in frustration. She didn't have a voice, and her paper voice had just drowned.

## The Eureka Moment

In the kitchen, ten-year-old Patrick was watching his mom, Ellen, chop vegetables for dinner. She was using a heavy-duty, black synthetic cutting board she had bought at a restaurant supply store.

Patrick watched her pick it up. She grabbed it by the cut-out handle on the left side. She carried it to the sink. She rinsed it under the faucet. The water beaded up and rolled right off.

Patrick stared at the handle. He stared at the waterproof plastic.

"Mom," Patrick said, pointing. "That."

"The peppers?" Ellen asked.

"No," Patrick said, his engineer brain firing its first sparks. "The board. It's waterproof. And it has a handle for holding."

He grabbed the soggy remains of Nichole's paper chart from the trash.

"If we put letters on the cutting board," Patrick explained, "she can talk in the tub. She can talk in the rain. It won't break."

Ellen put the knife down. She looked at the board. She looked at her son.

"Tom!" she yelled toward the garage. "Get the label maker!"

## The Workshop

Half an hour later, the "Bennett Machine" was in its first official design session.

They were in the garage. Tom had the black cutting board on the workbench. He had sanded the surface slightly to make sure the stickers would stick.

"Okay, Pat," Tom said, handing his son a ruler. "Measure it out. We need four rows. Keep 'em straight."

Patrick, tongue sticking out in concentration, marked the lines with a white grease pencil.

"Nikki," Patrick said, looking at his sister in her wheelchair. "Do you want A-B-C-D like school?"

Nichole shook her head violently. *No.*

She pointed to the old computer keyboard gathering dust on the shelf.

"QWERTY?" Patrick asked.

Nichole nodded. *Yes.*

"Smart," Tom muttered. "Muscle memory."

## The Build

It took three hours.

Patrick placed every sticker. They used high-visibility yellow vinyl mailbox letters—the kind meant to survive a hurricane.

- Center: The QWERTY grid.

- Sides: Patrick had the idea to use electrical tape (Red and Green) to make the massive "YES" and "NO" landing strips on the sides, so she could hit them even if she was shaking.

- The Corners: Ellen insisted on the "Needs" corners. *Bathroom. Pain. Thirst.*

When it was done, it looked industrial. It was heavy. It was rugged.

## The Test

They went back to the bathroom. They filled the tub.

Patrick held the board by the handle. He dunked it underwater. He pulled it out. The water cascaded off the plastic. The yellow letters shone bright against the black background.

He handed it to Nichole.

She grabbed the handle. It was heavy, but the weight felt good. It felt substantial. It didn't feel like a toy; it felt like a tool.

She typed her first message on the new interface.

C. O. O. L.

Patrick grinned. "It's the Board, Nikki. Waterproof edition."
