# Part 1: The Bachelor Pad (The Lab)

## Date: Friday, July 15, 1994 Location: Uncle Mark’s Apartment, The Point, Newport, RI

The heat in Newport wasn't like the heat in Virginia. In Virginia, the heat was a wet blanket that smelled of asphalt and swamp. Here, on Washington Street, the heat was salty. It stuck to your skin, smelling of drying seaweed and low tide.

Uncle Mark lived in a first-floor apartment in a shingled house that dated back to the 1800s. Like most houses in the neighborhood, it didn't believe in central air conditioning. The cooling system consisted of opening the front windows, opening the back windows, and praying for a breeze off Narragansett Bay.

"Welcome to the Bachelor Pad," Mark announced, throwing the front door open. He was wearing cargo shorts and a t-shirt stained with teak oil. "Step inside. It’s a cool... seventy-eight degrees in here."

Patrick rolled into the living room, followed by his father, Tom, pushing Nichole’s manual chair. The apartment didn't smell like their house in Virginia, which always smelled of lemon polish and aggressive laundry detergent. Mark’s place smelled of the ocean, stale coffee, cedar wood, and something sharp and metallic that Patrick would later identify as soldering flux.

It wasn't messy, exactly, but it was *dense*. The living room had a sofa, but the coffee table was buried under stacks of *Classic Keel* magazine and rolled-up blueprints for a hull redesign. A box fan sat in the window, rattling slightly as it churned the heavy air.

"We'll take the pull-out couch," Ellen said, eyeing the living room and the rattling fan. She wiped a sheen of sweat from her forehead. "Mark, are you sure about the kids in the office? Is there room?"

"It's the only other bed, El," Mark shrugged, grabbing two suitcases. "And honestly, it's the best room. It faces north. Doesn't get the morning sun baking the glass."

He gestured for them to follow. "Plus," Mark grinned at Patrick, "It's got the toys."

He led Patrick and Nichole down the narrow hallway. The floorboards creaked—old, solid wood that had settled over a century. Mark opened the door to the spare room. "Behold," Mark said. "The Cockpit."

Patrick stopped in the doorway. His jaw dropped. It wasn't a bedroom. It was a command center.

Along the right wall ran a massive workbench made of a solid-core door resting on two heavy sawhorses. It was a landscape of technology:

- A SignalMaster 5000 Transceiver with glowing amber dials and a heavy, coiled microphone cable.

- A Vulcan Soldering Station with a sponge that looked like a dried sea creature.

- A stack of Modern Tinkerer magazines teetering like a skyscraper.

- And the crown jewel: a Quantix Oscilloscope, currently humming, with a small green dot drifting lazily across the grid screen like a digital ghost.

"Whoa," Patrick whispered.

"And here's the crash pad," Mark said, pointing to the corner. It was a low, Japanese-style futon frame made of black lacquered wood. The mattress wasn't a soft, springy thing like Patrick’s bed at home. It was a dense, heavy slab of cotton batting covered in a navy blue canvas quilt.

"It's hard," Mark explained, pressing his hand onto the mattress. It barely gave. "But that's the point. Soft beds are quicksand. This?" He slapped the canvas. "This is a launchpad. Firm surface means easier transfers for Nichole. You can get leverage."

Patrick sat down on the edge. It felt like sitting on a gym mat. Solid. Predictable. He looked at Nichole. "It's low," Patrick noted. It was the exact same height as her wheelchair seat. No lifting required. Just a slide.

"Sliding transfer," Mark confirmed. "We designed it for efficiency."

Mark walked over to the workbench. He flipped a switch on a power strip. A small fan clipped to the desk started to whir, blowing air right over the bed. "You guys have the cross-breeze," Mark said. "Open the sash, turn on the fan, and you'll hear the bell buoys out in the harbor all night."

He looked at Patrick, who was eyeing the soldering iron. "Rules of the road," Mark said, his voice turning serious. "Do not touch the Vulcan unless I am in the room. It gets to 700 degrees. It will melt your skin." Patrick nodded, eyes wide. "Everything else?" Mark waved his hand at the radio, the scope, and the bins of parts. "Explore. Just don't transmit on the emergency frequencies."

Mark left the room to help Ellen with the cooler. Patrick and Nichole were left alone in the dim, humming room. It was hot. It smelled like solder and sea salt. The futon was as hard as a rock. But as Patrick looked at the green dot drifting on the Quantix screen, he thought it was the coolest place he had ever been.

He looked at Nichole. She was staring at the amber light of the radio. "This is gonna be a good trip," Patrick whispered.
