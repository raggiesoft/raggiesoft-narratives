# Part 1: Arrival at the Pathfinder Terminal (2:15 PM)

The hydraulic hiss and heavy diesel fumes of the Norfolk Pathfinder terminal were overwhelming, but they were nothing compared to the sharp, sudden sting of Valerie’s voice.

Nate flinched visibly as she screamed at him, his shoulders instinctively hunching defensively. The terminal was a blur of transient motion, but his attention was anchored entirely on the horrifying revelation she had just casually dropped.

Los Angeles. Nate’s mind raced, struggling to process the geographical leap. The agreed-upon parameters of this trip were already pushing his limits: they were supposed to be going to Washington, D.C., to visit her family. D.C. was a manageable, predictable distance from Virginia Beach. Los Angeles was the other side of the continent. It was an isolating, terrifying distance away from his sister, his mother, and everything that felt remotely safe.

"It's going to be perfect," Valerie insisted, her voice sharp and demanding, attempting to paint over his visible panic with her own forced reality.

Nate gripped the push-rims of his manual wheelchair. The hesitation was no longer just a nagging thought; it was a physical weight in his chest. "I don't want to go," he said, his voice trembling but honest.

Valerie screamed again, the sound cutting through the ambient noise of the waiting area. The hostility in her posture was palpable. She wanted compliance, not resistance.

"You don't have a choice!" she hissed, leaning in close, weaponizing her proximity. Then, she pulled the ripcord on what she believed was her ultimate, inescapable trap. "I'm pregnant, Nate. From last week. We are going to Los Angeles to start our family, and you are not abandoning your child."

For a fraction of a second, the terminal seemed to go completely silent. Valerie waited for the panic, the guilt, the inevitable submission she had ruthlessly conditioned out of him for two years.

Instead, Nate just stared at her.

The heavy, suffocating anxiety in his chest didn't spike; it vanished entirely, replaced by a cold, absolute clarity. The sheer audacity of the lie stripped away the last remaining illusion of the woman he thought he loved.

"I have Azoospermia, Valerie," Nate said. His voice was suddenly steady, stripped of all its former trembling. "My family and I have known about my medical records since I was fourteen. I can't get anyone pregnant. You are lying."

Valerie physically recoiled. Her eyes widened as the absolute certainty of his medical reality completely shattered her trap. She opened her mouth, scrambling to pivot, to invent a new angle to keep the cage closed, but the spell was already broken.

As she stood there sputtering in rage, Nate’s right hand moved discreetly down to the pocket of his jeans. His fingers brushed against cold metal. He tracked the shapes by touch—two quarters. Fifty cents. It was the price of a local phone call.

He made his decision.

Through the crowd, his eyes locked onto a bank of battered payphones bolted to the terminal wall. He thought of his bedroom. He thought of his mother. Most importantly, he thought of Sarah. He wanted to go home. He didn't want Los Angeles, and he didn't even want Washington, D.C.

"What are you doing?" Valerie demanded, stepping into his line of sight as he unlocked his brakes.

Nate completely ignored the question. He refused to give her the ammunition of a response. Gripping the rims, he wheeled himself deliberately away from her, navigating the crowded terminal floor until he reached the phones.

He pulled the receiver off the hook and deposited the two quarters into the slot. They fell with a heavy, mechanical clink. He punched in the seven digits—a number he had known for sixteen years, burned into his memory like a lifeline.

The phone rang twice.

"Hello?"

The tension in Nate’s chest cracked the moment he heard her voice. "Mom, it's Nate." He kept his voice low, shielding the receiver with his hand. "Mom, Valerie is being mean to me. I want to come home."

He swallowed hard, getting the rest of the tactical situation out as quickly as possible. "She dragged me to the Pathfinder terminal in Norfolk. She wants to take me to Los Angeles. Could you and Sarah come get me?"

On the other end of the line, the domestic routine of his mother's day instantly shattered. There was no hesitation, no interrogation about what he had done to provoke it. She dropped everything.

"We are on our way," his mother said, her voice fierce and absolute. "We will be there in thirty minutes."

"I promise I won't get on any Pathfinder bus," Nate said, the tears finally starting to blur his vision.

"I'm not mad at you, honey. I promise I'm not mad. Hold on, your sister is right here."

There was a brief shuffle of the receiver, and then the frequency changed to the one voice that grounded his entire nervous system.

"Nate?" Sarah asked, her voice brimming with a mixture of intense relief and protective urgency.

Hearing her voice again was like stepping out of a suffocating room and taking a deep breath of clean air. "Sarah..."

"We're coming to get you," Sarah promised. "We're leaving right now."

They said their quick goodbyes, the mission parameters set. Nate placed the heavy receiver back onto the metal hook with a definitive click.

A profound, unfamiliar feeling washed over him as he sat facing the terminal wall. He was happy. Sarah—his sister, his protector, his absolute favorite person in the whole world—was coming. His mother was coming.

The cavalry was on the way.

He turned his wheelchair around to face the terminal again. Valerie was still there, fuming, but her anger suddenly looked incredibly small. She could scream all she wanted now. She could wave the non-refundable tickets in his face, and she could make all the demands she wanted.

He was not getting on that bus. The fortress was coming to get him.
