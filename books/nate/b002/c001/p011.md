# Part 11: The Garrison Logistics (Saturday, 9:00 AM)

Nate woke up to the smell of brewing coffee and the quiet, rhythmic hum of the countertop dishwasher.

He opened his eyes, blinking against the soft, muted daylight filtering through the blinds of the garage apartment. For a split second, the heavy dread of the last two years tried to claw its way into his chest—the instinctual panic of waking up and wondering what mood Valerie was in. But as he looked around the familiar room and saw Sarah sitting at her desk, sipping coffee and scrolling through her laptop, the dread instantly evaporated.

He was home. He had survived.

"Morning, buddy," Sarah said softly, hearing the rustle of the blankets. She didn't turn on any harsh overhead lights.

"Morning," Nate replied, his voice still thick with sleep and the lingering exhaustion of yesterday's meltdown. He pushed himself up against the headboard of the queen-sized bed. "I didn't dream it, did I? They're actually coming today?"

Sarah smiled, a genuine, radiant expression that reached all the way to her eyes. "Flight 492. It touches down at 2:15 PM. They're already in the air."

Before Nate could respond, the door connecting to the main house clicked open. Evelyn stepped inside, carrying a plate of fresh toast. She looked at Nate, her eyes scanning him with a quiet, maternal assessment to ensure the sensory storm had completely passed. Satisfied with what she saw, she set the plate on the kitchenette counter.

"Alright, command center," Evelyn said, leaning against the counter. "We need to talk logistics. The girls aren't just visiting; they are moving back in for good. And in a month, the rest of the army arrives for Christmas. We are going to have sixteen people in this house."

Nate took a bite of his toast, a small, genuine smile pulling at his mouth. Sixteen people. Three husbands. Two toddlers. It was going to be loud. It was going to be absolute, wall-to-wall chaos. But for the first time in years, the noise didn't scare him. It was the right kind of noise.

"The main house is going to be the loud zone," Evelyn continued, her tone practical and clinical. "Mike, David, and James are perfectly happy taking the air mattresses in the living room if they have to. They know the drill. They know the girls just went to war for you, Nate, and their loyalty is exactly where it needs to be."

Nate nodded. He had always liked his older cousins' husbands. They were quiet, steady guys who possessed a deeply secure masculinity. They understood exactly what they had married into. They knew that this family operated as a fiercely protective unit. More importantly, they knew about the "puppy piles."

Growing up, whenever the sensory input of the world got to be too much, all six cousins, Sarah, and Nate would just collapse onto the living room floor or a bed, tangling their arms and legs together in a heavy, grounding pile. Valerie had tried to make Nate feel ashamed of that need for platonic, physical affection, weaponizing it as something childish or unnatural.

The husbands, however, completely understood it. They knew their wives had grown up as part of this matriarchal shield. They wouldn't bat an eye when Nate needed to bury his face in Jessica's or Lauren's shoulder, or when the entire group collapsed onto the living room rug for an hour to regulate his nervous system.

"But this room," Sarah interjected, gesturing to the perimeter of the garage apartment, "this is the decompression zone. When the toddlers are screaming and the main house is too much, this is where you, me, Brit, and Megan retreat to. And anyone else who needs a minute of quiet."

Sarah looked at the queen-sized bed Nate was currently sitting in. "Which brings up a structural issue. A queen bed fits two. It doesn't fit a four-person pile, let alone an eight-person one."

Evelyn reached into her pocket and pulled out a folded piece of paper. "Which is why I was on the phone with the furniture warehouse at eight o'clock this morning. They have an Alaska King in stock. It measures nine feet by nine feet. They are delivering it next Tuesday."

Nate’s eyes widened. A nine-by-nine bed wasn't just a piece of furniture; it was a literal island. It was enough space for all of them to sleep safely, surrounded by their own people, without ever feeling crowded.

"We'll push the desks against the far wall," Sarah planned, already visualizing the layout. "The bed goes dead center. It's going to be basically wall-to-wall mattresses. It'll be perfect."

"And the house rules remain absolute," Evelyn stated, her voice leaving zero room for debate. "Especially with the holidays coming. 100% dry. No alcohol crosses the threshold of this property."

Nate let out a slow exhale of relief. Alcohol was a massive, unpredictable variable. It changed how people sounded, how they moved, and how they reacted. Valerie had always been terrifyingly unpredictable when she drank. Evelyn enforcing a strictly dry house meant the sensory environment remained completely controlled and entirely safe.

"Mike is bringing three cases of Orchard Crown sparkling cider," Evelyn added with a warm smile. "So we'll still have something to pop open on Christmas Eve."

Nate looked between his mother and his sister. The fortress wasn't just being defended; it was being actively upgraded and reinforced to accommodate the incoming garrison.

"Now," Sarah said, closing her laptop and standing up. "You have two hours to get dressed and get your mind right. We need to leave for the airport by 1:00 PM."

Nate threw the covers back. For the first time in two years, he wasn't dreading leaving the house. He couldn't wait to get to the terminal.
