# Part 1: Homecoming

The drive home from Crossroads Memorial was quiet. Jermaine pulled the car into the driveway, and before he could even turn off the engine, the front door opened. Devin, Sam, Molly, and Clare were all waiting, their faces etched with a mixture of worry and profound relief.

They helped a weak but determined Sarah out of the car. The welcome was warm but gentle, a flurry of soft hugs and quiet questions. "How are you feeling?" "So good to see you home."

But Jack could see the deep, bone-weary exhaustion in his sister's eyes, an exhaustion he felt mirrored in his own soul. They bypassed the living room where the family was gathering. Sarah, leaning heavily on him for support, just looked toward the hallway. "Bed," she said, her voice a tired whisper. "Now."

They went straight to Jack's bedroom. The family understood, giving them the space they needed. Without a word, the two of them, still in the clothes they'd been wearing for what felt like an eternity, collapsed onto Jack's full-sized bed and pulled the comforter up, a shared, desperate need for sleep overriding everything else.

From the hallway, Jack could hear his cousins' hushed voices. "I'll get them some water for the nightstand," Molly whispered. "I'll close the blinds so the sun doesn't wake them," Clare added. They were taking care of everything, allowing the two siblings to finally, truly rest.

Just before sleep claimed him, Jack remembered his responsibility. He fumbled for his phone on the nightstand.

Jack to Jennifer: Hey Jennifer, it's Jack. We're home from the hospital. Sarah's been discharged. My dad is going to drop off her return-to-work paperwork with Occupational Health today.

Her reply was almost instant.

Jennifer: That's the best news, Jack! I will tell Scott, and we're all so relieved. You sound exhausted. Do you want to take today and tomorrow as a paid leave day too? Just to catch up on sleep?

Jack felt a wave of immense gratitude for his boss.

Jack: Yes, please. That would be amazing. Thank you so much.

One last message popped up as his eyes were closing.

Jennifer: Done. And good news, after HazMat cleared the area, we were able to get your shoes. They're safe in my office for you when you come back.

The thought of his own shoes, a small, mundane piece of his normal life, waiting for him was a disproportionately huge comfort. He set the phone down, snuggled deeper into the blankets next to his sister, and for the first time in days, let the overwhelming need for sleep pull him completely under.
