Jack sat holding Sarah's hand, watching the rhythmic rise and fall of her chest. The room was quiet again, but it was a heavy, anxious silence. He watched his sister shift uncomfortably in the narrow hospital bed, her gaze flicking for a moment to the empty space beside her, then to the pull-out sofa where he was supposed to be. He knew that look. He knew, with absolute certainty, that if the hospital bed were big enough, her silent plea would be for him to lie down beside her, a solid, comforting presence against the fear. But they both knew the reality: the bed was for her, the sofa was for him.

The door swished open, and Dr. Evans came back in, his expression grim. He held a tablet displaying a ghostly, grey-and-white image of a chest.

"The X-ray confirmed it," he said, his voice low and serious. "Sarah, you've developed some fluid in your lungs. It's the delayed chemical pneumonitis we were watching for".

Jack's grip tightened on his sister's hand. Sarah's eyes, wide and fearful, locked on the doctor.

"We're going to start a more aggressive treatment immediately," Dr. Evans continued. "We'll be giving you a strong diuretic through your IV to help your body get rid of that fluid. We're also going to increase your oxygen for a while."

As he spoke, the nurse, Karen, came in with a new IV bag, her movements swift and professional. "This means you'll have to use the restroom quite a bit, Sarah," the nurse explained gently. "Just use your call button, and I'll be right here to help you."

Jack watched them work, a feeling of helpless dread washing over him. As the new medication began to drip into his sister's arm, he glanced toward the window. The first pale, grey streaks of dawn were beginning to lighten the dark sky. A wave of sheer exhaustion hit him. It was morning. They had been up all night.

He looked at Sarah. Her face was a mask of fear, exhaustion, and now, a deep, weary grumpiness. The last thing she wanted was to be poked, prodded, and forced to the bathroom every hour.

Her raw throat formed a single, croaked word. "Hungry...".

"I know, sweetie," the nurse said sympathetically. "But you'll have to stay NPO until your breathing is much, much better. It's not safe for you to eat yet."

Sarah let out a frustrated sigh and turned her face away, a single angry tear escaping her eye. A few minutes later, the faint smell of coffee and bacon from the breakfast carts starting their rounds in the hallway wafted into the room. Jack's own stomach clenched with a painful pang of hunger. He hadn't eaten since yesterday afternoon at the rec center. But he looked at his sister, miserable and being denied even a sip of water, and made a silent vow. He would not eat. Not a single bite. To eat in front of her while she was suffering would be a profound disrespect, a betrayal he wasn't capable of.

The sun began to rise, flooding the room with the light of a new day. For the two exhausted, hungry, and grumpy siblings in the quiet hospital room, it promised only another long battle ahead.
