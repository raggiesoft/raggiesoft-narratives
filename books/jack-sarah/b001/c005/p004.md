# Part 4: The Advocate 🚑

The air in the parking lot was a chaotic symphony of noise and light. The fire alarm from the rec center still blared, a relentless shriek that grated on Jack's already frayed nerves. The flashing lights of the ambulance and HazMat truck painted stark, strobing patterns across the growing crowd of evacuated patrons.

Jack reached up and pulled the respirator from his face, gasping in the cool, clean air. The goggles came next, leaving red pressure marks around his eyes. He stripped off the gloves and the chemical-resistant apron, leaving him in his t-shirt, jeans, and the heavy, out-of-place rubber boots. His own shoes were locked inside the building, but that was a problem for another time.

He heard the murmurs from the crowd. Some patrons were visibly angry, complaining to Joe about being forced to evacuate. Others watched with quiet, understanding concern. It was all just background noise to Jack. His entire world was the small circle of space around the transport chair where his sister sat, lost in her own personal storm.

"Okay, son, I need you to focus," one of the paramedics said, kneeling in front of Jack but looking at Sarah. "Tell me what you can. We know she was exposed to chlorine gas. Is she allergic to any medications?"

Jack took a steadying breath, becoming her voice. "Her name is Sarah. No known allergies," he said, his voice clear despite the adrenaline. "She has autism. This is a severe meltdown. She can hear you, but she can't respond. It's a complete sensory overload for her".

The other paramedic tried to get a blood pressure reading, but Sarah flinched away from the cuff's touch, a low moan escaping her lips. When the first paramedic pulled out a penlight to check her pupils, Jack instinctively put a hand up.

"No," he said, his tone firm but respectful. "Please. No bright lights directly in her eyes. It will make it worse."

The paramedic paused, then gave a quick, understanding nod, tucking the light away. "Okay, son. No light. Her pulse-ox is stable for now, but her breathing is shallow. We need to get her to the hospital for observation. That gas can cause serious inflammation in the lungs."

"Yes," Jack agreed without a moment's hesitation. "Whatever she needs."

As the paramedics prepared the gurney on the ambulance, Jack overheard an angry patron yelling at Joe nearby. "This is ridiculous! My car keys are in my locker!"

"The building is not safe, sir," Joe's voice cut through the noise, firm and final. "No one is going back inside until HazMat gives the all-clear."

The ambulance doors swung open. As the paramedics began to carefully transfer Sarah from the chair to the gurney, the lead paramedic turned to Jack.

"We're taking her to Crossroads Memorial," she said, her voice firm and efficient. "It's the closest appropriate ER for a chemical exposure like this. We need to move now."

Jack nodded, his focus unwavering. He watched them secure Sarah onto the gurney, his hand resting on her arm until the last possible second. He knew riding in the ambulance, crammed into a tiny seat, would only put him in their way. He needed to be functional, and he needed their car.

"I'm not riding with you," he told the paramedic, making the decision in an instant. "I'll take our car. I'll meet you there".

The paramedic gave a curt, understanding nod. "See you there."

Jack leaned over the gurney just before they loaded it into the ambulance, his face close to his sister's ear. "Sarah, can you hear me? I'm right behind you," he said, his voice a low, steady promise. "I'll be at the hospital when you get there. I won't leave you."

He stepped back, watching as they slid the gurney into the back of the ambulance. The doors slammed shut, a sound of terrifying finality. With sirens blaring, the vehicle pulled away, accelerating out of the parking lot.

Jack stood for a moment, a solitary figure in his heavy rubber boots, watching the ambulance disappear. The fire alarm had finally been silenced, leaving an eerie quiet punctuated by the crackle of official radios. His mission was clear. He turned and began the long walk across the chaotic parking lot toward their car, his mind already navigating the route to Crossroads Memorial.
