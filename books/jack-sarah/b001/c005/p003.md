# Part 3: Code Orange ☣️

The wail of approaching sirens grew from a distant cry to a deafening roar, culminating in the flashing red and white lights of an ambulance and a large, imposing HazMat truck pulling up to the main entrance of Fairlawn. The doors slid open, and two paramedics rushed in, their expressions focused and all business.

Joe, the building supervisor's assistant, met them immediately. "Chlorine spill, at least one staff member exposed and non-responsive," he said, pointing them toward Sarah was still lost in the depths of her meltdown.

The paramedics were at her side in an instant. Jack, still in his rescue PPE, stayed right with them, a strange, muffled voice of authority amidst the chaos. "She's my sister," he explained, his voice tight. "She has autism and she's in a severe meltdown. She can't speak right now. She was in the pump room when a hose ruptured". He was her voice, her advocate, ensuring they understood her condition beyond just the chemical exposure.

While one paramedic began taking Sarah's vitals, the other started assessing a small, frightened group of patrons who had been on the pool deck and were now complaining of burning eyes and throats.

At the same time, the HazMat team, clad in much more comprehensive gear, entered the building. After a quick, terse briefing from Joe, they headed for the now-sealed second floor to assess the spill.

Jack's world narrowed to the small circle around his sister. He watched the paramedic check her pulse, her breathing. Sarah remained lost, rocking slightly, a low moan the only sound she made. A few tense, agonizing minutes passed.

The lead HazMat technician came jogging down the massive concrete spiral ramp, his face grim behind his mask. He went straight to Joe, the acting Facility Supervisor, who was coordinating the initial response near the front desk. The on-site Fire Chief was right beside him, monitoring the situation.

"The concentration is higher than we thought, and it's not contained to the natatorium HVAC," the HazMat tech said, his voice sharp with urgency. "It's already seeping into the main ventilation ducts. You have to evacuate the entire building. Now."

Joe’s head snapped toward the Fire Chief, who gave a single, decisive nod. "He's right. You need to get everyone out of this building immediately."

"Okay, Code Orange," Joe said, reaching for his radio, his mind already cycling through the protocols.

"Wait," the Fire Chief interjected, putting a hand on Joe’s arm to stop him for a fraction of a second. "Announcing a code is good, but people hesitate. They get confused. You want them out with no questions asked?" He pointed toward the front exit. "Pull the fire alarm. It’s the one signal everyone on the planet understands means 'get out now'".

The Fire Chief was already keying his own radio. "Dispatch, this is Chief Miller at Fairlawn Rec. Be advised, we have a chemical hazard, no fire. I have instructed the Facility Supervisor to activate the building's fire alarm system to facilitate an immediate and full evacuation. Repeat, this is a HazMat evacuation, not a fire".

Joe understood instantly. He keyed his own radio, his voice booming with authority. "All staff, all staff! At the direction of the Fire Chief, we are now at Code Orange! I repeat, Code Orange! Initiate full and immediate facility evacuation!".

He began moving toward the main entrance, directing staff and the last few lingering patrons out the doors. As he passed the exit, he reached up and activated the fire alarm pull station on the wall.

Seconds later, the piercing, intermittent shriek of the fire alarm blared through the building, a signal no one could misinterpret. The controlled chaos escalated as staff began ushering the remaining patrons from the gym, meeting rooms, and lounges toward the exits.

"We're moving her outside," one of the paramedics said to Jack, already starting to push Sarah's chair.

Jack followed without a word, a strange, anonymous figure in his respirator and goggles, his hand resting protectively on the back of his sister's chair. They moved through the stream of evacuating patrons, a small, urgent island in the current.

They emerged into the cool afternoon air, the sounds of the alarm and the emergency vehicles loud in the parking lot. The paramedics wheeled Sarah toward the designated muster area, a safe distance from the building, where other patrons were already gathering. Jack stayed right with her, his presence an unwavering constant. He watched as the paramedic placed an oxygen mask gently over Sarah's face, his heart aching for her terror. He was her shield, her voice, her only anchor until the storm of her meltdown passed.
