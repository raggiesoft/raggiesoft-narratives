# Part 8: The Diagnosis 🫁

The minutes in the ER bay stretched into an hour. The initial chaos had subsided into a tense, quiet vigil. Jack sat beside the gurney, holding Sarah's hand, his thumb rubbing slow, comforting circles on her skin. Jermaine stood on her other side, his expression a mask of worried stillness. The only sounds were the steady beep of the monitor and Sarah's occasional, dry, hacking cough that sounded painful even to Jack's ears.

Finally, Dr. Evans returned, his expression serious but calm. "Okay," he began, looking at Jermaine and Jack. "We have the results from Sarah's initial tests."

He turned his focus to Sarah, speaking to her directly and respectfully, despite her inability to reply. "Sarah, your blood oxygen levels are good right now, and the first chest X-ray doesn't show any significant fluid buildup in your lungs, which is excellent news."

Jack felt a flicker of hope, but the doctor's next words quickly tempered it.

"However," he continued, "with chlorine gas exposure, our main concern is a delayed reaction. The chemical irritates the lung tissue, and it can cause a condition called chemical pneumonitis. That inflammation can lead to fluid buildup—pulmonary edema—hours after the exposure. Because of that risk, and because you're still symptomatic with the cough and throat irritation, we are going to admit you to the hospital for at least 24 hours of observation".

Jermaine let out a tense breath. "So she's staying?"

"Yes," Dr. Evans confirmed. "We'll be transferring her from the ER up to a room on the respiratory floor as soon as it's ready. We'll keep her on the humidified oxygen, monitor her breathing very closely, and start her on a course of steroids to help reduce the inflammation in her lungs".

Jack looked at his sister. He could see the fear in her eyes, red-rimmed from the chemical irritation. She squeezed his hand, her knuckles white. She was scared, but she was listening.

"Is she going to be okay, Doctor?" Jermaine asked, his voice strained.

"The observation period is key," the doctor said honestly. "But we are being proactive. In most cases with this level of exposure, patients respond very well to supportive care. We're doing everything we need to do."

He gave them a reassuring nod. "The transport team will be here shortly to take her up to her room. I'll check in on her again once she's settled."

The doctor left, leaving the three of them with the new reality. Sarah was being admitted. Jack looked at his sister, who was now quietly crying, the tears tracking silently from her irritated eyes. He squeezed her hand back, a silent, unwavering promise. *I'm not going anywhere. We'll get through this too.*
