# Part 13: The Turn ⏰

The 2:00 AM vital check came and went just as jarringly as the one at midnight, leaving both Jack and Sarah tense, annoyed, and wide awake for another hour. Jack eventually managed to drift into a shallow, fitful sleep on the lumpy pull-out sofa.

He was jolted awake again by the now-familiar sounds: the swish of the door, the merciless snap of the overhead lights. He squinted at his phone. 4:03 AM. A groan of pure, sleep-deprived frustration escaped him.

He pushed himself up, rubbing his eyes. The night-shift nurse, Karen, was already at Sarah's bedside. "4 AM check, folks," she whispered, though her voice felt like a shout in the small, bright room. "Almost morning."

Sarah, who had been dozing, let out an irritated cough as the nurse wrapped the blood pressure cuff around her arm. But this cough was different. It wasn't the dry, hacking sound from earlier. This one was wet, heavy, with a slight gurgling quality.

Karen froze, her hand still on the cuff, her professional focus sharpening instantly. "Sarah," she said, her voice losing its cheerful lilt, "can you cough again for me?"

Sarah, grumpy from being woken up yet again, gave another annoyed, forceful cough. The wet, rattling sound was unmistakable. She was too tired and irritated to notice the change in herself, but the nurse heard it loud and clear.

The nurse immediately grabbed the stethoscope hanging from her neck, placing the cold diaphragm against Sarah's back. "Take a deep breath for me, sweetie." Sarah complied with a sigh. The nurse listened, her expression growing more concerned.

"Okay," she said, her voice now all business. "Your breathing has some crackles in it. I don't like the sound of that cough." She was already moving to the in-room phone. "I'm paging the on-call resident, and I'm ordering a stat portable chest X-ray. We're going to get a look at your lungs right now".

The word "stat" cut through Jack's sleepy frustration, replaced by a jolt of cold fear. He was wide awake now. He swung his legs off the sofa and went to Sarah's bedside, taking her hand.

"Just... a cough," Sarah rasped, looking at Jack, her expression more confused and annoyed than scared.

But Jack saw the look on the nurse's face. He knew. This was the delayed reaction the ER doctor had warned them about. The fluid was hitting her lungs. He squeezed his sister's hand, his own exhaustion forgotten, as the long, difficult night took a sudden, dangerous turn.
