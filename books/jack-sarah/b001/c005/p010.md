The hospital room was quiet and private, a stark contrast to the controlled chaos of the ER. Once the transport team had left, Jack pulled the visitor's chair close to Sarah's new bed. Jermaine stood on the other side, his hand resting gently on his daughter's shoulder.

Sarah, looking exhausted, coughed a raw, dry cough and gestured weakly toward her throat. "Thirsty?" Jack asked immediately. She nodded. Jack flagged down a passing nurse, who returned a moment later with a small cup of ice chips, which Sarah accepted gratefully.

"Alright," Jermaine said, seeing his children settled. "I'm going to head home now, but I will be back shortly. I'll pack an overnight bag for you, Jack, and I'll grab both of your laptops so you have them. Then I'll head home for the night". He leaned down and kissed Sarah's forehead, then squeezed Jack's shoulder. "Call me if anything changes."

After he left, a new nurse entered, her smile kind and professional. "Hello, Sarah. I'm Karen, I'll be your nurse for the evening. Now that you're admitted, it's time to get you out of your street clothes and into a hospital gown." She glanced at Jack, a polite suggestion that he should step out.

Sarah, seeing the look, took a ragged breath and forced her voice to work for the first time in hours. The sound was a raw, painful croak. "He... can stay," she rasped.

The nurse nodded, understanding. As she began to help Sarah sit up, she took in the uniform: the city-issued polo shirt over sweatpants, and the lifeguard swimsuit visible underneath. "You came straight from the pool?"

"Sarah is a lifeguard at Fairlawn Recreation Center," Jack explained. "The suit under her clothes, the flip-flops... it's all so she's ready for a water rescue at a moment's notice".

"That explains the no socks," the nurse said with a smile, retrieving a pair of non-slip hospital socks from a drawer. With practiced gentleness, she helped the weak and trembling Sarah out of her damp uniform and into the clean, soft hospital gown.

"Do you need to use the restroom before you get settled in?" the nurse asked.

Sarah gave a small, grateful nod. The nurse skillfully helped her out of the bed, supporting her as they made the slow trip to the adjoining bathroom. Jack, respecting their privacy, moved to the pull-out sofa on the other side of the room, his presence a silent, comforting anchor.

A few minutes later, the nurse helped Sarah back to the bed, making sure she was comfortable before checking her IV and the flow of her oxygen. "You get some rest now," she said softly before leaving them in the quiet of the room. Sarah looked exhausted, but clean and finally settled. She met Jack's gaze from across the room, a silent look of gratitude passing between them. The long, terrible day was finally drawing to a close.
