# Part 1: An Unsteady Step 😟

The next day in Sarah's hospital room at Crossroads Memorial was blessedly, almost boringly, calm. The frantic energy of the emergency had been replaced by the quiet, monotonous routine of recovery. Sarah, now upgraded to a soft food diet, was slowly making her way through a small container of applesauce, the simple act of eating a monumental victory.

Jack, feeling the kinks in his back from another night on the pull-out sofa, stood up to stretch. The room felt small and confining after nearly two days. "I'm just going to walk around a bit," he murmured, more to himself than to Sarah.

He took a step, and then another, and immediately knew something was wrong.

His right foot felt heavy, clumsy. It wasn't the familiar muscle tightness from his Cerebral Palsy; this was different. He tried to lift his toes to take a normal step, but the front of his foot seemed to drag, refusing to listen to the commands from his brain. To keep from tripping, he had to consciously lift his knee higher than usual, his gait becoming a forced, awkward march. *Foot drop,* a clinical term from a long-ago neurology appointment, echoed in his mind.

A strange, persistent tingling, like a limb that had fallen asleep and was just starting to wake up, began to buzz in his ankles. He stopped, placing a hand against the cool wall to steady himself, a wave of cold fear washing over him. He knew this feeling. It was his Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease, a sleeping giant in his life, now stirring, undoubtedly woken by the immense stress and exhaustion of the last 48 hours.

"Jack?" Sarah's voice, still raspy but laced with concern, cut through his thoughts. "You okay? You're walking funny."

He looked over at her, at her pale face and the IV still taped to her hand. She was the patient here. She was the one who needed to be cared for. He couldn't burden her with his own failing body, not now.

He forced a smile, pushing away from the wall. "Yeah, I'm fine," he lied, the words tasting like ash. "Just... my leg's asleep from this stupid sofa. Just need to walk it off."

He continued his slow, clumsy lap around the small room, each high, slapping step of his right foot a terrifying reminder of his own progressive condition. He kept the smile plastered on his face for her, but inside, his mind was racing, grappling with the chilling realization that the crisis at Fairlawn might have cost him more than just a few nights of sleep.
