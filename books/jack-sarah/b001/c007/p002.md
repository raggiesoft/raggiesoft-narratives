# Part 2: A Flare-Up

Lunchtime rolled around, and with it, some great news from the doctor: Sarah was cleared for solid, soft foods. A nurse delivered her lunch tray, and for the first time in days, it contained something other than broth or gelatin. On the plate was a small scoop of mashed potatoes, a side of applesauce, and a cup of vanilla pudding. To Jack, it looked bland and uninspiring, but to Sarah, it was a five-star feast. She ate slowly, savoring every spoonful with a look of profound relief.

They were just finishing when a soft knock came at the door. Scott, Sarah's aquatics supervisor, stepped in, followed by a couple of other lifeguards and, to Jack's surprise, his own boss, Jennifer. They carried two cards, one signed by the whole rec center staff and another signed by some of the regular patrons who had heard what happened.

"Heard you were taking an unscheduled vacation," Scott teased gently, his humor a welcome, if weird, slice of normalcy. "We're holding down the fort. It's not the same without you".

The visit was a welcome wave of connection to the world outside the hospital walls. As they were chatting, Jennifer pulled Jack aside.

"Jack," she said, her voice low and kind, "you look like you haven't slept in a week. I'm putting you down for a day of paid leave for tomorrow. Don't even think about arguing. You be here with your sister."

Jack was too tired to argue. He just nodded, a wave of gratitude washing over him.

As his coworkers prepared to leave, Jack stood up to walk them to the door. He tried to hide it, but the weakness in his right leg made his gait a clumsy, high-stepped march to avoid tripping. A physical therapist, who had just entered the room to work with Sarah, paused and watched him walk, her expression thoughtful.

After the Fairlawn crew had left, the therapist turned to Jermaine, who had been sitting quietly in the corner. "Excuse me, Mr. Bennett," she said politely. "I couldn't help but notice your son's gait. Is he experiencing new difficulties with his walking?"

Jermaine's face was a mask of gentle concern. "It's a pre-existing condition," he explained. "But this is... a bad flare-up."

Once the therapist had finished her session with Sarah and left the room, Jermaine turned to Jack. "Son," he said softly, his eyes full of a sad understanding. "She noticed. I've been noticing, too. The stress from all this... it's making your CMT worse, isn't it?".

Jack couldn't deny it. He slumped into the chair, the energy it took to maintain a brave face gone. He just nodded, staring at the floor.

"We knew this day would be coming, eventually," Jermaine said, his voice heavy. "This flare-up is a warning. I think it's time we get it properly looked at. As soon as Sarah is home and settled, we're making an appointment for you with a neurologist. Okay?".

Jack looked at his sister, who was watching him with wide, worried eyes. A new, cold fear settled in his stomach. He gave his dad a small, reluctant nod. It wasn't just fear holding him back. It was a sense of profound, unfair timing. He wasn't ready for this. He *couldn't* be ready for this. Not now. Not while Sarah still needed him. She was the patient. His own body betraying him felt like a burden he refused to place on his recovering sister. The immediate crisis with Sarah was starting to pass, but it was becoming terrifyingly clear that it had triggered a new, and perhaps more lasting, one for him.
