The shared laughter from the prom memory faded, leaving the quiet hum of the hospital in its wake. The afternoon stretched on, long and uneventful. Jermaine, Molly, and Clare had stayed, their quiet presence a comfort as they all waited for the results of Sarah's afternoon X-ray.

They were all dozing fitfully when Dr. Evans returned, his expression noticeably lighter than it had been that morning. He was smiling.

"Well, folks," he began, his voice warm and optimistic. "I have some very good news. Sarah's afternoon chest X-ray shows the fluid in her lungs has significantly decreased. You're responding beautifully to the treatments."

A collective, audible sigh of relief went through the family. Jack squeezed his sister's hand, Molly and Clare rushed to the other side of her bed, and Jermaine's tense posture finally relaxed.

The doctor's smile widened. "Which means," he said, "I am officially upgrading your diet from NPO to clear liquids".

A sound that was half a sob and half a laugh escaped Sarah's raw throat. Tears of pure, unadulterated relief welled in her eyes.

"Alright!" Jermaine said, his voice booming with happiness. "That is the best news I've heard all day. In that case," he announced, turning to the twins, "Molly, Clare, you're with me. We're going to go raid the cafeteria. Jack, you need to eat. What do you want?"

Jack looked at Sarah, who, despite her own hunger, gave him a firm, insistent nod. She wanted him to eat. "Just a sandwich, Dad. Anything."

Jermaine, Molly, and Clare returned a short while later, their hands full of trays laden with food. The savory aroma filled the small hospital room. At the same time, a nurse came in with a small, steaming styrofoam cup of golden broth for Sarah.

The family hesitated, their own full meals looking like a feast compared to Sarah's meager cup. Seeing their reluctance, Sarah's voice, still a rough croak but full of her old, bossy affection, cut through the quiet.

"Don't... you dare... not eat," she rasped, pointing a shaky finger at them all. "Eat. I'm fine".

She took a slow, careful sip of her broth, a look of sublime satisfaction on her face. "This is amazing," she declared. "And tomorrow," she added, a weary but definite twinkle in her eye, "I get the slop".

The callback to her joke from the day before, her insistence that they eat, broke the last of the tension. The family laughed, and finally, gratefully, began their own meals. The small hospital room was filled with the quiet, happy sounds of a family sharing a meal—or a version of one—together for the first time since the crisis began, united in their relief.
