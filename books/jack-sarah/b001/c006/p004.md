# Part 4: A Glimmer of Hope 😮‍💨

The fifteen minutes of the breathing treatment passed in a strange, humming silence. Jack and Jermaine sat quietly, watching the mist from the nebulizer swirl around the mouthpiece Sarah held to her lips. The movie remained paused, a frozen image of fantasy on the laptop screen.

When the respiratory therapist, Mark, returned and turned off the machine, the sudden quiet was almost startling. "All done for now, Sarah," he said kindly. "We'll do this again in about four hours. Try to take some deep, slow breaths."

Sarah took a tentative breath. Her chest still felt tight and sore, but maybe, just maybe, the raw, burning sensation was a fraction less intense. She gave a noncommittal shrug, still too grumpy and exhausted to acknowledge any potential improvement.

After Mark left, her stomach let out another loud, angry gurgle of protest. She glared down at her hospital gown, then at her father and brother, her frustration palpable.

"Still... hungry," she rasped, the words a familiar, miserable complaint. She understood she was NPO, but the understanding did nothing to soothe the gnawing emptiness.

A few minutes later, another cough wracked her body. Jack and Jermaine both tensed instinctively, bracing for the awful, wet, gurgling sound that had terrified them in the pre-dawn hours.

But this time, the cough was different. While still rough and painful-sounding, it was noticeably *drier*. The rattling, fluid-filled sound that had signaled the crisis was significantly less pronounced.

Jack's eyes shot up, meeting his father's over Sarah's bed. He saw the same flicker of surprise and cautious hope he felt himself.

"Did you hear that?" Jermaine murmured, his voice a low whisper so Sarah wouldn't feel like she was being analyzed. "It sounds... better. Not as wet".

"Yeah, Dad," Jack whispered back, a knot of fear he hadn't realized was still clenched in his stomach beginning to loosen just a tiny bit. "I heard it too".

It was the first, tangible sign that the treatments were working, that she was turning a corner. In the weary, sleep-deprived quiet of the hospital room, it was a small but profound glimmer of hope.
