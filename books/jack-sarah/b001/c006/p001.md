# Part 1: The Morning Report

The sun was fully up, but its bright morning light did nothing to dispel the grumpy, exhausted fog that had settled over the hospital room. Jack felt like his eyes were full of sand, having managed maybe an hour of fragmented sleep on the lumpy sofa. He looked over at Sarah, who was propped up in bed, glaring at the wall, her arms crossed tightly. She looked just as miserable as he felt.

The door opened, and Dr. Evans came in, looking entirely too fresh and cheerful for this hour. "Good morning, you two. The nurse tells me it was a bit of a rough night."

Sarah's stomach chose that moment to let out a loud, angry gurgle, a clear protest against its empty state. She shot a frustrated look at the doctor.

Dr. Evans gave a sympathetic smile. "I know, I know. Let's talk about that." He reviewed the chart in his hand. "Well, Sarah, the good news is the diuretics are doing their job. Your breathing is already sounding clearer than it was a few hours ago, and your body is getting rid of that excess fluid. You're responding well to the treatment."

Jack felt a small knot of tension unwind in his chest. That was good news.

"We're going to continue the IV diuretics throughout the day," the doctor continued, "and we'll get another chest X-ray this afternoon to see how much progress we've made."

Sarah's patience had clearly run out. Her voice, still a raw, painful croak, cut him off. "Food?" she rasped. "How much longer... NPO?".

"I know it's frustrating," Dr. Evans said kindly. "But I need you to stay NPO for a while longer. Your lungs are still working very hard, and we can't risk anything until you're breathing much more easily. However," he added, seeing the flash of angry disappointment in her eyes, "if the afternoon X-ray looks good and you continue to improve, I see no reason why we can't start you on a clear liquid diet by dinnertime."

It wasn't the steak dinner she was likely dreaming of, but it was something. Jack saw the fight in his sister's eyes soften just a fraction, replaced by a weary resolve.

The doctor gave them another encouraging nod and left the room. Jack looked at Sarah, and she looked back at him, a shared, grumpy understanding passing between them. They were tired, they were hungry, and they were stuck here for at least another day.
