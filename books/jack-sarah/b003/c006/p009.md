The afternoon light began to fade, and the quiet of the house was broken by the sound of the front door opening. Molly was home from work. She walked into the living room to find Jack lying on the sofa, his head resting in Sarah’s lap, while Clare and Grandpa Sam sat quietly nearby.

"Hey everyone," Molly said softly, her eyes immediately going to Jack, a look of concern on her face.

"He's okay," Clare whispered, getting up to give her twin a hug. "He did his big walk for the day. He made it all around the house and even out onto the back deck. But it completely wiped him out".

Molly looked at her cousin, who was dozing fitfully on his sister's lap. A wave of pride and love washed over her. "He tried," she said, her voice full of admiration. "That's what matters".

The quiet of the recovery was starting to feel heavy. A new, unspoken consensus began to form in the room.

"You know what we should do?" Sarah suggested from the sofa. "It feels like a movie night."

"The Silver Gauntlet of Aethel," the four cousins said in near-perfect, quiet unison.

Molly grinned. "For the millionth time? Absolutely." She went to the entertainment center and retrieved the well-worn DVD case, popping the disc into the player for the living room's large TV. She and Clare then began a quiet, careful negotiation of the sofa, figuring out how to arrange themselves so they could both be close to their cousins without disturbing Jack, who was still resting on Sarah's lap.

As the film's familiar, dramatic menu appeared on the screen, accompanied by its cheesy synthesizer score, Jack stirred. He looked up at the screen, his expression groggy. "Can you... turn on the closed captions?" he asked, his voice a tired mumble.

The three girls exchanged a quick, puzzled look. Jack had never asked for captions before. But without a word of protest, Molly picked up the remote and navigated to the settings, turning the captioning on. They respected his need, even if they didn't understand it.

"Snack time," Grandpa Sam announced from the doorway. He returned a moment later with a tray bearing four glasses of ice water and a large bowl of what was, he assured them, the "healthy kind" of popcorn. He settled into his armchair, a warm, contented smile on his face as he watched his four grandchildren, huddled together on the sofa, completely absorbed in the opening scene of their favorite movie. The world outside, with all its dangers and worries, could wait. For the next two hours, they were safe in their cheesy, 1980s sanctuary.
