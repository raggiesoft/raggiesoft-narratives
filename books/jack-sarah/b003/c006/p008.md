The next morning, the house was a little quieter. Jermaine, Devin, and Molly had all left for work, leaving Jack, Sarah, Clare, and Grandpa Sam to hold down the fort. After a quiet breakfast in The Quad, Jack felt a familiar restlessness stirring. The triumphant walk at Fairlawn a few days ago, fueled by the sheer joy of being in his second home, had felt like a massive victory. But the adrenaline had long since faded, leaving a deep, aching exhaustion in its place. He knew he had overdone it, and now he was paying the price.

"I want to walk today," he announced, his voice firm, trying to project a confidence he didn't feel. "I want to see the rest of the house."

"That's a great idea," Clare said immediately, already getting up.

"We'll go with you," Sarah added, her expression a familiar, comforting mix of pride and protectiveness.

With Sarah on one side and Clare on the other, Jack slowly and painfully pushed himself to his feet. Each movement sent a sharp, protesting ache through his chest, a stark reminder of the incision. The walk was nothing like the one at Fairlawn. Every step was a conscious, difficult effort. His legs felt heavy and weak, and the triumphant energy from his last outing was gone, replaced by a grim, painful determination. He leaned on the walker, his knuckles white, as the trio made their slow, careful way out of The Quad and into the main living area.

It was Jack's first real tour of the home he now shared with his entire family. He took in the spacious living room, the large dining area, and the bright, open kitchen. He was grateful, profoundly so, but the physical struggle made it hard to fully appreciate.

"It's my last day of paid leave," Sarah mentioned as they made a slow lap around the kitchen island. "I go back to work tomorrow".

Jack stopped, leaning heavily against the counter, a wave of dizziness washing over him. A flicker of his old anxiety returned. She was his constant, his primary anchor during this recovery. "Are you sure you're ready?" he asked, his concern for her momentarily overriding his own physical struggle.

"I'm ready," she said, her voice full of a quiet confidence. "And you'll be fine. Clare will be here with you and Grandpa tomorrow, and Molly will be home in the afternoon. You're never going to be alone here."

He knew she was right. He looked from his sister to his cousin, his two unwavering supporters, and nodded. He took a deep, shuddering breath and pushed off the counter. "Okay," he said, his voice strained. "Let's see the backyard."

Sarah pushed open the back door, which swung inwards, revealing a spacious wooden deck. A large grill sat in one corner, and a patio table with six chairs occupied the center, waiting for warmer days. The deck was large enough for Jack to walk around, and he took a few slow, painful steps, his walker scraping softly against the wood.

He looked out beyond the deck. Three steps led down to a concrete pad surrounding a large in-ground pool, its protective winter cover pulled taut over the surface. The sight of it sent a sharp pang through him—a memory of summer, of swimming, of a life that felt suddenly and impossibly far away. The effort of the walk, combined with the wave of melancholy, was too much. His strength vanished.

"I... I need to sit down," he gasped, his legs trembling.

Sarah and Clare were there in an instant, helping him into one of the patio chairs. The moment he was seated, Sarah sat beside him, wrapping him in a fierce, protective hug, careful of his tender incision. "You're doing so well, Jack," she whispered into his ear. "Every step is a victory".

He leaned into her, the cold air seeping into his bones. "I'm cold," he said, his voice trembling slightly. "Can we go back inside? Maybe... maybe have some hot chocolate to warm up?".

Sarah's hug tightened for a fraction of a second. She pulled back just enough to look at him, her expression full of a gentle, heartbreaking sympathy. "Oh, buddy," she said softly. "The doctor said you have to stick to a heart-healthy diet. All the sugar and fat in hot chocolate... it's probably a bad idea right now".

The words, as gentle as they were, hit Jack like a physical blow. It wasn't just hot chocolate. It was the pool he couldn't use, the rollercoasters he couldn't ride, another small, simple joy his own broken body had stolen from him. The unfairness of it all was overwhelming. He didn't get angry; he just crumpled, burying his face in his sister's shoulder as a wave of helpless, frustrated tears began to fall.

Clare, who had been watching with a look of deep concern, moved in on his other side, adding her own hug to Sarah's, creating a warm, protective cocoon around their grieving cousin.

"It's okay to be upset, Jack," Clare murmured. "This is hard. We've got you."

After a few moments, his sobs subsided into shuddering breaths. He was still upset, but the storm had passed. "Okay," he mumbled, his voice thick. "I'm just... really cold now. Can we please go inside?".

Getting him up and back into the house was a struggle. He had no energy left, and the short distance to the door felt like a mile. Sarah and Clare had to support most of his weight, their own strength straining as they half-carried him over the threshold. The bed in The Quad suddenly seemed a world away.

"The sofa," Sarah said, making an executive decision. "It's closer".

They managed to get him to the living room sofa, but as they lowered him down, he looked at them with a fresh wave of humiliation. "I... I need to use the bathroom," he admitted.

Without a word, the two women helped him back up and guided him the short distance to the first-floor bathroom. They helped him sit, helped him with his pajamas, and helped him clean up afterward, their movements full of a simple, practical love that asked for nothing in return.

Finally, they got him settled on the sofa. Sarah sat down first, and Jack lay down, resting his head in her lap. Clare took a spot on the floor beside them, a quiet, steadying presence. His energy was completely gone; his eyes were already drifting shut.

"If you need anything, Jack," Clare said softly, "you just say the word. Me or Grandpa Sam will get it. You just stay there and be comfortable with Sarah. We've got you".

Jack didn't respond. He was already succumbing to a deep, bone-weary exhaustion, his world narrowed to the comforting weight of his head on his sister's lap, safe in the circle of his family's unwavering love.
