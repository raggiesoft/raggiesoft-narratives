The first thing Jack was aware of was a low, steady beeping and the murmur of serious voices. He was floating in a groggy, grey fog, unable to open his eyes.

.”..simply wouldn't inflate," one voice said, the words sharp and clinical. "Complete blockage. We were losing him. Had to abort immediately."

"So it's a failure," another voice stated. "He'll need to be transferred to the Heart Hospital for bypass. As soon as they have a bed."

The voices stopped abruptly. Jack felt a gentle touch on his shoulder. "Jack? Can you hear me? The procedure is over."

He managed to force his heavy eyelids open. The world was a blur of ceiling tiles and masked faces. He was being wheeled somewhere. The fog was thick, but one thought cut through it, clear and urgent. "Sarah?" he rasped, his throat dry and sore.

"She's waiting for you in your room," a nurse's kind voice reassured him.

As they wheeled him back into his room, his vision started to clear. The first thing he saw was his sister's face. She was trying to smile, but her eyes were red-rimmed and full of a terror he'd never seen before. She rushed to his side as he was transferred back into his bed.

"Hey," he mumbled. "It's over?"

"Yeah, it's over," she said, her voice thick. She took his hand, her grip tight.

"Why do you look so upset?" Jack asked, a new anxiety coiling in his stomach. "Did it... not work?"

Sarah's face crumpled for a second before she regained her composure. "The doctors will be in to talk to you in a minute, Jack," she said, avoiding his eyes. "They can explain everything better." She felt it was the doctors' place, not hers, to tell him the full, horrifying story.

A moment later, a doctor entered the room, his expression somber but kind. He pulled up a chair. "Jack," he began gently, "I'm sorry to have to tell you this, but the balloon angioplasty was not successful. The blockage was too severe, and we encountered a life-threatening complication."

The doctor explained what had happened: the balloon failing to inflate, the vessel becoming 100% blocked, and the surgical team having to remove it immediately to save his life. "To be very direct," the doctor said, his gaze steady, "you came very close to death on the operating table. The team's decision to abort saved you."

Jack just stared, the words not fully registering. *Close to death.*

"Because the angioplasty failed," the doctor continued, "you're going to need a more significant surgery. We're arranging a transfer to the Heart Hospital as soon as they have a bed open for you. For now, you'll stay here with us."

The doctor left them to process the news. *Close to death.* The phrase echoed in the sudden, deafening quiet of the room. It took a moment for the reality to crash down on him. He hadn't just had a failed surgery; he had almost not woken up at all. A violent tremor ran through him. He turned his head, his eyes finding his sister's.

"Sarah," he choked out, his voice breaking. He lifted his free arm, a desperate, silent plea.

She was there in an instant, wrapping her arms around him as best she could. The doctor's words crashed over her, and a new, horrifying realization twisted in her gut, sharp and agonizing. *He almost died. He almost died on my birthday.* The thought was a silent scream in her mind. Her birthday present, the one she had asked for just this morning—for him to be okay—had almost been snatched away in the most brutal way imaginable. The day of her birth would have become the day of his death. Her own tears finally fell, hot and heavy, a torrent of grief for a tragedy that had come terrifyingly close to being real. Jack held on to her as tightly as the medical equipment attached to him would allow, burying his face in her shoulder, the terrifying, abstract concept of his own mortality suddenly, horribly real.
