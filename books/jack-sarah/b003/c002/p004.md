# Part 4: A Veteran's Reassurance

Jack clung to his sister, the stark reality of his near-death experience washing over him in waves of terror. The world outside the circle of Sarah’s arms felt distant and muffled. She held him tightly, her own tears soaking the shoulder of his hospital gown, her mind reeling with the horrifying thought: *He almost died. He almost died on my birthday*. The thought was a silent, agonizing scream.

When they finally, shakily, drew apart, Sarah quickly wiped her eyes, forcing herself to take a deep, steadying breath. She had to be strong for him. Now was not the time for her own fear. Jack looked around the sterile room, his expression lost and terrified, his need for his family a palpable, desperate thing.

“I want Dad,” he whispered, his voice trembling. “And I want my cousins. I want Uncle Devin. I want Grandpa.”

“I know,” Sarah said soothingly, her own voice remarkably steady despite the storm raging inside her. She was already pulling out her phone to start making the calls. “They’re all on their way.”

True to her word, the first to arrive was Grandpa Sam. Being retired, he was able to get to the hospital the quickest. He entered the room quietly, his presence a familiar, comforting anchor. He looked from Sarah’s pale, tear-streaked face to his grandson lying in the hospital bed, and his own expression was a mixture of deep love and profound worry. He came to Jack’s side, taking his hand in his own warm, steady grip.

“Jack, my boy,” he said, his voice a low, comforting rumble. “We’re here. We’re all here for you.” He didn’t offer platitudes or easy reassurances; he just offered his presence, a solid, unshakeable foundation for Jack to cling to.

A short while later, Jermaine and Devin arrived together, having clearly rushed from work the moment they got Sarah’s frantic, terrified call about Jack’s near-death experience. Their faces were masks of fear and paternal concern. Jermaine went straight to his son’s other side, his hand resting on Jack’s shoulder, while Devin stood by, his steady presence a comfort in the emotionally charged room. Jack looked at his uncle, his fear palpable.

Devin pulled a chair close to the bed. “Hey, kiddo,” he said softly. “Sarah told me. That’s… that’s a lot to take in. It’s okay to be scared out of your mind. I know I was.”

The simple validation, the admission of fear from his strong uncle, was a small anchor for Jack.

“They told me… they’re going to send me to the Heart Hospital,” Jack said.

Devin nodded, his expression serious but calm. “They are. And they’re probably going to do what they did for me. It’s called a CABG,” he said, the acronym sounding strange and clinical. “We all just called it ‘cabbage’ surgery. It’s a bypass.”

He leaned forward, explaining it in simple terms, as one veteran to another. “Basically, your artery is too blocked for that little balloon to work. So, they take a healthy blood vessel from somewhere else, usually your leg, and they build a little detour—a bypass—around the part that’s clogged up. It’s a long, grueling surgery,” he said, his voice full of a quiet honesty that was more reassuring than any platitude. “And the recovery… it’s a very long road. But you can do it. I did it.”

Jack listened, absorbing the daunting information. The thought of such a massive surgery was terrifying. But looking at his uncle, who had a heart attack, had the same surgery, and was sitting here now, calm and strong… it didn't erase the fear, but it gave it a face. It gave it a path. He was still scared, but for the first time since the doctor had delivered the news, he felt a fragile sliver of hope.

A few minutes later, after the initial, raw shock had begun to recede, two more figures appeared in the doorway, their faces pale and their eyes wide with fear. Molly and Clare had arrived, having rushed from their jobs, likely getting stuck in traffic on the way. Sarah had told them on the phone what the doctor had said—that Jack had almost died on the operating table—and the terror of that news was still fresh on their faces.

The moment they saw Jack, however, their expressions shifted. They put on brave, strong faces for him, masks of determined cheerfulness that Jack, knowing them so well, could see right through.

“Hey, you,” Molly said, her voice a little too bright as she came to his side. “Giving everyone a scare, huh?”

“We heard you were being dramatic,” Clare added, forcing a small smile as she took his hand.

Jack managed a weak smile back. “Had to keep you ladies on your toes.” He needed to see them, to have his whole family there, and their presence, even with the poorly concealed fear in their eyes, was an immense comfort.

For a brief moment, the family was all together, a tight, protective circle around Jack’s hospital bed. Devin looked at his two daughters, his expression grim but steady. He knew he had to explain the reality of what was coming next, just as he had for Jack.

“Girls,” he began, his voice low, “Jack is going to need the same surgery I had. A bypass. A CABG.”

The brave faces Molly and Clare had put on for their cousin crumbled instantly. Their own memories of their father’s grueling surgery and the long, difficult months of his recovery came flooding back. They knew exactly what Jack was in for: the pain, the exhaustion, the slow, arduous process of healing. They remembered taking care of their father, the worry, the setbacks, the small victories. The thought of their beloved cousin having to endure the same thing was a devastating blow.

Tears welled in Molly’s eyes, and Clare let out a small, choked sob. They each grabbed one of Jack’s hands, their earlier pretense of bravery replaced by a shared, empathetic sorrow. They didn’t offer empty platitudes or tell him it would be easy. They knew it wouldn’t be. Instead, their hugs and their tears conveyed a deeper, more powerful message: *We know how hard this is going to be. And we will be right here with you, every single step of the way.*
