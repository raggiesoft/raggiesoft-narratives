# Part 2: The Journey to the OR

The quiet of the morning was broken by a cheerful but firm knock. A hospital transporter entered the room with a gurney, the squeak of its wheels sharp in the tense silence. "Morning, Jack. Time to get you ready to go."

The moment had arrived. As the transporter and a nurse began the process of preparing him for the move, Jack glanced around the room. He was surprised Delores hadn't shown up, but he caught Sarah's eye and saw his own feeling reflected there: they were both not complaining.

As the transporter began to wheel him out, Jack reached out his hand, not towards the door, but directly to his sister. Sarah took it without a second’s hesitation, her grip firm and reassuring, just as she had during the long trek from the emergency room the day before.

She walked beside the gurney, matching its pace, their hands linked the entire way. The journey through the sterile hallways felt surreal, the squeak of the wheels and the distant, muted hospital sounds the only accompaniment to their silent communion. Finally, they arrived at a set of large, intimidating double doors marked "Surgical Suite." The transporter paused.

"This is as far as family can go," he said gently.

Sarah squeezed Jack's hand one last time before letting go.

"Wait," Jack said, his voice a little shaky. He looked at his sister. "One last hug?"

"Always," she whispered, stepping forward. She leaned over the gurney and wrapped her arms around him, holding him tightly. It was a hug that conveyed everything they couldn't say—her fear for him, her unwavering love, and a fierce promise of protection.

As she pulled back, she kept her hands on his shoulders, her gaze steady and sure. "Dad and I will be right here in the waiting room the whole time," she promised. "We're not going anywhere. I'll be the first person you see when you wake up, okay?"

Jack nodded, unable to speak past the lump in his throat. The surgical doors swished open, and the transporter began to wheel him inside. He looked back at his sister one last time before the doors closed, leaving her alone in the quiet hallway.

The world inside was a sudden, stark contrast. The hallway opened into a large, cold room that smelled sharply of antiseptic. A huge, multi-faceted light loomed directly over a narrow table in the center of the room. Machines with screens and wires stood like silent sentinels on all sides, beeping softly. Everyone was dressed in blue or green scrubs, their faces hidden behind masks, leaving only their focused, professional eyes visible.

“Alright, Jack, we’re just going to move you over to the table now,” a calm voice said. Hands gently but firmly shifted him from the gurney to the hard, cold operating table. The bright light overhead was blinding.

A woman with kind eyes leaned over him. “Hi, Jack. I’m Dr. Evans, from anesthesia. I’m going to be taking care of you today, making sure you’re comfortable and asleep for the procedure.”

He felt someone else placing sticky pads on his chest and an oxygen mask over his nose and mouth. “Just breathe normally for me,” another voice instructed.

“Okay, Jack,” Dr. Evans said, her voice a calm anchor in the disorienting preparations. “You’ll feel a little pinch in your arm now.” He felt the sharp prick of the IV needle. “I’m starting the medication. You might feel a little coldness in your arm. I just need you to count backward from ten for me.”

Jack stared up at the blinding light, the faces of the masked team blurring around him. He took a breath of the cool, clean oxygen, and his last conscious thought was of his sister’s promise. *Sarah will be there when I wake up.*

“Ten… nine…” he began, his voice muffled by the mask.

The beeping of the monitors seemed to grow distant.

“Eight… sev…”

The edges of his vision softened, and the bright light dissolved into a peaceful, welcoming darkness.
