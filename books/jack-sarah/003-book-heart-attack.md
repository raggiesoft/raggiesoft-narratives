# Book: Heart Attack

## Chapter 1: Hospital Arrival

### Part 1: Arrival at the Emergency Room

The ambulance dropped Jack off at the emergency department of Crossroads Memorial Hospital, and he was transferred to a hospital gurney. He was taken to a triage room where paramedics began explaining his condition to a nurse. It wasn’t too long before Sarah arrived, her face etched with worry. “Dad is on the way. How are you feeling?” she asked, her hand finding his.

“My breathing seems to have calmed down a little with the oxygen,” Jack said, his voice still a bit shaky. “I’m just waiting for them to start conducting a few tests.” He squeezed her hand, grateful she was there. A pang of obligation went through him. He and Delores were supposed to be on a Valentine's Day date tonight. He should probably let her know. He fumbled for his phone and sent a quick, simple text: Had to go to the hospital. At Crossroads. Chest pain. I’m okay.

A nurse then helped him move from the gurney to an oversized recliner chair in a bay separated only by a privacy curtain, which was left open as he didn't need privacy at the moment.

A few minutes later, Delores rushed into the bay, her eyes wide. She was putting on a mask of frantic concern to hide her genuine annoyance at the inconvenience. This was her last chance to prove to Jack that she was a good, caring person, and she knew it.

“Jack! Oh my God, your text… I came as soon as I could,” she said, her voice a little too breathless. “What happened? Are you okay?”

“I don’t know,” Jack replied honestly. “I started feeling chest pain at work, and now I’m here. How did you get here so fast, anyway?”

“It was the strangest coincidence,” she said, shaking her head as if in disbelief. “My mother’s office is literally right around the corner from here. I was just leaving her work when I got your text. I ran right over.”

Before Jack could answer, Sarah stepped in, her voice calm and even. “He’s stable for now. We’re waiting for the doctors to run some tests.”

Delores’s smile tightened slightly at the calm interruption, her frantic performance momentarily undercut by Sarah’s quiet authority. Delores leaned closer to Jack, lowering her voice so only he could hear. “I was just so looking forward to tonight… We had plans, remember?” she whispered. Her tone was one of disappointment, but to Jack, it felt like a guilt trip. He was in a hospital chair with an unknown heart issue, and she was talking about their canceled date. The final red flag he had been waiting for was waving right in front of him. He knew now, with absolute certainty, that Molly, Clare, and Sarah had been right all along.

A doctor came by, and Delores fell silent as he explained to Jack that they had found something concerning and wanted to run more tests.

“Listen,” Jack said to the doctor, his voice clear. “I have an appointment with my primary care doctor soon for a stress test. If you don’t find anything definitive, I’m fine leaving and following up with her. But… if you find something seriously wrong, and you feel in good medical conscience that discharging me is a bad idea, then I’ll agree to stay.”

The doctor nodded. “That’s a very reasonable approach, Jack. Let’s see what the tests show.”

Delores’s frustration was palpable. Her plans for the evening were ruined, and now Jack was setting conditions for staying. Jermaine arrived then, his expression a mixture of fear and determination. After getting a quick update from Jack, a new doctor came by.

"Jack," the doctor said, his expression serious. "The initial tests and the EKG show some significant irregularities. In our medical opinion, it would be a very bad idea to discharge you. We need to admit you to the main hospital’s cardiac unit immediately for monitoring and more tests."

Jack looked at his father, then at Sarah, and nodded. "Okay," he said to the doctor. "I'll stay."

"Stay?" Delores repeated, her voice a sharp, incredulous hiss the moment the doctor was out of earshot. "You're going to *stay* here? We had plans, Jack! Real plans!"

Delores ignored Sarah and Jermaine, her focus entirely on Jack. "This is ridiculous. You're fine. You're just being dramatic. I had a whole evening planned, a way to make things... special." She leaned closer, her voice dropping to a low, venomous whisper meant only for him. "I was even going to offer to finally fix your little virginity problem tonight. But I guess you'd rather lie here and be coddled by your sister."

A bolt of white-hot fury shot through Jermaine. His hands clenched into fists at his sides, every protective instinct screaming at him to step forward, grab this cruel, disrespectful young woman by the arm, and throw her out of the hospital himself. Sarah, standing beside him, went rigid, her face a mask of cold, silent rage. Her own protective instincts were roaring, and she took a half-step forward, ready to verbally tear Delores apart for daring to attack her brother when he was at his most vulnerable.

But they both stopped. They caught each other's eye, a silent, agonizing agreement passing between them. Jack was an adult. This was, for the moment, his relationship. As much as they hated it, as much as every fiber of their being wanted to intervene, they had to respect his autonomy to handle it himself.

Delores leaned back, a cruel smirk on her face, completely oblivious to the violent storm she had just unleashed in the other two adults in the room. She expected to see a flush of shame on Jack's face. Instead, he just looked at her with a completely blank, genuinely confused expression. The word "virgin" registered, but only in the academic context he knew.

He tilted his head slightly. "Virgin?" he repeated, his voice quiet and questioning. "Like... a Vestal Virgin? From ancient Rome? The priestesses who tended the sacred fire for the goddess Vesta?" He was genuinely trying to understand her reference. "Or are you talking about the concept in Islam, the Houris who are companions to the faithful in paradise? I read about that in my 'World Religions' class..."

Delores just stared at him, her smirk faltering, replaced by a look of utter bafflement. Her insult had clattered uselessly to the floor. "You're... you're unbelievable," she finally sputtered.

Standing by the side of the room, Sarah couldn't help it. A single, sharp giggle escaped her, which she tried to stifle behind her hand.

Delores's head snapped toward her. "What's so funny?" she demanded.

Sarah lowered her hand, her expression once again calm and unreadable. "Oh, nothing," she said, her voice cool and even. "You wouldn't get it."

"What wouldn't I get?" Delores rounded on her, her face a mask of fury.

"You wouldn't get that the insult you just tried to use didn't land because it doesn't mean to him what it means to you," Sarah said calmly. "You wouldn't get that trying to shame him is pointless because he has nothing to be ashamed of. You wouldn't get that this," she gestured to Jack, the IV stand, the heart monitor, "is a medical emergency, not an inconvenience. But mostly," she took a small step forward, her voice dropping to a low, final, and utterly dismissive tone, "you just wouldn't get *him*. At all. And you never will."

She held Delores's furious gaze for another beat, then turned her back, pointedly returning her attention to her brother, signaling that as far as she was concerned, Delores and the conversation were completely, irrevocably over.

Across the room, Jermaine watched the entire, ugly exchange, a cold fury settling in his gut. His hand was clenched into a fist at his side. He had half a mind to step in and throw this disrespectful, cruel young woman out of the room himself. But he stopped, his gaze falling on his son. Jack was an adult. This was his relationship, his decision to make. As much as every protective instinct screamed at him to intervene, he knew he had to respect Jack's agency.

Delores was lucky, in that moment, that the family did not dismiss her. She was lucky that Jermaine chose to honor his son’s autonomy over his own powerful impulse to protect him.

### Part 2: Transfer to Main Hospital

The transport team arrived with a gurney, and the process of moving Jack from the oversized recliner began. As they prepared to wheel him out, Jack’s eyes found Sarah’s, a silent plea for stability in a world that was spinning out of control. He reached out his hand, not towards Delores, but directly and only to his sister.

Sarah took his hand without a second’s hesitation, her grip firm and reassuring. “I’m right here, Jack,” she said softly. “We’re not going anywhere.”

The journey from the ER to the main hospital was a long, surreal trek through a maze of sterile hallways. Jack lay on the gurney, staring up at the ceiling tiles flicking past, the only constant being the steady, warm pressure of Sarah’s hand in his. She walked beside the gurney the entire way, her presence a silent, unwavering anchor.

Jermaine walked on Jack's other side, his face a mask of worried resolve. Delores trailed a few steps behind them, her face a frozen picture of polite concern. Internally, however, she was seething.

*He’s holding her hand?* she thought, her eyes fixed on their linked fingers. *His sister’s hand? Not mine? What is this? She’s treating him like a child, and he’s letting her.* The intimacy of the gesture, the clear preference he showed for his sister’s comfort over hers in this moment of crisis, felt like a profound and personal insult. But she knew she was being watched by Jermaine, by the nurses. She kept her opinions locked behind a tight, false smile.

As they continued, a new, uglier thought began to worm its way into Delores's mind, a suspicion born from her own toxic worldview and her experience with Leo's "precious sisters." *This is… weird. The way they touch, the way he looks at her. It’s not normal. Is there more to this? Are they… you know?* The thought was a disgusting, jealous whisper, a complete misreading of their innocent, platonic bond, but in Delores's mind, it was a plausible explanation for her exclusion. It was a seed of suspicion that, for her, explained everything.

Through fluorescent-lit corridors, past bustling nurses' stations, and into a large, echoing elevator, Jack never let go of Sarah’s hand. The simple, physical connection was the only thing keeping his rising panic at bay.

### Part 3: Rooming It at the Hospital

Jack was moved by stretcher from the emergency room to the main hospital where a bed was waiting for him. The plan was to do a few more tests to see how the hospital wanted to tackle the cause of this. Delores set herself up on the hospital sofa while Sarah stood at Jack’s side, her mission to comfort her frightened brother. Meanwhile, Delores sighed and checked her phone, functioning as if being in the hospital was an inconvenience to her plans for the day.

“You can always go home if you want,” Jack said, noticing her impatience. “My father and my sister can take care of me while I’m in the hospital, since you don’t seem to be happy here.”

Delores gave Jack a snooty look, about to retort, when Jermaine spoke, his voice low and firm. “Young lady, if that is how you’re going to treat my son, then I’m not sure if I can give you my blessing.”

Sarah added, her own voice quiet but solid, “There’s no reason to be rude to my brother like that. I’m in full agreement with my father and you won’t have my blessing either.”

Delores froze, the united front of Jack's family catching her completely off guard. She saw the look on Jermaine’s face and the unwavering resolve in Sarah’s. She was on thin ice. She realized she was about to make a huge mistake and quickly masked her true feelings, despite still seething that Jack had wanted to hold Sarah’s hand during the transfer. She forced a look of contrition. “You’re right,” she said, her voice suddenly soft. “I’m so sorry. I’m just… worried and stressed, and it’s coming out all wrong. I’m sorry, Jack.”

Jermaine just gave a curt nod, unconvinced. The conversation shifted as a nurse came in to explain the next step. "The doctor has ordered an ultrasound of your heart to get a better look."

“Ultrasound? The thing they use to look at babies?” Jack asked, his curiosity momentarily overriding his fear.

“Yes, it can also be used for looking at other things such as your heart and blood vessels,” Jermaine explained.

Jack’s eyebrows raised. “Wow. I honestly thought they were just for pregnant women. You learn something new every day, I guess.”

Delores just nodded, feigning interest. Internally, she was ready to explode. An ultrasound? Like for a baby? Of course. He holds his sister’s hand like a toddler, they talk to him like a child, and now they’re giving him a baby test. This is ridiculous. She kept the thought locked behind a polite, neutral expression, knowing she was being watched.

A few moments later, a stretcher was brought into the room. Jack was moved onto it, still on hospital-ordered bed rest. Jermaine, Delores, and Sarah followed as a transporter wheeled him down the hall until they got to the double doors of the nuclear medicine suite. The doctor asked them to wait outside.

The world became a series of unsettling moments. The doors swished open, and he was wheeled into a room that was dark and cold. Strange, large machines with blinking lights loomed in the corners. A person in scrubs moved him from the gurney to a narrow, hard table. They pulled his gown aside and squeezed a cold, thick goo onto his chest that made him flinch. The person then took a wand-like thing and began pressing it firmly against his skin, moving it around. On a monitor he could see out of the corner of his eye, blurry black-and-white shapes swirled like storm clouds or TV static. He had no idea what he was looking at. The person in scrubs would occasionally say, “Okay, hold your breath,” or “Now, turn onto your left side a little.” The silence was punctuated by the rhythmic beeping of a machine somewhere behind him. It felt like it took forever, and yet no time at all. Finally, the goo was wiped off his chest, and he was moved back to the gurney, feeling dazed and vulnerable. He was brought back to his room while the hospital made plans to have an operating room available for him the next day.

### Part 4: Back in the Room

Back in the quiet of his new room on the cardiac floor, a nurse helped get Jack settled, explaining the various buttons on the bed and the call remote. "The sofa pulls out into a bed for one overnight guest," she mentioned kindly before leaving them to rest. "Just let us know if you need linens".

The words hung in the air, creating a sudden, thick tension. Delores, who had been sitting on the sofa, straightened up, a flicker of proprietary expectation in her eyes. *Perfect,* she thought. *It’s my duty as his girlfriend. He has to pick me.*

But Jack’s eyes didn’t go to her. They immediately found Sarah’s, and his expression was a mask of sheer panic. He didn’t have to say a word. His look was a clear, desperate plea: Please don’t make me be alone with her. I need you.

Sarah understood his intentions perfectly. She gave him a small, warm nod, her expression a silent promise.

Of course. I'm not going anywhere.

Delores observed the entire silent exchange, and a familiar surge of cold fury washed over her. *He didn’t even look at me,* she seethed internally. He looked right at HER. His SISTER? Over ME? What is wrong with him?!

Trapped by his own non-confrontational nature, Jack knew he had to verbalize the choice he’d already made with his eyes. He took a shaky breath and looked at his sister. “Sarah?” he asked, his voice quiet but firm. “Will you… will you stay with me tonight?”

“Yes, Jack,” Sarah said immediately, her voice a warm, unwavering promise. “Absolutely. Of course, I will.”

Delores felt the rejection like a physical blow, but she knew she was being watched by Jermaine. She couldn’t make a scene. She quickly masked her true feelings, forcing a bright, considerate smile.

"Oh, that's wonderful!" she said, her voice dripping with artificial sweetness. "Jack, you should absolutely have Sarah stay with you. You need to be comfortable. I would just be in the way. I'll head home and stop by tomorrow to see how you are."

She gave Jack a quick, impersonal wave and left before anyone could respond. The moment the door clicked shut behind her, both Jermaine and Sarah let out a palpable, collective sigh of relief. The tension in the room instantly dissipated.

Now that the immediate threat was gone, the emotional toll of the day crashed down on Jack. He let out a small, choked sob, tears welling in his eyes.

“Hey, hey, what’s wrong?” Sarah asked, rushing to his side.

“I just… I hated that,” he whimpered, the stress of the confrontation overwhelming him. “I hate that I had to choose. She looked so hurt. It feels like my heart is breaking.”

"Oh, buddy," Sarah whispered, and she was there in an instant, wrapping her arms around him carefully, mindful of the IV lines. He leaned into her embrace, the hug a perfect anchor of safety and genuine love. She held him, her own heart aching for the emotional turmoil he was in, for the pain of what felt like a breaking heart. Sarah had no way of knowing, of course, that the real problem had nothing to do with a broken heart, but with a heart that was actively, physically, breaking.

"You made the right choice, son," Jermaine said simply from his chair.

Jack nodded against his sister’s shoulder, the truth of his own feelings finally cutting through the fog of guilt. "I know. I think... I think I'm done with Delores." The events of the day—her annoyance, her focus on their canceled date, her possessive jealousy—played back in his mind. Then, the chilling thought he’d had earlier clicked into place with terrifying certainty. Her story about the controlling ex… she wasn't describing a boyfriend. She was describing herself.

“Good,” Jermaine said firmly. “She’s not right for you”.

“She should definitely be kicked to the curb,” Sarah added, her agreement absolute as she gently pulled back from the hug.

With Delores gone and the truth settling in, it was time to get settled into this room, his new home for at least tonight, with the daunting reality of surgery scheduled for tomorrow.

### Part 5: A Quiet Vigil

The emotional storm of the afternoon settled into a quiet, anxious vigil. With Delores gone, the hospital room became a small sanctuary for the Bennett family. Sarah pulled the sofa out into its bed configuration, setting up her space for the night, while Jermaine sat in a chair by his son’s side.

The rhythm of the hospital soon took over. A nurse came in periodically for routine vital checks, her movements efficient and practiced. The squeak of the blood pressure cart, the soft beep of the thermometer, the gentle pinch of the pulse oximeter on Jack’s finger—these became the new sounds of their reality. Jack submitted to it all with a weary resignation, the seriousness of his situation sinking in with each check-up.

Around dinnertime, a staff member brought in a tray for Jack. It was his first experience with hospital food: a pale piece of chicken, some steamed green beans, and a scoop of mashed potatoes, all under a clear plastic lid. He poked at it with his fork, the taste as bland and sterile as the room felt.

A little while later, Jermaine returned with two styrofoam containers, the slightly more appetizing smell of the hospital cafeteria filling the room. He handed one to Sarah and sat down with his own. They ate together in a comfortable quiet, their cafeteria food a small act of solidarity with Jack.

“How’s the food, son?” Jermaine asked gently.

“It’s… food,” Jack replied with a weak smile.

As they were finishing, Sarah’s phone rang. It was Molly. Sarah answered, putting the call on speaker so Jack and her dad could hear.

“Hey!” Molly’s voice was a welcome burst of energy. “We just got off work. Clare and I are on our way to you now. We’ve got Grandpa Sam and Uncle Devin with us, too. We should be there in about twenty minutes.”

“Okay, we’ll see you soon,” Sarah said, a wave of relief in her voice.

Jack leaned his head back against the pillow. The thought of seeing the rest of his family, of having his full support system around him, was a comfort. The room was quiet again, but now it was a quiet filled with anticipation for the arrival of the rest of their clan.

### Part 6: The Family Visit

A quiet knock on the door heralded the arrival of the rest of the Bennett clan. Jermaine stepped out into the hall to greet his father, Sam, and his brother, Devin, while Molly and Clare slipped into the room to see Jack. Hospital policy only allowed two visitors at a time, and the family had a quiet, unspoken agreement: to keep Jack comfortable, one of them would always be Sarah, Molly, or Clare.

“Hey, you,” Molly whispered, her voice full of affection as she and Clare flanked his bed. “How are you really feeling?”

“Tired,” Jack admitted, managing a weak smile. “And a little scared. But better now that you guys are here.”

They chatted for a few minutes in hushed tones, their presence a comforting balm. Eventually, they switched out with their father and grandfather, with Sarah remaining as the constant.

Sam Bennett, the family patriarch, looked at his grandson, his expression a mixture of love and deep concern. “Your dad told us what happened with that… young lady,” he said, his voice a low rumble.

“She’s nothing but trouble, son,” Devin added, his arms crossed. “The way Jermaine described her behavior here? Unacceptable.”

Jermaine, who had returned to the room, nodded grimly. “We all agree, Jack. She needs to be kicked to the curb.”

Jack listened to the unified, protective front of his father, uncle, and grandfather. Their certainty bolstered his own. “You’re right,” he said, his voice quiet but firm. “You’re all right. I’m starting to agree. I’m done with her.”

With that difficult chapter closed, the conversation shifted to the more immediate and frightening topic.

“The doctor came by while you were all on your way,” Sarah explained, her voice steady. “Surgery is scheduled for tomorrow morning.”

She took a deep breath. “They’re going to try a procedure called a balloon angioplasty. They’ll go in and try to inflate a little balloon to open the blood vessel.”

“That sounds… simple enough,” Sam said, though his expression was still worried.

“That’s the problem,” Jermaine interjected grimly. “The doctor said there’s a good chance it won’t work. The blockage is 99 percent, and it’s located right at the entrance to his heart, which makes it very difficult and dangerous to get to. They’ll try the angioplasty first, but we need to be prepared for it to fail.”

A heavy silence fell over the room, the true gravity of Jack’s situation settled upon them. The stakes were incredibly high. Sarah instinctively reached out and took her brother’s hand again, her grip a silent promise of support in the face of the terrifying day that lay ahead.

### Part 7: The Night Before Surgery

As visiting hours began to wind down, the rest of the family said their loving but worried goodbyes. Grandpa Sam, Uncle Devin, Molly, and Clare each took a moment to squeeze Jack’s hand, offering words of love and encouragement before leaving the room to Jack, Sarah, and Jermaine.

The quiet that settled afterward was heavy with anticipation for the morning. Jermaine turned to his daughter. “Sarah, do you need anything for tonight? I can run home and grab whatever you need for your stay.”

“Actually, yeah, that would be great, Dad,” Sarah said, grateful. “I’ll need my pajamas and a change of clothes for tomorrow. I’ve already got my laptop, so I can keep myself busy and get some homework done.”

“I’ll grab your laptop too, Jack,” Jermaine offered. “Might help you pass the time before the surgery.”

About an hour later, Jermaine returned. He carried a bag with fresh clothes for Sarah and Jack’s laptop. He also brought pillows and a thick, familiar blanket from home for Sarah’s sofa bed. The hospital provided linens, of course, but these were comforts from their own house, a small way to make the sterile room feel more like a sanctuary.

As they were arranging the items, Jack shifted uncomfortably in the bed. “I, uh… I need to use the toilet,” he said, a note of frustration in his voice. He looked down at the IV line in his arm and the monitor wires attached to his chest. He pressed the call button for the nurse. “I’m going to need some help with all this stuff.”

Jermaine and Sarah exchanged a look of understanding. While either of them would have gladly helped, they knew this was a job best left to a professional who knew how to navigate the medical equipment safely. The nurse arrived promptly and, with practiced efficiency, helped Jack get untangled and to the restroom.

Once he was helped back into the hospital bed, the vulnerability of his situation seemed to hit him again. He looked over at Sarah, who was sitting by his side. “Sarah?” he asked quietly. He held out his hand.

She took it instantly, her grip warm and sure. “I’m right here.”

They sat in silence for a moment, and then they both said the quiet thing out loud. “Next time we see her,” Sarah began, and Jack finished, “we both tell her I’m done with her.”

Jermaine, who had been watching them, nodded firmly. “That’s for the best, kids. One hundred percent.”

With that final piece of business settled, Jermaine knew it was time to go. He leaned down and hugged his son tightly. “I love you, Jack. Be strong. I’ll be here first thing in the morning.” He kissed Sarah’s forehead. “Get some rest, sweetie.” He then took his leave for the night.

Sarah dimmed the main lights in the room, leaving only a soft glow from the lamp by her sofa-bed. She settled in for the night, her laptop open but untouched, her primary focus on the quiet, steady rhythm of her brother’s breathing as they waited for the dawn.

### Part 8: The Wait for Dawn

By 9:00 PM, a heavy exhaustion had settled over the hospital room. Jack, feeling the weight of the day and the dread of the morning to come, knew he needed to at least try to sleep.

“Goodnight, Sarah,” he whispered.

“Goodnight, Jack,” she replied softly from the sofa-bed. He motioned for her to come over, and she did, leaning down to give him a hug that was a little tighter, a little longer than usual. He clung to her for a moment, drawing strength from her presence before letting go. She settled back onto the sofa with the pillows and blankets from home, the familiar comforts a small rebellion against the sterile environment.

Jack closed his eyes, trying to quiet his racing mind. But sleep wouldn't come.

Just as a fragile quiet had taken hold, at exactly 9:12 PM, the door swung open and the main fluorescent lights snapped on, flooding the room with a harsh, sterile brightness. A cheerful night nurse bustled in. “Time for one last check of your vitals!” she announced.

Jack and Sarah both groaned, blinking against the sudden glare. But they understood. His health was more important than their sleep.

As the nurse wrapped the cuff around his arm, Jack stared at the ceiling, his mind fixated on the morning. He was nervous, a deep, coiling fear in his gut. He hoped the surgery would be a success. He understood the stakes perfectly: if the balloon angioplasty worked, the doctors said he could be back on his feet, maybe even back to work at the rec center, in just a couple of days. A quick recovery. But if it failed—and they had made it clear that was a real possibility—he was facing a long, arduous road to recovery, with more, bigger surgeries to come.

The nurse finished, dimmed the lights, and left them in the quiet darkness once more.

“Try to get some sleep,” Sarah murmured from the sofa.

“You too,” Jack whispered back. But he knew he wouldn’t. He lay awake in the dim light, holding onto the memory of his sister’s hug, staring into the dark and waiting for the dawn that would decide his future.

## Chapter 2: The Day of Surgery

### Part 1: The Last Meal

The first light of February 15th, 2007, filtered into the hospital room, grey and muted. Jack was already awake when a nurse brought in his breakfast tray. Sarah stirred on the sofa bed, awakened by the quiet sounds. In the chaos and terror of the past 24 hours—her brother having a heart attack, the tense confrontation with Delores, the sleepless night—she had completely forgotten what day it was.

“Good morning,” she murmured, sitting up. She looked at the tray placed in front of her brother: a small bowl of watery oatmeal, a piece of dry toast, and a carton of apple juice.

“Morning,” Jack replied, his voice subdued.

“Right,” Sarah said, stretching. “If you get that feast, I’m heading to the cafeteria. Be right back.”

She returned a few minutes later with a breakfast sandwich wrapped in foil and a steaming cup of coffee. They ate together in a comfortable, anxious silence.

“Nervous?” Sarah asked gently, breaking the quiet.

“Terrified,” Jack admitted, pushing his toast around the plate. “I just want this to be over. And I want it to work.”

“It will,” Sarah said with a confidence she didn’t fully feel. “The doctors are the best. And I’ll be right here in the waiting room the whole time. I’ll be the first person you see when you wake up.”

He gave her a small, grateful smile. He took another bite of oatmeal, his expression hardening with resolve. “After this is over,” he said, his voice firm, “we need to deal with Delores.”

Sarah nodded, her own expression serious. “Absolutely. We deal with her together.”

“When she shows up again,” Jack continued, laying out the plan they had discussed, “we’ll both be there. No more me being alone with her. We’ll tell her it’s over, and that it’s not up for discussion. That she needs to leave me alone, for good.”

“It’s the right thing to do,” Sarah agreed. “She’s not safe for you, Jack. We all see it.”

A nurse came in then. “Alright, Jack, that’s it for food and drink until after your procedure.”

He took one last gulp of apple juice. The finality of the moment settled over them. As the nurse cleared the tray, Jack looked at his sister, his expression softening despite his own fear.

“Hey, Sarah,” he said quietly.

“Yeah?”

“Happy Birthday.”

The words hit her with the force of a physical blow. Her birthday. It was her 23rd birthday. She had completely, utterly forgotten. Her eyes instantly filled with tears, not just from the sentiment, but from the incredible, selfless love of her brother, who, while facing a terrifying and life-threatening surgery, had remembered *her*.

“Oh, Jack,” she whispered, her voice thick with emotion as she took his hand. “Don’t you worry about that right now. That doesn’t matter at all.”

“I’m sorry it’s… this,” he started to say.

“No,” she cut him off gently but fiercely. “You listen to me. The only birthday present I want this year is for you to get through this and get better. That's it. You being okay is my gift. So you just focus on that, okay?”

He squeezed her hand, a silent thank you passing between them. The plan for Delores was set. Now, all that was left was to face the surgery.

### Part 2: The Journey to the OR

The quiet of the morning was broken by a cheerful but firm knock. A hospital transporter entered the room with a gurney, the squeak of its wheels sharp in the tense silence. "Morning, Jack. Time to get you ready to go."

The moment had arrived. As the transporter and a nurse began the process of preparing him for the move, Jack glanced around the room. He was surprised Delores hadn't shown up, but he caught Sarah's eye and saw his own feeling reflected there: they were both not complaining.

As the transporter began to wheel him out, Jack reached out his hand, not towards the door, but directly to his sister. Sarah took it without a second’s hesitation, her grip firm and reassuring, just as she had during the long trek from the emergency room the day before.

She walked beside the gurney, matching its pace, their hands linked the entire way. The journey through the sterile hallways felt surreal, the squeak of the wheels and the distant, muted hospital sounds the only accompaniment to their silent communion. Finally, they arrived at a set of large, intimidating double doors marked "Surgical Suite." The transporter paused.

"This is as far as family can go," he said gently.

Sarah squeezed Jack's hand one last time before letting go.

"Wait," Jack said, his voice a little shaky. He looked at his sister. "One last hug?"

"Always," she whispered, stepping forward. She leaned over the gurney and wrapped her arms around him, holding him tightly. It was a hug that conveyed everything they couldn't say—her fear for him, her unwavering love, and a fierce promise of protection.

As she pulled back, she kept her hands on his shoulders, her gaze steady and sure. "Dad and I will be right here in the waiting room the whole time," she promised. "We're not going anywhere. I'll be the first person you see when you wake up, okay?"

Jack started to think about it, the fog in his mind clearing. He remembered the date with Molly, the possessive way Delores had belittled his family. He remembered her roaring with anger when he had wanted to have a simple sibling and cousin day with Sarah, Molly, and Clare. “I couldn’t even have a family day with my three favorite women,” he murmured, the memory validating his decision completely.

Just then, there was a knock on the door, and a hospital worker popped his head in. “Good news,” he said after introductions. “We have a bed for you on the recovery floor. We can get you out of intensive care.”

The news was a welcome step toward normalcy. “This is the room where you’ll be allowed to have someone overnight with you,” Sarah said, her eyes shining with love. “I’m game if you want me. Everyone can come visit during the day.”

“I would appreciate the company,” Jack said, his voice catching. The reality of everything—the surgery, the breakup, the long road ahead—crashed down on him. “I guess I’m going to have to get used to being single,” he whimpered, a tear escaping his eye.

“You’re not alone, Jack,” Sarah said fiercely, squeezing his hand. “You have all of us. And yes, you’re single now, and it’s probably for the best. I agree she was horrible. Yes, you wanted her out of your life. And yes, you can be glad she’s gone.”

He looked at his sister, his anchor, and nodded, taking a shaky breath. She was right. He was glad as the two siblings took each other’s hand.

"Sarah," he said, his voice hesitant and thick with a profound, overwhelming wave of emotion. "I... I... I love you".

A beautiful, tearful smile broke across Sarah's face. "Oh, Jack," she whispered, her own voice unwavering. "I love you, too. Always."

"What is WRONG with you people?" a voice shrieked from the doorway. Delores stood there, her face a mask of fury, unexpected, uninvited, and unwelcome. The sudden explosion of rage made everyone flinch. "You just had major surgery, and you're saying 'I love you' to your SISTER? In front of your GIRLFRIEND? It's sick! It's not normal!"

Jermaine, who had been watching Delores with a cold, simmering anger, stood up, fully prepared to end this himself. But he stopped. He saw a change in his son.

A strange, quiet strength was settling over Jack's face. The trauma of the surgery, the clarity of his near-death experience, and the unwavering support of his family had forged something new in him. He was done being afraid.

Holding Sarah's hand tightly, feeling her presence as a sudden surge of emotional strength, he turned his head on the pillow to look Delores square in the eye as best he could from his bed in the ICU. His voice was still a weak rasp, but it was filled with an unshakeable, newfound finality. “Get… out,” Jack rasped, his voice still struggling to find itself.

Delores stared, her face a mask of disbelief and fury. "You can't do this! You're just confused from the surgery! It's them! They're poisoning you against me! Jack, tell them you love me!"

"No," Sarah said, her voice now like ice. "He's not confused at all. You heard him. Get out."

"You can't make me!" Delores spat.

"The only woman in this room that I love is my sister," Jack’s voice cut through her anger, a weak but unwavering rasp from the bed. "Now, get out."

The rejection snapped something deep inside Delores. The facade of the worried girlfriend didn't just crack; it exploded.

"You ungrateful little cripple!" she shrieked.

Then, she moved.

It wasn't a retreat. It was an attack. With a feral snarl, Delores lunged toward the bed, her hand raised high, fingers curled into claws. She wasn't aiming for his face; she was aiming for the center of his chest—directly for the fresh, terrifyingly vulnerable sternotomy incision that was currently holding his ribcage together.

Jack flinched, his eyes going wide with terror, unable to move or defend himself against the incoming blow that could literally kill him.

"NO!"

The roar came from Jermaine. He didn't just stand up; he exploded from his chair.

In a blur of motion, Jermaine threw his body between Delores and the bed. He caught her descending wrist in mid-air, his grip like a steel vice, stopping her hand inches from Jack's chest.

At the exact same moment, Sarah had thrown herself over Jack's upper body, shielding his incision with her own back, acting as a human shield against the blow.

The room froze. The only sound was Delores's ragged breathing and the frantic, accelerated beeping of Jack's heart monitor.

Jermaine stared down at Delores, his face inches from hers. His eyes were no longer just angry; they were terrifying. He twisted her arm away from the bed, forcing her back a step, physically neutralizing the threat but not letting go.

"You were going to hit him," Jermaine said, his voice dropping to a whisper that was far scarier than any shout. "He has a sawed-open chest... and you were going to hit him."

Delores stared back, her chest heaving, the adrenaline of the attempted assault fading into a sudden, cold realization of what she had almost done—and who had stopped her.

Jermaine released her wrist with a shove that sent her stumbling back toward the door, putting distance between her and his children. But he didn't scream at her to leave yet. Instead, he turned his head, looking back at the bed where Sarah was slowly peeling herself off Jack, checking him for damage with trembling hands.

He wanted Delores to see who was really in charge. He wanted her to see the wall she had failed to break.

"Sarah," Jermaine said, his voice steady and commanding, handing the power directly to his daughter.

Sarah looked up, her eyes burning with a cold, protective fury. She reached out and placed her hand firmly on the red nurse call button on the bed rail.

"Jack has made his wishes clear," Jermaine said to Delores, his eyes never leaving hers, but his words empowering his daughter. "You can either walk out of this room on your own two feet right now, or my daughter will press that button, and we will have hospital security escort you out in handcuffs. The choice is yours."

Delores looked from Jermaine's granite expression to Sarah's finger hovering over the button, and finally to Jack, who was safe behind the barrier of his family.

There was no weakness to exploit. The wall was absolute.

With a strangled cry of pure, impotent rage, she snatched her purse and stormed out of the room, running past the incoming nurses who had been alerted by the monitor alarms, escaping before the police could be called.

Sarah didn't press the button. She didn't have to. She turned back to Jack, her hands hovering over him. "Jack? Did she touch you? Are you okay?"

Jack was trembling, his monitor still blaring a high-tempo rhythm, but he nodded. He looked at his father, then at his sister. They had literally thrown their bodies in front of a bullet for him.

"She tried to..." he whispered, the reality sinking in. "She tried to hurt me."

"She tried," Jermaine said, his voice shaking with the aftershocks of adrenaline. "But she failed. She will never get close enough to try again."

Just then, the room was flooded with blue scrubs.

Three ICU nurses rushed in, breathless and wide-eyed, responding to the frantic, high-pitched alarm of Jack’s heart monitor, which was still screaming a warning about his dangerous spike in heart rate. They nearly collided with the fleeing Delores in the hallway but didn't stop to question her; their eyes were fixed on the patient.

"We have a rate spike! What happened?" the lead nurse demanded, rushing to the bedside and immediately checking the display. "Jack? Jack, can you hear me?"

Sarah stepped back just enough to give them space, but she kept a firm grip on Jack’s hand. "He's okay," she said, her voice trembling slightly. "There was... an altercation. A visitor became aggressive. She's gone now."

"Aggressive?" The nurse looked horrified. She immediately turned her attention to Jack’s chest, her hands gentle but urgent as she inspected the fresh sternotomy dressing. "Jack, did she make contact? Did anything hit your chest?"

Jack shook his head, his breathing shallow and rapid. "No," he wheezed, looking at his father and sister. "They... they stopped her. They blocked it."

"Okay, okay. Deep breaths for me, Jack," the nurse instructed, her voice dropping to a soothing calm. "You're safe. We're right here. I need you to try to slow that breathing down. Your heart is working too hard right now."

She checked his IV lines and adjusted the oxygen flow on his nasal cannula. Another nurse quickly checked his blood pressure, which was understandably sky-high.

Jermaine stood by the foot of the bed, his posture still defensive, looking like a sentinel ready to fight off an army. "Do we need security?" he asked the staff. "I don't think she'll be back, but I want it on record."

"I'm calling them right now," the third nurse confirmed, stepping out to the desk. "We'll have her flagged. She won't get past the front desk again."

Slowly, under the nurses' care and the steady presence of his family, Jack’s heart rate began to drop from its frantic peak. The alarm silenced, leaving only the steady, rhythmic beep of a fast but stable rhythm.

The lead nurse let out a sigh of relief. "Okay. Incision looks intact. No wires pulled. You're very lucky, Jack." She looked at Jermaine and Sarah with genuine admiration. "And you have an incredible security team."

Just as the adrenaline began to fade into exhaustion, the transport team arrived with the gurney, looking confused by the crowd in the room. "We... uh... have a transfer order for a Jack Bennett to the recovery floor?"

Jack looked at his sister, his eyes silently asking for one more moment before the chaos of moving began. She understood instantly.

She leaned over the bed, careful of the nurses and wires, and they shared a deep, heartfelt hug—a perfect, unspoken seal on everything they had just survived.

"I'm right here," she whispered into his ear. "I'm riding up with you."

The transport team waited patiently, respectfully giving the siblings their moment before beginning the next step of the journey.

Jack held Sarah's hand during the entire transport to his recovery room, his grip tight. He was profoundly glad that Delores wasn't waiting for him in the hallway; now, he truly did not want to see her or hear from her ever again. He was safe, and he was moving forward.

## Chapter 3: The Road to Recovery

### Part 1: Rooming with Sarah in the Hospital 

The journey from the beeping, sterile intensity of the ICU to the quiet of a private room on the cardiac recovery floor felt like traveling between two different worlds. Jack was transferred to his new room and was immensely glad to have his sister’s company. The new space was brighter, with a large window that looked out over a small courtyard. It felt like a step toward normalcy.

His dad was there, already helping Sarah unpack a small bag, arranging a few magazines on the bedside table and plugging in their phone chargers.

“When you get out of the hospital,” Jermaine said, his voice full of a quiet, determined love, “our plan is still in motion. We're all moving to the new place, just like we talked about. And it’s a good thing we planned ahead. The first-floor bedroom we designed for you is all set up and ready for you while you heal.”

Jack stared at his father, surprised and deeply moved. He knew about the move, of course, but hearing that they had already prepared his future room, that they had anticipated his needs so perfectly long before this crisis, was an overwhelming testament to their love.

"The new house?" he asked, his voice a little weak, the reality of it finally sinking in.

“In the meantime, I’ll stay with you at the hospital,” Sarah said, placing a comforting hand on his arm. “I’m not going anywhere.”

Jack smiled, a wave of profound gratitude washing over him. He was grateful he would have female company from the person he trusted the absolute most—and from someone whose touch was a comfort, not a demand; someone who treated him better than his (now) ex-girlfriend.

Just then, three familiar faces popped into the hospital room. “Molly! Clare! Uncle Devin! You came!” Jack exclaimed, his face lighting up. The three of them arrived, their expressions of worry giving way to visible relief to see that Jack had made it through surgery.

“How are you feeling?” Molly asked, her voice gentle as she came to his bedside. “We were all so worried about you.”

“I admit that I’m tired,” Jack answered honestly. “The hospital wants me to stay in bed for now. But I’m better, seeing you guys.”

“Makes sense. You look like you’ve been through a lot,” Clare said, her eyes full of sympathy.

“Yeah,” Jack said, a new resolve hardening his voice. He looked around at his family, his safety net. “You know, could one of you do me a favor? On the emergency contacts on my hospital profile: Delores’s name needs to be removed. Immediately. I would like to have all of you listed, but get rid of her, please,” Jack said adamantly.

“We can get that taken care of right now,” Jermaine said, placing a firm hand on his son’s shoulder. “I made my child a promise that he could come home whenever he wanted, and I am honoring my promise as soon as this hospital is ready to discharge you. You won't have to worry about her ever again.”

### Part 2: Bed Time with Sarah

The hospital room was quiet now that their family had left for the night. Sarah began to arrange the pillows and blankets Jermaine had brought from home onto the pull-out sofa, the familiar patterns a small comfort in the sterile environment.

“So, you’re offering to spend the night with me,” Jack asked from the bed, his voice quiet.

“Yep,” his sister replied, turning to him with a soft smile. “Dad thinks it is best if someone is with you, and I volunteered. Especially since Delores is out of the picture.”

It started dawning on Jack that he was newly single, and he began thinking out loud. “I won’t lie,” he said, his gaze distant. “If I had to choose a roommate right now, I would so much rather live with you than her. Back home you would let me climb into bed with you and cuddle up when things were bad. I think I’m glad you’re my first bed partner in all of this, and not Delores.”

He started to have tears in his eyes as the full weight of the day, and his sister’s unwavering love, really began to hit him. He was so grateful, so overwhelmed. “Come here,” he managed to squeak out as he started losing control of his emotions.

“I’m here. I’m here,” Sarah whispered, immediately walking over to his bedside. She sat on the edge of the bed and took his hand. “What’s up?”

“I just… you’re always here,” he sobbed quietly.

“And I promise I’m not going anywhere. Not tonight,” she said, her grip firm and steady. She noticed that her brother did not want to let go of her hand. “When we get to Uncle Devin’s place, we will all be there for you. Promise.”

Jack was getting overwhelmed at just how much his sister cared about him. Shooing Delores away was not the first time she had shooed problems away from him. At least while recovering from surgery, he wanted his sister to be the one to take care of him. Not his ex. He never wanted to see or hear from Delores ever again. He agreed with what his father and sister had said: he could do so much better.

Sarah offered Jack a gentle hug, careful of his chest. “I’ll always be here for you,” she whispered. “Tomorrow, I must go to work, but honestly, with the threat of snow they’re talking about, we’ll probably close early. They say we’re getting into a full-blown blizzard. I would so much rather be trapped here at the hospital with you. I’d rather you have someone here at your side.”

Just then, both of their phones buzzed with an identical, sharp alert tone. There was a city alert, and Sarah read it from her screen. A small, relieved smile touched her lips.

“Well, that’s that,” she said, showing him the screen. “City offices are closed tomorrow. We’re closed. Looks like I’ll just stay with you.”

## Chapter 4: A Blizzard Sanctuary

### Part 1: A Visitor in the Storm

The first flakes of the impending blizzard had just begun to fall as **Mandy** rushed her grandmother into the emergency room at **Tidewater General**. After getting her grandmother settled, a process that took over an hour, Mandy looked out the window. The gentle flurry had transformed into a thick, swirling curtain of white. The roads were already disappearing.

She knew she wasn't going anywhere. A wave of exhaustion and frustration washed over her. Then she remembered **Jennifer's** text from earlier: **Jack Bennett** was here, in this very hospital, recovering in the cardiac wing.

On a whim, she decided to seek out a friendly face in the middle of the storm. She found his room number at the main information desk and made her way up. A soft, hesitant knock at the door made **Jack** and **Sarah** look up.

"Mandy?" Jack said, his voice a weak, surprised rasp. "What are you doing here?"

"My grandmother had a fall," Mandy explained, her voice low as she stepped into the room. "I brought her into the ER, and now... well, it looks like I'm stuck here." She gestured to the television in the corner of the room, which was tuned to the local news. A weather map, painted in angry reds and purples, dominated the screen, with a bold "TRAVEL BAN IN EFFECT" banner at the bottom. "I just saw the forecast. Looks like none of us are leaving tonight."

"Wow. Come in, get comfortable," Sarah said, gesturing to a chair.

Mandy stepped further into the room, her presence a quiet and welcome connection to their life at Fairlawn. "I'm so sorry, Jack," she said, her eyes full of a sincere warmth. "We've all been so worried about you."

"Thank you," Jack said, genuinely touched. "That's... that's really nice of you to stop by."

The conversation was quiet, centered on safe, professional topics. Mandy asked how he was feeling, and he gave a tired but honest answer. Looking at her kind face and easy smile, a thought flickered in Jack's mind, quiet and unexpected. *She's so nice. She's easy to talk to. If things were different... if I was different... maybe...* He weighed the small, warm feeling her presence sparked against the profound, unshakeable safety he felt with his family. After Delores, the safety was what mattered more. But the small, warm feeling lingered.

Buoyed by that warmth, he decided to share the other piece of his recovery. "There is some good news, though," he said, his voice a little stronger. "Delores... we're not together anymore. It's over. For good."

A look of immense, genuine happiness washed over Mandy's face. "Oh, Jack," she said, her voice full of a sincere warmth. "That's wonderful. I'm so glad to hear that." She knew the stress that relationship had caused him. "You just focus on getting better now."

As Mandy prepared to find a quiet corner of a waiting room to settle in for the night, Jack felt a sudden, powerful urge. A hug. It was the way he and his family communicated comfort, gratitude, and safety. And in this moment, he wanted to offer that same comfort to Mandy, a thank you for her kindness, for being a friendly face in the middle of this storm. It was the closest he had ever come to wanting to offer her a hug. But he stopped himself. Hugs were for Sarah, Molly, and Clare. They were for his inner circle, his "wall." Mandy, as kind as she was, was still a coworker, and after Delores, the lines had to be clearer, safer. He didn't want to send the wrong signal, to make things awkward. So he just watched her, the unspoken gesture dying before it could be born.

Mandy smiled, a true, honest smile. "Thanks, Sarah. I might just take you up on that." She gave them both a final wave and walked out, leaving the siblings to face the long, snowbound night ahead.

### Part 2: The First Night

With the city alert confirming the impending blizzard, the hospital room became their sanctuary, a small island of warmth against the storm raging outside. For the next few days, this room would be as much Sarah’s home as it was Jack's. Sarah spoke briefly on the phone with their father, assuring him they were settled and safe before the roads became impassable.

By 9:00 PM, an exhausted quiet had fallen over the room. Jack, propped up carefully in his bed, finally felt his eyes grow heavy. Sarah, curled up on the pull-out sofa with the pillows and blanket from home, gave him a soft smile. "Try to get some sleep, dork," she whispered.

He was just drifting off when the door swished open. Jack and Sarah both flinched, not from surprise, but from a weary, shared memory. The lights snapped on, a harsh, unwelcome glare.

“Here we go again,” Sarah muttered under her breath, throwing an arm over her eyes.

“Sorry to wake you,” the nurse said cheerfully, already wrapping the blood pressure cuff around Jack’s arm. “Just need to get your midnight vitals.”

Jack groaned, blinking against the sudden brightness. It was an instant, frustrating replay of their nights at Crossroads when Sarah was the patient. He knew his health was the priority, but the interruption was just as jarring this time around.

The nurse was efficient and gone in minutes, leaving them once again in the dim, quiet room. But now, they were both wide awake.

"You'd think," Jack mumbled into the darkness, "that for a place that's supposed to be for 'rest and recovery,' they'd let you do one of those things."

"Right?" Sarah whispered back from the sofa. "At least this time I'm not the one who's NPO."

The shared, gallows humor was a small comfort. This pattern repeated itself at 2:00 AM and again at 4:00 AM. Each time, they were woken with a resigned, shared annoyance born of recent, identical experience. By 5:00 AM, they were both awake, exhausted and bleary-eyed, watching the thick, heavy snow fall outside the window.

“Well,” Jack said, his voice rough with lack of sleep. “At least we’re miserable together. Again.”

Sarah managed a tired smile. “Always,” she replied. They were both annoyed, sleep-deprived, and on edge, but they were facing it together, side-by-side in their small, snowbound sanctuary.

### Part 3: The First Full Day

The first full day of recovery was a lesson in humility. After their sleepless night, a cheerful day-shift nurse came in with Jack’s breakfast and a small paper cup of pills.

“Alright, Jack,” she said, “we’re putting you on diuretics to help get some of the excess fluid off your system after the surgery. It means you’ll have to pee a lot, so we’ve got you set up with the Conti-Vac System.” She indicated the external wick and tubing connected to a quiet pump and collection canister beside the bed. “It’ll let you go without having to get up.”

Jack looked at the device with a sense of dread. Sarah, sipping her cafeteria coffee from her spot on the pull-out sofa, gave him a look of weary, yet deeply empathetic, sympathy.

An hour later, the diuretics kicked in with a vengeance. Jack felt a sudden, urgent need to go. *Okay,* he thought, *this is weird, but just relax and let it happen.* He tried to relax. Nothing. The pressure in his bladder grew from an urgent request to a demanding, painful scream. He was acutely, horribly aware of Sarah sitting just a few feet away, tapping quietly on her laptop. He hated this. Hated the loss of dignity, the helplessness.

He shifted uncomfortably, a low groan escaping his lips. Sarah looked up. “You okay, Jack?”

“No,” he said, his voice tight with frustration, not looking at her. “I have to go so bad, but… I can’t. It’s this stupid machine. My body just… won’t.”

Sarah’s expression was full of empathy, but a small, teasing smile played at the corners of her lips. “You know,” she said, her voice a gentle ribbing, “that sounds awfully familiar. I seem to remember a certain brother of mine, not too long ago, telling me that it was ‘just a mental block’ and that I should ‘try not to think about it’ when I was hooked up to that exact same machine.”

Jack's cheeks burned with a fresh wave of embarrassment. He remembered saying that to her. He had been trying to be helpful, logical. Now, with the tables turned, his own words felt like a just punishment. He couldn't even admit to her that she was right.

“Easy for you to say,” he muttered, his voice low. “You don’t feel like your bladder is going to explode.”

The pressure was becoming unbearable. Just as he thought he was going to have to page the nurse and admit defeat, it happened. The dam burst. A sudden, unstoppable, and overwhelming release of pressure flooded through him, a wave of immense, almost painful, relief.

He fell back against the pillows, exhausted and drenched in a light sweat, but finally free of the agonizing pressure. He kept his eyes closed, the embarrassment still hot on his cheeks.

Sarah, who had discreetly focused on her laptop, glanced over. Seeing the relief on his face, she offered a small, supportive smile. “See? You got it.”

Jack just nodded, too tired to speak. He had won his first, bizarre, and utterly undignified battle on the long road to recovery.

### Part 4: The First Walk

Later that afternoon, a nurse came into the room, her expression cheerful but determined. "Alright, Jack. Doctor's orders. Time to get you up and moving."

Getting him from the bed to a standing position was a slow, agonizing process. A wave of dizziness washed over him as the nurse expertly secured a thick safety cord around his waist and helped him grip the metal walker.

"Okay, Jack," the nurse said, her voice encouraging. "Let's just try to make it to that window right there. Small steps."

The few feet across his own hospital room felt like a marathon. Each shuffle sent a jolt of fire through his chest, and he leaned heavily on the walker, sweat beading on his forehead. Sarah watched, her hands clenched, her face a mask of pride and worry. Finally, he reached the window.

He froze.

The world was gone, replaced by a blanket of impossible, perfect white. The hospital courtyard, the trees, the benches—everything was buried under a thick, undisturbed layer of snow. It was a sight so unusual for Norfolk that it felt magical. The snow muffled all sound, creating a profound and peaceful silence.

"Wow," he breathed, the pain momentarily forgotten.

After a moment, buoyed by the small victory, he felt a flicker of determination. "I want to try the hallway," he said.

"You got it," the nurse smiled. "Let's turn around."

The journey back to the door was just as difficult. He made it, taking his first triumphant step out of his room and into the main corridor. He took a second step, then a third. And then his strength simply vanished. A wave of utter exhaustion crashed over him, his legs turning to jelly, the walker suddenly feeling like it weighed a thousand pounds.

"I... I can't," he gasped, his arms trembling. "I'm done."

"That's perfectly okay, Jack! You did amazing," the nurse said instantly, her hand steady on the safety cord. "You made it into the hall. That's a huge win. Sarah, could you grab one of those transport chairs for me?"

Sarah was already moving, quickly wheeling a chair over. The humbling experience of sinking into it and being wheeled back the short distance he had just fought so hard to walk was a stark reminder of how long this road to recovery would be.

Back in bed, utterly spent, he looked at his sister.

"You made it to the hallway, Jack," she said, her voice full of love and pride. "That was huge."

### Part 5: A Call Home

After resting for an hour, the exhaustion from his first walk began to recede, replaced by a quiet sense of accomplishment. Jack felt a deep longing to connect with the rest of his family, who he knew were anxiously waiting for updates at the new house, snowed in just like he and Sarah were at the hospital.

“Think we should call them?” Jack asked.

“Already on it,” Sarah said with a smile, dialing their father’s number and putting her phone on speaker.

“Hey!” Jermaine’s voice answered, sounding relieved. “We were just about to call you. How’s he doing?”

“He’s doing great, Dad,” Sarah said, her voice swelling with pride. “I have some good news for everyone. Jack took his first few steps today!”

A chorus of excited and relieved voices erupted from the other end of the line—Jermaine, Sam, Devin, Molly, and Clare were all clearly gathered around the phone.

Sarah continued, painting a picture for them. “The nurse got him up with a walker. He made it all the way to his hospital room window first, so he got to see the snow in the courtyard. It’s really beautiful out there,” she said. “Then, he was determined to make it to the hallway. He did it! He only made it a few steps out of the room before he was completely wiped out, but he did it.”

“That’s my boy! Attaboy, Jack!” Jermaine’s proud voice boomed.

“Oh my gosh, Jack, that’s amazing!” Molly and Clare said in near unison.

“That’s the fighting spirit, son,” Grandpa Sam added. “We’re so proud of you.”

Jack, listening from his bed, felt a warm blush of happiness spread across his face. “It’s good to hear your voices,” he said, his own voice still a bit weak but full of emotion.

“It’s great to hear yours, kiddo,” Uncle Devin replied. “We just can’t wait for this snow to clear up so we can get over there.”

“Me too,” Jack said with feeling. “I can’t wait until you can all visit… or even better, for me to get discharged and come home.”

The conversation continued for a few more minutes, filled with love and encouragement, a vital lifeline connecting their small hospital sanctuary to their snowbound family at home. When they finally hung up, Jack felt emotionally recharged, his spirit bolstered by the unwavering pride and love of his family.

### Part 6: The Anthem of Aethel 🎶

The second full day of being snowbound in the **Tidewater General Heart Hospital** had settled into a quiet, monotonous rhythm. The blizzard still raged outside. True to her word, **Mandy** had returned, a welcome visitor bringing a deck of cards and stories from the hospital waiting room where she was camping out. The three of them were a small, comfortable island in the middle of the storm.

"I'm so bored," **Jack** announced from his bed, breaking a long, comfortable silence.

"Want to watch a movie?" **Sarah** asked from the pull-out sofa, already knowing the answer.

A slow smile spread across Jack's face. "You know which one."

As Sarah retrieved Jack's laptop, Mandy’s eyes lit up with a spark of genuine, fan-fueled excitement. "No way," she whispered. "Are we actually about to watch 'The Silver Gauntlet of Aethel'?"

Jack looked at her, surprised and delighted. "You're a fan?"

"Are you kidding?" Mandy laughed. "It's the greatest cheesy 80s fantasy movie ever made. I've probably seen it a hundred times."

Sarah set up the laptop on Jack's overbed table. The moment she hit play, the room was filled with the glorious, triumphant sound of the movie's opening theme. It was an anthem they all knew by heart.

A mischievous glint appeared in Jack's eyes. He took a breath, a little shallower than he was used to, and began to sing along, his voice a weak but determined rasp.

Turn around… face the coming night… A sister's love, a burning light…

Sarah and Mandy exchanged a surprised and delighted grin. He was actually doing it.

Just then, a night nurse, a kind man named David, entered the room to do a routine check of Jack's vitals. He stopped in the doorway, listening with a smile, as Jack, with a clear, happy focus, continued to sing.

For the Gauntlet of Aethel awaits the true… A hero's heart will see you through!

He sang the second verse, but as the powerful chorus began again, something shifted. The lyrics, which had been a fun script just moments before, suddenly hit him with the force of a physical blow.

A sister's love, a burning light…

He looked at Sarah, truly saw her sitting there, his unwavering protector. She *was* the burning light. The song wasn't about Kaelan and Kaela anymore. It was about him. It was about how his sister's platonic, unconditional love was the force that had truly rescued him from Delores.

The realization was a sudden, overwhelming emotional overload. His voice cracked, and he stopped singing abruptly. The joy on his face crumpled, replaced by a look of raw, dawning anguish. A choked sob escaped him, his body beginning to tremble.

David, the nurse, immediately saw the shift from joy to distress. Sarah was already moving, off the sofa and at her brother's side in an instant. Mandy shot to her feet, her own face a mask of shock and deep, helpless concern.

"Jack? Hey, what is it?" Sarah asked softly, her hand finding his.

He couldn't speak. He just looked at her, his eyes swimming with tears, and sobbed. As Sarah moved to hug him, Jack did something that surprised everyone, including himself. His free hand, the one not holding Sarah's, reached out, trembling, not towards his sister, but towards Mandy.

His eyes, full of a raw, pleading vulnerability, met hers. It was an unspoken question, a desperate, instinctual request for comfort that went against every rule of self-preservation he had built since Delores.

Mandy didn't hesitate for a second. She moved to his other side, her heart aching for him. Sarah, seeing her brother's silent plea and Mandy's immediate, gentle response, gave a small, almost imperceptible nod of approval. The wall was opening, just a crack, and she would not stand in the way of a trusted friend offering her brother comfort.

Mandy gently took Jack's outstretched hand, her touch warm and steady. Sarah wrapped her arms around her brother's shoulders, pulling him into a gentle, grounding hug. The three of them were a small, huddle of shared comfort.

David quietly stepped forward, his voice a calm presence in the room. "It's a lot, what you've been through," he said to Jack, his tone full of a gentle understanding. "Sometimes a song can unlock things we didn't even know were there. You just let it out. You're in a safe place."

He discreetly checked the monitors, saw that Jack's heart rate was elevated but steady, and then stepped back.

Jack clung to his sister, his other hand gripping Mandy's. He cried for the fear he had felt, for the relief of being free, and for the profound love of the two kind, gentle women who were, in that moment, his unwavering anchors in the storm. It was the first crack in his wall, a surprising, terrifying, and profoundly healing moment of trust.

The nurse, David, seeing that the situation was emotional, not medical, and that **Jack** was in the capable, loving hands of his sister, gave a final, reassuring nod. "You're in good hands," he said softly, and then he quietly slipped out of the room, leaving the three of them in the raw, intimate space he had created.

The moment the door clicked shut, the last of Jack's fragile control shattered. The sob that had been choked in his throat broke free, a wave of pure, overwhelming anguish and relief. He clung to **Sarah**, his body shaking with the force of his emotions. The ability to form words was gone, stolen by the sheer, overwhelming force of the emotional and sensory overload. **He was experiencing situational mutism, a familiar and terrifying part of his autism**.

He tried to tell her how scared he had been, how relieved he was, but all that came out were raw, ragged sobs muffled in his sister's shoulder.

"I know," she murmured, her own voice thick with tears as she held him, understanding his unspoken pain. "I know. But it's over now. You're safe."

But in the middle of his silent, emotional storm, one clear, terrifying, and undeniable thought cut through the chaos: *I need Mandy to hold me, too.* It was an impulse that went against every rule of self-preservation he had built since Delores. It made no sense. But it was there, a desperate, undeniable need.

He pulled back from Sarah just enough to look past her, his eyes, swimming with tears, finding **Mandy's**. She was standing a few feet away, her own face streaked with empathetic tears, looking like she wanted to run to him but was frozen by a deep respect for their sibling bond.

He couldn't speak. He couldn't form the words. So he just held out his free hand to her, a clear, desperate, and silent invitation.

That was all she needed. She was there in an instant, taking his hand, her other hand coming to rest gently on his arm. Sarah, seeing her brother's silent plea, didn't pull away. Instead, she adjusted her own hold, making space for their friend, a silent gesture of acceptance.

Jack looked from Sarah to Mandy, his expression a mask of pure, pleading vulnerability. He gently pulled on both of their hands, a clear, unspoken request: *Both of you. I need both of you to hold me. Please.*

And they did. Carefully, mindful of his surgical incision, Sarah and Mandy enveloped him in a double-sided hug, a cocoon of pure, uncomplicated comfort and support. Jack buried his face in his sister's shoulder and just cried, his other hand gripping Mandy's arm as if she were a lifeline.

He cried for the fear Delores had inflicted on him, for the relief of being free, and for the sudden, overwhelming realization that he might, just possibly, be able to trust someone again who wasn't family. The thought was terrifying. It was a crack in the wall he had so carefully constructed. But in the warm, steady, and completely safe embrace of these two kind, gentle women, it also felt, for the first time, like a fragile, terrifying, and desperately needed glimmer of hope.

Slowly, carefully, they were able to calm him. The ragged, overwhelming sobs subsided into shuddering breaths. The violent trembling eased into a slight tremor. He was still crying, but the storm was passing, leaving a quiet, cleansing rain in its wake.

He pulled back just enough to look at both of them, his eyes red-rimmed and full of a profound, exhausted gratitude. His voice, when it finally returned, was a raw, fragile whisper.

"The movie," he rasped, his gaze flickering toward the laptop where the triumphant, paused image of Kaelan still stood. "Can we... can we start it over?".

Sarah’s face broke into a beautiful, tearful smile. It was the most Jack thing he could have possibly said. "Of course, dork," she whispered, her own voice thick with emotion. "Absolutely".

She reached over and restarted the film. And for the next two hours, the three of them—Jack in his bed, Sarah on one side, and Mandy in a chair pulled close on the other—watched "The Silver Gauntlet of Aethel" together from the very beginning. The epic, cheesy fantasy adventure was a familiar, comforting blanket, a shared language that needed no words.

When the final credits rolled, a deep, peaceful exhaustion had settled over the room. Mandy knew it was time to go.

"I should get back and check on my grandmother," she said softly, standing up. "But thank you for... for letting me be here."

She moved to Jack's bedside, and this time, there was no hesitation from either of them. She leaned down and gave him a gentle, careful hug goodbye. "I'll come visit again tomorrow, if that's okay," she promised.

Jack nodded, a small, genuine smile on his face. "I'd like that," he whispered.

Mandy gave Sarah a final, warm look of solidarity and slipped out of the room, leaving the two siblings in the quiet, peaceful aftermath of a storm that had been a long time coming.

### Part 7: The Rec Center Rallies

The next day, the sun came out, making the thick blanket of snow outside Jack's window sparkle with a blinding intensity. The city was slowly starting to dig out, and the main roads were becoming passable again.

Around mid-afternoon, a cheerful group of visitors arrived. It was their "rec center people." Jennifer, Jack's boss, and Donald, the Lead Cashier, came in from the front desk, bearing a card signed by the entire member services team. They were followed by Scott, Sarah's supervisor, and a couple of other lifeguards.

"Well, look at you," Jennifer said with a warm smile, her professional demeanor softened by genuine concern. "Giving us all a scare like that on Valentine's Day."

"Heard you called a First Response on yourself," Donald added, shaking his head in admiration. "That's a new one for the books. The whole desk is rooting for you, man."

Jack, propped up in bed, felt a genuine smile spread across his face, happy to see his coworkers, a welcome slice of his normal life. They chatted for a while, sharing lighthearted stories from work and reassuring him that his shifts were covered and not to worry about a thing.

A little later, another woman from aquatics arrived. She was Kelly, the Lead Aquatics Supervisor and the person Scott reported to. After expressing her relief that Jack was recovering, she pulled Sarah aside for a quiet word.

"Sarah," Kelly began, her tone both professional and kind, "we're aiming to reopen the rec center tomorrow, assuming the city gives the all-clear tonight. I've already spoken with Scott, and we both agree your place is here with your brother."

Sarah's face was a mixture of gratitude and worry. "I want to be, I just don't know..."

Kelly held up a hand. "Don't you worry about scheduling. You have more than a week of leave saved up. Consider it approved as soon as you need it. For now, you're on paid bad weather leave, which is city policy and doesn't count against your hours. Once we reopen, your official leave will start. You just stay here. Aquatics will figure it out."

A wave of immense relief washed over Sarah. The logistical hurdle of work was gone. She could stay, without worry, and focus solely on Jack. "Thank you, Kelly," she said, her voice thick with emotion. "Really. Thank you."

Kelly just gave her a supportive nod before saying her goodbyes. With the support of their work family confirmed, and the promise that Sarah could remain by his side, the path to recovery felt a little less daunting.

### Part 8: A Mentor's Visit

Later that afternoon, after the stream of well-wishers had slowed, Jack was resting, listening to the quiet murmur of the television Sarah had on. From the hallway, he could hear the approaching voices of another group of visitors. His head lifted from the pillow. Among the familiar tones, one voice stood out—a warm, booming baritone with a thick, unmistakable Sydney accent that Jack would recognize anywhere.

A moment later, the man himself appeared in the doorway, his presence filling the room with a calm, reassuring energy.

“G’day, mate,” Anthony said, his eyes kind. “Heard you found a way to get out of all that scrubbing during Maintenance Week next month. Bit of a drastic measure, don’t you think?”

A huge, genuine smile broke across Jack’s face. “Anthony! I knew I heard you out there. Man, it’s good to see you.”

He was the person Jack had wanted to see more than anyone. Anthony was the building supervisor at another rec center now, but for one good, solid year, he had been the supervisor at Fairlawn. He was the first boss Jack ever had who seemed to just *get it*—who understood his disabilities without needing a formal explanation and went out of his way to make small accommodations that made a world of difference.

Anthony came to his bedside, clapping him gently on the shoulder. “Good to see you too, Jack, even under the circumstances. We’re all pulling for you.” He glanced at Sarah with a warm smile. “And you. You’re doing a good job looking after him.”

“Always,” Sarah said.

“Listen,” Anthony said, turning back to Jack, his tone shifting to one of quiet, practical support. “Don’t you worry about a thing with your paperwork. I’m going to have a word with HR first thing in the morning, make sure everything is sorted smoothly for your medical leave. Your only job is to focus on getting better. We’ll handle the bureaucracy.”

Jack felt a wave of relief. Navigating city paperwork was a daunting task, and Anthony’s immediate offer to help was exactly the kind of proactive support he was known for. He was the first male authority figure Jack had ever felt he could truly look up to, and in this moment, his presence was an immense comfort.

### Part 9: An Unexpected Goodbye

Later that evening, there was another soft knock on Jack's hospital room door. A woman with a kind, no-nonsense air stepped in. It was Brandi, the building supervisor from the Bayside Recreation Center, one of the other city facilities where Jack had recently been covering shifts.

"Brandi, hey," Jack said, a tired but genuine smile on his face.

"Evening, you two," Brandi said, nodding to both Jack and Sarah. "Heard you put your training to good use. The whole crew over at Bayside was asking about you."

"Thanks for coming," Jack said, his expression turning serious. "Listen, there's something I need to ask you. Can you get a message to Alex for me? I really need to thank her." He took a breath, the memory of his terror sharp in his mind. "She was the one who drilled me on that radio protocol that morning, a couple of weeks ago, before the center opened. She made me practice the 'First Response' call. I think she probably saved my life."

Brandi's smile was warm and understanding. "I will absolutely pass that along to Alex, Jack. I'll make sure she gets the message. She'll be so glad to hear it made a difference." She paused. "Speaking of people who wanted to see you, I actually brought someone with me. She heard you were here and wanted to say a proper goodbye before she moves."

She stepped aside, and another woman entered the room. It was Alexis.

"Alexis!" Jack exclaimed, his face breaking into a wide, surprised grin. "What are you doing here? I thought you left Parks and Rec."

Alexis's smile was warm but a little bittersweet. She had been the building supervisor at Fairlawn who had taken Anthony's place after the big supervisor shuffle. "I did," she confirmed, coming closer to his bed. "I left back in November. I just wasn't happy with how things were going after the shuffle."

She continued, her gaze direct and full of sincere appreciation. "I wanted to see you because... well, you and Sarah were always good to me. You were never hostile, and you always treated me with respect, even when some of the other staff..." she trailed off, not needing to elaborate. "I'm starting a new job next month at a brand new rec center up in Loudoun County, and I couldn't leave without saying a proper goodbye to you both."

Jack was deeply touched. "Wow, Alexis. It's really good to see you. Good luck in Loudoun County."

She smiled. "You too, Jack. You just focus on getting better."

They chatted for a few more minutes, a comfortable and final conversation. As Brandi and Alexis left, Jack felt a sense of closure, happy to have had a chance to say a proper goodbye to a respected former boss.

### Part 10: A New Home

The morning the doctors finally cleared Jack for discharge, the hospital room was filled with the bustling, happy energy of the entire Bennett clan. Sam, Jermaine, Devin, Molly, and Clare were all there, a formidable and loving force ready to bring him home.

The journey to the new house was a blur for Jack. He knew about the plan, but this was his first time seeing the place in person. As they pulled into the driveway of a handsome, spacious house he didn't recognize, the reality of it began to sink in. This was home now.

“Easy does it,” Jermaine said, as he and Devin helped Jack from the car into a wheelchair for the trip inside.

They wheeled him up a newly built ramp to the front door. “We’ll get you settled on the first floor, just like we promised,” Uncle Devin said with a warm smile.

They took him inside, and Sarah led the way down a hallway. “This is your room, Jack,” she said, pushing open a door.

The room was bright and airy, with a large window looking out onto a quiet backyard. But it was the bed that made Jack pause. It was a queen, much larger than his full-sized bed at their old house.

One we’ll always share.” She gestured to an adjoining door. “And Molly and Clare are right next door, so they are always welcome to join now that the bed is larger.”

Jermaine stepped forward, placing a gentle hand on his son's shoulder. “She’s right, son,” he clarified, his voice full of love. “While you were in surgery, we all talked. We made a decision about how this new house is going to work, to make sure you have all the support you need.”

Jack listened, completely overwhelmed. They hadn't just made a space for him; they had fundamentally restructured their lives to create a sanctuary of constant, unwavering support for him. He looked at his sister, at his cousins, at the three men who were the pillars of his life. He tried to say something, to express the enormous gratitude swelling in his chest, but no words came. Tears welled in his eyes as he was wheeled fully into the room, his new home, surrounded by the most incredible family he could have ever asked for.

## Chapter 5: Recovering from a Heart Attack

### Part 1: The New House

Jack’s first full day at home was a masterclass in quiet, coordinated care. The family had decided it was best for him to start his recovery on the living room sofa, which they had transformed into a comfortable nest of pillows and blankets. This way, he was at the center of their new home, a constant, visible part of the family's new rhythm.

Grandpa Sam, being retired, had appointed himself the day's watchful guardian. He sat in a comfortable armchair nearby, reading the newspaper, his steady presence a silent promise of support. He was the trusted adult who would be there all day, while others came and went.

The morning began with a gentle changing of the guard. Before heading off to work, Jermaine and Devin came in to check on Jack.

“You comfortable, son?” Jermaine asked, helping to adjust the pillows behind Jack’s back.

“Need a hand shifting before we go?” Devin offered. Jack nodded gratefully, and the two brothers, strong and practiced, carefully helped him adjust his position on the sofa, taking all the strain off of him.

Molly and Clare, dressed for their own jobs, came to say their goodbyes next, each giving his hand a gentle squeeze and promising to check in on their lunch breaks.

With the men gone to work and the cousins off, Sarah took her place in the armchair Molly had vacated. She was on her first official day of paid leave now that the rec center had reopened, and she was Jack’s primary daytime caregiver.

An hour later, Jack began to feel a dull ache in his back from lying in one position for too long. He shifted, a small grimace of pain on his face.

Sam looked up from his paper instantly. “Need to move, Jack?”

“Yeah, I just… I can’t get comfortable,” Jack admitted.

“Alright then,” Sam said, folding his paper decisively. “Let’s not have you suffering.” He knew his own days of lifting anything heavier than a bag of groceries were long over, but he was the coordinator of his grandson's care. “Sarah, honey,” he called out softly, “Jack needs a hand adjusting. Can you come help him out?”

Sarah was there in a second, her book left on the chair. “Of course. What do you need, Jack?”

With Sarah’s strong and steady help, Jack was able to shift his weight and find a more comfortable position. The maneuver was quiet, efficient, and full of a gentle understanding. He settled back into the pillows, the ache already subsiding. He was home. He was safe. And he was surrounded by a family that knew exactly how to care for him.

### Part 2: First Night at the House

As evening fell, Molly and Clare returned from work, their cheerful energy lighting up the new house. After a quiet dinner, they helped Jack up from the living room sofa. But instead of guiding him to a small, separate bedroom, they walked him to the converted garage, their new, self-contained apartment: The Quad.

Sarah, who had been waiting for them, pushed open the interior door, and Jack's breath caught. He had helped design it, had dreamed about it, but this was his first time seeing it ready. The centerpiece of the room, the thing he had been looking forward to the most, was the massive, nine-foot-by-nine-foot Alaskan King bed, a vast, welcoming island of comfort that dominated the space.

"Welcome home, Jack," Sarah said softly.

“I admit that sharing a bed with my sister, and my cousins, permanently was not on my bingo card,” Jack replied, a small, weary smile on his face. The idea of this being their permanent arrangement was new and overwhelming, but in the best possible way.

“The three of us felt it would be a good idea for you to not be alone, in case you had to get up in the middle of the night,” Sarah said.

“I understand,” Jack replied, his gaze sweeping over the incredible bed.

“Before we get you settled, do you want to be washed?” his sister asked.

“That might not be a bad idea,” Jack replied. “My head really needs a good scrub.”

“Oh, we already spoke about this,” Molly said as she started to help Jack toward the large, accessible Jack and Jill bathroom. “We’re comfortable with this. We planned on helping you with getting showered and everything.”

The four of them worked as a seamless team. Clare got the shower bench ready while Molly started the water. Sarah adjusted the temperature to Jack’s liking before she and Molly helped him onto the bench. As Sarah began to carefully wash his hair, being gentle with the surgical incision on his chest, Molly grabbed a fresh set of pajamas for Jack and a set for Sarah.

The shower was completed, and Jack was helped onto the toilet so he could be dried off. As Sarah gently patted him dry with a towel, she grabbed her own pajamas from Molly. Without a hint of self-consciousness, she began to change out of her day clothes right there, a decision they’d all made in the hospital to prioritize care over any awkward notions of privacy.

Molly chuckled. “You know, I think Delores’s head would have actually exploded if she saw this.”

“Total system failure,” Clare added with a grin.

Sarah laughed, pulling on her pajama shirt. “She’d probably call the cops.”

Jack, now being helped into his own clean pajamas, felt a genuine smile for the first time in a while. The easy, platonic comfort between them was the complete antithesis of the toxic jealousy he had escaped.

He was helped, not into a small bed, but onto the magnificent Alaskan King. Molly and Clare came to his bedside. “Goodnight, Jack,” Clare whispered, leaning down to give him a very gentle hug. Molly did the same. “Sleep good. We’re right here with you.”

As his cousins went to their side of the massive bed, Sarah slid in beside Jack. They settled in for their first night in their sanctuary, arranged just as they had always imagined: from left to right, it was Molly, Jack, Sarah, and Clare, with Jack and Sarah bookended by the protective presence of the twins.

Jack lay on his side, but his mind started to churn, thinking of the coldness in Delores’s eyes, the sharp edges of her anger. A shudder ran through him.

Sarah, ever attuned to him, sensed his distress in the darkness. “Hey,” she whispered. “Scoot a little closer.”

He shifted slightly, and she moved in behind him. With extreme care for his surgical incision, she draped an arm over his waist and pulled him gently against her, spooning him. She wanted him to feel cocooned, safe, shielded from the bad memories.

“It’s okay,” she murmured into his hair. “You’re safe now. We’ve all got you.”

And in the circle of his sister’s arms, on a bed big enough for them all, feeling completely protected, the thoughts of Delores finally faded, held at bay by the unwavering barrier of his family’s love. He was home.

### Part 3: A Taste of Home

The next day, the house was a sanctuary of quiet care. With Sarah on leave and Molly and Clare happening to have the day off, all three of his favorite women were there to keep Jack company. Grandpa Sam, having declared himself the "day shift supervisor," sat in his armchair, a watchful and comforting presence.

Jack was dozing on the sofa when a commercial came on the television—a cheerful, upbeat ad for a national coffee chain. Sarah, noticing Jack stir and frown slightly at the screen, picked up the remote and muted the volume.

Sam looked from the muted TV to his grandson. A slow, determined smile spread across his face. “I know what this situation calls for,” he announced, getting to his feet. “A real taste of home. Something to wash the memory of bad dates out of our mouths.”

He headed into the kitchen, and a few minutes later, the comforting scent of warming milk began to fill the house. He returned bearing a tray with four steaming mugs. They weren't just hot chocolate; they were works of art. Each was topped with a generous swirl of whipped cream, and drizzled over the top was a dark, sweet syrup.

“Is that…?” Sarah started, her eyes wide with recognition.

“The one and only,” Sam confirmed, handing a mug to Jack. “Proper Rhode Island coffee milk. Mixed in and drizzled on top, just the way we used to make it in Newport.”

Jack’s face lit up with a pure, unadulterated joy they hadn’t seen since before the heart attack. “Coffee milk!” he exclaimed, his voice full of excitement. “Grandpa, you’re the best!”

Sam handed mugs to Sarah, Molly, and Clare. The four cousins/siblings held their warm drinks. Sarah caught Jack’s eye over the rim of her mug.

“So much better than *coffee*,” she said with a small, knowing smile.

“Definitely,” Molly added, taking a sip. “No disastrous coffee dates here.”

The four of them shared a look, a silent toast. In that moment, the simple, sweet act of drinking hot chocolate together became an act of solidarity. They were reclaiming Jack’s comfort, erasing the memory of Delores’s impersonal and manipulative gesture with a taste of home, a flavor that was uniquely theirs. Jack took a deep, happy sip, the rich, sweet taste a confirmation that he was safe, loved, and finally free.

### Part 4: A Visit to a Second Home

Later that day, a familiar restlessness began to stir in Jack. “I want to try walking again,” he announced to Sarah and Sam. “It’s bitterly cold outside, and the house isn’t big enough. I… I want to go to Fairlawn. See my people. Maybe walk the hallway there.”

Sarah and Sam exchanged a look. It was a big step, but they understood his need. “That’s a great idea, Jack,” Sam said. “Let’s get you bundled up.”

When they arrived at Fairlawn Recreation Center, Jack felt a wave of nostalgia. He was glad to see the once-notorious potholes in the parking lot had finally been paved over since his childhood. He walked slowly from the car, his hand resting lightly on Sarah’s arm for balance, a trusted anchor when he felt his energy waver.

Inside, the first order of business was to check in. As was policy for everyone, employees included, all three of them pulled out their membership cards. The young man at the scan station, Paul, looked up as they approached, his face breaking into a wide grin of pure relief.

From behind the main row of cash registers, Mandy and Jean looked over. “Oh my gosh, Jack! Look at you!” Mandy called out, waving enthusiastically.

“This is Paul,” Mandy explained from her spot. “He’s from Bayside, helping us fill in your shift.”

“Hey man,” Paul said from the scan station. “Heard a lot about you. We’re all pulling for you to get back on your feet.”

After a few more minutes of happy, relieved chatter across the front desk, Jack looked down the long, familiar first-floor hallway that ran past the Youth Lounge and the meeting rooms. He took a deep breath. “Part of the reason I wanted to come,” he said to Mandy, “was to see if I could walk a bit. This hallway is perfect.”

With Sam on one side and Sarah on the other, Jack began his slow, determined walk. He passed the massive, dark grey concrete spiral ramp, a landmark he’d run up and down hundreds of times as a kid. He was weaker now, his steps measured, but here, in his second home, surrounded by the love of his family and the genuine care of his coworkers, each step felt a little stronger, a little more hopeful.

After his slow, determined walk down the first-floor corridor, Jack returned to the lobby, leaning on his walker and feeling a mix of exhaustion and pride. He had done it. But he wasn't finished. He looked at Mandy, who was watching him from behind the front desk with a proud smile.

The other lifeguards echoed the sentiment, their greetings a warm, welcome chorus. They were all genuinely happy to see him not just out of the hospital, but physically on his feet, a testament to his recovery.

Sarah stood beside her brother, her heart swelling with a fierce pride. Just a few weeks ago, he was lying in an ICU bed, pale and barely conscious. Now, here he was, standing tall, joking with her coworkers, reclaiming a piece of his life. Watching him, seeing the strength and determination on his face, she felt a wave of overwhelming love and admiration for how quickly and how hard her brother was fighting to recover.

After a few more minutes of warm conversation with the aquatics staff, Jack began to feel the full weight of his exertion. "I think," he said, his voice tired but happy, "I'd better get home and rest."

Scott grabbed his keys and led the small group back to the staff-operated elevator, unlocking it and riding down with them to the first-floor lobby. The doors opened to the familiar sight of the massive concrete ramp and the bustling front desk.

Jack, with Sarah on one side and Sam on the other for support, walked over to the desk for one last goodbye. Mandy, Jean, and Paul all smiled warmly as they approached.

"We're heading out," Jack said, his voice full of gratitude. "Thanks so much for everything, guys. It was really, really good to see you all."

"It was so good to see *you*, Jack!" Mandy replied, her voice genuine. "You look amazing. Keep up the good work with your recovery, and don't be a stranger, you hear?"

With a final wave, Jack and his family turned and walked towards the main entrance. He was exhausted, every muscle aching, but his heart felt lighter than it had in weeks. He had faced the place where his life had been turned upside down and had been met not with trauma, but with an overwhelming wave of love and support. As he stepped out into the crisp afternoon air, the day felt full of hope.

### Part 5: A Final Encounter

The very next day, Monday, Jack felt strong enough for another important outing. The visit to Fairlawn had bolstered his spirits, and he knew he had to take care of his college paperwork. He, Sarah, and Grandpa Sam drove to the Community College of Hampton Roads campus in Virginia Beach.

The walk from the parking lot was long, and Jack, still easily exhausted, draped his arm over Sarah’s shoulders, leaning on her for support. Sarah walked steadily, letting him set the pace, her presence a solid anchor.

Jack's face lit up. "Yeah! A new rec center. I'd like that".

The three of them arrived at the Windsor Woods Recreation Center a short while later. The building was a stark contrast to their beloved Fairlawn; it was new, modern, and gleaming. They checked in at the front desk, the familiar beep of their membership cards a small link to their old routine.

"The weight room is on the second floor, Grandpa," Clare said, pointing toward the nearby elevator and stairs. "We'll walk with Jack for a bit first".

Jack took a deep breath and, using his walker, took his first tentative step onto the polished floor of the main hallway. The entrance was in the middle of the long corridor, giving him a clear, straight path in either direction. He took another step, then a third, and then he stopped, a sharp gasp escaping him. He stumbled, catching himself on the walker, his face going pale.

"Whoa, Jack, you okay?" Clare asked, instantly at his side.

"My foot," he said, his voice tight with pain and a dawning, sickening dread. "It's... it's not working. It's aching, and I can't lift it." He tried to take another step, but his right foot dragged uselessly, a dead weight.

He looked at his grandfather, his eyes wide with fear. Sam's own expression was a mask of gentle concern. "It's your CMT, son," Sam said, his voice a low, steady rumble. "It's flaring up again".

Jack knew he was right. This was the same feeling he'd had in Sarah's hospital room, but this flare-up felt unusually severe, a hundred times worse than before. The weakness, the tingling, the complete lack of control—it felt like a permanent, catastrophic failure this time, not just a passing weakness.

He sank onto a nearby bench, the strength gone from his legs, his heart pounding with a new kind of terror. This was the moment he had been dreading, the one his father and uncle had been planning for, but he had never imagined it would feel this sudden, this absolute. He looked at the long, gleaming hallway in front of him, a path he had intended to walk just moments ago. Now, the simple act felt impossible. He didn't know if this was a temporary, albeit unusually painful, setback, or something more permanent. All he knew was the profound, soul-deep sense of defeat and the terrifying uncertainty of what his own body would do next.

### Part 2: The Long Walk Home

The front desk clerk at the gleaming Windsor Woods counter looked up, her professional smile faltering as she saw **Jack's** sudden distress. "Is everything alright over there?" she called out, her voice full of genuine concern. "Do you need any assistance?".

**Grandpa Sam**, who was already helping a pale and trembling Jack, looked over and gave her a grateful but firm shake of his head. "Thank you, dear, but I think we've got it," he said, making the executive decision to handle this within the family. "He just overdid it a bit".

"I... I need the restroom," Jack managed to say, the simple need now feeling like a monumental task.

"Of course, son," Sam said, his voice a steady, comforting rumble. He and **Clare** positioned themselves on either side of Jack, taking most of his weight. The walk to the Assisted Care restroom, just a short distance down the main hallway, was an agonizingly slow and methodical ordeal. Each step was a battle, Jack's right foot dragging uselessly, his arms shaking with the effort of holding himself up with the walker.

After he had taken care of his needs, the journey back to the car was even more of a struggle. Sam and Clare practically had to carry him, their own strength straining as they navigated him out of the building and to the car. By the time they arrived back at the new house, Jack's energy was completely gone. The thought of making it all the way to his bed in **The Quad** seemed impossible.

"The sofa," Clare said, her voice tight with effort and worry as they helped him through the front door. "It's closer".

They managed to get him settled on the living room sofa, where he collapsed against the cushions, his face ashen and his breathing ragged. Clare immediately sat at his side, and he gratefully rested his head in her lap, a familiar, comforting anchor in a sea of pain and fear.

Sam watched them for a moment, his expression grim. He pulled out his cell phone. "Alright," he announced, his tone all business. "Time to call the doctor."

He dialed the number for Jack's primary care physician and put the phone on speaker, placing it on the coffee table in front of his grandson. "It's your call to make, Jack," Sam said, respecting his grandson's agency even now.

A friendly receptionist's voice filled the room. "Dr. Miller's office, this is Susan speaking."

Jack took a shaky breath, his voice weak but clear. "Hi, Susan," he said. "This is Jack Bennett. I'm... I'm having a really severe flare-up of my CMT. I need to make an appointment to see the doctor as soon as possible."

### Part 3: The Sister-Shield

The house was quiet for a long time after the call. **Jack**, utterly spent from the physical and emotional ordeal, dozed fitfully, his head still resting in **Clare's** lap on the sofa. **Grandpa Sam** sat in his armchair, the grim, worried look never leaving his face.

A little while later, the front door opened, and **Molly** walked in, home from work. She took one look at the scene in the living room—her twin sister on the sofa, her cousin pale and asleep in Clare's lap, her grandfather looking grave—and her own cheerful demeanor vanished.

"What happened?" she asked, her voice a low, urgent whisper as she dropped her bag and rushed over.

"We went to Windsor Woods to walk," Clare explained softly, so as not to wake Jack. "He barely made it ten feet from the front desk. His CMT... it was bad, Molly. Worse than I've ever seen it. We had to practically carry him out of there".

Molly's face was a mask of deep, empathetic pain. She looked down at her cousin, at the lines of exhaustion and pain still etched on his sleeping face. Without a word, she sat down on the sofa, right next to her sister.

Gently, carefully, the two twins began to shift. They slid Jack's legs, mindful of his sternal precautions, until he was lying across both of their laps, his head now resting on Molly's thigh, his feet on Clare's. It was a silent, powerful gesture. They were sharing the burden, creating a broader, more stable foundation of support for him. They were a shield, a wall, and in that moment, they were letting him know, even in his sleep, that they would carry him through this new, frightening chapter of his life, together.

### Part 4: A New Kind of Housebound

The days following the disastrous walk at Windsor Woods were a blur of quiet, difficult adjustments. The severe flare-up of **Jack's** Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease did not subside. His right foot remained a useless, heavy appendage, and the weakness in his legs was profound. The walker now sat untouched in a corner; it was no longer enough.

His world shrank to the comfortable confines of the living room sofa during the day and the massive bed in **The Quad** at night. Journeys between the two spaces were accomplished with the help of a simple transport wheelchair, with **Sarah**, **Molly**, or **Clare** providing the pushing power. The easy independence he had fought so hard to reclaim felt like a distant, mocking memory.

On a quiet Tuesday morning, a week after his return from the hospital, the doorbell rang. **Jermaine**, who was working from home in the upstairs office, came down to answer it. A moment later, a woman with a kind, professional face and a large medical bag stepped into the living room.

"Jack?" she said with a warm smile. "Hi, I'm Carol Miller. I'm the home health nurse assigned to you from the Heart Hospital. Just here for your first post-op check-in. How are you feeling?"

Jack, who was propped up on the sofa, managed a weak smile. "I'm... okay."

Nurse Miller's experienced eyes took in the scene: the pale young man on the sofa, the transport wheelchair parked nearby, the slight but visible tremor in his hands. Her focus was his heart. "Alright," she said, setting her bag down. "Let's have a look at that incision, shall we?"

She was professional, gentle, and thorough. She carefully inspected the long, healing incision on his chest, nodding with approval. "Looks wonderful, Jack. Clean, dry, no signs of infection. You're healing beautifully there." She checked his blood pressure, listened to his heart and lungs, and reviewed his medication list.

"Okay," she said, making notes on her chart. "Everything on the cardiac front looks fantastic. Now, the next step is getting you moving. The doctor wants you to be walking a little bit every day, just to build your strength back up."

A heavy, awkward silence fell over the room. Jack looked down at his hands, a flush of humiliation creeping up his neck.

It was Sarah, who had just come in from The Quad, who finally spoke. "He... he can't, Ms. Miller," she said quietly. "He can't walk."

Nurse Miller looked up from her chart, her professional smile faltering into a look of genuine confusion. "I'm sorry?"

"My other condition," Jack mumbled, still not looking at her. "My Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease. It... it got a lot worse after the surgery. We tried to walk at the rec center a few days ago, and... and I can't. My foot just drags."

The nurse's expression shifted to one of deep, compassionate understanding. She put her chart down and pulled a small stool over, sitting so she was at his eye level. "Okay, Jack," she said softly. "Tell me what that feels like."

He explained the foot drop, the weakness, the pain. He explained the humiliating struggle at Windsor Woods, the fear, the defeat. She listened, her gaze never wavering, her presence calm and validating.

"That sounds incredibly frustrating and frightening," she said when he had finished. "And you're right to take it seriously. While I'm here for your heart, your whole body is my patient. It sounds like the physical trauma of the surgery has caused a severe flare-up of your CMT."

She looked from Jack to Sarah, her voice firm but kind. "You made an appointment with his doctor?"

"We did," Sarah confirmed. "It's next week. With his neurologist, too."

"Good," Nurse Miller said with a decisive nod. "That is the absolute right thing to do. In the meantime," she continued, her tone shifting to one of practical problem-solving, "we adjust the plan. No long walks. For now, your 'walking' is just practicing safe transfers. From this sofa to the wheelchair. From the wheelchair to the toilet. That's it. That's the victory for this week."

She looked at Jack, her expression full of a gentle strength. "This is a new, unexpected challenge, Jack. It's okay to be angry and scared. But we're going to deal with what's in front of us, one step at a time. And the first step is that neurologist appointment. You're not alone in this."

Jack looked at the nurse, then at his sister, and felt a fragile sliver of hope. It was a terrifying new reality, but he wasn't facing it alone. His family was his wall, and now, he had a new, unexpected ally in his corner.

## Chapter 8: A Quiet Refusal

### Part 1: The Midnight Showing

The lobby of the movie theater on the evening of **March 30th, 2007**, was not packed, but it was buzzing with a palpable, electric energy. This wasn't a crowd for a normal blockbuster; this was a gathering of the faithful. It was the midnight premiere of the 20th-anniversary re-release of **"The Silver Gauntlet of Aethel,"** and only the most dedicated fans were in attendance.

The small crowd was a sea of elaborate, homemade costumes. And in the middle of it all, five figures stood out, a perfect, complete set.

**Jack**, in his wheelchair, was dressed as the hero, **Kaelan**, his chair cleverly decorated with faux stone and moss to resemble a rolling throne from the film. Beside him, **Sarah** was a perfect **Kaela**, her silver pendant glowing softly. **Molly**, in a long, grey robe and a wise, knowing expression, was the spitting image of the Wise Old Wizard of Sunstead. **Clare**, in a dramatic, villainous-but-comical gown, was a flawless Seraphina, the bumbling temptress. And with them, her expression one of pure, happy belonging, was **Mandy**, dressed as the Keeper of the Sunstead Archives, a deep cut that earned her appreciative nods from other hardcore fans in the lobby.

They had invited Mandy without a second thought. She was one of them now, a part of their fellowship, a fellow fan who understood the profound importance of this night.

"I can't believe this is real," Jack said, his voice buzzing with an excitement that had been absent for months. "Seeing it on the big screen. The *widescreen* version for the first time."

"It's going to be epic," Sarah agreed, her eyes shining.

They found their seats in the half-full theater, a perfect spot with a clear view for Jack's chair. The lights began to dim, and a reverent hush fell over the costumed crowd. The opening logos appeared, and then, the room was filled with the glorious, triumphant, and digitally restored sound of the opening synthesizer chords of **"A Sister's Love."**

A collective, heartfelt cheer went up from the audience. Jack, Sarah, Molly, Clare, and Mandy all looked at each other, their faces illuminated by the glow of the screen, and grinned. They were home.

### Part 4: A Delayed Rejection

**Jack** listened as **Emily**, the kind patron from his high school days, finished her question.

"I was wondering," she said, her smile genuine, "if you might like to get a hot chocolate sometime? There's that nice little cafe down the road."

The words hung in the air, polite and direct. But for Jack, they were like a sudden, blinding spotlight. He could feel the eyes of the other patrons. He could feel **Mandy's** gaze from the register beside him. But Mandy's gaze wasn't a source of anxiety anymore; it was the steady, comforting presence of a trusted friend. His autistic brain, desperate to find the "correct" social script for a gentle but firm refusal, still began running a million disastrous simulations at once.

He was frozen, his processor overloaded, his face a blank mask. He stared just past Emily's shoulder, unable to make eye contact, the silence stretching into an awkward, painful eternity. This was how **Delores** had trapped him—by presenting a simple question for which he had no safe, pre-written answer.

*Could Emily be like Mandy?* The thought was a sudden, unexpected variable in his frantic calculations. The blizzard in the hospital had changed things. Mandy had seen him at his most vulnerable, and she had responded with nothing but kindness and respect. She had become a friend, someone outside the family wall that he knew he could trust. He looked at Emily, at her kind, open face, and for the first time, a new question entered his mind: *Could I trust her, too?* But the fear, the ingrained self-preservation from Delores, was still too strong. The risk was too great.

Just as the silence was becoming truly unbearable, the sound of wet flip-flops slapping against the tile announced a welcome arrival. **Sarah** came down the massive concrete spiral ramp from the natatorium, a towel slung over her shoulder, ready for her lunch break.

"Hey, Jack," she said, her voice a cheerful, normal sound that cut through the tension like a knife. "Ready to grab some lunch? I'm starving."

Jack's head snapped toward his sister, his eyes wide with a profound, desperate relief. An out. A perfect, logical, pre-scripted out. He didn't have to navigate the terrifying landscape of a potential new friendship; he just had to go to lunch with his sister, something he did all the time.

"Lunch," he repeated, the word coming out with a little too much force. "Yes. It's time for lunch." He looked at Emily, his expression a mixture of apology and sheer gratitude for the interruption. "I... I have to go to lunch now. With my sister."

Emily, a kind and perceptive person, took in the scene: Jack's obvious relief and the arrival of his sister. She simply saw a brother leaving for his regular lunch break. She smiled, her voice gentle and completely understanding.

"Oh! Of course. Family first. Don't let me keep you," she said, her smile not faltering in the slightest. "Well, the offer for a hot chocolate still stands for another time. I'll see you around, Jack."

And with that, she turned and walked away, leaving no drama, no guilt, no resentment in her wake, but leaving the door to a future date wide open. Jack watched her go, a profound, shuddering wave of relief washing over him, but it was now mixed with a fresh wave of anxiety. He had escaped the immediate moment, but he hadn't solved the problem. He looked over at Sarah, who was watching him with a slightly puzzled but supportive look, and felt an immense wave of love and gratitude. She had, without even knowing it, been his temporary shield once again.

### Part 5: A Protector's Concern

The first-floor break room at Fairlawn was a quiet, familiar sanctuary. **Jack** and **Sarah** sat at one of the small, round tables, their packed lunches spread out before them. Jack, still buzzing with a mixture of relief and residual anxiety, poked at his sandwich without really seeing it. He had escaped the immediate, terrifying social trap, but he knew the problem hadn't been solved.

Sarah took a slow sip of her water, her keen, observant eyes watching her brother. She had seen the whole thing from the bottom of the ramp: Emily's hopeful expression, Jack's frozen panic, and his almost explosive relief when she had appeared. She waited a moment, letting the quiet settle before she spoke.

"So," she began, her voice even and non-judgmental. "What was that all about with Emily?"

Jack flinched, a flush creeping up his neck. "She... she asked me if I wanted to get a hot chocolate sometime," he mumbled, not looking at her.

Sarah nodded slowly, her expression unreadable. "Okay," she said. "And what did you want to say?"

"I wanted to say no," Jack said immediately, his voice a little too loud. "I just... I didn't know *how*. My brain just... stopped."

"I saw," she said softly. She paused, choosing her next words with care. Her matchmaking days were over. Now, her priority was keeping him safe.

"You know," she said, her voice full of a deep, gentle sincerity, "it's okay if you want to have friends, Jack. I would be happy for you to have a friend like Emily. She's a kind person." She saw the flicker of fear in his eyes and immediately softened her tone. "But," she added, her voice now a firm, unwavering promise, "I also know what happened with... her. And I will *never* let anyone hurt you like that again. Ever."

She leaned forward, her gaze intense. "So, while I want you to be happy and have people in your life, anyone, and I mean *anyone*, who wants to get close to you will have to prove they are worthy of your trust. They will have to prove to *me* that they won't hurt you. That's the new rule."

Jack looked at his sister, at the fierce, unconditional love in her eyes, and felt a profound sense of safety wash over him. She wasn't pushing him. She was just reinforcing the wall, making sure he knew that she would always be its primary guardian.

"I don't think I'm ready for new friends yet," he admitted, his voice a quiet whisper.

"That's okay, too," Sarah said, her voice softening completely. She thought for a moment, a small, knowing smile touching her lips as she landed on the perfect metaphor. **"You just have to take it one step at a time, dork. Just like in the song. This is your 'Gauntlet's Path.' And right now, your 'step' is just being safe here with me, Molly, and Clare. And that's all it needs to be"**.

The reference to their beloved movie, to the hero's difficult but ultimately triumphant journey, was the perfect way to frame his struggle. He wasn't failing; he was just on a difficult part of his path.

Jack looked at his sister, a small, genuine smile finally breaking through his anxiety. She gave him a nod. "Now eat your sandwich. You've had a rough day."
