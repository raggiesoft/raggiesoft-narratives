# Part 3: First Response

The time was 10:44 AM. Jack came back to the front desk after using the restroom. He sat down at his station as his immediate supervisor, Donald, got up. Jack took his spot at the scan station, his heart rate still elevated from the long walk from the back of the building.

He suddenly felt an unfamiliar pressure in his chest. It wasn't a sharp pain, just a persistent, heavy twinge that felt deeply wrong. He sat there for a moment, trying to breathe through it, thinking it would go away. *It's nothing,* he told himself. *It'll pass.*

But it didn't pass. The alarming sensation remained, a constant weight that made it hard to focus. He thought back to the radio protocol briefing he'd gotten at another rec center a few weekends ago: a "First Aid" call for non-urgent issues, and a "First Response" for a true emergency. This warning sign was too big to ignore.

He paused for one last moment of indecision, wondering if he should really call for help or just try to let it pass. He looked over at Mandy, who was busy assisting a customer at her register, completely oblivious to the silent crisis unfolding just a few feet away. He was on his own.

He grabbed the radio and depressed the talk button, his hand trembling slightly. “First Response to the Front Desk please. First Response to the Front Desk!”

He saw the door to the administrative office open and the building’s assistant manager stepped out, a questioning look on his face. Seeing him, Jack instinctively grabbed his chest with his free hand, his eyes wide, trying to signal without words: *It’s me. I’m the one who needs help.*

The signal was understood. Almost immediately, supervisors and other staff came flooding out of the administrative office—among them his boss, Jennifer, and her assistant Kaitlyn right behind her. At the same time, a new commotion started at the top of the massive concrete spiral ramp. The aquatics emergency cart, pushed by Scott, appeared, followed by a contingent of lifeguards, including Sarah, their footsteps a rapid, urgent patter as they descended the ramp from the second floor.

Sarah, her lead lifeguard training kicking in, was at his side in seconds. She was wearing her city-issued swimsuit and her glasses, her expression a mask of professional focus warring with deep, personal alarm. “Jack, what’s going on?” she asked, her voice steady despite the situation.

His breathing was becoming erratic, shallow and fast, but his voice was clear. “You know what?” he said to the room at large, looking at Jennifer. “Call 9-1-1.”

The team sprang into action. Sarah and Scott were immediately at his side, getting his vitals, their movements practiced and efficient.

“Sarah? Jennifer?” Jack said, his voice clear but punctuated by the effort of his breathing. “Please let my emergency contacts know what’s going on.”

“Get the wheelchair,” Jennifer called to someone out of view, “Jack? We’re going to take you to the office so you can be in a calmer environment,” Jennifer said, her voice firm, understanding his struggle with crowds. He was wheeled into the back office where both Jennifer and Sarah started making phone calls.

Scott appeared at the door. “Sarah? I’m dismissing you for the day. Your brother needs you more than we do.”

The paramedics arrived quickly. As they entered the office, the rec center staff explained the situation. The paramedics immediately put an oxygen mask over his nose and mouth, and the flow of cool air was a welcome relief against his labored breathing. They took his vitals again, their movements swift and professional. Everything was a blur to him as the decision was made to send him to the hospital.

“Sarah,” he said, his voice slightly muffled by the mask. “My lunch is in the fridge. Take the car, go get Dad, and meet me at the hospital.” He took his sister’s hand, his nervousness evident despite his clear speech.

“I can do that. Which hospital are you taking him to?” she asked, looking at the paramedics.

“Crossroads Memorial,” They told her, and she sprang to her feet. “Let me clock out, get dressed, and get going. I will see you there!” She took her brother’s hand, while his boss kept promising that no job was worth his health and that his front desk job would be there when he returned.
