# Part 4: The Mobile Emergency Room

The journey from the back office to the ambulance bay was a surreal blur. The paramedics moved with a calm, focused urgency, Sarah walking alongside the stretcher, her hand a constant, reassuring presence on his arm until they reached the ambulance's rear door. Ï will meet you at the hospital,” she said, giving his hand one last squeeze. They loaded him in, the doors closed with a solid, final-sounding thud, and for the first time since this ordeal began, he was separated from his sister.

He was inside, the engine was running… but they weren’t moving.

*Why aren't we going?* Jack thought, a fresh wave of panic rising in his chest. *The hospital is the goal, right? Let's go!* He was lying there, staring at the ceiling of the ambulance, every second that passed feeling like a lifetime.

But the two paramedics inside were a flurry of controlled, efficient activity. One of them was attaching new, sticky pads to his chest, connecting them to a monitor that beeped in time with his frantic heartbeat. "Just getting another EKG to send to the hospital," the paramedic explained, his voice calm, not seeming to notice Jack's internal terror. "Lets them know exactly what they're dealing with before we even get there."

The other paramedic was gently palpating the veins on his other arm. "Alright, Jack, you're going to feel another little poke," she said. "We're starting a second IV line, just to be safe."

He felt the prick of the needle, then watched as she expertly taped the line down. He could hear the crackle of the radio, one of the paramedics giving a calm, detailed report to someone on the other end—a string of numbers and medical terms he didn't understand. They were talking about him, about the pressure in his chest, about his vital signs.

The first paramedic checked his blood pressure again. "Pain's still there? Okay, we're giving you a little more of that nitroglycerin."

He felt a strange tingling sensation under his tongue. All this activity, all this work, was happening while they were parked just outside the front door of the rec center. It felt like an eternity, a dangerous delay. But as he watched them work, a small, dawning realization began to cut through his fear. They weren't just waiting. They were *treating* him. This wasn't just a transport vehicle; it was a tiny, mobile emergency room, and the critical first steps of his care were happening right now.

Finally, after what felt like an age, one of the paramedics gave a nod to the driver. "Alright, we're good to go."

The ambulance lurched forward, the siren finally wailing to life. The ride was a dizzying blur of motion and the sensation of speed, but it felt surprisingly short. Before he knew it, the vehicle was slowing, backing into a bay. The rear doors swung open, flooding the small space with bright, harsh light and the sounds of a busy hospital. A team of nurses and doctors was already there, waiting for him, a gurney ready to receive him. The treatment that had started in the back of a rec center was about to continue.
