# Part 3: Closing Down a Rec Center

The front lobby of Fairlawn Recreation Center was quiet at 4:51 PM on a Saturday in late January. The last of the families had trickled out, leaving only the sterile scent of chlorine and the low hum of the vending machines. Sarah Bennett sat at a table, her laptop open, using the hour before her brother closed up to get a head start on a college paper. Her table faced the front desk, a habit born of a lifetime of looking out for Jack.

Behind the desk, the closing-time rhythm was underway. Bob, the acting shift supervisor, was quietly counting one of the cash drawers, his register already shut down. Jean, Tyler’s younger sister, had finished with her register as well and was tidying up the counters. Only Tyler’s register was still technically open, though there were no patrons left to serve.

The main doors slid open, and Tyler was the first to look up, his customer service smile ready. A young woman he didn't recognize walked in, her eyes scanning the lobby. Delores’s gaze swept past the empty tables and landed on Tyler, the only open cashier. She gave him a quick, dismissive side-eye, a look that cataloged and filed him away in an instant: *a potential backup, in case this doesn't work out*. Jean, standing nearby, caught the look. Her expression hardened slightly, a flicker of sisterly protectiveness rising in her. She was prepared to step in if this woman gave her brother any trouble.

Then, Delores saw Jack at the scan station, and her focus narrowed. Her target was here. She bypassed the main counter and walked directly to him, her expression shifting from predatory assessment to nervous vulnerability. Sarah, watching from her table, saw Jack’s posture shift instantly. His shoulders, relaxed just a moment before, were now tense.

From behind the desk, Jack felt the air change. When he looked up, a knot of ice formed in his stomach. It was Delores. The dread he’d felt in that dorm room came rushing back. He immediately straightened up, his hands resting on the counter, the shield of professionalism his only defense.

“Can I help you?” he asked, his voice even and cool.

“Hi, Jack,” Delores said softly. “I… I came to apologize. For the other day.”

Sarah leaned back slightly in her chair, her typing forgotten. Across the lobby, Bob paused his counting, and Tyler and Jean exchanged a quick, subtle glance. They could all feel the sudden shift in the atmosphere.

Jack didn’t respond. He just watched her, his mind a battlefield between memory and her seemingly remorseful performance.

“You were right to leave,” Delores continued, her voice gaining a slight tremor. “I was completely out of line. I scared you. I am so, so sorry.” Her eyes looked shiny. “The truth is,” she leaned in slightly, “I just got out of a really... controlling relationship. A guy who... he tried to isolate me, didn't like me having friends...”

Sarah’s eyes narrowed. *A controlling ex? How convenient.* The story sounded like a perfectly crafted script.

Delores focused back on Jack. “When we were talking, you were just so kind, and I haven’t felt that comfortable with someone in a long time. I completely misread everything and got carried away. That’s not who I am.”

Jack felt a flicker of uncomfortable empathy. His gut was screaming that this was a performance, but his heart ached for anyone who had been hurt that way.

“I was just hoping,” Delores’s plea came, soft and desperate, “you’d give me one chance to prove I’m not that person. Just one cup of coffee. Five minutes. If you still think I’m a total creep, I will disappear and never, ever bother you again. Please?”

The word "coffee" landed with an almost audible thud in the quiet lobby. Behind the desk, Bob stopped counting and shared a brief, knowing look with Tyler, who subtly shook his head. Jean had to quickly turn away to hide a smirk. Every single one of them knew about Jack’s well-established preference for hot chocolate. They’d seen him make it in the break room a hundred times. This girl, with her dramatic apology, clearly didn’t know the first thing about him.

That detail solidified it for Sarah. This was all a lie. But she watched her brother, saw the war happening behind his eyes. He was trapped by his own good nature.

He let out a slow breath. “Okay,” he heard himself say, the word tasting like a mistake. “Just coffee.”

A brilliant, relieved smile flashed across Delores’s face, and for a split second, both Jack and Sarah saw it—that triumphant, hollow intensity in her eyes. Then it was gone, replaced by a look of overwhelming gratitude. “Thank you,” she breathed. “You won’t regret it.”

As they exchanged numbers, Delores glanced down at the ID badge clipped to Jack’s belt. Her eyes flickered over his city-issued ID, which clearly read JACK BENNETT, and then to the simple plastic name tag above it that just said JACK. It was the first time she had ever seen his last name. The information registered with a detached coolness, a data point she was finally acquiring, far too late in the game.

Sarah said nothing. She watched Delores leave, her expression hardening from suspicion to a cold, quiet certainty. She knew manipulation when she heard it. She knew her brother. And she knew, with absolute conviction, that this woman was dangerous. She saved her document, closed her laptop, and began to pack her bag.

She is so lucky Jack is who he is, Sarah thought, her anger a cold, hard knot in her stomach. He could have turned to Bob and had her asked to leave the second she started her act. Instead, he’s giving her a chance she never earned, all because he wouldn't know how to hurt someone. She shook her head. Whatever Delores had done at the university to make Jack look like he’d seen a ghost, she was now trying to cover it up with the worst performance Sarah had ever seen. The ride home was going to be a very, very serious conversation.

Behind the front desk, Jean and Tyler shared a similar thought as they watched Delores exit. *She has no idea how good she just got it,* Jean thought, remembering the predatory way Delores had looked at her brother. *Jack’s too nice. Anyone else would have shut that down immediately.*

At precisely 5:00 PM, Bob looked over at Jean. "Go ahead, Jean."

Jean nodded, walked to one of the front desk phones, pressed the 'Page' button, and dialed '00'. A soft beep echoed from the phone, signaling the connection to the building's PA system. She picked up the receiver.

Her clear, professional voice echoed through the lobby and the rest of the facility: "May I have your attention please. The time is now 5:00 PM, and Fairlawn Recreation Center is now closed. Please make your way to the exit at this time. Again, the time is now 5:00 PM and Fairlawn Recreation Center is now closed. Please make your way to the exit at this time."

She hung up the receiver, then immediately pressed the 'Forward All' button on the phone, followed by the 'Voicemail' button. Any incoming calls to the main line would now be sent directly to a recorded message stating the center was closed and providing its operating hours. With the phones now secured for the night, Bob locked the main doors. He looked over at Jack, who was still standing at the scan station, looking pale and lost in thought.

“Hey, Jack,” Bob called out, his voice a calm, steady presence. “Come on back here and give me a hand with the final count, will ya?”

It was a plausible request, but the real reason was a quiet exercise of his duty of care as the shift supervisor. Bob had seen the whole strange encounter and wanted to make sure his employee was okay.

As Jack walked behind the counter, Bob turned to the two youngest members of his crew. “You two alright? That was… a little weird.”

Tyler just shrugged, but his younger sister Jean spoke up, her voice low but fierce. “I’m fine,” she said, glancing at her brother. “But I saw the way that girl looked at him when she came in. I saw right through her from the beginning. She was trouble.” Her protective stance was clear.

Bob nodded, appreciating her candor. He then focused on Jack, and together they finished the final cash counts and closing procedures in a comfortable, professional silence.

At 5:08 PM, everything was balanced and locked up. “Alright, that’s us,” Bob said, initialing the final report. “Go on home, Jack. Get some rest.” As far as the time clock was concerned, 5:08 was close enough to Jack's scheduled 5:15 PM departure to not matter.

Jack clocked out, grateful for the familiar, grounding routine and for his supervisor’s unspoken support. He grabbed his things and met Sarah by the door. At the same time, Tyler and Jean finished their own closing duties and headed for the exit as well.

As they walked out into the cold January night, Sarah, still feeling unsettled and protective after the encounter with Delores, put her arm around Jack’s shoulders, pulling him into a comforting side-hug. Jack immediately responded in kind, wrapping his own arm around her waist, leaning into her familiar, safe presence as they walked toward their car.

Jean, walking a few feet behind them with her brother, saw the easy, physical affection between the siblings. A small giggle escaped her lips. She imagined what that strange, intense woman, Delores, would think if she saw them now. She’d probably assume the worst, completely incapable of understanding what Jean saw so clearly: two very close siblings who loved each other and were not afraid to show it with simple, non-sexual affection. Feeling her own surge of sisterly protectiveness after witnessing the strange encounter, Jean moved a little closer to Tyler as they walked, a small, silent gesture of solidarity and protection for her own brother.
