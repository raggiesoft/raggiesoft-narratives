# Part 6: Coffee Date

The coffee shop in the Virginia Dominion University library was quiet for a Saturday afternoon. Jack pushed the door open, the scent of roasted coffee beans and old books filling the air. He felt a familiar knot of dread in his stomach. The conversation he’d had with Sarah and his cousins in the car echoed in his mind: *It sounded like a script... She doesn’t know the first thing about you.* He was here to end this, not to start it.

He saw Delores at a small table in the corner. She looked up and gave him a small, hopeful smile that didn’t quite reach her eyes. He walked to the counter, his hands shoved in his pockets.

“Hey,” she said, joining him in the short line. “Thanks for coming. My treat, I insist.”

“I can get my own,” Jack said politely but firmly.

At the register, Delores ordered a black coffee. The barista turned to Jack. “Can I get for you?”

“A hot chocolate, please,” Jack said. “With milk, and whipped cream and chocolate syrup on top.”

He pulled out his wallet to pay. Delores started to protest, her hand moving forward. “No, really, I—” she began, her tone insistent, but she caught herself, quickly masking the controlling impulse. She forced a small, apologetic laugh. “Sorry. Habit. My ex always made a big deal about who paid. You just go ahead.”

Jack noticed the brief flash of annoyance in her eyes before she covered it. It was a tiny crack in her performance, but it was there. It was the same hollow intensity he’d seen in the dorm. He paid for his decadent drink, the transaction feeling like a small but necessary victory for his own autonomy.

They sat down, and an awkward silence stretched between them. Delores was the one to break it, leaning forward earnestly.

“Look, Jack,” she began, “I just want to apologize again. For how I came on so strong. I told you I just got out of a really controlling relationship,” she said, launching into the projected version of her history with Leo. “He tried to isolate me, made me feel like I was always walking on eggshells. I guess I just forgot how to act around someone who is actually a good, gentle person.”

Jack listened, his guard fully up. He heard the words, but all he could think was, *Sarah was right. This is a performance.* The story pricked at his empathy, but the memory of her irritation at the counter was a fresh warning. His inability to be cruel was warring with every self-preservation instinct he had.

“I know I messed up our first impression,” Delores said, her eyes pleading. “But I would really like to get to know you, Jack. For real this time. No pressure. Please, just give me a second chance. One real date.”

He looked at her, at the practiced vulnerability in her expression. His gut was screaming no. His sister and cousins’ worried voices were a chorus in his head. But the thought of looking this person in the eye and saying, “No, I don’t believe you, go away,” felt impossibly cruel. He wouldn’t even know how to form the words.

A long, tense moment passed. He stared into his mug of whipped cream and chocolate, feeling trapped by his own compassion. He let out a slow, quiet sigh of resignation.

“Okay,” he said, the word feeling heavy and wrong. “One date.”

The relief that washed over Delores’s face was immediate, and again, for just a second, he saw that flash of triumph, of victory. She quickly hid it behind a grateful smile.

“Thank you,” she said, her voice soft. “You won’t regret it.”

But as Jack sat there, finishing his hot chocolate while she talked about a class she was taking, he knew he already did.
