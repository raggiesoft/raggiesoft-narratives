The silence in the car was a shared, weary comfort as Jack pulled out of the Fairlawn Recreation Center parking lot. He and Sarah were both tired, the end of a long week and the weight of the last few months pressing on them. Jack was driving, his hands steady on the wheel.

A shrill, tinny ringtone echoed from a basic flip phone resting in the center console. Jack glanced at the screen, saw it was his cousins, and tapped the button to connect it to the car’s speaker system.

“Hey! Are you guys on the road yet?” Clare’s voice came through the speakers, clear and bright.

“Just pulled out,” Jack said. “You’re on speaker.”

“Perfect!” Molly’s voice joined in. “We were just staring at this mountain of boxes and figured we needed a distraction. Have you guys started packing the kitchen yet?”

Sarah sighed, a smile touching her lips. “Don’t even say the word ‘packing.’ I think if I see another roll of tape, I’ll scream. But no, we’re saving the kitchen for last. Dad’s orders.”

“Smart man,” Clare said. “Uncle Devin is the same way. He says the coffee maker is the last thing to go in a box and the first thing to come out.”

They chatted for a few minutes about the upcoming move on February 17th. The logistics were daunting, but the thought of their two families finally being under one roof after the raw, recent pain of the divorce was a beacon for all of them. The anger at their mother’s betrayal was still fresh, making them cling to this new, redefined family unit even more fiercely.

“So, how was the rest of the shift?” Molly asked Jack. “Anything exciting happen in the last nine minutes of a Saturday at the rec?”

Jack hesitated, glancing at Sarah. “Actually, yeah. That girl from the VDU open house showed up.”

Clare’s voice sharpened with immediate recognition over the speaker. “Wait, the ‘intense’ one? The one you told me about? What did she want?”

Sarah took a deep breath. As the sole witness, she knew it was her turn. She recounted the entire scene—Delores’s hesitant entrance, her carefully worded apology, the perfectly crafted sob story about a controlling ex.

Over the speaker, Clare scoffed. “A controlling ex? That sounds way too convenient. That’s exactly the kind of manipulative vibe you were telling me about, Jack.”

“It was a total performance,” Sarah confirmed, her voice low. “And then she asked him for coffee.”

“Coffee?” Molly chimed in, incredulous. “She asked *Jack* for coffee? Jack, you hate coffee!”

“That’s what I told him!” Sarah said, relieved to be validated.

Faced with this unified front of concern, Jack felt his own unease solidify into certainty. He wasn’t defensive; he was just tired.

“I know,” he said, his voice quiet. “My gut was screaming the whole time. But… she was standing there, telling me this story about being hurt… I wouldn't even know how to be cruel enough to just shut her down.”

“We know, Jack,” Clare said gently through the speaker. “That’s what makes you, you. But it’s also what people like her count on.”

“So what did you do?” Molly asked.

“I agreed to get coffee,” Jack admitted. “Just once. To hear her out and then end it.”

There was a brief silence on the line. Then, Sarah spoke, her voice full of understanding. “Okay. That makes sense. Just… be careful, Jack. Please. The last thing any of us need right now is more drama from some random girl, not after everything with Mom.”

“Agreed,” the twins on the phone said in unison.

“I will,” Jack promised, and he meant it. He felt a profound sense of gratitude for them. They were his compass, his anchor.

“Okay, enough about that weirdo,” Clare announced, her tone shifting back to business. “Back to the move. Who gets the bedroom with the bigger closet in the new house, because Molly is already trying to claim it…”

The conversation shifted back to logistics, the impending danger of Delores acknowledged, assessed, and contained by their collective vigilance. Jack drove on, listening to their familiar, loving arguments, feeling profoundly safe in their circle of protection.
