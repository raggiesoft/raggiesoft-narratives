# Part 3: The Journey In

The morning started with the chaotic, yet comforting, ritual of a hotel continental breakfast. With their bellies full, they bundled up against the crisp spring air and began the short, pleasant walk from their hotel to the Caroline Street Station. At the station, Jermaine confidently navigated the ticketing process, purchasing three round-trip tickets on the Dominion Rail for their journey straight into the heart of Washington, D.C..

They found their seats on the train, a comfortable 2x2 layout. The siblings, Jack and Sarah, slid into a pair of seats by the window, while Jermaine took a seat directly across the aisle from them. As the train pulled smoothly out of the station, Jermaine leaned forward.

“Okay, so, first day,” he said, a genuine smile on his face. “What’s the plan, team? Jack, I know you’re dying to see the Air and Space Museum.”

“Definitely!” Jack said, his excitement bubbling up. “I want to see the old planes and the space capsules.”

“And the zoo for me,” Sarah added. “We have to see the pandas.”

“Alright, here’s what I’m thinking,” Jermaine said, pulling a small map from his jacket pocket. “We’ll get off at L’Enfant, which is super close to the National Mall. We can spend the whole morning at Air and Space, let you get your fill of rockets, Jack. Then, after lunch, we can hop on the Capital Metro and head up to the zoo for the afternoon. How does that sound?”

“Perfect!” they both said in unison.

The train ride was smooth and comfortable, the Virginia countryside rolling past their window. As they got closer to the city, the train began to parallel the infamous I-395 corridor. A little while later, the train slowed as it approached the Long Bridge, the massive steel artery that would carry them over the Potomac River.

“Look,” Jermaine said, pointing out the window to their left.

Jack and Sarah peered out. Parallel to their own tracks was the 14th Street Bridge. It was a parking lot. Thousands of cars were crammed together, barely moving, a river of frustrated commuters inching their way into the city. Horns blared faintly, a distant, angry sound.

“And *that*,” Jermaine said with a grim satisfaction, “is why we are sitting here, relaxed and moving, and not in the car. I’ve spent more hours of my life than I care to admit stuck in that exact spot.”

Just then, their own train picked up speed, zooming past the gridlock with a satisfying whoosh. Jack and Sarah looked at each other, a shared, silent understanding passing between them. Their dad had made a very, very good decision.

A little while later, the automated voice announced their destination: “Next stop, L'Enfant. This is the L'Enfant station.”

The train slowed and pulled into the open-air platform. They got off, and the sheer scale of the city was immediately apparent. They walked from the platform up a short flight of stairs to the main plaza, emerging directly into the bright morning sunlight and the bustle of the city. The massive, impressive structure of the National Air and Space Museum was just a short walk away, ready to begin the first adventure of their trip. There had been no need to transfer, no confusing subway maps to navigate—just one simple, direct trip.
