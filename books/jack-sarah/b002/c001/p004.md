# Part 4: Sarah’s Incident

The afternoon sun was warm, and the sounds of the zoo—a cacophony of animal calls, laughing children, and the general murmur of the crowd—filled the air. After a delightful morning at the Air and Space Museum, the Bennetts had taken the Capital Metro up to the zoo. The three of them were now walking along a winding, paved path that descended a surprisingly steep hill toward the lower valley of the park.

Sarah, wearing a pair of stylish but impractical sandals, found her footing becoming less and less certain on the incline. The steepness of the hill was something she wasn’t used to, and her ankles began to ache with the effort of keeping her balance. A wave of dizziness washed over her. “I think I need to sit down,” she said, her voice a little breathless as she came to a stop.

“Let’s look for a bench or something,” Jermaine replied, his expression immediately shifting to one of fatherly concern.

Jack, noticing his sister’s struggle, moved closer to her side. “Need a shoulder to lean on?” he offered gently.

“Please,” she replied, grateful. Jack positioned himself so she could drape an arm over his shoulders, and he put a supportive arm around her waist. They began to make their way slowly down the hill together, a careful, coordinated effort. But a few steps later, Sarah’s sandal caught on an uneven patch of pavement. They started to stumble; Sarah lost her grip on his shoulder, and with a small cry of surprise, she found herself tumbling onto the ground in a heap.

“Are you okay?” Jermaine and Jack asked in unison, rushing to her side.

“Well, that wasn’t any fun,” Sarah said, trying to make a joke, though her face was pale and etched with pain. She looked up at her brother. “Able to help me up?”

“I can try,” Jack replied. He knelt down, and she grabbed his offered hands as he gently tugged, helping pull her back onto her feet.

“You sure you don’t need anything?” Jermaine asked, his eyes scanning her for any obvious injuries.

The moment she put weight on her left foot, a sharp, searing pain shot up her leg. She winced, quickly shifting her weight to her other foot. “I want to sit down,” she said, her voice tight. Both Jermaine and Jack immediately noticed the pronounced limp in her walk, and they quickly guided her to a nearby bench where she sank down with a sigh of relief.

She carefully examined her ankle, which was already starting to swell. “This doesn’t feel right,” she said, her voice barely a whisper.

“We should probably get it looked at,” Jermaine said decisively. He flagged down a passing zoo employee in a golf cart. “Excuse me,” he called out, his voice firm and clear. “My daughter fell, and I am asking for medical attention.”
