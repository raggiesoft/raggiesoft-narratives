# Part 3: A Misfired Warning

It was Tuesday afternoon, February 13th. The Fairlawn lobby was a cacophony of after-school energy. The gymnasium was filled with the thud of basketballs from Open Gym, a group fitness class was blasting upbeat music from a meeting room, and a steady stream of patrons chattered as they checked in. At the front desk, Mandy was the acting supervisor, while Jean and Susan handled the registers.

Jack was at the Scan Station, trying to focus. But today, the familiar sounds of the rec center felt different. They were sharp, grating, and overwhelming. The beep of his scanner felt like a nail being driven into his skull. The overhead fluorescent lights seemed to hum at a painful frequency, making him squint. He felt a profound, bone-deep weariness, and the low-grade sense of impending doom that had been his constant companion for days was now a loud, screaming alarm in his mind.

He saw Mandy, his coworker, wave to get his attention from her register, but her voice was lost in the overwhelming noise. He just shook his head, unable to process her question, and pressed the heels of his hands against his temples for a second. It wasn't enough. The sensory assault was too much, and his carefully constructed composure finally cracked. A choked sob escaped his throat, and hot, heavy tears began to track down his face as he crumbled, utterly overwhelmed.

Mandy saw it instantly. She recognized the signs of a full autistic meltdown—the flinching, the pained expression, and now, the uncontrollable tears. *It's that awful girl,* she thought, her heart aching with sympathy for him. *He's so stressed about her, he's at his breaking point.* Her first instinct, born of her secret crush, was to rush over and comfort him herself. She had seen the easy physical closeness he shared with his sister and yearned to offer that same comfort. But she immediately pushed the thought aside. This wasn't about her feelings. This was about Jack's needs, and she knew perfectly well that the person he needed right now was Sarah.

She grabbed the radio handset. "Front Desk to Aquatics," she said, her voice clear and urgent. "We have a Code Purple at the Front Desk."

Upstairs, on the pool deck, Sarah heard the call crackle over the radio she wore on her hip. Her blood ran cold. She didn't hesitate. She signaled to the next senior lifeguard. "You have the deck," she commanded, and was already moving, her wet flip-flops slapping against the tile as she ran for the ramp.

She arrived in the lobby moments later to see Jack sobbing at the Scan Station, with Mandy, Susan, and Jean forming a quiet, protective screen around him. Sarah immediately put herself between him and the flow of patrons, creating a physical barrier.

"Jack," she said, her voice low and steady. "I'm here. Let's go to the break room. Now."

He just nodded, unable to speak, and allowed her to guide him away from the noise and the lights, into the quiet sanctuary of the empty break room. As the door closed behind them, he collapsed into a chair, his body wracked with deep, tearing sobs. Sarah was there in an instant, wrapping her arms around him in a tight, grounding hug, her hand rubbing firm, steady circles on his back, desperately trying to calm him.

A moment later, the door opened quietly. It was Jennifer, his main supervisor, her face etched with concern. She saw Sarah holding her sobbing brother, assessed that the situation was being handled, and gave a single, supportive nod. "Take all the time you need, you two," she said softly, before closing the door, leaving them in privacy.

After a few more minutes, under the steady pressure of his sister's embrace, Jack's meltdown began to subside. His sobs lessened into shuddering breaths. He finally pulled back, wiping his eyes, looking utterly spent. "I'm sorry," he whispered. "It was just… too much. Too loud."

"It's okay," Sarah said, rubbing his back. "It's the stress, Jack. This whole thing with Delores is tearing you apart. But it's almost over. We'll deal with her, and this will stop."

He took a few more deep breaths, his body still trembling, but the worst of the storm had passed. "I think... I think I can go back out," he said, wanting the normalcy of his routine.

Sarah looked at him, her heart aching, but she respected his resolve. She gave him one last, tight hug. "Okay," she said. "Call me if you need me. For anything." She watched him leave the break room, then turned and headed back up the ramp to resume her own duties, the image of her brother's pale, tear-streaked face burned into her mind.
