# Part 2: Dinner with Robyn and Delores

Robyn returned from the kitchen bearing a steaming casserole dish, the scent of tomato and cheese filling the small dining area. As she served generous, perfectly rectangular portions onto their plates, Molly confirmed her suspicion. The layers were a little too uniform, the sauce a little too generic. It was definitely a high-quality frozen lasagna from ValueMart, heated and served as homemade. She took a bite, offering a polite smile. *Mental Note \#2: They lie about small, pointless things.*

“It’s very good, Robyn. Thank you,” Molly said, her voice neutral.

“Isn’t it?” Delores chimed in, beaming at Jack. “My mom’s lasagna is the best. She’s been making this recipe for years.”

“Well, it’s nice to have someone in this house who appreciates a home-cooked meal,” Robyn said, her voice sweet but with a sharp edge. Her gaze flicked toward Delores for a fraction of a second before settling back on Jack. The comment hung in the air, a clear but unspoken accusation.

Molly’s eyes narrowed slightly. *Mental Note \#3: There’s serious tension between them. That wasn't a joke.* Jack, oblivious to the undercurrent, simply took another bite of lasagna.

Robyn then turned her full, intense focus onto Jack. “And Jack, Delores tells me you work at Fairlawn. It’s good you have a steady job. Responsibility is so important in a young man. Some people just don't seem to understand that making foolish choices has consequences that can follow you for a very long time.” Again, her eyes flickered to Delores, a silent judgment passing between mother and daughter.

Molly saw it clearly this time. The subtext was thick with a history Molly didn't know, but its weight was palpable. Robyn wasn't just talking about a job; she was making a pointed, veiled reference to some significant, costly mistake from Delores's recent past. The barb was personal, and it was sharp. *Mental Note \#4: The mother is deeply angry at Delores about something that sounds like it cost a lot of money, and she's using Jack to punish her for it.*

Jack, caught in the middle, just felt a prickle of discomfort, unsure why the compliments felt so loaded. “It’s a good job,” he said simply.

Delores, ignoring the silent jab, doubled down on her praise for Jack, her laughter a little too loud. “He’s so responsible. Not like some people.”

The passive-aggressive volley was dizzying. The conversation continued in this vein—superficially pleasant but lacking any real depth. Delores talked about a professor she liked at VDU. Robyn asked Jack about the types of programs the rec center offered. Jack and Molly gave polite, brief answers, volunteering little. Every topic felt like a carefully managed performance by Delores and her mother, designed to present a picture of perfect, unthreatening normalcy. Molly continued to observe, her initial unease solidifying with every rehearsed question and overly bright smile. This wasn’t a family dinner; it was an audition, and she was watching a silent, ugly war play out right in front of her.
