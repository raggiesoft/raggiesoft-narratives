The dinner conversation remained superficially pleasant, but Molly felt like she was watching a play. Every word from Delores and Robyn felt rehearsed, every question designed to gather information.

“We were thinking of going to that new sci-fi movie next weekend,” Molly said, turning to Jack with a familiar, easy smile. “All four of us, if you’re not working.”

“Oh, that’s so sweet that you’re all still so close,” Delores interjected, her voice dripping with artificial sugariness. As she spoke, she placed her hand on Jack’s forearm, a gesture that looked like affection but felt like an anchor. “It’s important for a man to have his own life, though. Not to just be tied to his family all the time.”

The jab was subtle but clear. Jack tensed at her touch, a flash of annoyance cutting through his polite demeanor. Molly’s smile tightened. *Mental Note \#5: She’s possessive and actively belittles his family bond.*

After the meal, Molly stood up. “Thank you for dinner, Robyn. It was lovely. But I should get going.” She turned to Jack. “Text me whenever you’re ready to be picked up, okay?”

“Will do,” Jack said, standing to walk her to the door.

In the entryway, Molly gave her cousin a firm, comforting hug. “Call if you need anything,” she whispered. “Anything at all.” Over Jack’s shoulder, she saw Delores watching them, her polite smile frozen, but her eyes were cold and hard.

As Molly walked to her car, her instincts screamed. The passive-aggressive digs from Robyn, Delores's possessive comment about their family, the frozen stare during their goodbye hug—it was all wrong. *She’s not leaving him here,* Molly decided. She drove down the block, parked where she had a clear view of the apartment building’s entrance, and settled in to wait.

Back inside, Jack returned to the living room to find Robyn staring at him. Her expression had shifted. Her eyes softened in a way that was deeply strange, her lips parting in a small, familiar smile that felt entirely inappropriate. She was looking at him, but as if she were picturing his father. It made his skin crawl.

“I know your father, Jermaine, from work, you know,” she said, her voice taking on a cooing quality. “Same employer, different offices. I heard through the grapevine about his difficult divorce.” She leaned forward, her gaze unwavering and unnervingly intimate. Robyn was still seething over the trouble Delores had brought to their door—the screaming phone calls from her sister, the humiliation, and most of all, the massive, unpayable debt Delores now owed to that Porter family after the disastrous trip to Washington state. But looking at Jack, a new, opportunistic thought took root. A single man, a good job… maybe *he* was the solution. Or rather, his father was. If Jermaine was lonely, maybe he’d be grateful for some company, grateful enough to help with a little financial trouble… a meal ticket.

“How is he *really* doing?” she pressed. “It must be so hard on him, being all alone now.”

“He’s getting by,” Jack said, his answer clipped. He felt a desperate need to protect his father’s privacy from this strange, invasive questioning.

“You have your father’s eyes,” Robyn continued, her voice a low purr. “He always has such a kind, handsome look about him at those company functions. A man like that… he shouldn’t be alone for long. Has he… started to get out at all? Seeing people?”

The questions were too personal, too invasive. And the way she was looking at him, with that strange, lovesick expression, was profoundly creepy. Delores watched them from the sofa, a small, knowing smirk on her face, enjoying his discomfort. This wasn’t a date; it was an interrogation with a bizarre, unsettling agenda. He felt the overwhelming urge to leave.
