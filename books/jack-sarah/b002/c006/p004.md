The questions were too personal, too invasive. The way Robyn was looking at him with that strange, lovesick expression was profoundly creepy. And Delores, watching from the sofa with a small, knowing smirk, was enjoying his discomfort. It was in that moment that another detail from earlier clicked into place for Jack: the cold, hard look in Delores’s eyes when he had hugged Molly goodbye. It hadn't just been a fleeting expression; it was a flash of raw, possessive anger. *Mental Note: She hates it when I hug my cousins.*

That was it. He was done. The creepy interrogation from the mother and the memory of his girlfriend's silent rage were the final red flags he needed.

“Wow, look at the time,” Jack said, forcing a casual tone as he stood up. “I should probably get going.”

“Oh, don’t be silly,” Delores said, moving from her chair to block his path to the door. Her hand rested on his arm. Internally, she was seething. *The way that girl hugged him, so familiar. Like she owned him. He’s mine.* But her voice was a soft purr. “Mom’s just being nosy. Don’t mind her. The night's still young.”

Jack felt a prickle of panic. He gently removed her hand. “No, really, I should go.”

“Please, Jack?” Delores said, her voice dropping, her expression shifting to one of profound hurt. “Don’t let my weird mom scare you off. I’m so sorry about that. I promise, I’ll never go too fast for you. I’ll go at whatever pace you’re comfortable with.”

Her promise felt wrong, like another line from a script. His gut told him to run. He pulled out his phone, turning away from her slightly, and sent a quick text to Molly: Ready now.

Delores saw the motion, and her eyes narrowed. “Are you texting your cousin to come get you? Already?” Her voice was no longer soft; it was wounded and accusatory.

Caught, Jack stammered, “Uh, yeah, she’s just waiting…”

“Wow,” Delores said, taking a step back and wrapping her arms around herself as if she were cold. “Okay. I get it. My mom was weird, and I was weird, and now you’re bailing. It’s… it’s actually exactly what my ex used to do.”

The guilt trip was so sudden and sharp it felt like a physical blow. Jack froze.

“Anytime things got a little complicated or weren't perfect,” she continued, her voice now thick with unshed tears, “he’d just shut down and run. He couldn’t handle it. I really, truly thought you were different, Jack. Kinder.”

The accusation hit him precisely where she intended. He hated the idea of being like some cruel ex. His entire being recoiled from the thought of causing someone pain. His own instincts were screaming that *he* was the one in danger, but her words twisted his self-protective impulse into an act of cruelty.

Just then, the apartment’s buzzer rang, a sharp, insistent sound. Molly was downstairs.

Delores flinched at the sound, a single tear tracing a path down her cheek. “Please,” she whispered, her voice breaking. “I know tonight was a disaster. My mom ruined it. Just… one more chance. Not here. A walk in the park. In the daytime. Just to prove to you I’m not… this. Please don’t let me think all guys are the same.”

The buzzer rang again. Molly was waiting. Delores was crying. Jack felt a dizzying wave of guilt and confusion. The request was so small. A walk. To refuse now would be a deliberate, final act of hurting her. He couldn't do it. It wasn't in him.

“Okay,” he breathed, the word feeling like a surrender. “One walk. Next week.”

He fled the apartment, Delores’s whispered “Thank you” following him down the hall. He felt her eyes on him the whole way, a mixture of victory and possession that he was too flustered to fully register.
