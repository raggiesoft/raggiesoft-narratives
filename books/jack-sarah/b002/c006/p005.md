The buzzer rang, a sharp, insistent sound that cut through the thick, manipulative atmosphere of the apartment. Molly was here. A wave of immense, profound relief washed over Jack, so potent it almost made him dizzy.

Delores flinched at the sound, her performance kicking into high gear as a single, perfect crocodile tear traced a path down her cheek. "Please," she whispered, her voice breaking. "I know tonight was a disaster. My mom ruined it. Just… one more chance. Not here. A walk in the park. In the daytime. Just to prove to you I’m not… this. Please don’t let me think all guys are the same."

The buzzer rang again, a welcome, insistent call from the real world. Molly was waiting. Delores was crying. Internally, Delores was seething with a white-hot fury. Her plan was in ruins. She had wanted him in her bed, vulnerable and obligated. She had wanted a repeat of the dorm room, but with a different outcome. She had failed. This pathetic, tearful plea was her last-ditch effort to keep him on the hook.

Jack, trapped between his own kindness and the waiting headlights of his escape, felt his resolve crumble. The request was so small. A walk. To refuse now would be a deliberate, final act of hurting her. He couldn't do it.

“We’ll talk next week, okay?” he heard himself say, the words a concession he immediately regretted.

The change was instantaneous. The moment the words left his mouth, the tears in her eyes simply vanished. The wellspring of her sadness dried up the second she got what she wanted. She gave a single, delicate sniffle for effect, but the performance was over.

“Okay,” she whispered, her voice suddenly steady. She leaned in, and before he could react, she gave him a quick, chaste, and completely calculated kiss on the cheek. She pulled back and opened the door for him. “Drive safe.”

She closed the door before he could respond, leaving him on the stoop, confused and unsettled. The abrupt shift from heartbroken tears to calm composure didn't fully register in his flustered mind, but it added to the deep, gut-level feeling that everything he had just witnessed was a lie.

He practically ran to Molly’s waiting car, pulling the door open and collapsing into the passenger seat. He was so glad she had waited, so happy to see her familiar, safe face. He pulled her into a fierce hug, a gesture of pure relief.

“Whoa,” Molly said, hugging him back tightly. “You okay? I had a feeling you might want to leave sooner rather than later.”

“You have no idea,” Jack breathed, finally feeling like he could breathe again. “Let’s just go home.”
