The clock on the wall read 9:00 AM sharp. The doors of Fairlawn Recreation Center slid open, and the Sunday morning rush began.

## Downstairs: Jack at the Scan Station

Jack took his position at the Scan Station, a familiar post he knew well. To his right, the three cash registers were staffed for the morning rush: Donald (next to Jack) on the right register, then Mandy next to Donald at the middle register. The morning unfolded in a familiar, rhythmic blur. The first hour flew by in a wave of families arriving for the Sunday morning Open Swim and groups of adults heading to the gymnasium. Jack greeted each one, his hands and mouth on autopilot while his mind replayed the conversation with Sarah in the car. The vague sense of doom he’d felt all morning was a low, persistent hum beneath the surface of his professional calm. He overheard a group of teenagers complaining to Mandy at the register. "What do you mean there's no basketball?" one of them grumbled. Mandy just offered a polite, practiced smile. "I'm sorry, we haven't had open gym basketball on Sundays for over a year now. We have volleyball and badminton available until this afternoon. The equipment is in the gym." The teens grumbled but headed towards the gym, a scene Jack and the other front desk staff were all too familiar with.

## Upstairs: Sarah in the Natatorium

As the acting Aquatics Supervisor, Sarah commanded the pool deck. The vast, 8-lane, 25-meter natatorium was her domain, and she moved through it with a quiet, confident authority. With the pool now open to the public for

Open Swim, the deck was a cheerful chaos of splashing children and watchful parents. Sarah wasn't in one of the high stands; she was on the deck, her eyes constantly scanning, not just her zone, but her entire team's. She made a subtle hand signal to a younger lifeguard, reminding him to keep his head on a swivel. Her leadership was quiet but absolute. She had seen how tired Jack looked that morning, and it bothered her more than she let on.

## 10:30 AM

The initial rushes began to subside, leaving a steady hum of activity. Downstairs, Jack felt a wave of dizziness as he stood up to stretch his back, leaning heavily on the counter for a moment. Upstairs, Sarah was walking the perimeter of the pool, her focus absolute, a necessary shield against the worry for her brother that still lingered at the back of her mind.
