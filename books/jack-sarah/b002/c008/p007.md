# Part 7: Peking Duck Inn

The Peking Duck Inn was a riot of festive color and cheerful noise on Sunday, February 11th. Simon, the owner, had decorated the day before for the upcoming Chinese New Year, and vibrant red lanterns and gold good luck symbols hung from the ceiling, casting a warm, celebratory glow over the bustling dining room.

The entire Bennett clan arrived together after Jack and Sarah’s long shift at the rec center. It was the first time Jermaine, Devin, and Sam would meet Delores. Simon, a kind man in his late 50s with a warm smile and a hint of a British Hong Kong accent, greeted them at the door like family.

"Jermaine! Devin! Sam! So good to see you all!" he boomed, before his eyes fell on Jack. "And Jack, my boy, you look tired! Long day at the rec?"

"You know it, Simon," Jack said with a weary smile. He then gestured to the young woman standing awkwardly beside him. "Simon, this is my girlfriend, Delores. Delores, this is our good friend, Simon. He owns the place."

"A pleasure to meet you, Delores," Simon said with a polite, professional smile, though his experienced gaze took in the subtle tension around her. He then led them to a pair of rectangular tables he had already pushed together in the center of the room.

The seating arrangement was a subtle, coordinated dance. The four cousins took one side, with Jack and Sarah in the middle, flanked by Molly and Clare. On the other side, Jermaine sat in the middle of his father and brother. This left one open seat, directly across from Molly, for Delores. The family had their analyst in the perfect position to observe.

Simon returned to the table, his pen and pad ready. "The usual for everyone?" he asked the Bennett clan.

"You know it, Simon," Jermaine said with a grateful smile.

Simon then turned to Delores, a single, elegant menu in his hand. "And for you, miss? Since it's your first time here, please, take a look."

Delores just stared, bewildered. *Doesn't he have menus for them?* she thought, looking at the family as they all nodded in agreement. *How do they even know what they want?*

The family conversation flowed freely as they waited for their food, a comfortable and chaotic mix of work stories, jokes about the upcoming move, and plans for Sarah's birthday. Delores sat in a pocket of resentful silence, a clear outsider to their easy intimacy.

She shifted constantly in her chair, the hard wood of the seat aggravating the dull, heavy ache that had started in her lower back an hour ago. She crossed and uncrossed her legs, trying to find a position that didn't make her want to scream. She felt bloated, cramped, and cornered.

It was Devin who unintentionally lit the fuse, trying to be polite. "So, Delores," he asked, "Jack mentioned you’re a student at VDU. Do you have any siblings?"

Delores froze, a flicker of something dark and panicked in her eyes. A sharp cramp seized her abdomen, forcing a grimace she tried to hide behind her water glass. "No," she said, a little too quickly. "I'm an only child."

The lie was sharp and sudden, but plausible enough. However, a few moments later, Jermaine, trying again to include her, asked, "So growing up, was it just you and your parents then?"

The physical pain flared again, syncing perfectly with her irritation at the intrusive questions. "No!" Delores snapped, the word exploding out of her louder than she intended as she shifted abruptly to relieve the pressure.

"I mean, yes. It's just... it's complicated. It’s hard when you have people who are always interfering, always judging you, like my last boyfriend's precious sisters, or... or like..." She stopped abruptly, her eyes wide.

She had been about to say, "...or like my brother," a reference to Dan, whose existence she had just denied. She had come dangerously close to revealing the entire pattern—her resentment of Leo's sisters, the existence of Dan, her history of targeting men with close female relatives. She had almost spilled her entire dirty secret right there on the table.

She caught herself, a flush rising on her cheeks. "I mean... my last relationship was just very difficult," she finished lamely.

A profound, telling silence fell over the Bennett side of the table. Every single one of them—from Sam's wise, old eyes to Molly's sharp, analytical gaze—had heard it. The slip. The raw, venomous anger in her voice when she mentioned "precious sisters." The way she had stumbled, catching herself just before revealing too much. They didn't know the name Leo Porter, they didn't know about Dan, but they all recognized the sound of a carefully constructed lie falling apart. It was a massive, collective red flag.

Molly, watching from directly across the table, had noticed the physical tell, too. The wince before the snap. The constant shifting. *She’s uncomfortable,* Molly noted, filing the data away. *She's in pain, and she's losing control of her temper because of it.*

The conversation after that was polite, but the dynamic had irrevocably shifted. The family continued to talk freely amongst themselves, their laughter and easy banter a wall that Delores could not penetrate. When they did address her, their questions were clipped, their responses short. Delores could feel it. She was on very, very thin ice, and she had no one to blame but herself.
