# Part 2: A Sunday Morning at Fairlawn

At 7:30 AM on the dot, Sarah swiped her employee badge, and the side door of the quiet, sleeping building clicked open. In the dim light of the lobby, she and Jack paused.

"Okay," she said, turning to him. "Have a good shift. Try not to miss me too much."

"I'll try," Jack said with a small smile, pulling her into a hug. "You too. Don't be too bossy up there."

"No promises," she replied, hugging him back before heading towards the massive concrete spiral ramp to begin her long day on the second floor. Jack made his way to the downstairs break room in the administration wing to wait for his own 8:45 AM start time.

## Upstairs: Sarah's Meticulous Routine (7:45 AM)

The moment Sarah clocked in and stepped onto the pool deck, a switch flipped. She was no longer just Jack's sister; she was the acting Aquatics Supervisor for the day, and the vast, 25-meter, 8-lane natatorium was her responsibility. The air was still and humid, the water a perfect, undisturbed sheet of turquoise under the low maintenance lights. She felt a familiar mix of nervous excitement and profound competence. This was her first time in charge, and she was determined to do it right.

She wasn't alone. Two of her most trusted senior lifeguards, Maria and David, clocked in with her, forming her core opening crew.

"Alright, team," Sarah said, her voice clear and confident, echoing slightly in the empty space. "Let's get this place ready for the public. David, you start the deck walk—check all the drains, lane lines, and make sure the rescue equipment is properly placed. Maria, you're on the safety kit and spineboard check. I'm going to run the water chemistry."

Her first task, as always, was the most critical. She moved with a practiced efficiency, taking water samples from multiple points in the massive pool. Back in the aquatics office, she meticulously conducted the tests—chlorine, pH, alkalinity—her movements precise and focused. The familiar, scientific routine was a comfort, a pocket of predictable order in a day that promised to be emotionally complicated. With the levels logged and confirmed to be perfect, she moved on to double-check the pump room readings.

By 8:30 AM, the atmosphere began to change as the next wave of four lifeguards trickled in, their cheerful chatter filling the break room as they got changed and ready for the day. Sarah gathered them all on the pool deck for a pre-shift briefing.

"Morning, everyone," she began, clipboard in hand. "It's Sunday, so expect a heavy family crowd right from the start. Rotations are on the board. Maria, you're on the main stand first. Let's make sure our scanning is sharp today. Stay hydrated, and call for a backup if you need one. Let's have a safe shift."

At 8:45 AM, the final two guards for the morning shift arrived, completing her team. The natatorium lights hummed to life, bringing the room to its full, bright intensity. The water sparkled. At 9:00 AM on the dot, the first rotation of guards were in their stands, rescue tubes across their laps, their gazes already methodically scanning their zones of coverage. Sarah stood on the deck, taking it all in—her team, her pool, her responsibility. With a final, satisfied nod, she picked up her radio.

"Aquatics to Front Desk. The deck is clear. You can open the doors."

## Downstairs: Jack's Quiet Hour (7:45 AM)

Jack settled into one of the uncomfortable plastic chairs in the empty break room. The silence of the building was a stark contrast to the loud, anxious thoughts churning in his mind. He went through his familiar routine, stowing his lunch in the fridge and filling his water bottle, but the small tasks did little to quell the feeling of dread about the dinner planned for that night. His stomach was in knots.

He sank back into the chair, a bone-deep weariness settling over him that felt heavier than just a poor night's sleep. *Probably just stress,* he thought, rubbing his temples. *Worrying about tonight is draining.* He felt a vague but heavy sense of doom that had been clinging to him for the past day, a feeling that something terrible was on the horizon. *The dinner,* he told himself firmly. *It's just the dinner.*

He pulled a well-worn science fiction paperback from his bag: *The Destruction of Veridia Prime*. He opened it to his bookmarked page, hoping to lose himself in the story. He was at the part where the hero twins, Kael and Lyra, having survived the annihilation of their world and a desperate firefight, had finally collapsed onto the deck of the freighter, the *Stargazer*. He read about their exhaustion, their grief, and their profound relief at finally reaching a safe harbor. The story of their escape resonated deeply, a mirror of his own desperate need to find a sanctuary from Delores. But the words blurred on the page. His mind kept drifting back to her fake, cloying smile and the look of cold fury he’d seen in her eyes.

He sighed, giving up on reading. The anxiety was too much. Instead, he pulled out his MP3 player, slipping on his headphones. He scrolled to one of his favorite bands, Origin, and selected the gentle, reassuring melody of "Wherever You Go". He turned up the volume, letting the song's promise of unwavering loyalty create a protective barrier, a shield against his own churning thoughts. He closed his eyes, completely unaware that he was trying to block out not just his thoughts, but the first, faint, desperate warning signals from his own body.

## 8:30 AM

Upstairs, the first of Sarah's lifeguards began to arrive. She held a quick, professional briefing on the pool deck, outlining the rotation schedule and reminding them to be extra vigilant on a busy Sunday. Her voice was confident and clear, the perfect picture of a supervisor in command.

Downstairs, at 8:30 AM, Jack saw Donald, his shift supervisor for the day, walk into the break room and give him a friendly nod. It was almost time. He took off his headphones, the comforting melody of Origin's "Wherever You Go" fading away, and mentally prepared himself for the social demands of the front desk. The last few minutes of his quiet solitude ticked away. The dread about the evening's dinner remained, a cold stone in the pit of his stomach.

At precisely 8:45 AM, Jack clocked in. Donald met him by the main office door. “Ready for your crash course in opening procedures, Bennett?”

“Ready as I’ll ever be,” Jack replied.

Donald led him to the office safe. “Okay, first things first. We take out all the bags. Change Purse, and registers one through four. They all get counted, every day, no exceptions.”

They laid the five canvas bags on the counter. “Each one needs to have exactly one hundred dollars to start,” Donald explained, his movements practiced and efficient. “Bags one through four should have fifty in ones and fifty in fives, if you can. Makes breaking twenties easier.” He opened the Change Purse and frowned. “Well, that’s not ideal. Just five twenties. Someone on closing shift last night was lazy. Means we’ll have no way of breaking larger bills until we build up some change. We'll have to ask patrons for smaller bills if we can.”

For the next ten minutes, Donald walked Jack through the meticulous process. They counted and verified each bag. Donald then had Jack sign the logbook under his own signature, a small but significant act of trusting him with the responsibility. They returned the Change Purse and the spare Bag 4 to the safe, then headed out to the front desk with the other three bags, loading the cash drawers into the three registers.

“Alright, last step before we open,” Donald said, pointing to the two large monitors mounted behind the desk. He showed Jack how to turn them on and load the day’s event schedule. The screens flickered to life, displaying the day's events: a child's birthday party in Room 1, a baby shower in the combined Rooms 3 & 4, and a "Pool Blast" party in the combined Rooms 5 & 6.

At 8:59 AM, Sarah’s voice came over the radio, crisp and professional. "Aquatics to Front Desk. The deck is clear. You can open the doors."

Jack, standing near the main radio, picked up the receiver. “10-4, Aquatics. Front desk is ready. Facility Supervisor is opening the doors now.”

The day began. The usual Sunday morning crowd flowed in. At 9:00 AM sharp, Mandy arrived, smiling a quick good morning before heading to the middle register to count her drawer. Donald took his position at the register on the right, next to the Scan Station, and Jack settled in at his spot at the Scan Station, the familiar rhythm of the job a fragile shield against his inner turmoil.
