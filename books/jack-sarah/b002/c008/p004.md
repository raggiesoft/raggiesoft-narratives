The morning rush at Fairlawn eventually gave way to the steadier, calmer rhythm of a Sunday afternoon. At 1:23 PM, during a quiet lull, the front desk radio crackled to life, the single channel shared by the whole facility carrying Sarah's familiar voice. "Aquatics to Front Desk."

Donald, the acting supervisor, picked up the receiver. "Go for the desk."

"Hey, Donald," Sarah's voice came through, "I'm about to take my thirty-minute lunch. Is Jack available to join me?"

Donald glanced at Jack, who nodded eagerly. It was a familiar routine for the siblings to try and coordinate their breaks when they could. "Sure, he can join you," Donald replied into the radio. "We're not that busy. He's all yours."

With his supervisor's approval, Jack then picked up the radio himself. "Hey, Sarah," he said, his voice a little weary, "can you meet me downstairs in the main break room? I'm feeling a bit exhausted."

There was no hesitation. "10-4, Jack," she replied, the professional radio lingo a familiar comfort. "I'm on my way."

A few minutes later, Sarah emerged from the second-floor ramp. As she approached the desk, Jack caught Donald's eye and gestured that he was taking his break. They walked to the time clock together, clocking out at 1:30 PM on the dot for their unpaid break.

In the break room, Jack sank into one of the chairs with a heavy sigh, pulling out the lunch he had packed that morning. Sarah sat across from him, her own lunch in hand, and for a few moments, they ate in a comfortable, familiar silence. It was Sarah who noticed it first. Her brother, who had seemed merely anxious and preoccupied that morning, now looked utterly, profoundly exhausted. There were dark circles under his eyes that hadn't been there a few hours ago, and his movements were slow, as if his limbs were weighted down.

“You okay, Jack?” she asked, her voice soft with concern. “You look completely wiped out.”

“I’m fine,” he said, but the words lacked conviction. “Just… really tired today, I guess.” He paused, looking at his sister. “And I just feel… weird. Anxious. I can't shake it.”

Sarah’s expression softened with empathy. Of course he was anxious. They both were. “It’s Delores, isn’t it?” she said, her voice full of understanding. “This whole situation is a mess. It’s completely normal that you’d be feeling anxious and drained before the dinner tonight.”

Jack nodded, latching onto the logical explanation. It had to be Delores. The stress was just getting to him.

Just then, Jean, the young part-time cashier, came into the break room to grab a drink from the vending machine. She offered them a shy smile. "Hey, you two." As she passed their table, she also noticed the deep weariness on Jack's face. He looked pale, almost grey, under the fluorescent lights. Her smile faltered into a look of concern, a silent echo of Sarah's own worry. She didn't say anything, not wanting to intrude, but she made a mental note. Jack did not look well at all.

Sarah watched her brother take a slow, weary bite of his sandwich. Her protective instincts, always on high alert, were now humming with a low-grade anxiety. His exhaustion seemed to go deeper than just a long week.

“Hey,” she said, making a decision. “I know you were going to drive us to dinner tonight, but you look like you could fall asleep right here. How about I drive instead?”

A wave of profound relief washed over Jack’s face. “Yeah?” he asked, his voice full of gratitude. “That… that would be great, actually. Thanks, Sarah.”

“Of course,” she said, her concern deepening. He had agreed without any of his usual token protests. He was really, truly, spent. They finished the rest of their thirty-minute break, the unspoken worry hanging in the air between them. They clocked back in together and walked back towards the front desk. Sarah gave his arm a final, reassuring squeeze before heading back up the ramp to the pool deck, leaving Jack to finish the last few hours of his shift, unaware that his body was already in the midst of a quiet, desperate battle.
