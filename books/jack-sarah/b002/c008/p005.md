# Part 5: Trial by Fecal Contamination

At 2:00 PM, Sarah returned to the humid warmth of the natatorium, feeling a little more settled after the quiet lunch with Jack. Her shift was halfway over, and everything was running smoothly on her first day as the acting Aquatics Supervisor. She began her rounds, checking in with the lifeguards on the stands.

At 2:04 PM, the entire facility's shared radio channel crackled to life, the sound sharp and urgent. "Stand Three to Sarah! We have a Code Brown in the shallow end! Code Brown!"

Downstairs at the front desk, Jack, Donald, and Jean all looked up from their tasks, their expressions instantly serious. They knew what that meant.

A moment later, a second, more panicked voice erupted from the radio. "Stand Two to Sarah! We have a Code Yellow at the source of the Code Brown! Repeat, we have a Code Yellow as well!"

Upstairs, Sarah’s Head Lifeguard training kicked into high gear. She was already moving, her voice ringing with a calm authority that cut through the chaos. "Everybody out of the water!" she commanded. "The pool is now closed!"

She quickly made her way to the source of the problem. A young child had gotten sick, and it was a worst-case scenario: a simultaneous vomit and diarrhea incident. After a quick assessment, she knew this wasn't a one-hour problem. This was a "shut it down" problem, requiring a full hyperchlorination process that would take over 12 hours.

She picked up her own radio, her voice firm and decisive, formalizing what the front desk already suspected. "Aquatics to Front Desk."

A familiar, steady voice came back instantly. "Go for the desk, Sarah. It's Jack. We heard the calls. What's the official word?"

Relief washed over her for a fraction of a second. "Jack, be advised, the natatorium is closed for the remainder of the day due to a major fecal incident. I repeat, the pool is closed for the rest of the day."

"10-4," Jack's voice replied, all business. He immediately relayed the message to Donald and Jean, who began the process of informing patrons and placing "Pool Closed" signs. Then, per city policy, Jack picked up the desk phone. He first called the Windsor Woods Recreation Center, then the Red Mill Recreation Center—the two facilities closest to Fairlawn. He quickly informed their front desks of the closure, knowing they would appreciate the heads-up so they wouldn't send any patrons looking for a pool their way. It was a proactive, considerate move, a small act of support for his sister on a very difficult day.

Upstairs, Sarah turned to face the line of disappointed patrons. To her profound relief, the grumbling was minimal. The patrons were disappointed, certainly, but they were also understanding. They recognized that safety had to come first, and none of them were angry at Sarah or her lifeguards for making the correct call.

It was a true trial by fire on her first day as acting supervisor, and she had met it head-on. With the public cleared, Sarah and her team spent the next two hours on the grueling decontamination process. Once the chemicals were in, their active work was done. She gathered her lifeguards in the aquatics office.

"Alright, team," she said, her voice full of the calm authority she had projected all day. "There's nothing more we can do up here. The pool's down for the count. For those of you who want to go home early, you're dismissed for the day. If anyone needs to finish their hours, I'll call the other centers and find a place for you."
