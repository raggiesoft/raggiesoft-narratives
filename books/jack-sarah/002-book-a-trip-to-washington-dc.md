# Book: A Trip to Washington DC

## Chapter 1: A Healing Journey

### Part 1: A New Beginning in Fredericksburg

The drive to Fredericksburg had been a quiet one, a conscious decision by Jermaine to avoid the dreaded Washington, D.C. traffic. He had driven the I-395 corridor and crossed the 14th Street Bridge countless times in his past, and each time had been a masterclass in stress and gridlock. For this trip, a trip meant to start the healing process for his family after his recent divorce, he wanted things to be different, easier. A hotel in Fredericksburg, just a short walk from the Caroline Street train station, was the perfect solution.

He pulled the Stark Paladin into the hotel parking lot and turned to his two teenagers in the back seat. “Alright, kids. We’re here.”

He headed into the lobby with Jack and Sarah trailing behind him. “Reservation for Jermaine Bennett,” he said to the clerk at the front desk. He then looked at his children, who were standing awkwardly, unsure of what to do. “You two go have a seat. This shouldn’t take too long.”

The two of them walked over to a sofa and sat together.

“This is going to be a wonderful spring break together. What are you looking forward to?” Sarah asked Jack, her voice a little brighter than she felt, a conscious effort to embrace their father's gesture.

“Air and Space Museum,” he giggled, a spark of genuine excitement in his eyes. Astronomy was one of his favorite subjects, and the thought of the bigger museum was a welcome distraction. He asked his sister, “What are you looking forward to?”

“The zoo,” she replied. “All those animals.”

Jack thought about it and decided, “The zoo sounds like fun too!” He started thinking about the reptile house and how he wanted to see the snakes. He planned on teasing his sister in the reptile house, but he kept quiet about it. He didn’t want to spoil the plans.

Their father came over, the room keys in his hand. “Let’s get our things and head to the room. You two are going to have to share a bed and I will be in the other bed. No fighting or complaining please.”

“Why would I complain about this dork? She kept me going when mommy cheated on you,” Jack said, wrapping his sister in a playful, sideways hug.

As his arm tightened around her, Sarah realized that this hug, coming at the end of such a painful chapter for their family, felt different. In that moment, she made a decision: she didn’t just like it when Jack hugged her; she absolutely *loved* it. She leaned into him a little bit, a silent, wordless communication that she wanted more of his hugs, a silent thank you for being her rock.

### Part 2: The First Night

The hotel room was simple, clean, and dominated by two large queen beds. Jermaine dropped his bag by the bed closest to the window, a silent claim. “Alright, I’m going to grab a shower,” he announced, his voice weary from the drive and the emotional weight of the last few months. “You two get settled.”

As their father disappeared into the bathroom, Jack and Sarah began to unpack their own small bags by the other bed. The unspoken arrangement hung in the air: this would be their bed for the trip. Jack was quiet, seemingly lost in his own world as he arranged his things, but Sarah was acutely aware of him. The memory of the hug in the lobby was still a warm, solid presence in her mind. It was a small, simple moment, but it had changed something for her. She was genuinely looking forward to climbing into bed with him, to the uncomplicated comfort and safety of his presence beside her.

A little while later, all three of them were in their pajamas. Jermaine was already in his bed, reading a book under the dim light of the bedside lamp.

“Okay, dork,” Sarah said to Jack with a playful smile, pulling back the covers on their bed. “Time to hit the hay. We’ve got a big day tomorrow.”

Jack climbed in, and Sarah slid in beside him, clicking off the main lights so only their father’s small lamp remained. The room fell into a comfortable, quiet darkness. As she settled under the covers, she felt Jack shift. He rolled onto his side, his back to her, but she could feel the quiet tension in his shoulders. Without a word, she moved a little closer, draping an arm gently over his side and pulling him into a soft, comforting cuddle. She felt him relax instantly, his body melting into her embrace with a quiet sigh of relief.

He was her brother, her rock through the storm of their parents’ divorce. And as she held him in the quiet darkness of the hotel room, she knew she was his rock, too.

### Part 3: The Journey In

The morning started with the chaotic, yet comforting, ritual of a hotel continental breakfast. With their bellies full, they bundled up against the crisp spring air and began the short, pleasant walk from their hotel to the Caroline Street Station. At the station, Jermaine confidently navigated the ticketing process, purchasing three round-trip tickets on the Dominion Rail for their journey straight into the heart of Washington, D.C..

They found their seats on the train, a comfortable 2x2 layout. The siblings, Jack and Sarah, slid into a pair of seats by the window, while Jermaine took a seat directly across the aisle from them. As the train pulled smoothly out of the station, Jermaine leaned forward.

“Okay, so, first day,” he said, a genuine smile on his face. “What’s the plan, team? Jack, I know you’re dying to see the Air and Space Museum.”

“Definitely!” Jack said, his excitement bubbling up. “I want to see the old planes and the space capsules.”

“And the zoo for me,” Sarah added. “We have to see the pandas.”

“Alright, here’s what I’m thinking,” Jermaine said, pulling a small map from his jacket pocket. “We’ll get off at L’Enfant, which is super close to the National Mall. We can spend the whole morning at Air and Space, let you get your fill of rockets, Jack. Then, after lunch, we can hop on the Capital Metro and head up to the zoo for the afternoon. How does that sound?”

“Perfect!” they both said in unison.

The train ride was smooth and comfortable, the Virginia countryside rolling past their window. As they got closer to the city, the train began to parallel the infamous I-395 corridor. A little while later, the train slowed as it approached the Long Bridge, the massive steel artery that would carry them over the Potomac River.

“Look,” Jermaine said, pointing out the window to their left.

Jack and Sarah peered out. Parallel to their own tracks was the 14th Street Bridge. It was a parking lot. Thousands of cars were crammed together, barely moving, a river of frustrated commuters inching their way into the city. Horns blared faintly, a distant, angry sound.

“And *that*,” Jermaine said with a grim satisfaction, “is why we are sitting here, relaxed and moving, and not in the car. I’ve spent more hours of my life than I care to admit stuck in that exact spot.”

Just then, their own train picked up speed, zooming past the gridlock with a satisfying whoosh. Jack and Sarah looked at each other, a shared, silent understanding passing between them. Their dad had made a very, very good decision.

A little while later, the automated voice announced their destination: “Next stop, L'Enfant. This is the L'Enfant station.”

The train slowed and pulled into the open-air platform. They got off, and the sheer scale of the city was immediately apparent. They walked from the platform up a short flight of stairs to the main plaza, emerging directly into the bright morning sunlight and the bustle of the city. The massive, impressive structure of the National Air and Space Museum was just a short walk away, ready to begin the first adventure of their trip. There had been no need to transfer, no confusing subway maps to navigate—just one simple, direct trip.

### Part 4: Sarah’s Incident

The afternoon sun was warm, and the sounds of the zoo—a cacophony of animal calls, laughing children, and the general murmur of the crowd—filled the air. After a delightful morning at the Air and Space Museum, the Bennetts had taken the Capital Metro up to the zoo. The three of them were now walking along a winding, paved path that descended a surprisingly steep hill toward the lower valley of the park.

Sarah, wearing a pair of stylish but impractical sandals, found her footing becoming less and less certain on the incline. The steepness of the hill was something she wasn’t used to, and her ankles began to ache with the effort of keeping her balance. A wave of dizziness washed over her. “I think I need to sit down,” she said, her voice a little breathless as she came to a stop.

“Let’s look for a bench or something,” Jermaine replied, his expression immediately shifting to one of fatherly concern.

Jack, noticing his sister’s struggle, moved closer to her side. “Need a shoulder to lean on?” he offered gently.

“Please,” she replied, grateful. Jack positioned himself so she could drape an arm over his shoulders, and he put a supportive arm around her waist. They began to make their way slowly down the hill together, a careful, coordinated effort. But a few steps later, Sarah’s sandal caught on an uneven patch of pavement. They started to stumble; Sarah lost her grip on his shoulder, and with a small cry of surprise, she found herself tumbling onto the ground in a heap.

“Are you okay?” Jermaine and Jack asked in unison, rushing to her side.

“Well, that wasn’t any fun,” Sarah said, trying to make a joke, though her face was pale and etched with pain. She looked up at her brother. “Able to help me up?”

“I can try,” Jack replied. He knelt down, and she grabbed his offered hands as he gently tugged, helping pull her back onto her feet.

“You sure you don’t need anything?” Jermaine asked, his eyes scanning her for any obvious injuries.

The moment she put weight on her left foot, a sharp, searing pain shot up her leg. She winced, quickly shifting her weight to her other foot. “I want to sit down,” she said, her voice tight. Both Jermaine and Jack immediately noticed the pronounced limp in her walk, and they quickly guided her to a nearby bench where she sank down with a sigh of relief.

She carefully examined her ankle, which was already starting to swell. “This doesn’t feel right,” she said, her voice barely a whisper.

“We should probably get it looked at,” Jermaine said decisively. He flagged down a passing zoo employee in a golf cart. “Excuse me,” he called out, his voice firm and clear. “My daughter fell, and I am asking for medical attention.”

### Part 5: Tending to Sarah’s Needs & Completing Their Vacation

The rest of the afternoon was a blur of fluorescent lights and the sterile smell of a D.C. urgent care clinic. The diagnosis was what they had suspected: a badly twisted ankle. Sarah was fitted with a pair of cumbersome aluminum crutches and given a bottle of pain relievers before being sent on her way.

Navigating the Capital Metro and then the Dominion Rail train back to Fredericksburg was a significant challenge. The crutches were awkward in the crowded train cars, and every lurch of the train sent a jolt of pain through Sarah’s ankle. Getting her onto the train had been a clumsy, difficult process, a frustrating end to what had started as a wonderful day. None of them had planned on Sarah getting injured.

“Tomorrow, I’m going to see if I can rent you a wheelchair so you can still enjoy this vacation,” Jermaine said as they finally found seats on the train back to Fredericksburg. He looked exhausted but determined. At least they were together, with the siblings sitting side-by-side and their dad across the small aisle.

Jack took Sarah’s crutches and carefully propped them against the window as the ticket inspector came around. Jermaine pulled out three tickets and explained the situation. “My daughter is on crutches and might need an extra moment getting off the train in Fredericksburg,” he told the inspector, who thanked him and moved on. Jermaine then turned back to his children. “We’re going to head straight to the hotel. You two, stay in the room and I will figure out dinner.”

“Got it, Dad,” Jack said.

“Yeah, it’s probably for the best,” Sarah answered, her voice weary. “I don’t want to walk any more than I have to.”

The train finally arrived in Fredericksburg, and just as expected, Sarah struggled to get up. The train conductor, already aware of her needs, patiently held the train so she could disembark with her father and brother. Now, they faced the three-block hike to their hotel. Three blocks that had seemed so easy and pleasant that morning were now a daunting, painful obstacle course.

Jack immediately moved to his sister’s side, offering his shoulder for support. She leaned on him, and together they began the slow, arduous journey. Jack helped his sister as best he could as she slowly and painfully figured out how to balance herself on the unfamiliar crutches. It wasn’t too long before they found themselves back in their hotel room, Sarah collapsing onto one of the queen beds with a groan of relief.

“I was going to take us out to a nice dinner,” Jermaine said, looking at his daughter’s pale, pained face, “but I don’t want you struggling any more than you already are. I’ll probably just order a pizza or Chinese or something.”

“Pizza sounds good,” Jack said immediately.

“Yeah, let’s have some pizza,” Sarah added, her voice muffled by a pillow.

“Alright, I will go to the front desk and see what is around here,” Jermaine said. “You two stay here.”

He returned a few minutes later, not with a menu, but with a triumphant smile on his face. “Okay, pizza is on the way,” he announced. “And Sarah, you won’t believe this. I found a place here in Fredericksburg that rents medical equipment. They were surprisingly still open, and they are on their way to the hotel *right now* to get you squared away. I got you a motorized wheelchair specifically for dealing with the hills in D.C., and we can keep it until Saturday when we go back home.”

Sarah pushed herself up, a look of genuine, profound relief on her face. The thought of not having to hobble around on crutches for the rest of their vacation was the best news she could have possibly imagined. Maybe this trip could be salvaged after all.

### Part 6: A New Level of Trust

Back at the hotel, after they had finished their pizza, a weary quiet settled over the room. Sarah, feeling grimy from the day's events and wanting to soak her aching muscles, decided a hot bath was in order.

“I’m going to try and get cleaned up,” she announced, pushing herself up from the bed and grabbing her crutches.

She managed to hobble into the bathroom on her own, but was immediately confronted with the logistical nightmare of the standard hotel bathtub. The room was not an accessible one; they hadn't anticipated this. She looked from the high, slick wall of the tub to her swollen, throbbing ankle, and then at the crutches. A wave of frustrated helplessness washed over her. There was simply no way to do this alone.

She hobbled back to the doorway, a look of defeat on her face. “Jack?” she called out quietly.

He looked up from the TV, his expression immediately concerned. “What’s up?”

“I can’t… I can’t do this,” she admitted, her cheeks flushed with embarrassment. “There’s no way I can get into the tub by myself, let alone wash. It’s impossible.”

Jack got up and came to the bathroom door, assessing the situation. He saw her predicament immediately. The unspoken awkwardness of what she needed hung in the steamy air for a moment. He was the one to finally address it, his tone entirely practical.

“Look, Sarah,” he said, his expression one of simple, honest care. “This is… weird. But you need help, and I’m the one who’s here. We can either make this a big, awkward thing, or we can just let the cat out of the bag and be adults about it.” He looked her straight in the eye. “I’m just trying to take care of my big sister because you’re hurt. There are no inappropriate thoughts of any kind here. We’re just… us.”

A wave of profound relief washed over Sarah. He had taken the burden of the awkwardness away and replaced it with simple, practical care. This wasn't about anything other than him helping her.

“You’re right,” she said, a small, grateful smile on her face. “Okay. Let’s just handle it.”

With that new, matter-of-fact understanding in place, the last barrier of conventional privacy between them dissolved. Jack helped her get situated, providing a steady presence as she carefully and painfully maneuvered herself into the tub. What followed was an act of quiet, clinical care. He gently washed her back and her lower legs, his touch gentle and his demeanor entirely practical.

As he was helping, the practical thought occurred to him. “You know,” he said, breaking the quiet, “since I’m already in here, I should probably just take my turn and shower after you’re done.”

Sarah looked at him, and for the first time since her fall, she genuinely smiled. The weirdness was gone, replaced by a deep, unwavering trust. “That’s a great idea,” she said.

With their new, matter-of-fact understanding in place, the last barriers of conventional privacy between them dissolved, replaced by a mature and trusting partnership born of necessity. Jack helped Sarah out of the tub, his movements steady and careful, providing a solid presence for her to lean on. He assisted her in drying off and getting into her soft, comfortable pajamas, a process that would have been impossible for her to manage on her own. His care was entirely clinical, his focus only on helping his sister without causing her any pain or embarrassment.

Once she was safely situated on the closed toilet lid, he quickly dried himself off and pulled on his own pajamas. A few minutes later, they emerged from the steamy bathroom together, both dressed for the night.

Jermaine looked up from the book he was reading on his bed, his expression one of quiet understanding. He didn’t ask any questions. He simply saw his two children having navigated a difficult situation with maturity and love. “Alright,” he said, closing his book. “My turn for the shower.” He gathered his things and headed into the bathroom.

As the bathroom door closed, Sarah turned to her brother, her eyes shining with an emotion that went deeper than just gratitude. “Jack,” she said, her voice a little thick. “Thank you. Really. I… I was so embarrassed and frustrated, and you just… you made it okay.”

“Of course, Sarah,” he said simply. “I’m your brother. I’ve always got your back.”

“I know,” she said. “I really appreciate you.” And because words weren’t enough to convey the depth of what she was feeling, she reached for him. It wasn’t a casual, playful hug like the one in the lobby. This was a powerful, fierce embrace. She wrapped her arms around him, pulling him close and holding on tight, a profound and heartfelt thank you for his kindness, his maturity, and his unwavering, unconditional support. In that moment, their bond, already strong, was forged into something even deeper.

### Part 7: A Trip Made Easier

Morning rolled around, and Sarah was excited to try out the motorized wheelchair. She was looking forward to trying the zoo again and spending the afternoon at the museum her brother wanted to see. “I really appreciated how you helped me get back to the hotel last night,” She said, “What did you want to do today?”

“We were going to finish with the zoo and go to the Air & Space Museum,” Jermaine said. He then turned to his son and said, “I really appreciate how you took care of your sister last night, young man. When we get home, she’s going to be your responsibility when I’m at work.”

“I understand dad, and I’ll do my best,” Jack replied. They started making their way to the train station, and soon they were on the platform with tickets in hand and a 5-minute wait for the train to Washington, D.C.

After an uneventful train ride into DC, Jermaine bought their passes to ride the metro to the zoo. He grumbled wishing that there was a stop closer to the zoo, but at least his daughter would have an easier time. They took a second elevator down from the mezzanine to the platform and it wasn’t too long of a wait for the train to the zoo. As it was the last time, the train was crowded and Sarah struggled to find a spot now that she had to negotiate with a bulky wheelchair. “Just face forward and put the brakes on. It’s the best you’ll be able to do,” Her father said as he and his son found a seat where they could at least keep an eye on her.

They soon arrived at the zoo and Sarah was grateful that she didn’t need to wobble on crutches for the day. They skipped the attractions they saw the other day and made their way through the rest of the zoo without any issues. Every step of the way, Jack was there to take care of his sister’s needs as they made their way through the zoo, down the road, and back to the rails. It was time for lunch as well as his part of the trip. “I believe there’s a café in the museum, and we’ll eat there just to make it easier,” Jermaine said, “Unless either of you see something that catches your eye when the train drops us off.”

They got on the train and Jack was happy to sit down, and grateful that he got to sit near his sister. It was weird: when the trip started, she was this dork that he tolerated, but now – especially with her injury – he started really enjoying her company. She started enjoying her brother’s company just as much and no longer felt weird about sharing the hotel bed.

## Chapter 2: Sarah with an Injury

### Part 1: Welcome Home to a Twisted Ankle

“Jack, will you do me the weirdest of favors,” Sarah asked.

“For you? Anything,” He replied.

“I can’t lift my leg into the tub,” She replied, “I wish a female was here, but this is the hand we’re dealt with.”

“I understand. Like I just said: ‘for you? Anything.’ And I meant it. If I need to bathe you then I will bathe you. I guess all I ask is that while I’m in there, I get my turn also,” Jack said.

“That sounds fair,” Sarah replied, “I think we should keep this to ourselves though.”

“Yeah,” Jack answered.

“Come into my room please and help me get undressed,” She replied.

“OK,” Jack said, and followed her as she wobbled on crutches to her bedroom. Once they arrived in her room, he said, “I suppose I should shut my eyes.”

“Honestly, I don’t care if you see stuff. If you’re going to help me in the tub, you’re going to have to look anyway,” she said.

“Yeah, that makes sense,” Jack replied, “Now, what do you need help with?”

“Honestly, I simply can’t get my foot into the tub,” She started to explain. She began to blush at the thought of taking a bath, and what it entailed, with company in the bathroom with her.

Jack soon realized what he was about to see and decided to stay professional about it. He loved his sister – but not like that – and he did not want to scare her as she started pulling her shirt off. It was time for her to get a badly needed bath, and he understood that he needed to be careful with her twisted ankle.

The four were gathered in the backyard, and Sarah started pitching the tent. “Just like our childhood, but now we’re older,” she said.

“This is going to be a tight fit, but I’m sure we will manage,” Clare said.

## Chapter 5: Delores

### Part 1: University Open House

“Yeah, I have to work,” Sarah groaned, her voice full of genuine regret as she pulled the Stark Paladin up to the curb. “I’m sorry I can’t be with you.”

“It’s okay,” Jack said, though he felt a familiar knot of anxiety tightening in his stomach. The Sinclair Center at Virginia Dominion University loomed before them, a massive, modern building bustling with students and their parents. It was a lot to take in.

“I’ll drop you off, and then Clare said she will pick you up later,” Sarah confirmed.

Jack hugged his sister, a quick, comforting gesture. “Yeah, I know. Still, I wish you could join me. I think Clare is going to bring me to the Rec Center and we’ll go swimming after or something.”

“I’d love to see the two of you in the pool,” Sarah replied with a warm smile as they pulled away from each other. “Go on. You’ll be great. Call us if you need anything.”

With a final wave, Jack got out of the car and headed into the Sinclair Center. The lobby was a cacophony of cheerful chatter and nervous energy. He followed the signs to a series of tables set up for the open house check-in. After getting his name tag and a folder full of university brochures, he saw a long table laden with pastries, fruit, and coffee. He gratefully grabbed a plate with a danish and a carton of juice.

He found a seat at an empty table in the cavernous main ballroom where the opening speeches were set to take place. The room was already filling up, the noise level rising. He took a bite of his danish and looked around at the other prospective students, most of whom were sitting with their parents or friends. He felt a sharp pang of loneliness, wishing his sister or one of his cousins could be here with him, a familiar anchor in this overwhelming new place. But he knew they all had to work. He was on his own for this part. He sighed and settled in, waiting for the program to begin.

### Part 2: The Tour

After the university president's welcoming address, the massive crowd in the ballroom was broken up into smaller groups for the campus tours. Jack found himself in a group of about twenty other prospective students and their parents, led by a bubbly and energetic junior named Madison.

For the next ninety minutes, Madison led them on a whirlwind tour of the main academic buildings. She was incredibly knowledgeable, pointing out the science labs in the chemistry building, the massive lecture halls in the arts and letters building, and the quiet, studious atmosphere of the library. She kept up a steady stream of cheerful patter, sharing anecdotes about her favorite professors and offering tips on the best places to study.

Jack tried to keep up, but the sheer scale of the campus was overwhelming. The buildings were huge, the distances between them felt vast, and the constant stream of information was a lot to process. The crowds of students walking between classes, the sheer number of new faces, and the constant noise began to wear on him.

By the time the tour group made its final stop and Madison cheerfully dismissed them, Jack was completely exhausted. The social energy required to be part of the group, combined with the sensory overload of the sprawling campus, had drained him. He thanked the tour guide and slowly made his way back towards the Sinclair Center, the central hub where the open house had begun. All he wanted was to find a quiet corner, sit down, and wait for Clare to rescue him.

### Part 3: An Unexpected Guide

Jack found an empty bench outside the Sinclair Center and collapsed onto it, the exhaustion from the tour washing over him. He just wanted to sit quietly and wait for Clare.

“You look like you’ve been through the wringer,” a voice said.

He looked up. A young woman in a VDU polo shirt and khaki pants was standing in front of him, a look of friendly concern on her face. She looked like one of the official tour guides.

“Is there anything I can help you with? Any questions I can answer?” Delores asked, sitting down beside him without waiting for an invitation. She launched into a series of well-rehearsed, generic questions about his interests, seeming to answer his unspoken questions about the university with an easy confidence. Jack, still overwhelmed, was just grateful for the quiet, one-on-one attention after the chaos of the group tour.

After talking for a few minutes, Jack asked, “Where is the nearest restroom?”

A small, predatory glint appeared in Delores’s eyes, so quick that Jack missed it. “This building right next to us. Chemistry. Here, I’ll show you,” she said, her voice full of helpfulness. She led him to the doors, and as they stepped inside, the bright January sunlight was replaced by a deep, disorienting gloom. She escorted him down a hallway that was almost completely dark. “They’ve been trying to fix the lights in here for a while now,” she said, her voice a low murmur in the darkness.

“I can’t see very well,” Jack said, instinctively slowing down.

This was her plan. She knew this hallway was unlit, a pocket of manufactured privacy in the middle of a busy campus. She had thought, in the anonymous dark, she could press him against a wall, let things happen, see how far he would go. It was her first attempt to test his boundaries. But she felt him tense up beside her, his discomfort a palpable thing in the enclosed space. She immediately backed off. It was too soon to show her true colors.

“Here, just hold on to my arm,” she said, her voice all business. “The men’s room is just down here on the left.”

She legitimately guided him through the darkness to the restroom door and waited for him in the hallway while he was inside. When he came back out, she led him back towards the exit. They stepped out of the building and back into the bright, sunny afternoon. Jack had to stop and blink for a moment, his eyes struggling to adjust from the profound darkness to the brilliant light, feeling disoriented and even more exhausted than before.

### Part 4: The Conversation Shifts

They stepped out of the building and back into the bright, sunny afternoon. Jack had to stop and blink for a moment, his eyes struggling to adjust from the profound darkness to the brilliant light, feeling disoriented and even more exhausted than before.

“Whoa,” he said, shielding his eyes. “That was really dark in there.”

“I told you,” Delores said with a small laugh, watching him. “So, when you’re not getting blinded by faulty university lighting, what do you do? Do you have a job?”

“Yeah, I work part-time,” Jack said, his vision starting to clear. “I’m a clerk-cashier at the front desk at Fairlawn Recreation Center over in Virginia Beach.”

“Fairlawn? I’ve heard of that place. The really busy one, right?” she asked, filing the information away. “You must be really patient to deal with all those people.”

“It’s okay,” Jack shrugged. “My sister works there too, she's one of the lifeguards, so it’s not so bad.”

At the mention of his sister, Delores’s friendly, professional demeanor subtly shifted. The tour guide mask began to slip, replaced by something more personal, more intense.

“You’re really close with your family, aren’t you?” she asked, her voice a little softer now. “That’s… really nice.” She moved a little closer to him on the sidewalk. “It must be nice to have people you can always count on.”

The conversation slowly turned away from the university and became focused entirely on him. She started asking about his interests, his friends, what he liked to do for fun. She seemed genuinely interested, hanging on his every word, her eyes locked on his. The shift was so gradual that Jack didn’t notice he was being led away from the central campus area, away from the crowds, until they were walking down a quieter path lined with residential buildings. The conversation, which had started as a simple tour, was now something else entirely. It was becoming more personal, more intimate, and Jack, still feeling off-kilter from the dark hallway, found himself being pulled along by the sheer force of her focused attention.

### Part 5: Lunch in the Lion's Den

Feeling disoriented from the sudden shift from darkness to bright sunlight, Jack was about to suggest they head back to the Sinclair Center when Delores put a light hand on his arm.

“I don’t know about you, but all that walking made me starving,” she said with an easy smile. “The open house is providing lunch for everyone in the main cafeteria. My treat. Come on, it’ll give us a chance to talk more.”

Before Jack could protest, she was gently guiding him toward another large building. His defenses were down, and the idea of a simple, public lunch felt safe enough. Inside, the cafeteria was a loud, bustling space. Delores navigated the lines with a practiced ease, and soon they were sitting at a small table in a slightly quieter corner, trays of surprisingly decent food in front of them.

Here, in the bright, open space, Delores turned the full force of her attention on him. The tour guide persona was completely gone, replaced by a captivating intensity. She asked him about his family, his friends, his favorite classes at the community college.
