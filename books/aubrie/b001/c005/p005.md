While Jordan remained in the quiet sanctuary of Room 214, carefully organizing his heavy astrophysics textbooks on his desk, the five women congregated next door in Room 216. The connecting bathroom doors were shut, giving them a rare moment of privacy.

Madison, sitting cross-legged on the lower bunk, was the first to address the massive, undeniable gravitational pull that had formed during breakfast.

"Alright, we need to talk about the elephant in the room," Madison announced, a wide, affectionate grin spreading across her face. "Or rather, the nebula in the cafeteria. Aubrie, you are completely, helplessly smitten with him."

Aubrie, who was leaning against the cinderblock wall, didn't even try to deny it. A deep, honest flush colored her cheeks, but she met Madison's gaze head-on. "I am," she confessed, her voice soft but entirely certain. "I am completely gone for him. He's... he thinks the way I do. When he looks at me, I don't feel like I have to translate myself."

"It is visibly obvious," Zoë added from the desk chair, her crisp British consonants clipping through the air. "Your heart rate elevates when you touch his wheelchair, and your protective instincts are operating at maximum capacity."

Aubrie smiled, but her expression quickly grew serious as she looked around the cramped room. She took a deep breath.

"But I'm not blind," Aubrie said, looking directly at Madison, and then to Zoë. "Madison, you volunteered to help him in the bathroom before I could even get the words out, and you tied his shoes this morning like it was a privilege. And Zoë, you casually handed him a thousand-dollar piece of hardware last night just to make sure he felt safe."

"It was an unused Vanguard Pro running a lightweight, custom distribution of Aura Glade," Zoë corrected smoothly, though a faint pink tinge appeared on her own cheeks. "It was a simple, logical reallocation of resources."

"It was because you fancy him," Aubrie countered gently. "Both of you do."

Silence fell over the small room. Madison looked down at her hands, while Zoë suddenly found a loose thread on her jeans absolutely fascinating. In a traditional college dorm, this was the exact moment where lines were drawn, alliances were formed, and a toxic, dramatic love triangle was born.

Aubrie refused to let that happen.

"Jealousy feels selfish," Aubrie explained passionately, stepping into the center of the room. "Look at what he's been through. He has spent his entire life being isolated, abused, and made to feel like he is nothing but a massive burden. His mother controlled everything he did. How could we possibly turn around and make him the center of a bunch of territorial drama or competition?"

She looked at her suitemates, her eyes shining with a radical, neurodivergent logic. "What if we just... don't compete? What if we don't force him to choose, or make him feel guilty for accepting comfort? What if we all just love him, and support him, equally?"

Madison’s head snapped up, her bright blue eyes widening as the concept clicked into place. The tension in her shoulders vanished. "You mean... you wouldn't be mad? If I wanted to hold his hand, or cuddle with him?"

"I would be thrilled," Aubrie said, her voice fiercely sincere. "Because it means he's getting more love, and more anchors. He needs all the safe harbors he can get."

Zoë adjusted her glasses, her analytical mind rapidly processing the emotional logistics. "A poly-affectionate dynamic," she murmured, her British accent lending a clinical gravity to the profound sentiment. "It eliminates the stress of competition and maximizes his emotional support network. It is... an incredibly stable system."

"I think it's beautiful," Lauren chimed in from the top bunk, her voice warm and gentle. "I mean, obviously, I have Tom. We're really serious, and I love him, so I am strictly in the platonic camp here. But Jordan deserves exactly this kind of safety. I'm fully on board to be the protective sister."

Hannah, sitting beside Zoë, nodded in firm agreement. As the oldest of the group, her "mom friend" instincts were already deeply invested in Jordan's well-being. "Mark and I are solid," she said with a warm, grounded smile. "But Jordan needs a family. I'm with Lauren. I'm his sister now, and I will fiercely protect whatever beautiful, unconventional thing you three are building with him."

A silent, powerful agreement bloomed in the cinderblock room. The traditional rules of dating and jealousy had been completely discarded, replaced by a unique, unbreakable pact of mutual respect and radical generosity. They were not rivals. They were a constellation, and their collective purpose was to illuminate his world and ensure he never had to brave the dark alone again.
