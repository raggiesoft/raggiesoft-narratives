At ten-fifty, the massive lecture hall erupted into the chaotic sounds of three hundred freshmen packing up their bags. As the crowd funneled up the tiered stairs toward the exits, Jordan’s group stayed exactly where they were.

Jordan carefully closed the lid of his Vanguard Pro laptop, sliding it into his backpack alongside his new mPad and FM receiver. Once the room had mostly cleared out, Aubrie unlocked the brakes on his wheelchair, and the massive ten-person entourage made its way down to the front of the stage.

Dr. Evans was at the podium, clicking out of her presentation. She looked up, her eyebrows raised in mild surprise as an absolute wall of students approached her.

"Well," Dr. Evans said, leaning against the podium with an amused smile. "I usually have to beg for a single peer note-taker. I don't think I've ever had an entire platoon volunteer on the first day."

Sarah laughed, stepping forward. "We're a package deal, Professor."

"And which one of you is the student registered with Educational Accessibility?" Dr. Evans asked, looking over the group.

Jordan raised his hand slightly, offering a shy, polite smile.

Dr. Evans looked at Jordan, then back at the nine people standing defensively and affectionately around his wheelchair. "Let me guess. You guys all live on the same floor?"

"Same suite, actually," Madison grinned. "Well, five of us. Plus a boyfriend, and three friends from the fifth floor. Have you heard the rumor about the housing clerical error in Bennett Hall?"

Dr. Evans’ eyes widened in sudden realization. She let out a sharp, genuine laugh. "Wait. You're the Suite 214 kid? The one who got dropped into a room full of women?"

Jordan nodded, pulling his laptop back out of his bag for a second just to type a quick response. He turned the screen so she could read the glowing green text.

*I am. They decided to keep me.*

"I can see that," Dr. Evans smiled, her tone softening with genuine warmth. "Alright, tell you what. My office hours start in ten minutes. Why don't you all follow me up to Room 412, and we'll get everyone's student ID numbers written down. If you're all willing to share the note-taking load for Jordan, I am more than happy to authorize Priority Registration for the whole group."

A collective cheer went up from the freshmen.

They followed Dr. Evans out of the auditorium and took the massive freight elevator up to the fourth floor. Room 412 was a standard, slightly cramped faculty office, filled with towering bookshelves and the smell of old paper. Sitting right on the edge of her desk, exactly as promised, was a massive, crystal-clear glass bowl overflowing with brightly colored foil condom wrappers.

It was a tight squeeze getting ten students into the office, but they made it work, shuffling around Jordan's wheelchair. Dr. Evans pulled out a legal pad and passed it around, letting Mark, Sarah, Jessica, Chloe, Hannah, Lauren, Zoë, Madison, and Aubrie write down their names and CPI ID numbers.

While the pad made its rounds, Jordan opened his Vanguard Pro again. His fingers hovered over the keyboard. The panic he had felt during the lecture was still sitting heavy in his chest, but Dr. Evans’ announcement at the beginning of class—her promise of total privacy and zero judgment—gave him the courage he needed.

He took a deep breath and began to type.

When the legal pad finally made its way back to Dr. Evans, Jordan pushed his wheelchair forward slightly, turning his laptop screen toward her desk.

Dr. Evans adjusted her glasses and read the bright green text.

*Professor Evans, you said to let you know if we grew up extremely sheltered. My mother opted me out of every Family Life and health class since the fourth grade. She told me the human body was shameful. I don't know what any of the diagrams on the syllabus mean. I don't know any of the slang. I don't know how any of this works. I am completely lost.*

The easy, amused energy in the office instantly vanished. Dr. Evans read the paragraph twice. When she looked up at Jordan, her eyes were filled with profound, quiet compassion.

Around him, the suite fell completely silent. Aubrie stepped a little closer, resting her hand gently on the back of his wheelchair in a silent show of absolute support. Lauren felt a sharp pang of empathy in her chest, the contrast between Jordan's deeply vulnerable honesty and Tom's toxic paranoia striking her all over again.

"Jordan," Dr. Evans said softly, her voice entirely devoid of pity, replaced instead with a fierce, protective academic respect. "Thank you for telling me that. I know that took a lot of courage."

She turned to her own desktop computer, her fingers flying across the keyboard.

"You are not the first student to sit in that chair with this exact problem," she told him firmly. "When parents completely isolate their children from basic biology, it does real damage. But you are here now, and we are going to fix it. There is absolutely nothing shameful about the human body, and you are not going to fail this class."

She hit print, and the machine in the corner of her office whirred to life. She grabbed the pages and handed them to Jordan. It was a list of highly reputable, clinical web resources—university health sites and basic anatomical guides designed for young adults.

"Start here," Dr. Evans instructed gently. "Look over these sites at your own pace. They explain the absolute basics. Read them over the next few days. If you are still feeling lost, you email me, and we will sit down one-on-one, and I will explain whatever you need. Okay?"

Jordan looked down at the printed URLs, a massive wave of relief washing over him. He looked back up and nodded emphatically. *Thank you.*

Aubrie leaned over Jordan's shoulder, looking at the printed pages. She saw the URLs for the 2D diagrams and clinical explanations. It was a great start, but she knew Jordan. She knew how his brain worked, and she knew how deeply ingrained his mother's psychological conditioning was. Staring at flat, sterile drawings on a computer screen wasn't going to break eighteen years of trauma.

Aubrie slowly looked up, catching Madison's eye across the cramped office.

Madison, who was looking at the exact same printout, raised an eyebrow. An unspoken, radical, and intensely practical idea passed between the two girls. They had built an ecosystem of absolute trust in Room 214. They had already broken down so many physical barriers just by sharing a bathroom and a sleeping space.

Aubrie looked back down at Jordan, squeezing his shoulder.

"Don't worry, J," Aubrie said softly, a warm, protective promise in her voice. "We'll go over the printouts with you. We'll figure out a way to help you study tonight when we get back to the dorm. I promise."

Dr. Evans smiled, completely unaware of the unconventional lengths the girls of Suite 214 were willing to go to for their friend. "You've got a great support system here, Jordan. I'll see you all on Thursday."
