The classroom in Carter Hall was much smaller and older than the massive science amphitheater, smelling faintly of lemon polish and old paper. Jordan wheeled himself into a spot at the end of the front row just as the professor—a tall, older man with a neatly trimmed beard—walked in and set his leather briefcase on the desk.

"Welcome to History 101," the professor announced, turning to the chalkboard to write his name. "I am Dr. Gable."

From his spot in the front row, Jordan immediately leaned forward. The white chalk on the dusty green board was completely unreadable, and Dr. face had been turned toward the wall when he spoke, muffling the sound. Jordan instinctively cupped his hand behind his ear and squinted, a familiar spike of anxiety hitting his chest.

Dr. Gable turned around. His eyes swept the front row, immediately landing on Jordan’s strained posture.

The professor didn't sigh. He didn't ask Jordan what was wrong, and he didn't draw the attention of the entire room to the boy in the wheelchair. Instead, he simply set the piece of white chalk down. He reached into his desk drawer, pulled out a thick, heavy-duty black marker, and walked over to the whiteboard mounted on the adjacent wall.

When Dr. Gable spoke again, he took two steps closer to Jordan's side of the room. He squared his shoulders, faced the class directly, and projected his voice with crisp, booming clarity that easily cut through Jordan's hearing deficit.

"Let's try the whiteboard instead," Dr. Gable boomed pleasantly. "Now, before we dive into Mesopotamia, I prefer to run this class as a discussion rather than a straight lecture. To make that work, I want everyone paired up. Can I get a volunteer to partner with our gentleman in the front row and help ensure he catches all the notes?"

A chair scraped against the linoleum a few rows back.

"I can do it, Professor."

Jordan’s head snapped up. The voice cut through the air with a distinct, undeniable cadence. He recognized the accent immediately—it was the exact same Sydney inflection that his EchoType software used.

A girl with dark hair pulled into a messy ponytail walked down the aisle and casually dropped her backpack into the empty desk right next to his wheelchair.

"Hey, mate," she smiled, extending her hand. "I'm Sarah."

Jordan blinked, slightly taken aback by her easy, radiant confidence. He shook her hand, finding her grip firm and friendly.

"I live over in Bennett Hall," Sarah continued, settling into her chair and pulling out a notebook. "Up on the fifth floor. You're the bloke from the second floor, right? The famous suite?"

Jordan tensed slightly, bracing himself for the inevitable invasive questions or the weird, pitying looks he usually got when people figured out his living situation.

But Sarah just smiled, completely unbothered. "Good on ya. I heard you've got the biggest bathroom in the building down there. Absolute luxury compared to the nightmare we're dealing with on five."

She made absolutely no big deal out of the fact that he was living with five women. To her, it was just another quirky piece of campus logistics. Her total lack of judgment made Jordan’s shoulders drop two inches in relief.

Jordan pulled open his Vanguard Pro. Because he didn't want to use his EchoType software and disrupt the quiet classroom with its digitized voice, he opted for a completely different approach. He opened a sleek, dark-themed terminal window and launched NeoEdit—a streamlined, text-based command-line interface that Zoë had pre-installed for him. It was simple, completely distraction-free, and most importantly, it allowed him to scale the neon-green text up massively against the stark black background so he could actually read it.

*I'm Jordan,* he typed, angling the screen so Sarah could see the large letters. *Thank you for helping me.*

"No worries at all, Jordan," Sarah whispered, her Australian accent warm and perfectly clear. "Let's smash this class."

The next hour was a revelation.

Dr. Gable was a masterful professor. He kept his voice loud, always making sure he was facing the front row when he spoke, and wrote his key points in massive, bold letters on the whiteboard. When the class shifted into a discussion about the agricultural revolution, Sarah proved to be the ultimate partner. She casually slid her notebook over so Jordan could read her meticulously organized bullet points.

When Dr. Gable posed a question to their side of the room, Jordan’s fingers flew across his keyboard.

*The shift to agriculture created static borders, which required the invention of standing armies to defend them,* Jordan typed into his terminal.

Sarah glanced at his screen, didn't miss a single beat, and raised her hand. "Jordan points out that the shift to agriculture created static borders," she announced clearly to the room, seamlessly acting as his voice. "Which required the invention of standing armies to defend them."

"Excellent point, Jordan," Dr. Gable nodded approvingly.

Jordan sat back in his chair, completely stunned. It was working. He was actively participating in a college-level discussion. He wasn't a burden, he wasn't a disruption, and he wasn't failing.

When the class finally ended, Jordan felt a rush of genuine, unadulterated triumph.

"Brilliant work today," Sarah smiled, packing her notebooks into her bag. She pulled out a pen and scribbled a string of numbers onto a scrap of paper, handing it to him. "Here’s my mobile number. Text me if you need the notes for whatever reason, or if you just want to go over the reading before Friday."

Jordan typed a quick *Thank you* into his terminal, taking the slip of paper with a massive smile.

"Catch you later, Jordan," Sarah waved, heading for the door.

As Jordan rolled out of Carter Hall and back into the bright Virginia sunshine, the brutal, rolling hills of the CPI campus didn't look nearly as terrifying as they had an hour ago. He had survived his first solo trek. He had found a professor who actually cared. He had made a new friend.

And most importantly, it was almost noon. It was time to go meet Aubrie.
