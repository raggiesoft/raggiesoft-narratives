# Part 2: Into the Stars – 8:50 AM

Outside the glass doors of Newman Hall, the sprawling campus was alive with a nervous, electric energy. Thousands of freshmen were crisscrossing the paved walkways, hunting for their first lecture halls. The suite family naturally began to splinter toward their respective academic buildings.

"Alright, duty calls," Madison sighed, adjusting the strap of her heavy backpack. She stepped forward and wrapped her arms around Jordan’s shoulders in a warm, lingering hug. "Have a great morning, J. See you this afternoon."

Zoë was next. She gave him a brisk but entirely sincere squeeze. "Do try to survive the chaos. We will reconvene at the suite this evening to analyze the day's data."

Jordan smiled, tapping his fingers against the armrest of his chair in a happy, staccato rhythm. As Madison and Zoë merged into the river of students heading toward the engineering quad, Aubrie stepped up beside him.

"Ready?" she asked, her eyes bright.

Jordan nodded, taking a deep breath.

Their 9:00 AM Astronomy 101 class was located in one of the older, brutalist concrete structures of the Science Complex. As they navigated through the heavy double doors and into the massive, tiered lecture hall, the architectural reality of the aging building immediately became apparent. The room was a steep amphitheater. A long flight of carpeted stairs led down to a stage at the very bottom, meaning the only wheelchair-accessible seating was a single row of desks bolted to the floor at the very top, in the absolute back of the room.

They settled into two seats near the exit. The room was already buzzing with the chatter of two hundred freshmen, and models of planets hung suspended from the high ceiling.

Jordan stared down at the massive projection screen at the front of the hall, his hands gripping the armrests of his chair. This was it. His mother’s weapon. The insurmountable mountain she had deliberately chosen for him, perfectly designed to ensure his academic failure.

He pulled his Vanguard Pro onto his lap, his fingers trembling slightly as he opened a blank document and typed. He angled the screen so Aubrie could read it without him having to trigger the Australian text-to-speech voice in the quiet room.

*This is a lot,* he typed, the cursor blinking rapidly.

"It's okay," Aubrie beamed, leaning in close so her shoulder brushed against his. Her excitement was a physical shield against his fear. "It's the real deal. No more high school physics. We're in this together, remember? I've got you. I'm not going to let you fail."

At exactly 9:00 AM, Dr. Alistair walked onto the stage far below them and turned on his microphone. A comfortable silence fell over the room as he brought up his first slide.

For Aubrie, the lecture hall instantly became a sanctuary—a place where the universe operated on clear, understandable laws. But for Jordan, the nightmare was just beginning.

From the very back row, the projection screen was nothing but a glowing, blurry white rectangle. The text on Dr. Alistair's slides smeared together into illegible gray smudges. Worse, the acoustics of the cavernous room were terrible. The professor’s voice echoed off the concrete walls, washing over Jordan in a muffled, distorted hum. He leaned forward, instinctively cupping his hand behind his ear, squinting so hard his eyes watered.

Panic spiked in his chest. *This was it.* His mother was right. He couldn't even perceive the class, let alone pass it.

Aubrie, hyper-observant as always, noticed his rigid posture immediately. She looked at his squinting eyes, then down at the way he was cupping his ear. The reality of his untreated sensory deficits became glaringly obvious.

She reached under the desk and gently, discreetly, took his hand.

Jordan flinched slightly at the unexpected contact, but her grip was firm, warm, and entirely certain. She laced her fingers through his and squeezed. With her free hand, she pulled her notebook directly between them and began writing in large, clear, block letters.

*I will take all the notes today. Do not panic.*

Jordan exhaled a shaky breath, staring at her neat handwriting. She quickly scribbled another line.

*You have that 1:00 PM meeting with Ms. Higgins at Educational Accessibility today. This is exactly what that meeting is for. Tell her you need front-row seating, a note-taker, and whatever else helps you see and hear. They HAVE to fix it.*

Jordan looked at her. In his mother's world, a barrier like this was a final judgment—proof that he was broken. In Aubrie's world, it was just an administrative error waiting for a logistical solution.

He squeezed her hand back, the terror in his chest slowly beginning to recede.

For the next fifty minutes, Dr. Alistair spoke about stellar nurseries and hydrostatic equilibrium. Jordan didn't understand the complex words, and he couldn't see the diagrams, but he stopped trying to force it. Instead, he just held Aubrie's hand and watched her. She was fascinated, hanging on every muffled word, her free hand flying across the notebook as she translated the muffled echoes of the professor into crisp, legible facts.

She was his safe person, and she loved this. Her excitement made the terrifying subject feel manageable. His mother's weapon suddenly seemed entirely defused. He had someone to climb the mountain with.

When Dr. Alistair finally dismissed the class, the spell was broken. The lecture hall erupted back into noise as students began packing their bags. Jordan let out a breath he felt like he'd been holding for nearly an hour. He had survived.

He looked at Aubrie as she finally let go of his hand to pack up her notebook.

"See?" she whispered directly into his ear, her smile bright. "Not so bad. And once Ms. Higgins gets your accommodations sorted, it'll be a breeze."

*Not so bad,* Jordan typed on his screen, offering her a small, genuine smile.

As they moved out into the crowded hallway, the mass of students jostled past them. Jordan tensed, his anxiety flaring at the claustrophobic press of bodies, but Aubrie seamlessly stepped in front of his wheelchair, acting as a small, sturdy buffer against the crowd.

"I guess we have to go our separate ways for our mid-morning classes," Aubrie said, her own expression dropping a little at the thought of leaving his side.

Jordan nodded, his heart sinking a fraction.

"Well," she said softly. And then, right there in the crowded hallway, she leaned down and wrapped her arms around his neck. This hug was different from the desperate, grounding embraces of the weekend. It was warm, lingering, and entirely voluntary—a reassuring physical promise that she wasn't disappearing.

He held onto her for a moment, absorbing her steadying presence. As she pulled back, her cheeks were slightly pink.

"Are you free for lunch?" she asked.

Jordan quickly pulled up his master schedule on his Vanguard Pro, though he already knew the answer. His heart gave a happy jolt.

*Yes. At noon,* EchoType replied in its crisp Australian cadence.

"Perfect," she beamed. "Meet me at the dining hall? Newman Commons?"

*Yeah. Okay. Lunch.*

"It's a date," Aubrie said brightly. Then her eyes went wide, realizing what she had just said. A deep, furious blush rushed all the way up to her hairline. "I mean... not a... you know. A literal date. Just... a lunch... with a time. I'll just see you then!"

She spun around, completely flustered, and hurried off toward the math building.

Jordan sat in the hallway, watching her go, a massive, uncontainable smile spreading across his face. He still had to face his mid-morning class entirely alone, a prospect that was undeniably intimidating. But as he maneuvered his wheelchair toward the elevators, his mind was remarkably at ease.

He had a schedule. He had a system. He knew he would see her on Mondays. Wednesdays, and Fridays. He knew he would see her in the dorm tonight. And now, he knew he would see her in just a couple of hours.
