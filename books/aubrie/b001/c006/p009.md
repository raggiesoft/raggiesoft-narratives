# Part 9: Evening Data – 8:00 PM

The first official day of classes had been a whirlwind. By eight o'clock that evening, the suite had gathered in Room 216 to decompress.

It was Jordan’s first time crossing the threshold into the other half of their suite, and it required careful maneuvering. Because Bennett Hall was a traditional 1960s cinderblock dorm, the room was incredibly small—originally built for two students, but now crammed with three. It held a single Twin XL bed, a heavy wooden bunk bed, two standard-issue desks, and two dressers. Floor space was at an absolute premium, and Jordan’s wheelchair took up a good chunk of the narrow center aisle.

But no one minded the tight squeeze. The cramped quarters just forced a comfortable, domestic closeness. Aubrie was perched on the edge of the bottom bunk, her shoulder brushing comfortably against Jordan’s arm. Madison was sitting cross-legged on the floor, leaning her back against one of the dressers. Zoë and Hannah were crammed together on the single Twin XL bed, sharing a bag of pretzels with Hannah’s boyfriend, Mark, who was leaning casually against the adjacent desk chair.

Up on the top bunk, her back pressed against the cool cinderblock wall to carve out a tiny pocket of privacy, Lauren was on a video call with her boyfriend, Tom.

"...and my last lecture finished late, so I had to sprint to Newman Commons before the grill closed," Lauren finished, keeping her voice low so she didn't disrupt the laughter echoing in the small room below her. "It was a crazy day. How was yours?"

"Boring without you," Tom said through the phone speakers. He was sitting in his own dorm room, tossing a lacrosse ball up and catching it. "So, how are the roommates? Not driving you crazy yet?"

"Never! They're the best," Lauren smiled, looking down over the edge of the mattress. Aubrie was playfully nudging Jordan's shoulder as he showed her something on his screen. "Actually, the most unbelievable thing happened. You're not going to believe it. One of our suitemates... is a guy."

She said it with a light laugh, expecting Tom to share in the sheer, bizarre comedy of the situation.

But on the screen, Tom’s smile didn't just fade—it tightened. He stopped tossing the lacrosse ball. "A guy? What are you talking about, Lauren?"

The playful warmth in his voice vanished instantly, replaced by a sharp, suspicious edge that caught Lauren completely off guard.

"Housing made a huge mistake with his name," Lauren explained quickly, her stomach giving a weird, unexpected flutter of anxiety. "His legal name is Aubrey, but he goes by Jordan. They accidentally assigned him to our suite."

"So they moved him, right?" Tom interrupted, his tone suddenly flat and demanding.

"Well, no," Lauren said, trying to keep her voice light. "He's in a wheelchair, and we have the only accessible bathroom on the floor. And honestly, Tom, he is incredibly sweet. He and Aubrie just clicked instantly. We all just decided to make it work."

Tom stared at her through the screen. His eyes darted slightly, his jaw clenching as his mind immediately jumped to the most toxic, cynical conclusion possible.

"Right. A guy living with five girls," Tom scoffed quietly, shaking his head. "You know exactly what he's doing, Lauren. Don't play dumb."

Lauren blinked, recoiling slightly against the cinderblock wall. "What? Tom, he's in a wheelchair. He's totally innocent."

"I know how guys operate. You think I'm an idiot?" Tom sneered, his voice dropping to a harsh whisper. "One guy, five girls, locked in a suite together? He's playing the pity card to get you all into bed. He's probably trying to work his way through the whole room."

"Tom, that is disgusting!" Lauren hissed, horrified by the accusation. "He's not like that at all! Aubrie loves him, and he loves her. It's completely wholesome. Even Mark—Hannah's boyfriend—hangs out here all the time. Mark knows exactly what the living arrangement is, and he's totally cool with it! He thinks Jordan is a great guy!"

"Then Mark is a naive idiot if he's letting some random guy sleep a few feet away from his girlfriend," Tom snapped defensively.

Because the room was so small, and because Tom's voice had spiked with defensive anger, the sharp, tinny audio easily bled through the phone's tiny speaker. Down by the desk, Mark paused mid-chew, a pretzel halfway to his mouth. He looked up at the top bunk, his eyebrows raised in utter disbelief.

"I'm not Mark," Tom continued, completely oblivious to his audience. "Look, my roommate just got back with pizza. I gotta go. Just... keep your distance from the guy, alright? Don't get sucked into whatever weird, messed-up thing they've got going on over there."

"It's not messed up—"

"I'll call you tomorrow," Tom cut her off. "Bye."

The screen went black.

Lauren sat on the top bunk, staring at her reflection in the dark glass of her mPhone. A cold, heavy stone of unease settled perfectly in the center of her chest. It wasn't just that they had disagreed. It was the sheer, absolute certainty in Tom's voice as he accused Jordan of something so vile.

The quiet chatter in the room below had paused.

"Well," Mark said dryly, tossing his pretzel back into the bag. "I guess I need to find a new study partner for PolySci. Because there is absolutely no way I'm sitting next to that guy every Monday, Wednesday, and Friday."

Hannah rested her head on Mark's shoulder, shooting Lauren a sympathetic look. "I'm so sorry, Lauren. He's completely out of line."

Lauren hugged her knees a little tighter, her gut screaming at her that Tom was dangerous. The way he had so easily twisted a beautiful, supportive friendship into something dirty made her feel sick to her stomach.

"Hey, Lauren!"

Lauren jumped slightly, looking over the railing of the bunk bed.

Down in the narrow aisle, Jordan had angled his wheelchair so he could look up at her. His sleek Vanguard Pro was resting on his lap. He typed quickly, and the neon-green text on his NeoEdit terminal expanded so she could read it clearly from the top bunk.

*Madison said you like Dark Aero aesthetics. Look what Zoë just did to my desktop.*

Lauren forced a smile, grateful for the distraction, and carefully climbed down the wooden ladder. She squeezed into the space between Jordan's chair and the desk to look at his screen.

Zoë had managed to skin Jordan's Aura Glade window manager with a flawless, glossy, early-2000s Dark Aero theme—complete with deep glass transparencies, dark metallic taskbars, and glowing neon-blue accents. But sitting right in the center of the glossy desktop, completely untouched, was his NeoEdit terminal window, still rocking the classic, retro 1980s black-and-green aesthetic.

Jordan kept typing. *The darker colors actually really help cut the glare. It's so much easier on my eyes when everything isn't bright white.*

"Oh, wow," Lauren said, her smile becoming genuine as she looked at the sleek interface. "That looks incredible, Jordan. And if it helps you see better, that's even more perfect."

Jordan smiled, turning to his backpack hanging off the back of his chair. He pulled out the FM receiver box and the silver mPad Ms. Higgins had given him, but he didn't stop there. He reached into the front pocket and pulled out two crisp, official-looking referral slips from Educational Accessibility, handing them to Aubrie.

*Ms. Higgins gave me these, too,* he typed, turning the screen so the whole room could see. *Referrals to the Student Health Center. For a real optometrist and an audiologist. They're going to get me glasses and see about hearing aids.*

A collective gasp of excitement swept through the tiny room. Aubrie threw her arms around his neck, burying her face in his shoulder, while Madison reached up from the floor to high-five him. Even Mark leaned over to clap him on the shoulder. It was a massive, tangible step toward independence, entirely outside his mother's control.

Lauren sat down on the edge of the bottom bunk, her shoulder pressing against Aubrie's as she watched the celebration unfold. She looked at Jordan, who was grinning ear to ear as Hannah examined the FM receiver. He wasn't a predator. He wasn't playing a game. He was just a kind, resilient boy finally getting the medical care he deserved, surrounded by people who genuinely cared about him.

Lauren looked back down at her dark phone resting on the desk. The contrast between the cold, toxic delusion of her boyfriend and the absolute, wholesome safety of Room 216 was jarring.
