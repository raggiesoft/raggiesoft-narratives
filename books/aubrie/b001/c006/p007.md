Aubrie pushed Jordan’s wheelchair across the sunlit quad toward Miller Hall, the administrative heart of the campus. The building housed the Office of Educational Accessibility, and as they rolled through the heavy glass doors into the air-conditioned lobby, the reality of the clock finally caught up to them.

It was 12:55 PM. Aubrie had a 1:00 PM calculus lecture on the exact opposite side of campus.

She stopped the chair near the waiting area, her hands lingering on the push handles. She walked around to the front, her bright, energetic demeanor suddenly softening into quiet reluctance. She didn't want to leave him. After the panic in the kitchen and the triumph of the dining hall, stepping away felt wrong.

"Okay," Aubrie said softly, checking her watch. "I really have to run, or I'm going to fail calc before the syllabus is even handed out."

Jordan nodded, offering her a brave, reassuring smile. He pulled his Vanguard Pro onto his lap. *Thank you for lunch,* he typed in NeoEdit. *Go to class.*

Aubrie looked at him. She looked at the boy who had survived a terrifying morning, who had successfully navigated a college cafeteria, and who was now sitting tall in his chair, ready to face an administrator all by himself.

She leaned down, wrapping her arms securely around his neck, burying her face in his shoulder for a long, tight hug. When she pulled back, she kept her hands resting on his shoulders. She leaned in again, pressing a warm, lingering kiss against his cheek.

"Go to your meeting, Jordan," she whispered, her voice fierce and full of absolute certainty. "You got this."

She started to turn away, but paused. She looked back at him, her eyes shining with an emotion that was entirely unguarded and completely real.

"I love you," she said.

It wasn't a casual goodbye. It wasn't a joke. It was a firm, undeniable statement of fact.

Before Jordan could even process the words—before his brain could comprehend that someone had finally, genuinely said those three words to him and meant them—she was jogging backward toward the doors, a brilliant blush spreading across her cheeks. She gave him one last wave and disappeared out into the afternoon heat.

Jordan sat frozen in the lobby. His heart was hammering a chaotic rhythm against his ribs. *I love you.* The words echoed in his mind, wrapping around him like a physical shield.

"Jordan?"

He blinked, pulling himself out of his shock. Ms. Higgins was standing in the doorway of her office, a warm, welcoming smile on her face.

Jordan froze again, but this time for a completely different reason. She had called him *Jordan*.

He wheeled himself into her office, a massive wave of relief washing over him. Henry, the RA, had actually done it. The preferred name change they had entered into the CPI Student Portal on Friday night had cascaded through the university's administrative systems. To the professors and the staff, Aubrey Sinclair’s ghost was gone. He was officially Jordan.

Ms. Higgins closed the door behind them and took her seat at the desk. Jordan immediately opened his terminal.

*Can my Poly Pass be changed to say Jordan Fuller?* he typed, turning the screen toward her. *Right now it says Aubrey Jordan Fuller.*

Ms. Higgins read the bright green text and offered a sympathetic smile. "I'm so glad the preferred name system is working for your rosters, Jordan. Unfortunately, university policy mandates that the Poly Pass ID matches your legal government documentation for security and financial reasons. Until your name is legally changed in a courtroom, the ID has to bear your full legal name. But I promise you, in the eyes of this office and your professors, you are Jordan."

He nodded, accepting the bureaucratic reality. It was a small compromise for the massive victory of hearing his actual name out loud.

Ms. Higgins leaned back, her sharp eyes scanning the sleek, matte-black Vanguard Pro resting on his lap. "I see you got a serious tech upgrade over the weekend. And it looks like this one actually holds a battery charge."

Jordan smiled, typing quickly. *A friend gave it to me.*

"Well, your friend is a lifesaver," Ms. Higgins said, her tone shifting slightly as she opened his sparse manila file. The polite, administrative mask she had worn on Thursday when his mother was hovering over him dropped away completely. Now, it was just the two of them, and she was entirely focused on the student sitting in front of her.

"Alright, Jordan. Let's have a real conversation," Ms. Higgins said, making sure she was facing him directly. "When you were in here last week, your mother submitted a medical file that only requested a wheelchair-accessible dorm and basic exam extensions. But sitting across from you, I noticed you struggling to see your screen, and cupping your ear to hear me. Tell me what happens in a lecture hall. When you look at the board from the back of the room, what do you see?"

Jordan frowned, slightly confused by the question. He opened his terminal.

*I see what everyone else sees,* he typed. *Just colored blurs. You can't read the letters until you get to the front row. That's just how distance works.*

Ms. Higgins stopped. Her pen hovered over her notepad. She stared at the bright green text on his screen, a cold realization washing over her.

"Jordan," she asked, her voice dropping to a very soft, careful register. "When you listen to a professor speak from across a large room... does it sound like they are underwater? Like a muffled hum?"

Jordan nodded, typing quickly. *Yes. The echo makes the hum worse. That's why I cup my ear, to catch the sound waves better. Doesn't everyone?*

Ms. Higgins leaned back in her chair. The sheer, horrifying scale of his mother's neglect suddenly snapped into focus. It wasn't just that Aubrie Sinclair had denied him medical care—she had actively normalized his suffering. She had kept his world so incredibly small that he had simply assumed his severely compromised perception was how the entire universe operated.

"Jordan," Ms. Higgins said gently, holding his gaze. "No. That isn't how distance works. Most people can read the board from the back row. Most people hear words clearly from across the room, without a hum. You aren't supposed to be struggling like this. And we are going to fix it right now."

She turned to her computer, her fingers flying across the keyboard. "I am authorizing an immediate, comprehensive override for your classroom logistics. Now, some of your classes, like History, are small enough that we can just move a desk to the front row. But that Astronomy lecture hall? There are no ramps to the floor. We physically cannot move you to the front row. So, we are going to bring the front row to you."

Jordan watched her, stunned, as she opened a locked cabinet behind her desk and pulled out a small black case and a sleek, silver tablet.

"First, the audio," Ms. Higgins said, opening the black case. "This is an FM Assistive Listening System. Dr. Alistair will be legally required to wear this lapel microphone during every lecture. You will plug a pair of over-ear headphones into this receiver box. His voice will bypass the room's acoustics and beam directly into your ears. It will sound like he is standing right next to you."

"Second, the visual," Ms. Higgins continued, tapping the silver tablet. "What operating system is your laptop running?"

Jordan typed: *Aura Glade.*

Ms. Higgins nodded knowingly. "Aura Glade is fantastic for stability, but it famously hates the university's proprietary screen-mirroring software. So, we bypass it. I am checking out this dedicated MagnaByte mPad to you for the semester. Dr. Alistair's projector will broadcast directly to this mPad in real-time, allowing you to zoom in on the slides from the back row. Furthermore, I am legally mandating that he email you high-resolution PDFs of all lecture materials twenty-four hours in advance."

"Third, your note-takers," Ms. Higgins said, a slight smile returning to her face. She pulled open a file drawer and extracted a thick stack of NCR-carbon-copy packets, sliding them across the desk.

"I am sending out an official email blast to all of your professors this afternoon to formally recruit peer note-takers for your remaining classes," she explained. "Usually, the professor makes an anonymous announcement. But since you already have Aubrie in Astronomy, and you mentioned your new friend Sarah in History, you can bypass the recruitment phase for those two entirely. Just hand these Authorization Forms directly to them."

She tapped the extra packets on the desk. "I'm giving you a few extra copies to keep in your bag, too. Just in case you make another friend in your afternoon classes who wants to volunteer."

Jordan looked at the packets. *Will they get in trouble for doing it?* he typed quickly.

"The exact opposite," Ms. Higgins laughed. "Once they sign these forms and return them to my office, the university will formally compensate them. They will both be granted Priority Course Registration for next semester."

"However," Ms. Higgins said, her tone becoming gentle but firm. "My academic overrides only treat the symptoms. We need to address the root cause. You need actual medical support, Jordan. Glasses. Possible hearing aids. Real evaluations."

She pulled a referral slip from a stack on her desk and filled it out with a crisp, fluid motion.

"I am officially referring you to the CPI Student Health Center. They have an optometrist and an audiologist on staff. I want you to make an appointment this week. They will get the ball rolling to get you the actual hardware you need, completely independent of your mother's consent."

Jordan looked down at the slip of paper resting next to the FM receiver, the mPad, and the stack of note-taker forms. It was a golden ticket. He looked back up at Ms. Higgins, his fingers trembling slightly as he typed his response.

*Thank you.*

"Don't thank me yet, Jordan," Ms. Higgins smiled warmly, closing his file. "We're just getting started."

Ten minutes later, Jordan rolled out of Ms. Higgins's office and back into the sunlit lobby of Miller Hall. His backpack was heavier now, but he felt lighter than he had his entire life.

He parked his wheelchair near the glass doors, pulled his mPhone from his pocket, and opened a new text message to Sarah.

**Jordan:** *Educational Accessibility gave me an official note-taker form for Western Civ. If you sign it, the university gives you Priority Registration for next semester. Do you want it?*

Less than thirty seconds later, his mPhone buzzed.

**Sarah:** *Are you joking?! Priority registration is absolute gold! I would have done it for free anyway, mate! Absolutely YES. Bring it to class on Wednesday!!!*

Jordan grinned, a massive wave of relief washing over him. He immediately opened Aubrie's contact thread.

**Jordan:** *Ms. Higgins gave me the mPad and the microphone for Dr. Alistair. She also gave me a note-taker form for you. You get Priority Registration.*

The reply from Aubrie came through almost instantly.

**Aubrie:** *YES!!! Best news ever. Though I’d take your notes just to sit next to you anyway. 😊 Tell you all about Calc tonight! Go crush your afternoon classes! ❤️*

Jordan stared at the little red heart emoji on his screen. He carefully locked his phone and slipped it back into his pocket. He wasn't navigating this campus alone. He had an entire constellation of people who enthusiastically wanted to be by his side.

He grabbed the hand rims of his wheelchair and pushed out into the afternoon heat, ready to tackle the hills.
