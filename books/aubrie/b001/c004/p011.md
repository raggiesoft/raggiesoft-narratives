# Part 11: The Trust Fall – 8:00 PM

It was 8:00 PM, and the steady drum of rain against the window made the small cinderblock room feel like a quiet, isolated cocoon. Jordan shifted uncomfortably in his wheelchair, a sudden, urgent pressure making him wince. He hurriedly pulled the Pinnacle Vanguard Pro onto his lap, his fingers finding the keys.

*I need to use the bathroom again,* the crisp, Australian-accented voice of *EchoType* announced to the room.

"I'll help!" Aubrie volunteered instantly, springing up from the lower bunk.

The words tumbled out a fraction of a second too quickly. Madison, sitting at the desk, slowly turned around and raised a highly skeptical, teasing eyebrow.

"Shut up," Aubrie muttered, her cheeks instantly blooming into a vivid, glowing crimson.

Madison just grinned. "Actually," she said, standing up, "do you mind if I join you two? If we're going to be a suite, I should probably learn how to help him safely, just in case you aren't around."

Now both women were blushing, a rosy tint coloring Madison's cheeks as the reality of their shared, cramped living situation settled over them. Aubrie nodded, gripping the handles of Jordan's wheelchair, and steered him into the small, connecting ADA-compliant bathroom with Madison following close behind.

Inside the small room, the fluorescent light hummed. Jordan looked between the two women. Even with his poor vision, he could see the lingering flush on their faces. He found himself wondering, with a tentative, fragile hope, exactly how they felt about him. The memory of Aubrie’s warm, grounding hug from earlier that evening was still a vivid, comforting weight on his shoulders. She was the absolute antidote to his mother, and he desperately wanted more of those hugs.

But right now, the physical necessity of the moment was pressing.

As they prepared to help him with the transfer, a sudden, awkward realization hit both Aubrie and Madison simultaneously: they both desperately needed to use the toilet, too.

The radical "no privacy" rule they had casually agreed upon earlier in the afternoon was suddenly put to the ultimate, immediate test. They looked at Jordan, who was medically vulnerable, trapped in his chair, and prone to severe system overloads. A silent, shared logic passed between the two women: they could not risk looking away or stepping out, fearing he might suffer a silent seizure, a sudden fall, or another panicked meltdown if left unanchored.

The clinical reality of their shared space overrode any lingering social conditioning. With a profound, judgment-free respect, they navigated the cramped quarters. They didn't force him to face the wall, and they didn't avert their eyes. They assisted him with his transfer to the grab bars and, out of pure, pragmatic necessity, shared the space to take care of their own needs. It was an incredibly vulnerable moment, stripping away the last vestiges of pretense, and replacing it with an absolute, unbreakable foundation of trust.

When it was over, they smoothly coordinated his transfer back into the wheelchair. Madison took the handles and wheeled him back out into Room 214. The three of them took turns washing their hands at the roll-under sink, a quiet, domestic rhythm already forming between them.

"Okay," Madison sighed, stretching her arms. "It's early, but today has been a marathon. I'm getting into my pajamas."

She pulled a t-shirt and sleep shorts from her dresser and casually began to change right there in the room, true to their pact. Aubrie followed suit, reaching for her own sleepwear.

Midway through pulling her shirt over her head, Aubrie paused. She noticed Jordan sitting completely still in his wheelchair. He was staring longingly at the closed drawer of his dresser where his pajamas were folded, and then down at the stiff, restrictive denim jeans he had been trapped in all day.

Aubrie’s heart broke as the realization hit her. He wasn't just tired. He couldn't get changed. He literally did not know how to do it himself.

She quickly finished pulling her shirt down and knelt in front of his wheelchair. Madison, noticing the shift in energy, stopped and stepped over as well.

"Jordan?" Aubrie asked softly. "Do you... do you need help getting changed?"

Jordan swallowed hard, his hands trembling as he rested them on his laptop keyboard. He felt a deep, burning wave of mortification, but he nodded. *Yes,* he typed quickly.

"We've got you, J," Madison said, her voice completely devoid of pity, filled only with a practical, sisterly warmth.

Working together, they helped him navigate the difficult process. They assisted him out of his stiff day clothes and carefully guided his arms and legs into the soft, comfortable fabric of his pajamas.

"There," Aubrie said, smoothing the collar of his shirt. "Much better. And don't worry about tomorrow morning. We'll help you into your day clothes, too."

Jordan looked at them, his chest tight. A cold, terrifying thought crept into his mind. He waited for the sneer. He waited for the laughter. He was absolutely terrified that this—this gentle, uncomplaining assistance—was just a cruel, elaborate joke, and that at any second, they would realize what a massive, helpless burden he truly was.

Aubrie and Madison saw the raw, unmistakable terror flash in his green eyes.

"Hey," Madison said fiercely, dropping to one knee so she was at eye level with him. "Look at me. Helping you is *never* going to be a problem. Ever. Do you understand?"

"You are not a burden, Jordan," Aubrie whispered, her voice thick with emotion as she rested her hand over his. "We promise."

The absolute sincerity in their voices slowly chipped away at the ice in his chest. He let out a long, shaky breath, allowing himself to believe them.

"Alright, let's get you into bed," Madison said cheerfully, breaking the heavy tension.

They maneuvered his chair next to Bed A and provided the steady leverage he needed to transfer onto the mattress. Once he was settled under the blankets, the room felt incredibly peaceful.

Aubrie leaned over the bed. She didn't hesitate; she wrapped her arms around him in a warm, lingering hug. "Goodnight, Jordan," she whispered into his shoulder, giving him exactly the comfort he had been craving.

"Goodnight, J," Madison called out softly, giving his arm a gentle squeeze before she climbed the wooden ladder to the top bunk, Bed C.

Aubrie walked over to the wall switch, plunging the cinderblock room into a quiet, safe darkness. She climbed into the bottom bunk, Bed B, pulling her own blankets up to her chin.

For the first time in his entire life, Jordan lay in the dark, surrounded by the sound of steady, peaceful breathing, and realized he wasn't afraid. Good night.
