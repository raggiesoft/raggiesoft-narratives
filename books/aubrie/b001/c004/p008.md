As the laughter from Aubrie's embarrassed flush began to settle, Zoë turned her analytical gaze toward Jordan's dead, bulky machine.

"The data indicates your current hardware is entirely insufficient for continuous use," Zoë observed, her crisp British consonants cutting through the room's chatter. "A dead battery poses a critical communication failure. Fortunately, I have a logical solution. My family resides just down the road in Christiansburg. I have a spare, fully functional laptop at home."

She pulled out her mPhone. "I will ring my father and have him bring it round."

Jordan's body instantly locked up. His eyes went wide, and his chest tightened with a sudden, suffocating panic. A gift. She was offering him a gift. In Aubrie Sinclair’s household, there was no such thing as a free offering. Everything had a string attached; everything was a transaction, usually paid for with guilt, shame, or severe punishment. He frantically shook his head, waving his hands to stop her, but lacking his text-to-speech software, he was trapped in his own silence.

"It's no trouble at all," Zoë added, misinterpreting his panic as mere politeness as she held the phone to her ear. "Dad? Yes, it's Zoë. Could you do me a massive favour..."

For the next twenty minutes, Jordan sat in a state of mounting dread. It wasn't just the fear of the gift that paralyzed him; it was the realization of *who* was bringing it. A father. He was an eighteen-year-old male sitting in a college dorm suite assigned to five young women, and a protective "girl dad" was on his way. He knew the stereotypes. He expected rage. He expected to be yelled at, accused, and immediately thrown out of the room.

At 6:05 PM, Zoë's phone buzzed. "Right, he's in the lobby," she announced. "I'll just pop down in the lift and escort him up."

When the heavy wooden door clicked shut behind her, Jordan's breathing grew shallow. Aubrie, noticing his sheer terror, gently rested her hand on his arm. "It's okay, Jordan," she whispered softly.

A few minutes later, the door swung open again. Zoë walked in, followed by a tall, broad-shouldered man with kind eyes and a sturdy, protective presence. This was Daniel Brooks.

"Dad, as I was explaining downstairs, there has been a massive administrative error," Zoë began, gesturing around the cramped space. "Housing mistakenly assigned a male to our suite due to a naming confusion."

Daniel stepped into the room. He carried a sleek, silver laptop case under one arm. He stopped, his eyes sweeping over the crowded space. He saw Mark sitting respectfully on the floor near Hannah. He saw his daughter’s new roommates. And then, his gaze landed on Jordan. He saw the boy in the wheelchair, practically vibrating with fear, his knuckles white as he gripped his armrests, bracing for the inevitable explosion of paternal anger.

Daniel's brow furrowed slightly in confusion. He slowly turned his head to look back at the heavy wooden door they had just walked through. His eyes traced the brass fixtures embedded in the solid wood. He saw the standard peephole at standing height. Then, his gaze dropped lower, resting on the second peephole drilled perfectly at wheelchair level.

The pieces clicked together instantly in Daniel's mind. The university hadn't just put a boy in a girls' suite; they had put a boy who desperately needed an accessible room into the *only* accessible room available on the floor.

Daniel looked back at the five young women, taking in their relaxed posture and the protective, caring way Aubrie was sitting next to Jordan's chair. They weren't uncomfortable. They weren't threatened. They had already accepted this frightened young man as their own.

The tension in Daniel's broad shoulders melted away, replaced by a profound, warming wave of empathy. His heart ached for the sheer terror radiating from the boy.

Daniel bypassed the girls and walked directly over to Jordan. He didn't loom over him; he knelt down so they were eye-to-eye, offering a warm, incredibly gentle smile.

"You must be Jordan," Daniel said, his voice a deep, reassuring rumble that carried the same distinct London accent as his daughter, but with absolutely zero anger. "Zoë tells me your computer gave up the ghost. I'm Daniel."

He gently placed the laptop case on Jordan's desk and popped the lid open. The machine booted almost instantaneously.

"It's an older Pinnacle Vanguard Pro," Zoë explained, stepping up beside her father. "I wiped Quantum OS off it ages ago. It currently runs a custom distribution of Aura Glade. It is visually identical to what you are used to, but requires a fraction of the processing power."

She tapped the trackpad, bringing up a clean, minimalist text box. "I also pre-installed a program called *EchoType*. It synthesizes speech locally, meaning you won't experience latency issues. And don't worry about network connectivity; the wireless card driver on this model was notoriously stubborn, so I manually compiled a patch in the terminal to force the hardware handshake a while back. The connection is entirely stable."

"It's yours to use for as long as you need it, son," Daniel finished for her, placing the power adapter gently on the desk. "No strings attached."

Jordan stared at the man, his mind struggling to process the interaction. There was no shouting. There was no accusation. There was just a father looking at him not as an intruder, but with the same unconditional kindness his daughter had shown. In that simple, quiet moment in Room 214, a tiny seed was planted in Jordan's chest—the fragile, miraculous hope that maybe, just maybe, he was finally safe.
