# Part 13: The Gander Lift – 8:15 PM (Newfoundland Daylight Time)

After the emotional welcome at the airport, the massive group of eighteen split into two convoys. Daniel Brooks, managing the primary logistics, guided the majority of the chosen family to a cozy, local hotel in the center of town.

However, Jordan, Aubrie, Madison, and Zoë were going with Lauren. To truly complete the recreation of Operation Yellow Ribbon, they were staying in Lauren’s childhood home.

The van pulled into the driveway of a charming, two-story house with a wide wooden front porch. The evening air was crisp, carrying the faint, clean scent of the North Atlantic and woodsmoke. Lauren’s parents had already headed inside to prepare the guest rooms and warm up the food they had brought from the terminal.

As the hydraulic lift lowered Jordan’s massive, 350-pound power chair onto the driveway, his stomach suddenly twisted into a knot of familiar panic.

He looked at the front of the house. There were four steep, wooden steps leading up to the porch, and a raised threshold at the front door. The house was a classic Newfoundland build, but it was fundamentally not wheelchair accessible.

For eighteen years, architectural barriers like this were the catalyst for Aubrie Sinclair’s most vicious abuse. If a building lacked a ramp, his mother would loudly sigh, roll her eyes, and berate him for being an immovable, burdensome anchor. The physical obstacle was always his fault.

Jordan instinctively pulled his hands off his joystick, his breathing growing shallow. He didn't want to be a problem. He didn't want to ruin the night.

Before Aubrie could lean down to comfort him, the front door swung open. Two tall, broad-shouldered men stepped out onto the porch. They had Lauren's dark hair and the same bright, observant eyes. These were her older brothers, Liam and Connor.

"Alright, let's get you out of the cold, mate," Liam smiled, jogging down the wooden steps with Connor right behind him.

"The house isn't accessible," Jordan quickly typed on his mPhone, holding the screen up with trembling fingers. *I am too heavy. The chair is too heavy. I don't want to be a burden.*

Liam stopped at the bottom of the steps. He looked at the phone screen, then looked Jordan directly in the eyes. His expression softened, completely devoid of pity, replaced entirely by a fierce, protective warmth.

"Jordan, look at me," Liam said, his voice carrying the deep, melodic lilt of the island. "You are in Gander. The word 'burden' doesn't exist in our dictionary."

Connor stepped up next to his brother, clapping a heavy, reassuring hand on Liam’s shoulder.

"I was twelve years old, and Liam was fourteen when the airspace closed in 2001," Connor explained softly. "We didn't know what was happening, but suddenly our town was flooded with seven thousand stranded, terrified people. We spent three days straight doing nothing but carrying luggage, setting up heavy canvas cots in the school gymnasium, and hauling supplies. We moved literal tons of gear for absolute strangers."

"So, trust me," Liam grinned, slapping the heavy metal armrest of the power chair. "Moving one bloke and his chair up a few steps so he can sleep in a warm bed? That's barely a warm-up for us."

The brothers didn't hesitate, and they didn't ask for permission to engineer a solution. They just went to work.

"Lauren, Aubrie, you two take his arms. Madison, Zoë, grab his legs," Connor directed with easy, cheerful authority. "We're going to do a simple team lift, just like moving a king onto a throne."

With Aubrie and Lauren supporting his upper body and Madison and Zoë gently lifting his legs, they hoisted Jordan seamlessly out of the heavy chair and carried him up the four wooden steps, setting him gently into a plush armchair just inside the front doorway.

Then came the machine.

A 350-pound power chair is a massive, awkward piece of engineering. But Liam and Connor didn't sigh. They didn't complain about the weight or roll their eyes at the inconvenience. They grabbed the heavy steel anchor points on the base of the chair, counted to three, and heaved.

With a grunt of sheer Newfoundland willpower, the two brothers muscled the massive machine up the stairs, over the threshold, and directly into the living room, setting it down gently on the hardwood floor right next to Jordan.

"Easy as pie," Liam laughed, shaking out his arms and wiping a bead of sweat from his forehead.

"Not a scratch on the paint," Connor added proudly, patting the joystick.

Jordan sat in the armchair, his heart hammering in his chest, staring at the two men. They were breathing hard, their faces flushed from the intense physical exertion, but they were smiling. They were genuinely, radiantly happy to have done the heavy lifting.

Lauren knelt beside Jordan’s chair, taking his hand.

"I told you," Lauren whispered, her eyes shining in the warm light of the living room. "You are safe here. You take up space, Jordan, and we are more than happy to make room for it."

For the very first time in his life, faced with a physical barrier that he could not conquer alone, Jordan wasn't made to feel like a broken inconvenience. He was just a guest, and his family was simply building the bridge he needed to get inside.

He reached out, pulling Lauren into a tight hug, the warmth of the Newfoundland home finally chasing away the last lingering shadows of Virginia.
