# Part 4: The Ten-Thousand Foot Rule – 7:30 AM

The boarding area at the Meridian Express gate was quiet, populated entirely by the eighteen members of the chosen family. Through the floor-to-ceiling glass windows, the small regional jet sat parked on the tarmac, its engines whining with a low, mechanical hum.

Before the gate agent called for pre-boarding, Daniel Brooks knelt beside Jordan’s heavy-duty power chair. He carried the sleek Vanguard Pro laptop case over his shoulder.

"Alright, Jordan, let's talk about the logistics of this aircraft," Daniel said, his deep London accent exuding a calm, steady authority. "The aviation administration updated their electronic device policies a couple of years ago. We are allowed to use small devices like an mPhone from gate to gate, as long as they are in airplane mode. However, there is a strict rule regarding larger hardware."

Daniel tapped the Vanguard Pro case. "Because your laptop is heavy, it is classified as a physical safety hazard during takeoff and landing. It must remain zipped in a bag and stowed completely under the seat in front of you until the aircraft reaches ten thousand feet. And honestly, Jordan, the time we actually spend cruising above that altitude today is only going to be about ten minutes. It is hardly worth the effort to unearth the machine just to immediately pack it away again. I recommend just using your mPhone for this leg."

Jordan’s breath hitched slightly. His Vanguard Pro was his primary voice. Transitioning to the smaller screen of his mPhone meant typing with his thumbs, which his cerebral palsy made clumsy and exhausting.

"I know it is daunting to lose your primary communication tool," Daniel reassured him, reading the anxiety perfectly. "But it will only be for a very short time. And you will not be alone."

Aubrie immediately stepped up beside the wheelchair. She rested her hand on his shoulder, her dark eyes fierce and protective. "I'm right next to you, J," she promised. "You don't need a heavy laptop to talk to me. If you're scared, you squeeze my hand. If you need me to stop talking, you tap my knee. We have our own system."

Jordan let out a shaky breath, the tension in his shoulders dropping. He pulled his mPhone from his pocket, making sure it was secure, and gave Daniel and Aubrie a firm nod. He was ready.

The boarding process for a regional jet required a heavily coordinated effort. Because the aircraft door was too narrow for the massive 350-pound power chair, Jordan had to leave his mobility fortress at the end of the jet bridge to be loaded into the cargo hold. With seamless, practiced grace, Mark and Lauren executed a supportive transfer, helping Jordan walk the few halting steps onto the aircraft and into the second row of the cabin.

The regional jet featured an asymmetrical seating arrangement: a single row of seats on the left, a narrow aisle, and two seats on the right.

Daniel took his assigned place in the single seat on the left side of the aisle. Jordan was carefully guided into the window seat on the right side, giving him an unobstructed view of the wing and the Roanoke landscape. Aubrie slid into the aisle seat directly next to him, completing the protective perimeter.

As the rest of the eighteen-person family filled the rows behind them, the flight attendant secured the cabin doors.

Aubrie reached down and took Jordan’s left hand, lacing her fingers securely through his. Jordan leaned his head back against the headrest, his right hand gripping the plastic armrest tightly as the regional jet pushed back from the gate.

Across the narrow aisle, Daniel caught Jordan's eye. He didn't offer a patronizing thumbs-up; he simply gave a slow, reassuring nod, projecting absolute safety.

The aircraft turned onto the active runway. The twin engines roared to life, a deep, vibrating mechanical thunder that shook the cabin. Jordan squeezed Aubrie's hand with white-knuckled intensity. The acceleration pressed him heavily into the back of his seat, a sensation unlike anything he had ever felt.

For eighteen years, his world had been incredibly small, confined to a single apartment and the crushing gravity of his mother's control. But as the nose of the Meridian Express jet pitched upward, the wheels leaving the Virginia tarmac, that gravity finally broke.

Jordan looked out the small oval window. The ground fell away, the buildings and roads shrinking into miniature models. A sudden, overwhelming wave of exhilaration flooded his chest. He was flying. He was actually flying.

He looked away from the window and turned to Aubrie. He didn't have his laptop, and he couldn't speak a word, but the massive, brilliant smile lighting up his face communicated absolutely everything she needed to know.

Ten minutes later, a soft, melodic chime echoed through the cabin.

The PA system above them crackled to life with a brief burst of static.

"Ladies and gentlemen," the flight attendant's voice announced over the cabin speakers. "We have reached ten thousand feet, and you are now free to use your approved electronic devices. However, due to our extremely short time in the air today, we will not be offering a beverage or snack service. Please sit back, relax, and enjoy the quick flight to Washington."

Jordan’s brow furrowed. He stared at the back of the seat in front of him, his highly analytical, literal-processing mind immediately catching the geographic discrepancy.

He pulled out his mPhone, his thumbs moving deliberately over the glass keyboard. He angled the screen toward the aisle so both Aubrie and Daniel could read it.

*Why did she say Washington?* the text read on his screen. *IAD is Dulles International Airport. Dulles is in Loudoun and Fairfax counties. We are flying within Virginia, from Roanoke to Dulles. We are not flying to Washington D.C.*

Aubrie leaned over to read the screen, and a massive, delighted smile broke across her face. Even while hurtling through the sky at four hundred miles an hour for the very first time in his life, Jordan’s brilliant, logical brain was firing on all cylinders.

Across the narrow aisle, Daniel Brooks let out a warm, booming chuckle.

"You are geographically and absolutely correct, Jordan," Daniel smiled, his crisp British accent carrying easily over the hum of the twin engines. "The physical tarmac is squarely in Virginia. But the aviation industry bases its destination names on the primary metropolitan zone the airport serves. Because Dulles is the primary international gateway for the United States capital, Meridian Airlines simply calls it 'Washington' to avoid confusing international tourists who don't understand state boundaries."

Jordan tapped his fingers thoughtfully against the plastic armrest, processing the data. He typed another quick message on his mPhone.

*That is highly illogical. They should just use accurate coordinates.*

"I couldn't agree more, mate," Daniel grinned, leaning back in his seat.

Jordan swallowed, his throat suddenly feeling incredibly dry. Between the nervous adrenaline of the TSA checkpoint, the sheer thrill of the takeoff, and the dry, recirculated cabin air, he was parched. But the flight attendant had just explicitly announced there would be no service.

Aubrie, hyper-observant as always, noticed him swallow hard and lick his dry lips.

"Well, since Meridian Express is depriving you of a beverage service on your inaugural flight, I came prepared," Aubrie announced.

She reached down into her backpack, which was stowed beneath the seat in front of her. Knowing the strict, predictable timelines of short regional hops and understanding that Jordan might need a quick comfort item, she had stopped at a kiosk in the Roanoke terminal right after they cleared Officer Miller's security check.

She proudly produced a cold bottle of apple juice, twisting the plastic cap open for him to bypass his fine-motor struggles, and handed it directly to him.

Jordan looked at the apple juice, and then up at Aubrie. His mother had never anticipated his physical needs without turning it into a heavy, transactional burden. But here, suspended thousands of feet in the air, Aubrie was simply taking care of him because she wanted to.

He took a long, refreshing sip of the cold juice, the sweet taste cutting right through his dry throat, and looked back out the oval window. The rolling green patchwork of the Virginia landscape slipped past below them. He wasn't just surviving his first flight; surrounded by his chosen family and sipping his favorite drink, he was completely safe.
