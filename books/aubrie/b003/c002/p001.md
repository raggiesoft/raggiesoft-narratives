# Part 1: The Gander Morning – 8:00 AM (Newfoundland Daylight Time)

The morning light filtering through the living room curtains of Lauren's childhood home was soft and cool. Jordan woke up slowly, the heavy, weighted Newfoundland quilt pressing down securely against his chest.

He didn't move immediately. For the first time in his life, waking up didn't trigger an instant spike of adrenaline. He was perfectly sandwiched in the massive, makeshift floor bed. Aubrie was still fast asleep on his right side, her arm draped comfortably over his waist. Madison was curled up on his left, breathing evenly. Zoë and Lauren were already up, their spots at the edges of the mattress neatly made.

Jordan lay there, staring at the rustic wooden ceiling, listening to the quiet sounds of the house. From the kitchen, he could hear the gentle clatter of plates, the low, melodic lilt of Lauren's parents talking, and the rich smell of brewing coffee and frying bacon.

There was no yelling. There was no heavy, oppressive tension waiting for him to make a mistake.

Aubrie stirred next to him, blinking her dark eyes open. She looked at Jordan, a soft, sleepy smile spreading across her face. "Morning, J. We're actually in Canada."

Jordan couldn't type without his Vanguard Pro, which was parked next to his power chair near the hearth, but he nodded, his own smile mirroring hers.

Ten minutes later, with Lauren's help, Jordan was transferred into his power chair and rolling into the wide, sunlit kitchen.

The dining table was entirely covered. Lauren’s parents had prepared a massive, traditional Newfoundland breakfast: thick slices of homemade bread, eggs, bacon, fresh berries, and steaming mugs of tea. Liam and Connor were already sitting at the table, casually drinking coffee.

"Morning, Jordan! Grab a spot right here, mate," Liam grinned, pulling a chair away so Jordan could roll his heavy Vanguard Pro perfectly up to the edge of the wood table.

Lauren’s mother immediately placed a fully loaded plate on his tray table, completely unbothered by the logistics. "Eat up, darling. Let me know if you want more tea."

Jordan looked at the massive plate of food, and then at Lauren's parents. His hands hovered over his keyboard. The old, traumatic programming installed by his mother, Aubrie Sinclair, flared up hard. Food, shelter, and care were never free in Virginia. They were heavy, transactional burdens held over his head to prove how useless he was. He was consuming their resources. He was taking up their space.

He looked down, his breathing hitching slightly as the anxiety built in his chest. *I am sorry,* he typed quickly, his bright green text illuminating the screen. *I am taking too much. I do not want to be a burden on your family.*

Lauren, who was sitting right next to him, gently put her fork down. She didn't roll her eyes or sigh. She reached out and placed her hand firmly over his.

"J, look at me," Lauren said, her voice entirely devoid of pity, replaced by a fierce, grounding warmth. "We talked about this on the porch last night. The word 'burden' does not exist in this house. You are our guest. My parents made this food specifically because they wanted you to have it. There is no debt here. You owe us absolutely nothing except to eat your breakfast."

"She's right, son," her father added from across the table, his eyes kind but unyielding. "You're family now. And family takes up space."

Jordan let out a shaky breath, the tight knot of anxiety in his chest completely unraveling. He picked up his fork, taking his first bite of the homemade bread. It was the best thing he had ever tasted.

"Now," Lauren smiled, leaning back in her chair. "Once everyone is fed, we are heading out into town. We've got an itinerary."

Jordan immediately booted up his NeoEdit terminal, ready to log the data. *Where are we going?*

"We are going to explore the exact history we talked about on the flight," Lauren explained. "First stop is the North Atlantic Aviation Museum. They have a specific exhibit called 'Safe Haven' that documents everything this town did during the five days the airspace was closed. They actually have a piece of steel from the World Trade Center on display there, donated by the firefighters in New York."

Jordan's eyes widened. He hadn't realized the physical history was actually preserved here.

"It's not just the museum, either," Connor chimed in, taking a sip of his coffee. "There's the Compassion Monument right outside the Gander Town Hall. It's a massive steel girder from the Twin Towers sitting on top of a 9,000-pound piece of local granite. The Americans gave it to us to commemorate the lives lost and to thank the town for taking in the seven thousand passengers."

"We're also going to follow the Interpretive Panels," Lauren added. "They are set up all around town at the key locations—the schools, the ice rink, the churches—explaining exactly what happened at each site during Operation Yellow Ribbon. You're going to see exactly why this town operates the way it does."

Jordan looked around the kitchen, at the warm, smiling faces of the family who had carried him into their home without a second thought. He finally understood. The radical hospitality wasn't an act. It was completely baked into the DNA of the ground he was sitting on.
