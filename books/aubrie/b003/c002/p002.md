# Part 2: The Cognitive Redirect – 8:45 AM

The thick slice of homemade bread on Jordan's tray table felt impossibly heavy.

He managed to take two bites, but the sheer, overwhelming magnitude of the morning was causing a neurological traffic jam in his brain. For his entire life, meals with Aubrie Sinclair were battlegrounds of guilt and transaction. Here, sitting in a warm Newfoundland kitchen, being served a massive breakfast by people who genuinely wanted him to eat it, the emotional contrast was simply too much to process.

His autistic nervous system began to short-circuit under the weight of the profound gratitude. His hands dropped to his lap. He stopped chewing, his breathing growing shallow as he stared blankly at the plate. He was completely freezing up, trapped in an emotional shutdown.

Aubrie, who knew his subtle physical cues better than anyone, immediately recognized what was happening. She didn't draw attention to it or ask him if he was okay—questions only demanded more processing power. Instead, she calmly picked up his fork.

"I've got you, J," Aubrie murmured softly. She cut a small piece of eggs and bacon and gently held the fork to his lips.

Jordan opened his mouth, accepting the bite. On his other side, Lauren smoothly picked up his mug of tea, holding it carefully by the handle so he could take a slow, warm sip without having to coordinate his trembling hands. Between the two of them, they seamlessly took over the physical motor demands of eating, eliminating the friction so he could just breathe.

But Jordan was still sinking, his eyes bright with unshed tears, completely overwhelmed by the radical safety of the room.

From across the table, Madison watched his chest hitch. She immediately bumped her shoulder against Zoë's, leaning in to whisper a quick, tactical plan.

Zoë pushed her glasses up the bridge of her nose, her expression shifting into one of intense, clinical pragmatism. She unlocked her mPad and cleared her throat, projecting her voice clearly over the ambient noise of the kitchen.

"I was just reviewing our playlists for the drive today," Zoë announced, her tone completely detached and matter-of-fact. "And I noticed a massive discrepancy. Madison has logged *Vector Sequence's* album *Velocity* as being released in 1986. However, my data clearly indicates the band transitioned to that specific pop-synth structure in 1983."

Jordan’s eyes snapped away from his plate, locking onto Zoë.

"I'm telling you, Zoë, it's 1986!" Madison argued playfully, throwing her hands up. "The title track literally hit number one that summer."

"That is statistically improbable based on Julian Vance's solo touring schedule," Zoë countered smoothly, not breaking character. "I will need external verification."

It was a brilliant, highly targeted cognitive redirect. By deliberately introducing a blatant factual error regarding one of Jordan's deepest, most established special interests, Madison and Zoë provided an emergency exit for his brain. They gave him a structured, logical puzzle to solve, pulling his processing power away from the overwhelming emotional data and redirecting it toward concrete, objective facts.

Jordan's hands immediately flew up from his lap to his keyboard.

*Your data lacks structural nuance, Zoë,* the bright neon-green text flashed across his NeoEdit terminal. *The 1983 self-titled 'Vector Sequence' album was strictly transitional. They were still clinging to their prog-rock roots while only hinting at pop-synth.*

Jordan paused for a fraction of a second, his fingers flying across the keys with absolute, grounding certainty.

*Madison is correct. The pop-synth structure did not fully solidify until 'Velocity' in 1986,* the text continued to scroll. *But they never completely abandoned prog-rock. The 'Velocity' album still contains the ten-minute suite 'Cascade (Part 1: In the Glow of the Grid / Part 2: The Last Cascade).' They continued hiding heavy prog-rock architecture inside pop albums even into 1991's 'We Can't Rewind,' specifically with the track 'Driving the Final Relay.'*

"See? The master has spoken," Madison grinned, pulling out her phone and immediately hitting play on the album's lead single. The heavy, familiar 1980s synthesizer beat instantly filled the kitchen.

Zoë offered Jordan a crisp, respectful nod. "I stand corrected. Thank you for the precise data log and structural analysis, Jordan."

The heavy, paralyzing weight in Jordan's chest instantly vanished. The familiar, structured comfort of the music and the successful execution of his specialized knowledge completely reset his nervous system. He let out a long exhale, his shoulders dropping back into a relaxed posture.

He looked at Aubrie, then at Lauren, and gave them a firm nod. He reached out, taking the fork back from Aubrie’s hand. He was ready to finish his breakfast on his own.

Thirty minutes later, the massive eighteen-person pact was bundled into their rented, accessible passenger vans. The crisp, clean North Atlantic air blasted through the open windows as Daniel Brooks put the lead van in drive.

They pulled out of the driveway and onto the quiet, tree-lined streets of Gander. It was time to leave the sanctuary of the house and head straight into the heart of the town’s history, starting with the North Atlantic Aviation Museum and the steel girder of the Compassion Monument.
