# Book 1: The Eleventh Child, Off to College

## Chapter 1: The Administrative Prelude – Wednesday, August 20th, 2014

### Part 1: The Drive – 10:00 AM

The journey began on a Wednesday in late August. The seven-hour drive from the flat, humid landscape of Hampton Roads to the rolling hills of the New River Valley was a tense affair. Aubrey sat silently in the passenger seat, watching the world change outside his window. Without corrective lenses, the passing scenery was a permanent, impressionistic blur of green and gray, forcing him to squint until a dull, familiar ache began to throb behind his eyes.

Beside him, his mother, Aubrie Sinclair, filled the car with a steady stream of anxious instructions. Due to his untreated hearing impairment, her voice was often a muffled, droning cadence, though her sharp, reprimanding tone always managed to cut through the static.

"Are you even listening to me, Aubrey?" she snapped, a sudden movement in his peripheral vision demanding his attention. "It is incredibly rude to ignore your mother when she is trying to prepare you for the real world."

She completely refused to acknowledge the reality of his silence. For eighteen years, Aubrie Sinclair maintained the delusion that his absolute inability to speak was simply a behavioral choice—a prolonged act of stubborn defiance, fueled by a desire to simply not talk to her.

With a heavy, resigned exhale, Aubrey reached down to the floorboard and hauled his battered laptop onto his lap. It was a massive, obsolete machine running Quantum OS 7 Home Basic, its cooling fan instantly whirring to life with a strained whine. His mother's jaw tightened visibly. She despised the machine, viewing it as an enabling crutch for his "attitude," but she tolerated it reluctantly because it was the only way she could force a response from him.

He hunched awkwardly over the keyboard, leaning his face uncomfortably close to the dim display just to make out the letters. Slowly, he typed a single sentence and pressed the execute key.

*I am listening,* the operating system's built-in text-to-speech engine announced, the voice a flat, robotic, and heavily digitized monotone.

"Well, you could just open your mouth and say it," she muttered, turning her eyes back to the road.

They eventually pulled off the highway and checked into a hotel in Christiansburg, one of the several clustered around the busy I-81 interchange at exit 118C. Aubrey dragged his heavy suitcase down the corridor, his head pounding, longing for a quiet space to rest his eyes and ears.

When his mother pushed open the door to their room, Aubrey squinted through the haze of his poor vision. The large shape occupying the center of the room was undeniable. There was only one queen-sized bed.

"Oh, dear," his mother said with a sigh that held no real disappointment. "I asked for two beds, but this must have been all they had available with the students coming back to town. Well," she said with a shrug that made it clear it wasn't a question, "I guess we'll just have to make do for a couple of nights".

### Part 2: The Shared Bed – 9:00 PM

Aubrey lay stiffly on the very edge of his side of the bed that night. Without the aid of corrective lenses, the unfamiliar hotel room was a featureless, terrifying void, and the low, muffled hum of the air conditioner barely registered through his hearing impairment. What he could sense, with agonizing clarity, was his mother's breathing—a familiar, oppressive presence just inches away. He stared into the darkness, his body rigid with tension, counting the hours until Friday. Friday meant he would finally have his own bed in his own room.

“Aubrey dear, come comfort your mother,” Aubrie murmured in the dark.

Before he could brace himself, she reached out and pulled him close. Her arm draped heavily over his chest, pinning him against her side like a plush toy meant to soothe her own anxieties. The forced physical proximity made his skin crawl. It was a suffocating, infantilizing boundary that she crossed routinely, completely ignoring the fact that he was an eighteen-year-old man.

Without his heavy laptop resting on his knees, he was entirely voiceless. He couldn't even attempt to type out a protest, and his cerebral palsy made it strenuous and exhausting to try and physically pry himself away from her unyielding grip. He felt smothered, trapped in the oppressive heat of the hotel blankets, but he was used to it by now.

He hated it, a quiet, desperate revulsion that burned in his chest, but he put up with it. What could he do anyways? She was his mother, the absolute dictator of his small, silent universe, and she was all he had ever known.

## Chapter 2: Verification – Thursday, August 21st, 2014

### Part 1: The Poly Pass – 11:00 AM

Thursday was spent on the bustling campus of Commonwealth Polytechnic Institute. The sheer volume of students moving around the wide brick walkways was overwhelming, their voices a muffled, chaotic drone against Aubrey's ears. Their first stop was the University Card Center to get his official student ID, known on campus as the Poly Pass.

Aubrey wheeled himself up to the counter, his heavy, outdated laptop resting securely on his lap. His mother hovered right behind his chair, her rigid posture radiating a silent, familiar disapproval.

The student worker behind the desk smiled brightly. "Hi there! Welcome to CPI. Are you ready to get your Poly Pass?"

Aubrey hunched forward, leaning uncomfortably close to his dim screen as he slowly typed out his response. He hit the execute key.

*Yes, I am,* the robotic, monotone voice of the OS 7 text-to-speech engine announced to the room.

Aubrey instinctively braced himself for the usual reaction—a confused stare, a pitying tilt of the head, or his mother's inevitable, exasperated sigh. Instead, the student worker didn't miss a single beat.

"Awesome," the worker said cheerfully, completely unbothered by the synthesized voice. "Let's get you set up in the system. Just look right at that camera for me."

It was a tiny, startling moment of pure, uncomplicated acceptance. Aubrey felt a small flicker of relief as the camera flashed.

A minute later, the card printer whirred to life, and the worker handed the fresh plastic card across the counter. Aubrey took it in his hands. He brought the card up inches from his nose, squinting hard at the glossy surface just to force the blurry shapes into focus. After a few seconds of intense concentration, the letters finally resolved. It was a plastic card with the name AUBREY JORDAN FULLER printed starkly beneath his nervous face.

### Part 2: Educational Accessibility – 2:00 PM

Their next and final stop was the Office of Educational Accessibility, to verify that all his accommodations—both academic and residential—were in place.

"Hello," Aubrie Sinclair said to the counselor, leaning over the desk to hand over a thin folder. "We're just here to verify that all of my son Aubrey's accommodations are in order for the semester".

The counselor, a sharp-eyed woman named Ms. Higgins, took the folder with a polite smile, but immediately shifted her gaze past the mother and addressed her questions directly to the student.

"Yes, Aubrey, I have your file right here," she said.

To Aubrey, her voice was a dull, muffled hum. He leaned forward in his wheelchair, instinctively tilting his head and cupping a hand slightly behind his ear to catch the sound. Ms. Higgins paused, her gaze dropping to his cupped hand for a fraction of a second. Without making a spectacle of it, she subtly raised her volume and began to enunciate her words much more clearly.

"We have you down for extra time on exams and access to a university-provided note-taker," she continued. "Does that all sound correct to you?"

Aubrey hauled his heavy laptop onto his lap to reply. He hunched awkwardly over the keyboard, bringing his face mere inches from the dim screen. He squinted intensely, his eyes straining just to make out the letters on the keys. Ms. Higgins watched this process closely, a slight frown creasing her forehead as she noted his extreme proximity to the monitor and the stiff, strained way his fingers struck the keys.

Before Aubrey could finish typing his response, the screen flickered and went entirely black. The old battery had died.

Aubrey froze, a familiar spike of panic hitting his chest. He braced himself for his mother's inevitable, exasperated sigh. But it didn't come from across the desk.

"Oh, looks like you need a charge," Ms. Higgins said gently, standing up. She gestured to an outlet right next to her desk. "You can plug it in right here. Take your time."

Relieved, Aubrey maneuvered his chair, plugged in the bulky adapter, and waited for the slow Quantum OS 7 Home Basic operating system to boot back up. Once it did, he opened *SimpleSpeak*, a free, clunky third-party text-to-speech program featuring a basic text box and a crude animated face that moved along with the words. He relied on it because it was the only application his mother had ever allowed him to download. He finished typing and clicked play.

*Yes. That is correct,* the robotic, digitized voice announced to the room.

Aubrie Sinclair's jaw tightened instantly at the mechanical sound. She despised the software, viewing it as nothing more than an enabling crutch for a prolonged act of defiance. She was entirely convinced that if he just stopped being so stubbornly dramatic, he could open his mouth and talk perfectly well.

Ms. Higgins looked back down at the sparse medical file Aubrie Sinclair had submitted. It noted a physical disability requiring a wheelchair and requested basic academic extensions, but nothing more. As she looked back at the young man—who relied entirely on free software to speak, visibly struggled to see the screen, and showed clear signs of a hearing impairment—she realized the documentation was woefully incomplete.

More importantly, she recognized the rigid, suffocating posture of the mother hovering over him. Aubrie Sinclair was controlling the narrative, ensuring her son remained dependent. Without official medical paperwork, Ms. Higgins's hands were bureaucratically tied, but she knew she needed to speak to Aubrey alone.

"Excellent," Ms. Higgins continued, looking back at her screen. "And I also see that our office coordinated with Housing to get you an early move-in accommodation due to your disability. You are all set for tomorrow, Friday, at 9:00 AM. You'll be in Bennett Hall, Room 214, Bed A".

She then pulled a small appointment card from her desk drawer. "Now, Aubrey, because you're utilizing a university-provided note-taker, we require all students to complete a brief 'Accommodation Tech Orientation.' We use that time to link your student portal to our specific campus services."

Aubrie Sinclair frowned, her grip tightening on her purse. "Can't we just do that right now? I don't want him having to navigate all the way back here alone next week."

"I apologize, ma'am, but the system doesn't open the orientation portal until the first day of classes," Ms. Higgins lied smoothly. She wrote a date and time on the card, stood up, and deliberately reached past the mother to hand the card directly to Aubrey. "I've scheduled you for this coming Monday at 1:00 PM. It will just be you and me, Aubrey, and we'll make sure you have absolutely everything you need."

Aubrey took the card, his blurry vision making out the bold numbers.

With all the administrative tasks verified, they returned to the hotel for one final night in the shared bed. Tomorrow, he would move in. Tomorrow, his new life would begin.

## Chapter 3: Early Move-In – Friday, August 22nd, 2014

### Part 1: Room 214 – 9:00 AM

After his mother, Aubrie, left with a final wave and a promise to call that evening, Aubrey Jordan Fuller found himself truly alone for the first time in his new dorm room. With his untreated hearing impairment, the quiet of the empty room was a muffled, heavy silence, but it was a welcome change from the earlier chaos of moving in. He took a long, steadying breath and began to take a proper inventory of the small space—his space.

The room was tiny, clearly designed for two people but now cramped to fit three due to the university's housing crisis. He flipped the main switch, and a large fluorescent light recessed into the drop ceiling flickered on, bathing the painted cinderblock walls in a harsh, sterile glow. He immediately squinted; without corrective lenses, the abrupt, glaring light caused the sharp corners of the room to blur, sending a familiar, dull ache throbbing behind his eyes.

He rolled forward and looked back at the heavy, dark wood door he'd just entered. From the hallway, he'd tapped his new CPI Poly Pass on the reader, and the door had automatically swung *inward* to let him pass. On the inside wall, tucked over by the frame on the right, he saw the corresponding silver handicap push button. Rolling a few inches closer and narrowing his eyes to focus, he could just make out the brass fixtures embedded in the solid wood of the door: two distinct peepholes, one drilled at standard standing height, and a second one positioned perfectly at his eye level in the wheelchair.

To his immediate left was a closed door, which he knew led to the shared toilet room. He wheeled over and opened it, confirming what he'd seen on the housing tour: a small, functional room containing only a toilet, but fitted with the necessary ADA-compliant grab bars. He saw the second door on the opposite wall, the one that connected to Room 216.

Back in the main room, he noted the sink and mirror area next to the toilet door. He rolled his wheelchair closer, a small smile touching his lips as he saw the open space underneath. It was a roll-under sink, intentionally designed without a cabinet so he could get close to the basin.

He wheeled toward the single window set in the center of the far wall and turned his chair around to take in the full layout of the room.

To his right, looking back toward the wooden entry door, was a standalone, single Twin XL bed with a solid wood frame—Bed A, his assigned spot. A matching wooden desk was wedged tightly between the headboard and the front wall of the room.

To his left, occupying the opposite cinderblock wall, was a sturdy set of wooden bunk beds.

Both bed setups currently held nothing but bare, clinical mattresses, waiting for the pillows and blankets he'd brought in his suitcase.

He counted the rest of the furniture. Two desks, two dressers, and two nightstands. This confirmed the room's original two-person design. His future roommates, the ones assigned to the bunk beds where the push button was hidden, would have to share a desk and a dresser. This was it. College.

### Part 2: The Awkward Outlet – 1:00 PM

He decided to set up his most important tool first: his laptop. He wheeled himself over to his desk, placed the heavy machine on it, and opened the lid. The battery icon blinked red, and a moment later, the screen went completely black. Dead.

He sighed, a silent puff of air, and squinted around the room for an outlet. He spotted one on the cinderblock wall, but its standard height from the floor was just awkwardly out of comfortable reach from his wheelchair.

He maneuvered his chair closer, stretching to try and plug in the bulky adapter. He could just barely touch the outlet, but he couldn't get the leverage to push the prongs in securely. He grunted with frustration, a small but significant reminder that even in an "accessible" room, the world wasn't always designed with him in mind.

"Hey there, need a hand?"

A muffled, friendly voice came from his open doorway. Aubrey flinched and turned, instinctively cupping a hand slightly behind his ear to catch the sound. A young woman with a lanyard and an "RA" name tag stood there, her expression kind. "I'm Isabel, one of the RAs for one of the other floors. Just doing my rounds, helping the RA of this floor".

Aubrey felt a flush of embarrassment at being caught struggling. With his laptop completely dead, he was entirely voiceless. Unable to speak, he simply gave a frantic nod and gestured helplessly to the power cord dangling from his hand.

Isabel saw the problem instantly. "Oh yeah, that's a terrible spot for an outlet. No problem at all. Let's make this easier".

He handed her the power strip that he pulled out of his backpack. She plugged the power strip into the wall outlet and then neatly ran the cord up to his desk, securing the strip on top with a piece of reusable adhesive she produced from her pocket. "There," she said with a smile, raising her volume slightly after noticing his cupped ear. "Now you can plug anything you want in right from your desk".

Aubrey immediately plugged in his adapter and waited for the slow Quantum OS 7 system to boot up. Once the desktop appeared, he opened *SimplySpeak*, hunching uncomfortably close to the monitor and squinting intensely to make out the keys as he typed. He hit the execute key.

*Wow. Thanks. Thank you,* the robotic, digitized voice announced to the room. Aubrey looked up, offering a genuinely grateful smile.

Isabel didn't bat an eye at the synthesized voice. "Don't mention it. Do you need a hand with anything else? Getting your bed set up?"

Aubrey hesitated, then nodded. He turned back to the screen and typed again.

*Actually, yeah. The pillows and blankets are kind of a pain.*

For the next ten minutes, Isabel helped him make his bed, placing his pillows and blankets exactly how he liked them. She even helped him unpack his main suitcase, putting his clothes away in the dresser drawers. She was efficient, friendly, and didn't make him feel like a burden.

"There you go," she said finally. "All set". She paused at the door. "Seriously, Aubrey, if you need any help with anything, you just find me or any of the other RAs, or the hall director. That's what we're here for".

Aubrey leaned close to the keyboard and typed a final message.

*I will. Thank you, Isabel. Do you know who my two roommates are? Or when they will arrive?*

Isabel gave him an apologetic look. "I'm sorry, I don't have that information," she explained. "Since I'm not the regular RA for this floor, I don't have access to the detailed room roster with names and arrival times. I'm just helping out with early check-ins today".

She then offered a reassuring smile. "But I do know that the main move-in day starts tomorrow morning. So, you can expect them to start arriving tomorrow at the soonest. Looks like you've got the whole suite to yourself for tonight!"

Aubrey nodded, a wave of both relief and nervousness washing over him. One night of peace before meeting the two strangers who would become his new roommates.

### Part 3: Stranded – 2:00 PM

Isabel gave him a final wave and left. Aubrey looked around his room, which now felt much more functional and comfortable. He easily plugged his heavy laptop into the power strip on his desk and watched the dim screen spring to life.

His original plan for the afternoon had been to venture outside, explore the sprawling campus, and learn exactly where his classes and the dining halls were located. However, as he wheeled toward the window and squinted through the glass, the clouds that had been gathering all morning finally opened up. A steady, pouring rain began to wash over the campus, thwarting his plans and trapping him inside.

He spent the rest of the day in his room alone, content in his new space. He had snacks and sandwiches his mom had packed in his bag, so he didn't have to brave the weather anyway. The steady drum of the rain made his quiet, solitary room feel like the coziest place in the world.

As evening eventually fell, the rain continued its soothing rhythm against the window. Aubrey finished the last of his sandwiches and felt a wave of exhaustion from the emotional and physical toll of the day. It was time for bed. He wheeled himself over to his dresser where Isabel had so neatly placed his clothes and pulled out a soft t-shirt and a pair of pajama pants. He looked at them, then down at the jeans and layered shirts he was still wearing.

A familiar knot of anxiety tightened in his stomach. At home, his mother would help him. Her assistance was a routine part of his day, a seamless act of care. Here, alone, the simple act of changing clothes felt like an insurmountable mountain. He thought about what Isabel had said: *if you need any help with anything*. But the thought of asking a complete stranger for help with something so personal filled him with a profound sense of mortification.

He sat there for a long time, weighing the physical discomfort of sleeping in his day clothes against the social agony of asking for help. The denim of his jeans felt restrictive, and he knew he would be hot and twisted in them by morning. With a sigh of resignation, he put the pajamas back in the drawer.

He maneuvered himself from his wheelchair onto his bed, a practiced but still strenuous motion. At least he could get his shoes off. He hooked the heel of one sneaker on the toe of the other and pushed until it came loose, dropping to the floor with a soft thud. He did the same with the second shoe. It was a small measure of comfort, but it came with a sharp cost. He stared at the shoes on the floor. He wouldn't be able to put them back on. He couldn't reach them from his chair, and even if he could, he didn't know how to tie the laces. His mother had never taught him. She had never prepared him for any of this.

Her parting words echoed in his mind, dripping with a pride that now felt corrosive. "I'm so proud of you, a real college kid now! An adult, living on your own".

Living on my own, he thought with a bitter pang of clarity. She had said it with a beaming smile, but what did it really mean? She had dropped him in a dorm room hours from home without ever teaching him how to dress himself, cook a simple meal, or navigate the hundred small tasks she had always just... done. The honest, painful truth began to settle in his chest: maybe she wasn't proud of his independence. Maybe she was just glad to be rid of the burden. The thought was a cold stone in his gut. All her talk of him being an adult felt like a convenient excuse to finally be free of him.

### Part 4: Bedtime – 10:00 PM

As evening fell, the rain continued its soothing rhythm against the window. Aubrey felt a wave of exhaustion from the emotional and physical toll of the day. It was time for bed. He wheeled himself over to his dresser where Isabel had so neatly placed his clothes and pulled out a soft t-shirt and a pair of pajama pants. He looked at them, then down at the jeans and layered shirts he was still wearing.

A familiar knot of anxiety tightened in his stomach. At home, his mother had always changed him; her assistance was a routine part of his day that she completely controlled. Here, alone, the simple act of changing clothes felt like an insurmountable mountain. He simply didn't know how to get himself changed. With a heavy sigh of resignation, he put the pajamas back in the drawer.

He maneuvered himself from his wheelchair onto his bed, a practiced but still strenuous motion. At least he had managed to get his shoes off earlier. Lying on top of the covers, still in his constricting day clothes and shoeless, he stared up at the dark ceiling.

At least he had the room to himself for tonight.

Tears welled in his eyes, blurring his already poor vision, and slipped silently down his cheeks. He cried himself to sleep in the dark, unfamiliar room. The tears were born of a deep, agonizing conflict: on one hand, he was overwhelmingly grateful to finally be away from his mother's suffocating presence, but the chilling, painful truth settling heavily in his chest was that she had purposefully left him helpless. He did not know how to take care of himself.

## Chapter 4: The Mix-Up – Saturday, August 23rd, 2014

### Part 1: Morning Noise – 8:00 AM

Aubrey woke up stiff and sore, the restrictive denim of his jeans clinging uncomfortably to his legs. He’d tossed and turned all night, his sleep shallow and plagued by a recurring sense of falling. Without corrective lenses, the ceiling above him was just a fuzzy, gray expanse, contributing to the dull ache already throbbing behind his eyes and the deep soreness in his back. He felt a profound exhaustion that had nothing to do with the physical effort of moving in. He was an adult on his own, just as his mother had said with that bright, brittle smile, and he was already failing.

The fragile morning quiet was shattered by the official start of freshman move-in day. Because of his untreated hearing impairment, the cacophony of shouting, laughing, and the rolling of heavy carts in the hallway outside his door wasn't crisp; it was a wall of muffled, booming pressure. The noise was a persistent, grating hum that vibrated straight through the floorboards and up into his bones, putting his entire nervous system on edge.

He managed to transfer back into his wheelchair and tried to focus on arranging his desk, but the anxiety from the night before mingled dangerously with the sensory assault. He had to lean inches away from the bindings just to read the titles of his textbooks, his head pounding with every passing minute. After struggling for nearly an hour just to get his books stacked, the exhaustion from his sleepless night and the overwhelming sensory overload became too much.

*I just need to rest,* he thought, rubbing his temples to fight back the headache. He didn't even have the energy to open his heavy laptop to type it out.

Defeated, he transferred from his wheelchair back to his bed. Still fully clothed in yesterday's layers, he pulled the blanket up over his head, desperate for just a few minutes of quiet. The steady, grating hum of vibrations from the hallway eventually lulled him back into a fitful, uneasy sleep.

### Part 2: An Unlikely Welcome – 10:30 AM

Around mid-morning, his door swung open. A young woman with her arms full of bags stopped short, seeing the room clearly already occupied, and a boy fast asleep in the single bed.

"Oh! I am so sorry," she said, her eyes wide with confusion. "I must have the wrong room".

Her voice cut through his sleep. Aubrey jolted awake, his heart hammering. He slowly sat up, disoriented, squinting at the blurry figure of a girl standing in his room. His stomach clenched; he was stiff, uncomfortable, and felt a flush of shame.

He frantically reached for his heavy laptop resting nearby, popping the lid open. He hunched close to the screen, his fingers clumsy as he typed into his *SimpleSpeak* software. He hit the execute key.

*Is this room 214?* the robotic voice asked, flat and emotionless.

"Yeah, but..." She didn't flinch at the synthesized voice. She just glanced at the name card on the open door. "My room is 214. I'm Aubrie Jean Kelly".

Aubrey’s own confused gaze followed hers to the door card. He squinted, struggling to make out the letters until they finally resolved. He saw her name, and his own right below it: *Aubrey Jordan Fuller*. He also saw another name: *Madison Elizabeth Scott*. The names hit him with the force of a physical blow. Females. His roommates were females.

The booming, grating noise vibrating from the hallway, the immediate presence of a stranger of the opposite gender, and the terrifying certainty that he was the one in the wrong room all converged at once. In a blind panic, he threw the blanket off and clumsily transferred himself from the bed into his wheelchair. He had to get out. He had to pack. But the panic rose too fast, and his control shattered entirely. The harsh light of the room became agonizingly bright, and the noise from the hallway transformed into a sharp, punishing physical pressure. He squeezed his eyes shut, and a loud, piercing cry tore from his throat. He began to rock back and forth violently in his wheelchair, his hands flying up to cover his ears as he tried desperately to block out a world that felt like it was crushing him.

The girl, Aubrie Jean, watched his reaction, and her own expression shifted from confusion to a deep, immediate understanding.

"It's okay," she said to her parents, who were standing worriedly in the hall behind her. "He's not angry. He's overwhelmed".

She put her things down and took a slow, deliberate step into the room. "Aubrey," she said, her voice quiet and even, steady enough to reach him through his panic. "My name is Aubrie. It's okay. You're safe. Focus on my voice".

He continued to rock and cry out, unable to stop.

"Is it okay if I put my hand on your shoulder?" she asked gently.

Slowly, fighting through the sheer terror gripping his chest, Aubrey gave a short, sharp nod. Aubrie Jean placed her hand firmly on his shoulder. Aubrey’s shoulders shuddered, and he gave another, almost desperate nod, a silent plea for more grounding. Understanding washed over Aubrie’s face. She moved behind his wheelchair and wrapped her arms around his shoulders in a firm, secure hug.

This was the first time in his life he had ever received an unconditional hug.

It was at that exact moment that the door opened again. A second young woman, Madison Scott, entered hauling a large box. She froze, taking in the scene and the sound of his fading cries. "Uh... is this room 214?"

Aubrie didn’t break her focus. "It is," she said quietly. "He's really overwhelmed. Could you maybe close the door?"

Madison immediately pushed the door closed, dulling the hallway noise. She looked at Aubrey, at his wrinkled clothes and the sheer distress on his face, and her heart went out to him. "What can I do?" she whispered compassionately.

"Just... be calm," Aubrie replied. "It'll pass. We just have to ride it out".

Madison nodded, pulling up a desk chair and sitting a few feet away, offering a quiet, non-judgmental presence. Over the next hour, as Aubrey’s piercing cries slowly subsided into shuddering breaths, the chaotic move-in continued. A girl with vibrant purple hair, Zoë Brooks, poked her head in from the connecting bathroom door, her eyes wide. "Uh, hi. I'm in 216. Is everything okay? There's a... guy in here. Is he helping you two with move in?"

Before Aubrie could explain, two more girls, Hannah Parker and Lauren Barnes, appeared behind Zoë, their expressions ranging from curious to concerned. Zoë took one look at the door card for Room 214 and the exhausted boy in the wheelchair, and her face set with determination. "Okay, something's definitely wrong here. I'm going to get the RA".

### Part 3: The Hall Director – 11:45 AM

Zoë, ever the pragmatist, slipped past the gathered girls and stepped into the chaotic hallway. It took her only a minute to locate a harried-looking young man with an "RA" lanyard directing a family with a rolling bin.

"Excuse me," Zoë said, tapping his shoulder. "Are you the RA for this floor? We have a significant logistical error in Room 214."

Henry followed Zoë back to the suite. He stepped inside, his eyes darting between the five young women and the exhausted, frightened boy in the wheelchair with a heavy laptop resting on his lap. He glanced at the door card, then back to the boy.

"Aubrey Fuller?" Henry asked.

Aubrey, drained but calm now that the initial sensory storm had passed, gave a small nod. A look of dawning horror crossed Henry's face. "Oh no. I think... I think Housing made a mistake. 'Aubrey' is usually a girl's name. I... I have to get the Hall Director."

Henry backed out of the room, pulling a black two-way radio from his belt. "Henry to Kelsey, priority," he said, his voice tight with panic. "I need you at Room 214. Immediately. Housing messed up big time."

The urgency in Henry's voice, combined with his frantic pacing outside the door, immediately caught the attention of the surrounding freshmen. Families paused mid-lift with mini-fridges and boxes. Whispers began to ripple down the corridor over the sounds of rolling carts.

"What's going on?" a girl from across the hall murmured to her roommate.

"I don't know, the RA looks freaked out," the other replied, craning her neck to look down the hall.

They glanced at the door card for 214—*Aubrey Jordan Fuller, Aubrie Jean Kelly, Madison Elizabeth Scott*—and naturally assumed the names belonged to three girls. Catching a brief glimpse of the boy in the wheelchair through the doorway, they, much like Zoë had initially, simply assumed he was a brother or a friend helping the girls with the heavy lifting. It made the staff's frantic reaction all the more baffling; why would an RA need to call the Hall Director on a two-way radio just because a family member was helping someone unpack?

A few minutes later, Kelsey, the Hall Director, hurried down the corridor and pushed through the small gathering of curious onlookers. She stepped into the crowded suite, taking in the scene. Her expression was weary but kind.

Henry quickly stepped to her side, his voice a frantic whisper that carried in the small room. "Kelsey, the roster lists 'Aubrey Fuller' for Bed A," he explained, gesturing helplessly toward the young man in the wheelchair. "Housing put him in a female suite. They thought he was a girl."

Kelsey blinked, looking from Henry to the door card, and finally to the six students staring back at her. She rubbed her temples, absorbing the reality of the situation.

"Okay, folks," Kelsey began, sighing heavily. "It appears we have a significant clerical error from the Housing Office."

Aubrey instinctively leaned forward and cupped a hand behind his right ear, trying to isolate Kelsey's words from the low, rumbling hum of the hallway traffic behind her. From her spot near the bathroom door, Zoë's eyes tracked the movement. She tilted her head, a quiet realization dawning as she noted the subtle, practiced way he angled his head to hear.

"Aubrey, they assigned you here assuming you were female," Kelsey continued. She laid out the grim reality of the university's housing crisis. The dorms were overbooked, and overflow students were already being placed in hotels up to thirty minutes away by bus.

"Here's the situation. Aubrey is staying. This is the designated handicap-accessible suite on the floor, and we have no other place for him," Kelsey explained, looking directly at the five young women. Without Aubrie or Lauren walking behind him to help push, the true, brutal weight of his own momentum settled squarely onto his shoulders.

"Hey, man," a voice called out.

Jordan flinched, instinctively bracing himself for an insult or a sharp reprimand for blocking the walkway.

Without making a spectacle of it, the lacrosse player grabbed the push handles and easily rolled Jordan the rest of the way up the ridge, bringing them to the flat plateau near the library.

"Have a good one," the guy smiled, giving Jordan a quick pat on the shoulder before jogging off to his own class.

Ten minutes later, as Jordan approached the final, sweeping incline leading up to Carter Hall, his arms were practically shaking with exhaustion. The burn was intense, and his progress ground to a halt.

Once again, the universe intervened. A girl carrying a massive stack of art portfolios noticed him struggling. She didn't ask; she simply shifted her heavy folders to one arm, stepped behind his chair with the other, and leaned her weight against the handles to help him crest the final slope.

"They really should put an elevator on the outside of this hill," she joked, breathing heavily as they reached the top. She smiled warmly at him. "See you around."

As Jordan rolled toward the entrance of Carter Hall, a dark, familiar shadow crept into the back of his mind. *His mother's voice.*

He had needed help twice in the span of fifteen minutes just to get to a basic freshman lecture. His mother’s harsh words echoed in his head, sharp and undeniable: *You are a burden. You are fundamentally incapable. You don't belong here.*

For a terrifying moment, the anxiety threatened to swallow him whole. Maybe she was right. Maybe he should have just stayed home in Hampton Roads, hidden away in his bedroom where the ground was flat and the expectations were zero. If he couldn't even push himself to class, how was he supposed to survive four years here?

He thought about Zoë shielding him from the overwhelming noise of the cafeteria, gently reading the menu directly into his ear. He thought about Mark’s firm, respectful handshake.

And he thought about his cheek, which still felt impossibly warm.

They had seen every limitation he had. They had seen his absolute most vulnerable moments. And none of them had looked at him like he was a burden. They had looked at him like he was family. The random students on the pathway hadn't looked at him with pity, either—they had just seen a fellow student fighting a steep hill, and they had stepped in to help.

Jordan pushed open the door to Carter Hall and wheeled himself into the air-conditioned lobby. His arms ached, his chest was heaving, and he still had to figure out how he was going to hear the history professor.

But as he navigated toward the classroom door, a quiet, defiant thought blossomed in his mind.

*Maybe,* he thought to himself, *I'll just get used to the hills.*

### Part 4: Western Civilization – 10:30 AM

The classroom in Carter Hall was much smaller and older than the massive science amphitheater, smelling faintly of lemon polish and old paper. He reached into his desk drawer, pulled out a thick, heavy-duty black marker, and walked over to the whiteboard mounted on the adjacent wall.

"I can do it, Professor."

Jordan’s head snapped up. The voice cut through the air with a distinct, undeniable cadence. He recognized the accent immediately—it was the exact same Sydney inflection that his EchoType software used.

*I'm Jordan,* he typed, angling the screen so Sarah could see the large letters. *Thank you for helping me.*

Dr. Gable was a masterful professor. He kept his voice loud, always making sure he was facing the front row when he spoke, and wrote his key points in massive, bold letters on the whiteboard. When the class shifted into a discussion about the agricultural revolution, Sarah proved to be the ultimate partner. *The shift to agriculture created static borders, which required the invention of standing armies to defend them,* Jordan typed into his terminal.

Jordan sat back in his chair, completely stunned. It was working. He was actively participating in a college-level discussion. He wasn't a burden, he wasn't a disruption, and he wasn't failing.

When the class finally ended, Jordan felt a rush of genuine, unadulterated triumph.

As Jordan rolled out of Carter Hall and back into the bright Virginia sunshine, the brutal, rolling hills of the CPI campus didn't look nearly as terrifying as they had an hour ago. He had survived his first solo trek. He had found a professor who actually cared. He had made a new friend.

Jordan stopped the wheelchair. The anxiety from the broken elevator, the claustrophobic sensory overload of the steamy kitchen, and the sheer frustration of being lost had all collided inside his chest. But underneath it all was a deeper, more profound terror.

Looking at Aubrie—radiant, safe, and waiting for him—a dark, invasive thought seized his mind. *What if this isn't real?*

She realized something was deeply scaring him, something far heavier than just a broken elevator. But she couldn't. They both had to keep moving forward.

The hug in the middle of the chaotic dining hall had grounded him. Jordan let out a shaky breath as Aubrie finally pulled back just enough to look into his eyes, though she kept a firm, reassuring hand on his shoulder.

She offered him a soft, affectionate smile, leaned in, and pressed a quick, gentle kiss to his cheek.

Jordan blinked, his breath hitching in his chest. It was only the second kiss of his entire life—the second one *today*—and the sudden, undeniable warmth of it chased away the last lingering chill of his panic.

"Come on," Aubrie said, her voice bright, deliberately ignoring the stares of a few passing upperclassmen. "Let's go get lunch. Together."

### Part 6: The Taste of Freedom – 12:15 PM

*I couldn't hear him at first, and he was writing on a dusty chalkboard. But he noticed me squinting. He stopped, switched to a whiteboard with heavy black markers, and moved closer so I could hear him. He didn't even make a big deal out of it.*

### Part 7: The True Assessment – 12:55 PM

Aubrie pushed Jordan’s wheelchair across the sunlit quad toward Miller Hall, the administrative heart of the campus. "Okay," Aubrie said softly, checking her watch. "I really have to run, or I'm going to fail calc before the syllabus is even handed out."

"I love you," she said.

Ms. Higgins leaned back, her sharp eyes scanning the sleek, matte-black Vanguard Pro resting on his lap. "I see you got a serious tech upgrade over the weekend. And it looks like this one actually holds a battery charge."

Jordan smiled, typing quickly. *A friend gave it to me.*

The polite, administrative mask she had worn on Thursday when his mother was hovering over him dropped away completely. When you look at the board from the back of the room, what do you see?"

*I see what everyone else sees,* he typed. *Just colored blurs. You can't read the letters until you get to the front row. That's just how distance works.*

"Jordan," she asked, her voice dropping to a very soft, careful register. "When you listen to a professor speak from across a large room... does it sound like they are underwater? Like a muffled hum?"

Ms. Higgins leaned back in her chair. The sheer, horrifying scale of his mother's neglect suddenly snapped into focus. It wasn't just that Aubrie Sinclair had denied him medical care—she had actively normalized his suffering. She had kept his world so incredibly small that he had simply assumed his severely compromised perception was how the entire universe operated.

I am checking out this dedicated MagnaByte mPad to you for the semester. Dr. Alistair's projector will broadcast directly to this mPad in real-time, allowing you to zoom in on the slides from the back row. *Thank you.*

**Jordan:** *Educational Accessibility gave me an official note-taker form for Western Civ. If you sign it, the university gives you Priority Registration for next semester. Do you want it?*

**Sarah:** *Are you joking?! Priority registration is absolute gold! I would have done it for free anyway, mate! Absolutely YES. Bring it to class on Wednesday!!!*

### Part X: Primary Care – 3:30 PM

The CPI Student Health Center was a bustling, brightly lit clinic on the edge of the campus quad. Jordan sat in his wheelchair in the crowded waiting room, his sleek Vanguard Pro resting securely on his lap. He was vibrating with a nervous, hopeful energy. In his limited, highly sheltered understanding of how the world worked, he assumed this was a one-stop shop. He fully expected to walk through the clinic doors and roll back out an hour later wearing a pair of glasses and hearing aids.

He wasn't alone. Aubrie sat in the chair immediately to his left, while Mark and Madison leaned against the wall nearby, acting as a supportive, physical barrier against the chaotic energy of the waiting room.

"Jordan Fuller?" a triage nurse called out from a heavy wooden door leading to the back hallways.

Jordan quickly unlocked his brakes, expecting the familiar, protective phalanx of his family to move with him. He rolled forward a few inches, but when he looked over his shoulder, Mark, Madison, and Aubrie hadn't moved.

Jordan stopped dead. His hands clamped down on his push-rings, his knuckles turning white. A cold, sharp spike of panic hit his chest. He frantically pulled his laptop open, his fingers flying across the keys to pull up his dark-themed NeoEdit terminal.

*You said you wouldn't leave me,* the bright green text read, his screen angled desperately toward Aubrie.

Aubrie immediately stood up and stepped in front of his chair, recognizing the rising tide of an autistic meltdown. She knelt so they were eye-to-eye.

"We aren't leaving you, J," she said, her voice soft but steady, making sure to enunciate clearly so he could read her lips and catch the muffled sounds. "But we have to stay out here. It's a clinic rule for privacy. Only the patient is allowed in the exam rooms."

Jordan stared at her, his breathing turning shallow. He had never done a medical appointment without his mother hovering over him, controlling the narrative and dictating his reality. The thought of rolling through those doors entirely alone was terrifying.

Mark stepped up next to Aubrie, offering a warm, reassuring smile. "You're an adult now, brother. You've got this. We are going to be sitting right in these exact chairs when you come back out. We're not moving an inch."

"We're right here," Madison added cheerfully, giving his shoulder a gentle, grounding squeeze.

Jordan looked at the heavy wooden door, then back at his three friends. They believed in him. He swallowed hard, forcing his hands to loosen their death grip on the wheels. He gave a small, shaky nod, turned his chair around, and rolled through the doors by himself.

He was guided into a small, sterile exam room. A few minutes later, a Nurse Practitioner with a kind face and a tablet stepped in. She glanced at the referral Ms. Higgins had sent over.

"Jordan, hi. I'm Nurse Collins," she said, raising her volume slightly and speaking clearly. "Ms. Higgins sent over some notes regarding your visual and auditory baselines. Let's do a quick screening to get you officially into the university system, and then we'll get you where you need to go."

Jordan typed a quick *Thank you* into his terminal, but his brow furrowed. *Where I need to go?*

The screenings were brief but painfully conclusive. When Nurse Collins asked him to read the eye chart on the wall, Jordan couldn't even make out the large 'E' at the top; to him, it was just a fuzzy, gray smudge against a white background. When she moved to the auditory test, utilizing a basic headset and a series of high-frequency tones, Jordan sat in complete silence. He didn't raise his hand once for any tone above a low, mid-range rumble.

Nurse Collins lowered her tablet, her professional demeanor softening into a look of quiet, profound empathy. She realized instantly that these were not minor deficits; this was years of profound, uncorrected medical neglect.

"Okay, Jordan," she said gently. "You have severe myopia, and what looks like progressive sensorineural hearing loss. Left untreated, it will eventually result in total deafness. I am officially logging these deficits into your CPI medical file, which legally locks in your academic accommodations."

She printed out two slips of paper and handed them to him. "But we are a primary care clinic. We don't dispense hardware here. I am referring you to an excellent dual-practice clinic in Christiansburg—an optometrist and an audiologist right next door to each other in Pepper Plaza. They have the specialized equipment to get you properly fitted."

Jordan looked down at the slips of paper in his lap, the reality of the adult world settling over him. It was a process. But these papers were physical tickets out of the blur and the static.

He wheeled himself back out into the lobby. True to their word, Aubrie, Mark, and Madison were waiting in the exact same spot. Aubrie's face lit up the second she saw him, and she rushed over to give him a warm, grounding hug.

"You did it," she whispered.

Jordan smiled, handing the referrals to Mark.

"Montgomery Vision and Hearing Clinic," Mark read, an easy, confident smile spreading across his face. "I know exactly where this is. It's right next to the Montgomery County Courthouse in Christiansburg. We can catch the Route 1 bus straight down Business 460. We'll take you on Friday."
