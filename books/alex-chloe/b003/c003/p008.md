# Part 8: The Montréal Rule (Summer 2000)

The next morning dawned bright and clear at the Derby Line Inn. A palpable sense of excitement buzzed among the six of them as they gathered after breakfast. The plan for the day was their most ambitious yet: a day trip to Montréal. For the Millers, it was a chance to see a world-class city they'd never visited. For Alex, it was an adventure into the heart of the French-speaking world he was just beginning to explore.

"Alright," James Miller said with a laugh as they all piled into the Mason minivan. "Should I even bother asking if we need our passports at this point?"

"Always good to have them, just in case," Thomas replied with a knowing smile.

As Thomas guided the minivan to the Canadian Port of Entry at Stanstead, a different officer was on duty. He was younger than Jean-Guy, with a sharp, energetic air about him, but his face broke into an equally familiar smile as he saw the Masons' van.

"Bonjour, Thomas! Bonjour, Sophie!" he called out. "Heading out for another adventure?"

"Bonjour, Michel!" Sophie replied warmly. "We're taking our friends on a big trip today. All the way to Montréal to see the Old Port and maybe get some smoked meat!"

Michel's friendly expression immediately became a little more professional, though his tone remained pleasant. "Ah, Montréal! C'est super! Okay, well, since you're heading that far into the country, you know the drill. I'm going to have to do this one by the book. Can I see everyone's passports, please?"

In the middle row, Emily Miller looked surprised. "Oh! Really? I was starting to think you didn't use them here," she said with a light, curious laugh.

"Ah, that's the 'Montréal Rule,'" Sophie explained gently to her and James. "It's different when you're leaving the immediate border area. The agents here know us, but if we were to get pulled over for something way up in the city, the police there wouldn't know us from Adam. The stamp in the passport proves we all entered Canada legally today. It's an unwritten rule, but an important one."

"Well, that makes perfect sense," James said, a look of dawning understanding on his face as he passed his family's passports forward to Thomas.

Thomas gathered all six passports—three dark blue Canadian ones for his family and three navy American ones for the Millers—and handed the mixed stack up to Michel. The officer took them without a second glance; this, too, was a normal part of life on the line. He opened each one, looking from the photo to the person inside the bustling van.

"Chloé," he said with a nod, "you're getting as tall as your mother. And Alex, welcome to Canada. First time to Montréal?"

"You'll love it," Michel said, stamping the documents with a series of firm thunks. He asked the standard questions about what they were bringing into the country, which Thomas answered clearly.

"Alright, you're all set," Michel said, passing the stack of stamped passports back. "Have a fantastic time in the city!"

As they pulled away, James Miller let out a low whistle from the middle row. "Well," he said with a chuckle, "I finally got to use my passport!"

With their documents properly stamped and their entry into Canada officially recorded, the minivan merged onto the highway, heading north towards the glittering skyline of Montréal, ready for a new kind of adventure in a city full of history, culture, and the promise of world-famous smoked meat sandwiches.
