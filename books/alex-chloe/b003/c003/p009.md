# Part 9: Montréal

The drive north from the borderlands to Montréal was a journey into a different world. The rolling hills gave way to a flat, expansive plain, and soon, the iconic, cross-topped peak of Mount Royal rose from the horizon, heralding their arrival.

Their first stop, as planned, was the mountain itself. Thomas expertly navigated the winding road up its slope, parking near the grand stone chalet. They all got out, the air cooler and fresher at this elevation. From the Belvedere Kondiaronk, the view was breathtaking. The entire downtown skyline of Montréal spread out before them, a dense forest of skyscrapers giving way to the vast, shimmering expanse of the St. Lawrence River.

"Whoa," was all Emily Miller could say, her eyes wide.

"You can see everything from up here," James added, equally impressed.

Alex, positioned at the stone balustrade by Chloé, took in the panoramic view. It was immense, a landscape of human ambition and natural grandeur all at once. Chloé pointed out landmarks her mother had described to her. IT’S SO… BIG, Alex typed, the simple words imbued with a profound sense of scale. Chloé squeezed his shoulder in agreement, the two of them sharing the moment of awe.

Next, a pilgrimage for the taste buds. Thomas drove them to the vibrant, eclectic Mile End neighborhood, a place buzzing with a creative, bohemian energy. He double-parked for just a moment in front of a small, unassuming storefront with a line spilling onto the sidewalk: "La Flamme Ronde Bagels."

"Stay here," Sophie said, hopping out. "I'll be quick!" She returned minutes later with two large paper bags, a wonderful, warm, sweet-and-savory aroma wafting from them.

"Fresh from the wood-fired oven," she announced, passing a still-warm, sesame-seed-covered bagel to each person in the van. The Millers took their first bites and their eyes widened in surprise. It was nothing like an American bagel—denser, chewier, with a distinct, subtle sweetness from being boiled in honey water.

"Oh my," Emily managed through her mouthful. "That's incredible."

For their main lunch, they headed to the bustling Plateau neighborhood and found a parking spot near "Levine's Montréal Delicatessen." The place was a glorious assault on the senses: loud, crowded, and smelling divinely of smoked meat, pickles, and rye bread. They squeezed into a booth, with Alex's wheelchair at the end of the table. The order was simple: six smoked meat sandwiches, medium-fat, on rye bread with a smear of mustard, served with giant sour pickles and bottles of crisp, cold black cherry cola.

When the sandwiches arrived, stacked impossibly high with hand-carved, spice-crusted brisket, the Millers stared in respectful silence for a moment before digging in. It was a culinary revelation. The only downside to the authentic atmosphere was the faint blue-gray haze hanging in the air, drifting from the designated smoking section near the back of the restaurant. The four non-smoking parents exchanged a brief, resigned glance—an unavoidable annoyance, the price of admission for a truly great meal.

For Alex, however, with his slight breathing difficulty, the secondhand smoke was more than just an annoyance. It started as a small, dry, persistent cough, a tickle in the back of his throat. He tried to ignore it, focusing on the amazing taste of his sandwich.

Chloé, ever attuned to his slightest discomfort, noticed immediately. She saw the subtle tension around his eyes and heard the change in his breathing between bites. She leaned closer to him, her brow furrowed with concern. "You okay, Alex? Your breathing sounds a little... tight."

Alex, who had been trying to hide his discomfort, met her gaze and gave a slight nod, bringing up his AAC. THE SMOKE. IT’S A LITTLE HARD TO BREATHE.

The moment the synthesized voice spoke, all four parents immediately focused on him. James Miller's hand was instantly on his son's shoulder, his expression shifting from relaxed to concerned. "Okay, buddy. Let's get you some fresh air."

"We can get the rest of these wrapped up," Sophie said immediately, already flagging down a busy waiter. "*On peut avoir le reste pour emporter, s'il vous plaît?*"

The rest of the meal was a blur of quick, decisive action. James helped Alex navigate his chair towards the exit while Thomas settled the bill. Within a minute, they were all back out on the bustling sidewalk. Outside on the bustling sidewalk, Alex took a deep, grateful breath of the clearer city air, the tightness in his chest already beginning to ease. Chloé stood beside him, her hand resting reassuringly on his arm. The smoked meat had been a triumph, a new favorite taste of Montréal logged away. The smoky haze they'd left behind, however, was an experience they were all happy to leave in the past as they continued their afternoon.

The afternoon was dedicated to Old Montréal. Here, the modern city fell away, replaced by the narrow, cobblestone streets and stone facades of the 18th century. James and Thomas took turns helping push Alex's chair over the bumpiest sections of Rue Saint-Paul, the old stones a charming but practical challenge. They browsed the windows of art galleries and artisan shops, soaking in the distinctly European atmosphere.

Their walk led them to a grand public square, dominated by the magnificent, twin-towered facade of the Basilique Sainte-Anne-de-Montréal. Stepping inside, they were plunged into a world of breathtaking beauty. The cavernous nave was bathed in a deep, ethereal blue light, filtering through stained glass and reflecting off a ceiling dotted with countless golden stars. The altar was a masterpiece of intricate, gilded woodwork. They walked the aisles in hushed reverence, their footsteps echoing softly. Alex tilted his head back, mesmerized by the starry ceiling, while Chloé stood beside him, her hand resting on his shoulder, both of them sharing the profound, silent beauty of the sacred space.

They ended their city tour with a stroll along the Old Port. The wide, flat promenade was perfect for Alex's wheelchair. They watched street performers juggle fire, listened to the clip-clop of the horse-drawn carriages, and looked out over the wide, flowing river. The two families, a comfortable and happy unit, found a bench. Sophie and Emily shared one of the warm bagels from La Flamme Ronde, while James and Thomas watched the boats.

Alex and Chloé sat a little apart, just watching the people go by. THIS WAS A GOOD DAY, Alex typed, a contented sigh in the rhythm of his fingers.

Chloé leaned her head on his shoulder, a familiar, comforting gesture. "The best day," she agreed softly. "I knew you'd love it." The city had been grand and exciting, but for both of them, the best part was simply being there, seeing it all, together.
