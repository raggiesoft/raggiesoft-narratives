# Part 10: An Interrupted Lesson and an Inevitable Summons (A Week Later, Fall 1999)

The hushed, studious atmosphere of the Northwood High library was a welcome change from the usual lunchtime cacophony. At a table tucked away in a quieter section near the history stacks, Morgan McAllister was patiently explaining a particularly tricky algebra concept to Ben Carter. Her own grades, particularly in subjects she enjoyed, were surprisingly good; her primary academic failing had always been a spectacular inability to remember to clean up the resultant mess of study snacks and scattered papers in Mrs. DeMarco's cafeteria. Ben, on the other hand, was genuinely struggling. The weeks spent under Josh Riley’s toxic influence at the start of freshman year had done his concentration and homework completion no favors, and he was now diligently trying to catch up, with Morgan, his neighbor, offering to tutor him.

"Okay, so when you're isolating the variable, Ben," Morgan was saying, pointing to an equation in his well-worn textbook, "you have to do the same operation to both sides of the equation to keep it balanced. See how if you subtract '2x' from this side..."

Ben leaned in, his brow furrowed in concentration, nodding slowly. "Right, so then it cancels out here, and I do it on the other side too..."

"Exactly!" Morgan said, offering an encouraging smile as he started to grasp it. "You're getting it!"

As they paused, Ben looking down at the equation with a new flicker of understanding, Morgan reached across the table and gave his arm a quick, friendly squeeze-hug. "See? Not so bad when you break it down."

Ben looked up, a shy but genuine smile spreading across his face. "Thanks, Morgan. You explain it way better than Mr. Henderson does in class." The hug, a simple gesture of her friendly encouragement, felt… nice. Comfortable. Morgan was tough and had a reputation, but she was also surprisingly patient and a really good teacher. He was starting to genuinely look forward to these tutoring sessions.

Their quiet moment of academic progress was shattered by a loud, sneering voice.

"Well, well, well. If it isn't 'Morgue' McAllister playing teacher with *my* loyal goon, Carter. What's this, a study date? Getting all cozy with the homework, are we?"

Josh Riley stood there, practically vibrating with contempt, his backpack slung carelessly over one shoulder. He had evidently finished his own miserable attempt at library study (likely procrastinating on his ten-page essay for Mrs. DeMarco) and had spotted them. The sight of Ben Carter, *his* goon, willingly associating with Morgan, *the girl who'd disgustingly rejected his advances by the dumpsters just last week*, and them actually sharing a *hug*? A girl *hugging his goon*? It was an intolerable affront to his perceived ownership and status.

Ben flushed, instinctively trying to shrink down. Morgan, however, straightened up, her expression hardening. "We're studying, Josh. Something you clearly wouldn't understand. Now, if you don't mind, we're busy."

Josh scoffed, stepping closer, his gaze raking over them with open disdain, lingering on where Morgan’s hand had just been on Ben’s arm. "Busy? Looks more like Carter's trying to get some extra credit with the teacher's pet. Or maybe you're finally desperate enough to go for a loser like him, McAllister, since no one else will have you?" His eyes were full of a possessive rage, particularly as he looked at Ben.

He leaned over their table, deliberately knocking a pencil to the floor. "So, Carter, you actually think *she* can help your failing grades? Or are you two just planning your next romantic rendezvous behind the bleachers? Hope you're using protection, kids!" His internal monologue, had anyone been privy to it, would have been a torrent of even viler, sexually suggestive assumptions about them.

Before Morgan could unleash a verbal volley, or Ben could stammer a response, another voice, calm and carrying unmistakable authority, intervened from the direction of the main library desk.

"Mr. Riley. It appears your definition of 'library study' involves harassing students who are genuinely attempting to learn."

All three turned. Mrs. DeMarco stood a few feet away, a book she had perhaps been returning in her hand. As she spoke, she glanced briefly towards the main circulation desk, where Mrs. Periwinkle, the notoriously no-nonsense head librarian, met her gaze and gave a single, almost imperceptible nod. The unspoken dialogue was clear: Mrs. Periwinkle had already observed Josh's disruptive entrance and rude behavior and was silently confirming to Mrs. DeMarco that he was, indeed, the problem. Mrs. Periwinkle, in fact, rather enjoyed having Morgan and Ben using the quiet study tables; their hushed voices were entirely respectful for a high school library, and she had noted that Morgan was actively and productively tutoring Ben, a vast improvement over Morgan's past cafeteria habits.

Mrs. DeMarco's gaze, now reinforced, fixed on Josh with that familiar, icy disapproval. "I was just talking to them, Mrs. DeMarco!" Josh blustered, trying to sound innocent.

"Indeed," Mrs. DeMarco replied, her voice dangerously soft. "You were 'talking.' Loudly. Disruptively. And, if my ears and Mrs. Periwinkle’s silent attestation did not deceive me, with a tone and content that was both demeaning and entirely unwelcome." She glanced at Morgan and Ben, who looked immensely relieved at her intervention. "It seems your extended essay on 'The Inevitability of Consequences and the Folly of Evading Assigned Penance' has not yet fully permeated your understanding of appropriate interpersonal conduct, Mr. Riley."

She took a step closer. "Given your clear inability to utilize the library for its intended scholarly purpose, and your persistent desire to interfere with students who *are* making positive efforts, I believe a more… structured… after-school activity is once again in order for you."

Josh’s face fell. "What? But… I'm already on grease traps! And the essay!" he whined, his voice cracking slightly. He looked like he was about to complain further about the unfairness of it all.

Before Josh could fully launch into his lament, Mrs. Periwinkle, the head librarian, stepped forward from behind her desk, her own expression stern. "Mr. Riley," she interjected, her voice crisp and carrying an authority that brooked no argument within her literary domain. "If your after-school schedule is feeling a bit too monotonous with sanitation duties, perhaps I can offer an alternative enrichment activity right here in the library."

Josh looked from Mrs. DeMarco to Mrs. Periwinkle, a new kind of dread dawning on his face. This was not looking like an appeal to a higher, more lenient authority.

"I've noticed," Mrs. Periwinkle continued, her gaze sweeping over the meticulously organized shelves, then landing pointedly on Josh, "that a few sections of our non-fiction collection could use a thorough review to ensure every volume is in precise Dewey Decimal order. It’s a task requiring intense concentration, silence, and meticulous attention to detail. Perhaps several afternoons spent ensuring our history of ancient civilizations is perfectly aligned would be a more… intellectually stimulating form of penance for disrupting the learning environment?" She paused, then added, as if an afterthought, "And I must say, the library's own sanitation bins are looking a bit… neglected. They could certainly use some dedicated care after you’ve completed your cataloging review."

Josh’s jaw, which had been ready to argue with Mrs. DeMarco, snapped shut. The thought of spending hours in enforced silence, meticulously arranging books by a system he likely didn't understand, and *then* cleaning library trash cans, possibly for days on end, sounded infinitely worse than the familiar horrors of the outdoor dumpsters. It was a laborious, tedious, and utterly unglamorous alternative.

Mrs. DeMarco observed this exchange with a flicker of what might have been amusement in her eyes, though her expression remained stern. She gave a slight, almost imperceptible nod to Mrs. Periwinkle – a clear acknowledgment of a perfectly executed tag-team maneuver by two of Northwood High's most formidable disciplinarians.

"Well, Mr. Riley?" Mrs. DeMarco prompted. "The dumpsters, which offer fresh air and vigorous physical activity? Or the hallowed halls of learning with Mrs. Periwinkle, ensuring the enlightenment of your fellow students through impeccable shelf order? The choice, as always, is yours."

The "choice," of course, was no choice at all for Josh. Faced with the prospect of mind-numbing, silent, detailed work under Mrs. Periwinkle's exacting gaze, on top of whatever Mrs. DeMarco had already decreed, he visibly deflated.

"The dumpsters, Mrs. DeMarco," he mumbled, utterly defeated. "I’ll… I’ll do the dumpsters."

"A wise decision, Mr. Riley," Mrs. DeMarco said. "Then your additional week of standard sanitation detail, commencing upon the completion of your current sentence and essay, is confirmed. See that it is carried out with diligence."

"As for you, Ms. McAllister, Mr. Carter," Mrs. DeMarco said, her tone softening slightly as she turned to them. "Please continue your studies. I trust Mr. Riley will no longer be a distraction." She gave Josh one last pointed look, then turned and walked calmly towards the library exit, perhaps offering Mrs. Periwinkle another subtle nod of thanks and mutual understanding as she passed the main desk.

Morgan McAllister and Ben Carter watched Josh shuffle away, defeated and muttering under his breath about "stupid librarians" and "unfair lunch ladies," likely towards the only place he truly felt he belonged: the vicinity of the dumpsters. They exchanged a look – a mixture of relief, grim satisfaction, and perhaps a shared, unspoken understanding that they were definitely on the right path by distancing themselves from him and focusing on positive actions. Their own quiet efforts at reform had just been indirectly, and very publicly, validated.
