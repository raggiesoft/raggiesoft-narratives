# Part 2: The Grant (Late 2022)

Quitting their jobs transformed their lives. Their apartment was no longer a home where they worked on their project; it was a full-time fundraising and design studio where they also happened to sleep.

By the late fall of 2022, they were in the final, agonizing stages of submitting their first massive seed grant application to the "Cascade Family Trust," a major Seattle-based philanthropic foundation. This single application was the culmination of all their years of preparation.

The scene was one of controlled chaos. Chloé was the lead grant writer, the architect of their story. She spent her days translating their vision into the compelling, data-driven prose the foundation required.

Alex, at his workstation, was the technical expert, the provider of the hard data that gave their dream its credibility. He would design an innovative, accessible habitat, and Chloé would explain why it was essential.

And Megan, the project manager and editor-in-chief, was the one who brought it all together. She took the raw, passionate prose from Chloé and the dense, technical data from Alex and, with her professional technical writer's eye, wove them into a single, polished, and irrefutable document. She designed the layout, created the infographics, and ensured every single sentence was perfect.

"Okay, team," Megan announced from her desk, her expression one of focused intensity. "Final checklist. Chloé, I need the final version of the community impact statement by three o'clock." She then turned to Alex. "And cuz, I need the final projected water usage numbers for the river otter habitat's filtration system. For the environmental impact infographic."

Alex pulled up a complex spreadsheet. SENDING IT TO YOU NOW, his device replied. IT INCLUDES THE RECIRCULATION EFFICIENCY STATS. WE'RE AT 98% WATER RECLAMATION.

"Perfect," Megan murmured, already dropping the numbers into a beautiful, easy-to-read chart she had designed.

They worked for weeks, a seamless, three-person machine, fueled by cheap coffee and a shared, unwavering belief in their mission. Finally, one rainy November afternoon, it was done. The three of them gathered around Megan's computer, a single, unified team.

With a deep, collective breath, Megan moved the mouse cursor over the "Submit" button. She looked at Alex and Chloé, a silent, final check-in. They both nodded. She clicked the mouse.

An automated email confirmation appeared a moment later. "Thank you for your submission. You will be notified of the board's decision in the first quarter of next year."

A profound silence fell over the room. They had done everything they could. Now, all they could do was wait.
