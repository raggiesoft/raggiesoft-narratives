# Part 4: A Living Building

The final construction sign came down in the late spring of 2025. The Lynnwood Discovery and Care Center was no longer a muddy construction site; it was a beautiful, quiet, and profoundly empty building. The real work was just beginning.

Their first priority was to bring the aquatic habitats to life. The massive acrylic tanks for the river otters and the blind sea otter, Luna, had been installed, but they were just sterile glass boxes. For weeks, the three of them worked tirelessly, rinsing tons of specialized substrate, arranging rocks and driftwood, and planting hundreds of live aquatic plants. Finally, they filled the tanks with water. The long, patient, two-month process of cycling—of establishing the delicate colonies of beneficial bacteria that would make the water safe for animals—had begun.

While the tanks cycled, their focus shifted. They were no longer just founders; they were employers. Their apartment, and now their new office at the LDCC, was a constant parade of interviews. They hired their first small, dedicated team: a head veterinarian, a few experienced animal care technicians, and a volunteer coordinator.

The one area where they *weren't* hiring was Echo Bridge.

The company was a runaway success. The two enterprise contracts had given them a massive influx of capital, and individual sales of Axon 2.0 were climbing every week. The workload was becoming immense.

"Cuz," Megan said one evening, looking over at Alex's desk, which was littered with schematics for a new hardware project. "You're buried. We have the money now. You need to hire another developer, at least a part-timer, to help you with bug fixes and customer support. You can't do it all yourself."

I CAN DO IT, his Quantum Design Studio voiced, its tone firm and completely non-negotiable. AXON IS MY CODE. I KNOW THE SYSTEM. IT IS MORE EFFICIENT IF I DO IT MYSELF.

Megan and Chloé exchanged a long, worried look over his head. They saw what he couldn't, or wouldn't, see. They saw the new, deeper lines of exhaustion etched around his eyes. They saw the way he would have to stop typing, wincing as he flexed his cramping hands. They saw the sheer, punishing hours he was putting in—a full day of LDCC design and consultation, followed by a full night of coding for Echo Bridge.

His passion had become a relentless, all-consuming engine, and the physical toll was becoming undeniable. They knew they couldn't push him. The decision to ask for help had to be his. But as they watched him turn back to his screen, his shoulders slumped with a weariness he refused to acknowledge, they both felt a new, cold knot of fear. Their dream was finally, beautifully, coming to life. But they were terrified of what it might be costing its brilliant, stubborn, and irreplaceable architect.
