# Part 4: The Airport and the Call

The two-hour drive south on I-91 was a tense, silent journey through a sleeping Vermont. When they finally pulled into the rental return lot at Burlington International Airport, it was past one in the morning. The airport was a ghost town, an eerie cathedral of silence and anxiety. The only signs of life were the flickering departure boards—a sea of red "CANCELLED" notices—and a small, desperate cluster of stranded travelers.

They dropped the keys to the Canadian van in the after-hours box, a simple act that felt like severing their last tie to a world that no longer existed. They found a deserted corner of the terminal, the vast, empty space amplifying their sense of isolation. Pulling out their laptops, the dim glow of the screens illuminated their tired, anxious faces as they began the frantic search for a way home.

The results were a digital brick wall.

Every flight to Seattle for the next three days was completely sold out. The few, precious seats that did pop up on connecting flights a week out were being sold at astronomical, predatory prices. Compounding the problem, airlines were already slashing their schedules. Flights were being cancelled faster than they could be booked.

A cold, hard knot of fear tightened in Chloé's stomach. They were well and truly stuck.

Alex, watching the same sold-out flights blink on his own screen, looked at Chloé. His usual calm was beginning to fray, replaced by a look of genuine fear. His hands stilled on his keyboard.

CHLOÉ, his device voiced, the single word hanging in the vast, empty terminal. WE ARE OUT OF OPTIONS.

Megan, who had been methodically and silently working on her own laptop, looked up. While Alex and Chloé had been focused on the flights, she had been treating this like a complex logistical problem, exploring every possible contingency: rental cars (all sold out), trains from the nearby station (also sold out), and flights from other, larger airports (a sea of cancellations). The situation was dire, but her expression was not one of defeat. It was a mask of pure, fierce determination.

"No," she said, her voice a low, steady anchor in their sea of uncertainty. "We are not out of options. We are just out of *easy* options. We will get home. If I have to buy a used car off Craigslist and drive us across the country myself, we are getting home. We just need to find the next right move."

Chloé took a deep, shuddering breath, the full weight of their situation pressing down on her. Megan's strength was a comfort, but it couldn't magically create a solution. For the first time in their adult lives, they were faced with a problem they could not solve on their own.

She looked at Alex, at his wide, frightened eyes, and a profound, humbling realization washed over her. There was only one thing left to do, one lifeline they hadn't yet used.

"You're right," she said, her voice shaking slightly as she pulled out her phone. She met Megan's determined gaze. "The next right move. We have to call our parents."
