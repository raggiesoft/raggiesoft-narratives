# Part 1: The First Sign (April 2020)

It had been nearly a month since their parents had departed for Virginia, leaving Alex, Chloé, and Megan to navigate the strange, quiet reality of a world in lockdown. Their days had settled into a new, contained rhythm. In their shared office, Alex and Megan would work remotely for their respective companies, while Chloé would be at the dining table, deep in research for the Lynnwood Discovery and Care Center. They were a self-contained, productive unit, the global crisis a constant, low-grade hum of anxiety from the news, but safely outside the walls of their apartment.

The rhythm broke on a quiet Tuesday morning.

It started with a cough. It wasn't dramatic, just a dry, persistent tickle in Alex's throat.

ALLERGIES, his AAC device stated with a hint of annoyance after a particularly stubborn coughing fit. THE POLLEN COUNT MUST BE HIGH TODAY.

Chloé looked over from her laptop, a flicker of concern in her eyes. "You think so? It sounds a little rough."

ALWAYS GETS LIKE THIS IN APRIL, he typed, waving off her concern.

Megan, however, looked up from her own screen, a different, more analytical kind of concern on her face. "I don't know, cuz," she said, her voice quiet but firm. "I was just reading the new CDC bulletin. A persistent dry cough is one of the big ones."

"It's probably just allergies," Chloé said, though a new seed of doubt had been planted.

But by early afternoon, something else began to creep in. A profound, bone-deep weariness settled over Alex, a level of fatigue that was completely alien. He found himself re-reading the same line of code over and over, his brain unable to process it.

He pushed back from his desk, intending to get a glass of water, but the simple act of maneuvering his chair felt like a monumental effort. He was sitting by the window when Chloé came into the living room. She stopped, her head tilted, her gaze sharp. She had spent a lifetime reading his subtle, non-verbal cues, and what she saw now made a knot of unease tighten in her stomach. It was the uncharacteristic stillness, the sheer exhaustion radiating from him.

"You're not just allergic," she said, her voice quiet but firm. It wasn't a question.

Alex started to type a protest, but another dry, hacking cough interrupted him, leaving him breathless.

At the same moment, from the office, came another, softer cough. Megan appeared in the doorway, a blanket wrapped around her shoulders, her own face looking pale and tired. "You know," she said, her voice a little rough, "I've been feeling a little off all day, too. A bit of a headache, and just... wiped out."

Chloé's expression hardened with a protective resolve. She looked from Alex's flushed face to Megan's, and the full, terrifying reality of their situation crashed down on her. She was the only one left standing.

"That's it," she said, her voice now a calm, steady command in the face of a rising crisis. She walked purposefully towards the bathroom. "I'm getting the thermometer."
