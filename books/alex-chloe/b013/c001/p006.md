# Part 6: The Preclearance Predicament (2010)

After a final, tease-filled goodbye with the Leclercs, the three of them drove their rental van to Montréal-Trudeau International Airport (YUL) to begin their journey home. They returned the van and checked their luggage for their direct Gateway Airlines flight to Seattle.

As they walked through the main terminal toward the U.S. Departures area, they were a sight to behold. Alex and Megan were still in their full hockey jerseys and patriotic fleece pants. The airport was buzzing with other victorious fans heading home. A group of travelers spotted their jerseys and called out, "*Félicitations! Quelle partie!*" (Congratulations! What a game!)

Chloé beamed, waving back. Then, seizing the opportunity, she pointed a thumb directly at Alex and Megan, who were trying to look as inconspicuous as possible.

"*Merci! Et regardez-les!*" she announced loudly and proudly to the strangers. "*Ce sont des Américains! Ils ont perdu le pari!*" (Thanks! And look at them! They're Americans! They lost the bet!)

The travelers roared with appreciative laughter, their cheers doubling in volume. The news spread like wildfire. Now, Alex and Megan weren't just fans; they were legends—the defeated American ambassadors, living trophies of Canada's national victory. They endured the pointing, the grins, and the mercilessly cheerful teasing with the stoic resignation of people who knew they were utterly beaten.

As they finally approached the signs for "U.S. Customs and Border Protection Preclearance," Chloé stopped. "Okay," she said, "this is where I go neutral. No sense in poking the bear. A U.S. officer might not appreciate me showing up in full celebration mode."

She quickly pulled her Canadian jersey off, revealing a plain t-shirt underneath, and stuffed the jersey into her carry-on. She was now in regular jeans and a t-shirt. Alex and Megan watched her, their expressions a perfect, shared portrait of good-natured misery.

I WISH I COULD DO THAT, Alex's AAC device stated plaintively.

"Nope! A bet's a bet, and the bet was for the rest of the trip," Chloé said with a merciless grin. "Should've cheered for Canada in the bar."

They joined the line for U.S. Customs. When their turn came, they approached the CBP officer's booth as a trio. Chloé, looking like a normal traveler, handed over her U.S. Passport and NEXUS card. The officer glanced at them, then at her. Then his gaze fell on Alex and Megan.

Two rolling tributes to the Canadian hockey federation. They handed over their own U.S. Passports and NEXUS cards.

The officer paused. He looked down at the proud American eagles on the passports in his hand. He then looked up at the giant, unapologetic red maple leaves on their chests. He looked up again at the bright red "CANADA" toques on their heads. His professional expression did not change, but he blinked slowly, processing the deeply contradictory information.

After a long, deadpan moment, he looked Alex straight in the eye.

"Lost a bet, son?"

Alex's AAC, with perfect, defeated clarity, voiced the reply: YES, SIR. A VERY BAD ONE.

The officer's gaze then shifted to Megan. "You too, ma'am?"

"Yes, sir," Megan sighed, her voice full of resignation. "We both did."

The officer's lips twitched, the barest hint of a smile quickly suppressed. He stamped all three of their passports with a firm *thunk, thunk, thunk*.

"Welcome to the United States," he said, the emphasis on the location carrying a world of meaning, given that they were still standing in Montréal. "Try to have a better flight."

The moment they were clear of the booth and walking into the U.S. departures lounge, Chloé finally let out the peal of laughter she'd been desperately holding in. Alex and Megan's public humiliation was now complete, officially recognized by two federal governments before they had even left Canadian airspace. Now, all that was left was the long flight of shame home.
