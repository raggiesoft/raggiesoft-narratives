# Part 4: The Uniform of Defeat (2010)

The morning after the Golden Goal, Alex Miller and Megan Carter woke in their Stanstead hotel room to the sight of two bright red piles of fleece and polyester lying on two chairs. Their sentences.

With deep, theatrical sighs of condemned prisoners, they began the process of dressing. The slightly-too-large Canadian jerseys were first, followed by the ridiculously bright red fleece pants. They pulled on the maple leaf socks. The final piece for both was the red and white headwear.

"I can't believe I have to wear this," Megan muttered, pulling the toque down over her ears. "It's an affront to my Bostonian soul."

IT IS THE ENEMY'S BEANIE, Alex's device voiced with grim finality as he pulled on his own.

Just then, Chloé emerged from the bathroom, still glowing with victory, having heard his pronouncement. "*Excusez-moi?*" she said, her voice a perfect blend of mock offense and pure delight. "*C'est une TOQUE, et c'est la couronne des champions!*" (Excuse me? That is a TOQUE, and it's the crown of champions!)

Megan just grinned at her, a look of pure, unapologetic solidarity with her cousin on her face. "Nope. Pretty sure it's the enemy's beanie."

Chloé just rolled her eyes, her smile widening. "You two are impossible," she declared. She wore her own Canadian jersey, but she paired it stylishly with comfortable jeans. She was a proud, happy fan. They were two people in costumes. The distinction was sharp and intentional.

"Morning, champs," she said, trying and failing to suppress a triumphant grin. She picked up their neatly folded American jerseys. "Don't want to forget your... souvenirs." She tucked them under her arm like captured flags. "Okay," she announced cheerfully. "Let's go. We need to grab some snacks for the road at the dépanneur, and then we have to say goodbye to the family before we head for the border."

Alex and Megan knew what this meant. They had to go out in public. Like this.

As they walked through the main part of Stanstead, the town was still buzzing. Locals, seeing the two Americans in their enthusiastic attire, immediately broke into wide grins, giving them thumbs-ups and cheerful shouts of "*Vive le Canada!*"

As they passed the local *boucherie*, Uncle David "coincidentally" stepped out, a wide grin splitting his face. He saw them and boomed in loud, proud, Québécois French for the whole street to hear.

"Alex! Megan! Mes Américains préférés! Les chandails, ils vous vont à ravir! Ça prend des Américains courageux pour honorer un pari avec autant de style!" (Translation for the reader: "Alex! Megan! My favorite Americans! The jerseys look magnificent on you! It takes brave Americans to honor a bet with such style!")

The handful of locals who overheard this burst into a new wave of appreciative, knowing laughter. Alex and Megan were no longer just fans; they were the Americans who had lost the bet. Their humiliation was now specific, public, and complete.

An older woman wiping flour from her hands stepped out from the doorway of the *boulangerie*. "*Ah, les pauvres Américains!*" she called out, her eyes twinkling. "*N'vous inquiétez pas, mes p'tits. Le rouge et blanc, ça vous va bien au teint!*" (Translation for the reader: "Ah, the poor Americans! Don't you worry, little ones. The red and white suits your complexion!")

Chloé, for her part, just laughed and playfully brandished the captured American jerseys, solidifying their victory. She leaned in and whispered to them, "See? They love you. You're honorary Canadians for the day."

LUCKY US, Alex's device deadpanned.

"Yeah, lucky us," Megan grumbled, but a small smile finally broke through. The teasing was merciless, but it was also undeniably warm and, in its own strange way, welcoming.
