# Part 4: Breakfast with the Enemy

The next morning, the family convoy set out for a Rhode Island institution: BayView Creamery. They went to Store Number 1, the original location in Middletown, a place James Miller had been going to his entire life.

"Ah, out in the country," James said with a nostalgic sigh as they pulled into the parking lot. "This was all farmland when I was a kid. You came out here for an ice cream, it felt like a real trip."

Inside, the air smelled of coffee and sizzling bacon. The seven of them slid into a large booth. Alex and Megan, looking like proud, happy fans in their Boston Brawlers jerseys and jeans, sat on one side. Chloé, however, sat beside Alex in her full, glorious "Uniform of Shame"—the Brawlers jersey, the black and gold fleece pants, and the offending black knit hat pulled down over her ears. She looked less like a fan and more like a mascot who had lost a very public, very painful bet.

The waitress, a cheerful woman with a thick local accent, came over. "Well now, look at you!" she said, smiling directly at Chloé. "Biggest Brawlahs fan in the whole place, I bet! What can I get for yah?"

"Just coffee, please. Black," Chloé managed, her smile tight and pained.

James, Megan, and Alex all ordered the restaurant's signature platter, the "BayView Bovine Breakfast," and with it, a regional delicacy. "And we'll all have a Coffee Milk," James added.

Sophie Mason, seated next to her husband Thomas, looked curious. "Coffee Milk? What is that?"

"It's a Rhode Island thing," Megan explained happily. "It's like chocolate milk, but with a sweet coffee syrup instead. It's the best." She then turned a brilliant, wicked smile on Chloé. "You know, Chloé, you really should try one. You can't get the full, authentic New England experience without it."

Chloé's eyes narrowed. "Wait a minute. Is this... part of the bet?"

Alex and Megan exchanged a knowing, silent smirk but didn't answer. Chloé let out a long, theatrical sigh. She knew it wasn't *technically* in the terms, but it was absolutely part of the punishment.

"Fine," she conceded to the waitress. "One Coffee Milk. But I'm officially lodging a protest with the international governing body of hockey wagers."

As the food arrived, the happy, boisterous chaos of seven family members catching up over breakfast began to swell. The clatter of silverware, the multiple, overlapping conversations, the scraping of chairs on the linoleum floor—a familiar, buzzing static began to build in Alex's mind. He tensed slightly, his focus starting to drift as the sensory input became overwhelming.

Chloé, sensing his shift, instinctively moved a little closer, her presence a quiet buffer against the noise. She engaged him in a low, direct conversation about their plans for the day, giving his brain a single, calm thread to focus on. On his other side, Megan subtly steered the main family conversation towards a less chaotic topic, lowering the overall volume. He appreciated their seamless, unspoken teamwork more than he could ever say.

A few minutes later, as the noise subsided, a quiet moment settled between Alex and Chloé. He reached out under the table and took her hand. She looked at him, her expression a mixture of mock annoyance and deep affection. He gave her a slow, brilliant smile, and his AAC device voiced a new message, quiet and just for her.

I WOULDN'T TRADE YOU FOR THE WORLD, CHLOÉ. EVEN IF YOU CHEER FOR THE WRONG TEAM.

A genuine, warm smile finally broke through her theatrical misery. She squeezed his hand. "I love you too, you big jerk," she whispered. "But for the record, it's still a toque."

BEANIE, his device voiced, a final, perfect jab.

"He's right, you know," Megan added, her voice full of a triumphant, loving grin. "It's a beanie."

The three of them burst into a shared, happy laugh. The sensory overload from a few minutes ago was already a distant memory, chased away by the familiar, comforting rhythm of their favorite argument.
