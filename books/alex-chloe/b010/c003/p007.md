# Part 7: A Walk Through a Changed Town (Summer 2006)

After finishing their Koko-Bons on the park bench, a sense of satisfaction settled over the three of them.

"So," Chloé said, checking the time. "Still four and a half hours to kill before we can check in. What's next on the grand tour?"

SHOW ME MORE OF YOUR CHILDHOOD, Alex typed. WHERE DID YOU GO TO SCHOOL?

Chloé’s face lit up. "Great idea! Come on."

They drove a few blocks to a handsome, older brick building surrounded by a chain-link fence: École des Pionniers. The grounds were quiet and empty for the summer. Chloé parked the van, and they got out. Alex used the standing feature of his new chair, allowing him and Megan to see easily over the fence as Chloé stood between them.

"This was it," she said softly, a wave of nostalgia in her voice. "My whole world from kindergarten to second grade. That was my classroom window, right there." She shared a few more stories, her memories painting a vivid picture of her childhood for both Alex and Megan.

After a while, their stomachs started to rumble. Lunch was a simple, delicious affair of steamed hot dogs (*steamies*), french fries, and Habitant Cola at a small, family-run *casse-croute* that hadn't changed in twenty years.

With a couple of hours still left, Chloé had one more stop. "Time for the main event," she said. "The library."

She parked the van on Rue Church, just as her family had done six years ago. The stately building of The Stanstead-Derby Line Free Library and Arts Centre stood before them. But the atmosphere was subtly different. A Canadian Border Services Agency (CBSA) vehicle was parked unobtrusively on their side of the street, and across the invisible line, a U.S. Border Patrol SUV was visible near the American entrance.

"Okay," Chloé said, her voice dropping slightly as she grabbed a small bag containing their passports and NEXUS cards. "The rules are different now. We can still walk across the American sidewalk to get to the front door, but they watch it like hawks. We have to have our documents on us. Just act normal, and don't be surprised if we get stopped."

Megan looked from the two official vehicles to Chloé's serious expression, a flicker of genuine confusion on her face. "Stopped? Just to go to the library?"

"Welcome to the new border," Chloé said with a wry, sad smile.

They got out of the van. The simple act of walking towards the corner of Caswell Avenue now felt charged with a tension that hadn't existed before. As they stepped onto the American sidewalk, a uniformed U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) officer who had been standing near the entrance politely but firmly held up a hand.

"Afternoon, folks," he said, his tone professional. "Just doing some spot checks today. May I see your travel documents, please?"

"Of course, officer," Chloé replied, her voice steady. She handed over her U.S. Passport and her NEXUS card. Alex and Megan presented their documents as well.

The officer examined them carefully, looking from the photos to their faces. "Citizenship?" he asked.

"U.S. citizens," Chloé answered simply and clearly for all three of them.

"And the purpose of your visit to the library today?" the officer continued.

"Just revisiting," Chloé explained. "I grew up around here, and wanted to show my husband and his cousin. We're staying on the Canadian side for the week."

The officer nodded, satisfied. The entire exchange was polite, professional, and took less than a minute, but it was a world away from the complete lack of scrutiny from their youth. He handed back their documents. "Everything's in order. Enjoy the library."

"Thank you, officer," Chloé said.

They walked the final few feet and entered the quiet, familiar sanctuary of the library, the tension from the encounter slowly ebbing away. Megan just shook her head, a look of profound, new understanding in her eyes. The black line on the floor was still there, but the invisible line outside was now very, very real.
