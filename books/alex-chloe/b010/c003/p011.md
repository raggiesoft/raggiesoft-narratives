# Part 11: Tensions at the Deli (Summer 2006)

Their second day trip to Montréal was dedicated to the classic tastes and sights. The morning was a triumphant success, from the view at Mount Royal to the fresh bagels from "La Flamme Ronde."

For their main lunch, they headed to the bustling Plateau neighborhood and found a parking spot near "Levine's Montréal Delicatessen." The place was a glorious assault on the senses: loud, crowded, and smelling divinely of smoked meat. They were seated at a small table. A few feet away sat a middle-aged woman, alone, with a tourist map spread out beside her.

The atmosphere at their table was a happy, impromptu French lesson.

"Okay, so when the waiter comes, the first thing you say is '*Bonjour, hi*,'" Chloé instructed Megan, who was listening with rapt attention. "It's the unofficial password of Montréal."

THEN YOU ASK FOR THE SMOKED MEAT 'MEDIUM-FAT', Alex's device chimed in, its Québécois voice a perfect mimic of a local. IT IS THE ONLY CORRECT WAY.

"Right, medium-fat," Megan repeated, practicing the French pronunciation. "And then I want a Black Cherry soda. How do I say that?"

As Chloé was coaching her, the woman at the nearby table grew more agitated. Finally, she stood up and approached their table, her gaze fixed with a sharp, condescending disapproval on Megan, the only one speaking English.

"*Excusez-moi, mais c'est le Québec ici. On parle français!*" she said sharply, her own French very formal and Parisian-sounding. (Excuse me, but this is Quebec. We speak French here!)

Megan looked up, startled and confused. Before she could even respond, Chloé’s expression hardened. "*Oui, on sait, Madame,*" Chloé replied, her own Québécois calm but firm. "*Et c'est exactement ce qu'on fait. On lui apprend.*" (Yes, we know, Ma'am. And that's exactly what we're doing. We're teaching her.)

The tourist was taken aback. "*Mais... un peu de respect, s'il vous plaît! C'est la loi! Je vais porter plainte à l'Office québécois de la langue française!*" (But... a little respect, please! It's the law! I'm going to file a complaint with the Office of the French Language!)

A ripple went through the surrounding tables. The other patrons—local Montréalers who knew the tourist was wrong—exchanged looks of weary amusement. Then, a quiet, unspoken act of solidarity began. A man at one table, who had been speaking French, deliberately switched.

"So anyway," he said to his wife in perfect English, "the traffic on the Décarie was a nightmare."

Slowly, contagiously, the low hum of French in the restaurant began to be replaced by the clear, crisp sounds of English. Alex and Chloé exchanged a look, a shared, mischievous glint in their eyes. Alex, with a few deft commands, switched his Vocal-Path Suite Pro to its American English voice.

The tourist looked around, completely bewildered as the room turned against her in a way she couldn't comprehend.

Before she could process this subtle rebellion, the seasoned waiter intervened, his Québécois French calm but firm. "Madame, avec tout le respect, cette jeune femme essaie d'apprendre notre langue. C'est une belle chose. S'il vous plaît, profitez de votre repas et de votre visite dans notre ville." (Ma'am, with all due respect, this young woman is trying to learn our language. It is a beautiful thing. Please, enjoy your meal and your visit to our city.)

The woman, having been publicly and correctly identified as a tourist trying to police a local teaching an American, flushed a deep crimson. She returned to her table, defeated.

Alex and Chloé watched her go, both feeling a sense of secondhand embarrassment. Alex typed on his device, his synthesized American English voice quiet but clear. THAT WAS... UNCOMFORTABLE. SHE IS WRONG. LEARNING A LANGUAGE IS ABOUT CONNECTION. NOT ABOUT FORCING PEOPLE.

Chloé reached across the table and squeezed his hand. "Exactly," she said, also in English. "That's not what being proud of your language is about. That's just being a bully."

Megan, who had been sitting in stunned silence, finally found her voice. "Wow," she said softly. "I... I'm so sorry. I didn't mean to cause a scene."

"You didn't cause anything," Chloé said, her voice fierce and protective. "She did. And now," she added, a determined grin returning, "your next lesson. How to order a smoked meat sandwich. In French. Very loudly."

The woman, having been publicly and correctly identified as a tourist trying to police a local teaching an American, flushed a deep crimson. She returned to her table, defeated, but not without shooting a final, withering glare in their direction.

Alex and Chloé watched her go, both feeling a sense of secondhand embarrassment. Alex typed on his device, his synthesized American English voice quiet but clear. THAT WAS... UNCOMFORTABLE. SHE IS WRONG.

Chloé reached across the table and squeezed his hand. "Exactly," she said. "That's not what being proud of your language is about. That's just being a bully."

Megan, who had been sitting in stunned silence, finally found her voice. "Wow," she said softly. "I... I'm so sorry. I didn't mean to cause a scene."

"You didn't cause anything," Chloé said, her voice fierce and protective. The glare from the tourist's table intensified. Chloé noticed it, and a wicked, defiant glint appeared in her eyes. "And now," she added, her voice suddenly loud and theatrically cheerful, "your next lesson. How to order a smoked meat sandwich. In French. Very loudly."

She turned to Megan, ignoring the tourist completely. "*Répète après moi, Meg,*" she instructed. (Repeat after me, Meg.) "*Je vais prendre un smoked meat, medium-fat, s'il vous plaît.*"

Megan, catching on to the game, repeated the phrase, her voice a little hesitant but much louder than necessary. The tourist shot them another furious glare. Chloé just rolled her eyes and leaned in to whisper to Megan, "Don't give a damn about the tourist, Meg. She's a lunatic."

Just then, the waiter arrived, a knowing, amused look on his face. He turned to Megan. "*Et pour vous, mademoiselle?*" (And for you, miss?)

Megan took a deep, theatrical breath and, with a proud look at Chloé, declared in her best, loudest Québécois accent, "*Je vais prendre un smoked meat, medium-fat, s'il vous plaît! Et un Coke aux cerises!*"

The tourist had finally had enough. "We're cussing now?" she snapped, her voice dripping with a condescending, Parisian disdain.

The comment was a match thrown on gasoline.

A burly man at the counter, who had been watching the whole exchange, lowered his newspaper and muttered, just loud enough for the whole room to hear, "*Tabarnak...*"

From another table, an older woman shook her head and added a quiet but firm, "*Câlisse...*"

Suddenly, as if on cue, a low, rumbling chorus of classic Québécois *sacres* began to fill the small deli. It wasn't a shout; it was a powerful, unified, and deeply cultural act of social expulsion. The entire room, united in their defense of the young woman who was trying to learn their language, had turned on the one person who was trying to gatekeep it.

The tourist stared, her face a mask of pure, horrified bewilderment as she was met with a symphony of words she had only ever heard in anger. She looked around, expecting an ally, and found only the cold, hard stares of a dozen Montréalers who had unanimously decided she was the problem.

Utterly defeated and completely humiliated, she grabbed her purse and, without having eaten a single bite of her food, fled the restaurant.

The moment the door closed behind her, a spontaneous, warm, and deeply appreciative round of applause broke out in the deli, all of it directed at Alex, Chloé, and Megan's table. The waiter just grinned. "*Excellent choix,*" he said to Megan. (Excellent choice.)
