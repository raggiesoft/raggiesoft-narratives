# Part 2: Senior Week

Ashley Parker's grand plan was simple, cruel, and hinged on a single, crucial element: the privacy of the back row of the movie theater. In her mind, she envisioned getting Alex and Jennifer settled into the dark, secluded back row, the perfect spot to pressure them into making out and whatever other inappropriate "shenanigans" she could orchestrate for her friends' amusement.

The plan, however, crumbled the moment they walked into the actual theater.

"Okay, back row, let's go!" Ashley declared, gesturing towards the stairs.

Alex, however, didn't move. He simply pointed his Quantum Communicator Jr. towards the large, clearly marked accessible seating area in the middle of the theater.

"What are you doing?" Ashley said, her voice sharp with annoyance. "The accessible seats are, like, right in the middle of everything. We need to be in the back." She looked at him, a dismissive smirk on her face. "You can get out of that thing, right? Just transfer into a regular seat."

Alex looked at the standard theater seats, with their fixed, immovable armrests. He could, in theory, transfer on his own, but the location of the armrests made it a difficult and potentially unsafe maneuver. And there was absolutely no way he was going to trust these girls, who he barely knew and instinctively distrusted, to help him. He gave a single, firm, and decisive shake of his head.

I AM STAYING IN MY CHAIR, his device voiced, its tone polite but completely non-negotiable.

Ashley stared at him, a look of pure, thwarted rage on her face. Her entire, carefully constructed plan for an afternoon of cruel entertainment had just been demolished by a fixed armrest and Alex's quiet, unshakeable refusal to compromise his own safety.

"Fine," she hissed, her voice dripping with frustration. "Whatever. Accessible row it is."

Grudgingly, the entire group settled into the designated wheelchair accessible row, right in the middle of the theater, surrounded by other moviegoers. The semi-privacy Ashley had counted on was gone. Her plan was in ruins.

The movie was Ashley Parker's choice, a raunchy, just-barely-PG-13 teen comedy called "Senior Week." The trailer, which had been in heavy rotation for weeks, had promised a non-stop barrage of hilarious, over-the-top pranks, culminating in a now-infamous scene involving a rival school's cheerleading squad and a malfunctioning sprinkler system. That scene was the sole reason Ashley had insisted, with a feverish excitement, that they all had to see it.

The reality, however, was a crushing disappointment. The movie was a dud, a string of flat, predictable jokes destined for the dustbin of crappy cinema. But the friends were there, and so was Alex, so for Ashley, the show must go on.

They were all situated in the accessible seating row at the middle of the theater. Alex was at the end, with Jennifer beside him, and the rest of the girls, led by Ashley, next to her. The movie droned on, a tedious backdrop to the real drama happening in their row.

Then, the moment from the trailer finally arrived on screen. The nerdy main character, Kevin, stumbled into the girls' locker room, crashed into a rack of basketballs, and accidentally triggered the fire alarm, unleashing the overhead sprinklers. The scene descended into pure, (barely) PG-13 chaos: drenched, shrieking cheerleaders, bouncing basketballs, and comedic panic.

It was the one genuinely funny scene in the entire film, and the teenagers in the audience roared with laughter. It was also Ashley's cue.

Under the cover of the on-screen chaos, she leaned over and whispered loudly to Jennifer, "Aren't you going to hold his hand? You're supposed to hold hands at the movies. It's, like, the law."

Jennifer, who was not a physically affectionate person to begin with, felt a hot flush of embarrassment. This was all a performance, and she was forgetting her lines. She could feel the expectant, mocking gaze of her friends on her in the dark.

"Go on," another girl hissed from the other side.

With a deep, internal sigh of resignation, Jennifer tentatively reached for Alex's hand, which was resting on his armrest. Her touch was hesitant, her palm a little sweaty. She didn't interlace their fingers; she just sort of... placed her hand on top of his. It was a cold, clinical, and deeply awkward gesture.

Alex, surprised by the sudden, unexpected contact, didn't move his hand away, but he didn't respond either. It felt strange, a foreign object resting on his skin. He turned his attention back to the movie, trying to ignore the weird, disconnected sensation.

Ashley, however, was not satisfied. "That's it?" she whispered aggressively. "What about a cuddle? You're supposed to cuddle up with him! Show him you like him!"

But that was a bridge too far for Jennifer. She just shook her head slightly in the dark and kept her focus rigidly on the screen, her hand remaining limply on top of Alex's. The other girls let out a collective, disappointed sigh.

The "moment" was over, leaving behind only a feeling of hollow, performative awkwardness. The movie's raunchy humor, which was supposed to be a catalyst for their own teenage fumbling, had only highlighted the complete lack of any real connection between them. Ashley’s ream of orchestrating a steamy back-row make-out session had been reduced to this: a single, awkward, and completely boring hand-hold. Ashley and her friends exchanged frustrated, scheming looks in the flickering light of the movie screen. Clearly, Jennifer wasn't up to the task. They were going to have to show her how it was done.
