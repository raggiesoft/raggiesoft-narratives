# Part 6: A Better Kind of Friendship

They left the food court and began to wander through the mall, their conversation easy and inclusive. Megan's friends asked him about his favorite video games, and they actually listened to his typed-out, detailed answers.

About an hour into their mall adventure, Alex gave Megan a subtle, familiar look. She understood instantly.

"Hey, guys," she said to her friends, her tone casual. "We'll be right back. I just need to help my cuz with something."

She started to wheel him in the direction of the family restroom near the department store. One of her friends, a kind-faced girl named Sarah Jenkins, asked, "Do you need any help?"

"No, we're good," Megan said with an appreciative smile. "This is old hat for us."

As they disappeared down the hallway, Ashley Parker and her friends happened to be coming out of a nearby clothing store. Ashley saw them go into the family restroom together. A slow, ugly, and triumphant smirk spread across her face. "Oh my God," she hissed to her friends, just loud enough for Megan's friends to overhear. "I knew it. She's totally doing him. How disgusting."

Megan's friends, who had been waiting patiently, all turned and stared at Ashley with a unified look of pure, unadulterated disgust.

"What is wrong with you?" Sarah snapped, her voice low and furious. "She's helping her cousin, who's in a wheelchair, go to the bathroom, you creep. Get a life."

Ashley just scoffed and walked away.

A few minutes later, when Megan and Alex emerged from the restroom, her friends were waiting, their expressions a mixture of anger and concern.

"Hey," Sarah said, "just so you know, that Ashley girl was over here saying some really gross, messed-up stuff about you guys."

Megan's face hardened, but then she just shook her head, a look of weary pity in her eyes. She looked at Alex, who had also overheard. "See, cuz?" she said, her voice full of a quiet, protective love. "That's the difference between them and us. We've got your back. Always."

She then looked at him, at the lingering tension in his shoulders, and made a decision. It was time to actively reclaim the ground that had been stolen from him.

"You know," she said, her voice now a gentle, therapeutic murmur. "That 'affection shower' thing they did to you at the theater? That wasn't a real hug. And I know you know what a real hug feels like, but sometimes, you just need a reminder." She opened her arms. "This," she said, wrapping him in a firm, grounding, and completely platonic hug, "is a real hug. Just 'I've got your back.' That's all."

Alex leaned into her, a profound sense of relief washing over him. This was the antidote. This was real.

As she pulled back, Sarah Jenkins stepped forward, her own expression kind and gentle. "And that awkward hand-holding thing at the movie?" she said. "That was just… weird. And performative." She looked at him, a genuine, uncomplicated smile on her face. "If you want, I can show you what a real, no-drama, 'just friends hanging out' hand-hold is supposed to feel like. No pressure at all."

The other girls in their group murmured their encouragement. "Yeah, Sarah, that's a great idea." "He should know what it's really like."

Alex looked at Sarah, at her kind, open face, and he gave a small, trusting nod. She gently took his hand in hers. Her touch was warm, firm, and completely uncomplicated. She didn't just place her hand on top of his; she interlaced their fingers, a simple, powerful gesture of connection. "See?" she said softly. "No big deal. Just friends."

It felt nothing like the cold, awkward, and performative hand-hold he had endured with Jennifer. This was safe. This was real. He gave her hand a small, grateful squeeze.

"Okay," Megan said, her voice full of a triumphant, protective joy. "Now, who wants to go to the comic book store? My treat. Alex needs to show us which new issues are actually worth reading."

The mood lifted instantly. As they began to walk, a new, beautiful, and healing dynamic formed. Megan took the handles of Alex's wheelchair, pushing him through the main hallway of the mall. And Sarah Jenkins walked beside them, still gently holding Alex's hand. The simple, public act was a powerful, silent declaration. He was not a victim to be mocked; he was a friend to be cherished and protected.

They walked like that all the way to "Cosmic Comics," a haven of colorful heroes and fantastical worlds. When they got to the entrance, Sarah gently squeezed his hand one last time before letting go, knowing he would need both of his hands to point out his favorite titles.

Just inside the store, she turned to him, her eyes shining with a genuine, uncomplicated affection. "Thanks for letting me do that, Alex," she said. She then opened her arms. "One more for the road?"

Alex, his heart full of a new, profound sense of safety and acceptance, smiled a genuine, happy smile and leaned in for the hug. This was the first hug he had received from someone who wasn't family since the "affection shower," and he realized, with a sense of quiet astonishment, that he really, really liked it. It was just a hug. A real hug. From a real friend.

He had survived the Jennifer arc. And he had come out the other side with a new, powerful piece of knowledge: he knew what real friendship felt like, and he knew he would never settle for anything less again.
