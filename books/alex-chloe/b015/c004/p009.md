# Part 9: La Poutine Célébratoire (The Celebratory Poutine)

As the catering staff began to serve the beautiful, three-tiered wedding cake, Alex looked at Chloé and Megan, a look of profound, almost mischievous satisfaction on his face. He had enjoyed the cake, but it wasn't the meal he had truly been waiting for. He typed on his Quantum Design Studio, his voice clear and full of a quiet, triumphant demand.

NOW, the device stated. IT IS TIME. THE CELEBRATORY POUTINE.

Chloé and Megan looked at each other and burst into a shared, knowing, and deeply happy laugh. They understood.

Uncle David, who had been waiting for this exact moment, threw his hands up in triumph. "*Enfin!*" he roared. (Finally!) "A man of taste! I told you he was a true Canadian at heart! Your wish, my boy, is my command!"

He gestured dramatically to the far side of the ballroom. On his cue, a curtain was pulled back with a theatrical flourish, revealing a surprise he had arranged with the caterers: a full, glorious poutine bar.

The reaction in the room was immediate and split perfectly along national lines. The entire Leclerc clan from Québec let out a collective, joyous roar of approval and immediately began to form a line. The American contingent, however—the Millers and the Carters from Virginia—just stared, a look of profound, baffled confusion on their faces.

Sophie Mason, beaming with the pride of a cultural ambassador, rushed over to lead the curious but hesitant group of Americans to the poutine bar, explaining the heavenly combination of fries, cheese curds, and gravy.

Uncle David himself stood behind the station, a chef's apron now tied over his tuxedo, a look of intense, professional focus on his face. He took a large, clean plate and began to construct the first, perfect poutine for Alex. As he worked, he spoke, his voice a low, serious monologue of culinary passion.

"This is not just any poutine, Alex," he began, his tone that of a grandmaster explaining a complex chess move. "This is a statement. The potatoes? Not just any potato. These are Russets, from a farm just outside of Saint-Hyacinthe, chosen for their perfect starch content. We hand-cut them this morning and blanched them at precisely 300 degrees Fahrenheit to cook the inside." He paused, gesturing to the vat of hot oil. "Then, and only then, are they fried a second time at 375 degrees, for that perfect, crispy shell."

He then moved to the cheese curds. "And the cheese!" he declared, holding up a fresh, squeaky curd for inspection. "This is not that sad, silent cheese you find in a plastic bag. This cheese was milk in a cow less than thirty-six hours ago. I went to the *fromagerie* myself yesterday to ensure the squeak was just right. It has to be room temperature, you see, so the gravy only melts it a little, not turns it into a soup."

Finally, he stood before the cauldron of gravy. "And the sauce," he said, his voice dropping to a reverent whisper. "This is not some simple beef broth. This is a true *sauce brune*, a family secret. It has been simmering for two days. It has layers. It has depth. It is, if I may say so, a work of art."

He ladled the rich, dark gravy over the fries and curds, the steam rising in a fragrant cloud. He handed the plate to Alex, his expression one of pure, unadulterated pride. "This," he declared to the room, "is a poutine that even that angry English chef on the television would have to respect. It is perfect. For you, my boy. For your wedding."

The Americans looked at the concoction, their expressions a mixture of horror and intrigued delight. James Miller, ever the good sport, was the first to step forward. "Well," he said, "when in Rome... or, you know, Québec. I'll try it."

One by one, the other Americans followed suit. A few minutes later, the entire, united, cross-border family was seated at their tables, all with plates of poutine. After a moment of tentative silence, a series of surprised, delighted exclamations came from the Virginia side of the family.

"My word," James said, his eyes wide after his first bite. "That's... that's actually incredible."

The poutine bar was an instant, massive success. It was the final, perfect, and delicious fusion of their two families. Alex, sitting between his wife and his cousin, a plate of his favorite comfort food in front of him, looked around the room at his loud, happy, and now poutine-loving family.

As he ate, a memory surfaced, clear and sharp: sitting in the quiet of his room on Lisa Court, weak and frail, dreaming of this exact moment. This wasn't just a taste of Québec; it was the taste of victory. It was the final, delicious, and hard-won symbol of a long journey. He looked at Chloé, who was watching him with tears in her eyes. He looked at Megan, who just gave him a huge, proud grin. A look of pure, unadulterated joy spread across his face. He was home.
