# Part 2: The Missing Semicolon (Fall 2002)

The ninety minutes of 4th period on Purple Days had become a weekly exercise in endurance for Alex. AP Computer Science, a class he should have loved, was a source of constant, low-grade anxiety. It was the one place in his structured, predictable world where Chloé wasn't. The absence was a physical ache, a persistent hum of loneliness that made it difficult to focus.

Today, Alex was deep into a project, building a graphical user interface for a small database application using the complex QFC (Quantum Foundation Classes). But his brain felt like it was full of static. He kept hitting the F5 key to compile his code, and each time, the same, infuriating error message popped up on his screen: ERROR: Line 147: Expected: end of statement;.

He stared at line 147, reading it for the tenth time. He knew the logic was sound, but his mind, which was mostly occupied with counting the minutes until the bell would ring and he could see Chloé again, simply couldn't find the mistake. The frustration was mounting, his shoulders tensing with each failed attempt to compile.

Hannah Peterson, sitting at the adjacent desk, noticed his growing agitation. She had come to recognize the subtle signs of his stress—the slight tightening of his jaw, the way his fingers would still on the keyboard. She leaned over slightly, her gaze respectful, not intrusive. She wasn't an expert in the complex QFC framework Alex was using, but she was a talented programmer in her own right, and she could read basic C++ as easily as she could read a novel. She saw the error message on his screen.

Without a word, she gently, very gently, reached out and tapped a single spot on his monitor with her finger.

"Alex?" she whispered, her voice barely audible above the hum of the computers. "I think... I think you're just missing a semicolon. Right there. At the end of line 147."

Alex blinked, his focus snapping back from his internal countdown to the screen. He looked where she was pointing. And there it was. A simple, stupid, missing semicolon. An error he would have caught in a microsecond on any other day, but one that his distracted, Chloé-less brain had completely overlooked.

He felt a flush of embarrassment, but it was immediately washed away by a wave of profound gratitude. She hadn't made a big deal of it. She hadn't called the teacher over. She had just quietly, respectfully, offered the solution.

He typed the semicolon, his fingers moving with a newfound clarity. He pressed F5. The compiler ran, and this time, the graphical application window he had designed popped up on the screen, working perfectly.

He let out a slow, shuddering breath, the tension of the last half-hour draining out of him. He turned to Hannah, his eyes full of a gratitude that went beyond just a simple coding fix. She had done more than just find a bug; she had thrown him a lifeline in the middle of his ninety-minute storm.

He looked at her, at her kind, understanding smile, and an instinct, a new and unfamiliar one, took over. He opened his arms.

Hannah's eyes widened for a fraction of a second in surprise, but then her smile softened into something warm and genuine. She understood the significance of the gesture. She leaned in and accepted his hug. It wasn't a "big squeeze" hug like Chloé's or Megan’s; it was a gentle, comforting embrace between two friends, a silent acknowledgment of a shared, quiet victory.

"Anytime, Alex," she whispered as she pulled back.

When the bell finally rang, and Chloé appeared in the doorway, she saw Alex and Hannah laughing quietly over a shared piece of code. A sense of profound relief and happiness washed over her. Alex had found his own quiet miracle, a different kind of anchor to get him through the one part of their day they couldn't share. And for that, she was incredibly grateful.
