# Part 2: The First Separation (First Purple Day, Fall 2002)

The bell signaling the end of third period—their shared Lunch and Study Hall—jarred Alex with a new and unwelcome anxiety. For three years, that bell had simply meant it was time to head to their last class of the day, together. But today, for the first time, it meant something else. It meant separating.

Chloé finished packing her books, a small, sad smile on her face that mirrored the ache in Alex's own chest. "Well," she said softly, "I guess this is it. My first solo flight to AP French."

Alex just nodded, the simple act feeling heavy. He hated this. He hated that for the next ninety minutes, his anchor, his safe person, would be in a different room, a different world.

"I'll meet you right here, by these lockers, the second the bell rings, okay?" she promised, her hand resting on his shoulder for a moment, a familiar, grounding pressure he already missed.

OKAY, his device voiced, the single word feeling small and inadequate.

She gave his shoulder a final, reassuring squeeze, then turned and headed down the crowded hallway in the opposite direction. Alex watched her go, a feeling of profound, terrifying loneliness settling over him. He took a deep, shaky breath and turned his own chair towards the computer lab at the other end of the school.

He arrived at the AP Computer Science classroom a minute later, his anxiety a low, persistent hum. The room was familiar, but it felt wrong without Chloé there to share a quick, whispered joke before the teacher started. He maneuvered his chair to the accessible workstation the school had reserved for him.

He was so focused on the empty space where Chloé should have been that he didn't notice someone else sitting at the adjacent desk until she spoke.

"Hey, Alex," a soft, familiar voice said.

He looked over. It was Hannah Peterson, his lab partner from sophomore year Biology. She was a senior now, too, her quiet, intelligent presence a familiar part of the school's advanced science track. She offered him a small, kind smile.

"It feels weird, doesn't it?" she said, her gaze full of a surprising, gentle understanding. "Seeing you come into a class without Chloé."

Alex stared at her for a moment, surprised by her directness and her perception. He gave a small, almost imperceptible nod.

Hannah didn't try to fill the silence or make small talk. She simply acknowledged the truth of the situation. "I get it," she said quietly. "My best friend moved to California last year. It's... hard. When your person isn't there."

She then turned her attention to her own computer, giving him space, but her quiet, empathetic observation was a small, unexpected comfort. She wasn't trying to replace Chloé. She wasn't making a romantic move. She was just a kind person who saw the empty space beside him and, for a brief, compassionate moment, acknowledged his grief.

The teacher started the lecture, and Alex tried to focus on the complex algorithms on the screen. But for the entire ninety-minute period, he was acutely, painfully aware of the empty air to his right, the space where Chloé was supposed to be. Hannah's quiet, respectful presence beside him was a small island of kindness in a sea of loneliness, but it couldn't change the fundamental truth: for the first time in their high school lives, he was navigating a part of his world alone, and he hated every single second of it.
