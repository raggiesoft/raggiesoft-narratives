# Part 5: A Line too Far (for Josh Riley)

The Northwood High cafeteria pulsed with its usual lunchtime chaos. Seniors Alex Miller and Chloé Mason were at their familiar table near the windows. Alex, in his "Quantum Glide" wheelchair, might have been showing Chloé something on his "Quantum OS Tablet Pro" AAC device, while Chloé, ever attentive, listened, her Québécois French occasionally peppering her replies. Across the room, Luna and Leo Porter were at their own table. Luna, her noticeable limp a familiar part of her rhythm as she moved around the cafeteria earlier, now sat comfortably, one hand perhaps resting on the armrest of Leo’s "Quantum PowerChair XT" as she often did for support when standing or shifting. Leo was engaged with his own "Quantum Communicator XT."

Josh Riley, also a senior and a four-year veteran of Mrs. DeMarco's "special programs" (a status accompanied by a faint, lingering aroma of old school lunches), was, unfortunately, also present. He spotted Chloé first, perhaps as Alex was momentarily engrossed in his AAC.

"Hey there, Mason," Josh swaggered over, leaning uncomfortably close, his voice carrying a practiced, unearned confidence. "Still playing nursemaid to Miller? You know, a pretty girl like you could do a lot better than someone who needs a robot to talk for him. What are you doing after school?" He smirked.

Chloé looked up, her eyes instantly turning to ice. "It's Chloé, Josh. And what Alex and I do is none of your business. Back off."

Josh's smirk widened. "Ooh, feisty. I like feisty. So, ditch the Tin Man later for a real man?" He winked, leaning closer.

Before Chloé could unleash the torrent of Québécois French that was clearly brewing, Alex’s AAC voiced, calm but firm: "JOSH. LEAVE. CHLOÉ. ALONE. NOW."

Josh just laughed, a harsh, dismissive sound. "Or what, Miller? You gonna beep at me louder?" He made to lean even closer to Chloé, his intentions clear.

Chloé stood up abruptly. "I said, back off." Her voice was low and dangerous.

Undeterred, or perhaps just that monumentally obtuse, Josh simply rolled his eyes. Seeing his approach with Chloé thwarted, his gaze then fell on Luna Porter, who was laughing at something Leo had just typed. New target, his expression seemed to say.

He sauntered over to their table. "Hey there, Porter," he said, directing his attention solely to Luna, completely ignoring Leo. "You know, you're actually kinda cute when you're not limping around with your brother all the time. What say you ditch the chair-warmer and hang out with a real senior sometime? My parents are out tonight..." He leered, making his crude implication unmistakable.

Luna stared at him, her good humor vanishing instantly, replaced by a cold fury. Leo's head snapped up, his fingers flying across his AAC.

"She's not interested, Riley," Leo’s machine echoed, "And she's my twin sister. Show some respect, if you're capable of it."

"Respect?" Josh scoffed. "I'm just saying, a girl like you shouldn't be tied down. I could show you a much better time than he ever could." He took another step closer to Luna, his intentions clear.

"That's quite enough, Mr. Riley."

The voice, calm, clear, and carrying an arctic chill that could freeze the infamous mystery meat solid, cut through the cafeteria din. Every head in the vicinity turned. Mrs. DeMarco stood there, arms crossed, her expression one of profound disappointment mixed with something that looked suspiciously like… a grim satisfaction.

Josh Riley froze, a deer caught in the headlights of an oncoming sanitation truck. He knew that tone. Oh, he knew that tone. This was worse than the Pig Latin incident. Worse than the Gaelic meatloaf debacle. This felt… final.

"Mrs. DeMarco," he stammered, "I was just... talking to them."

"Indeed, Mr. Riley," she said, her voice still deceptively mild. "You were 'talking.' First, you were attempting to harass Miss Mason. When understandably rebuffed by both her and Mr. Miller, you then moved on to Ms. Porter, with equally unwelcome, disrespectful, and frankly, rather pathetic advances. In both instances, you were clearly told your attentions were unwanted, and yet you persisted. Is that an accurate summary of your lunchtime social endeavors, Mr. Riley?"

Josh swallowed hard, his gaze darting around like a trapped animal. "I... uh..."

"This is not merely a case of poor judgment, Mr. Riley, of which you have demonstrated a consistent and lamentable abundance over your four years here," Mrs. DeMarco continued, her voice now beginning to resonate with the authority that made even seasoned football players tremble. "This is harassment. This is a profound disrespect for your fellow students, for the sanctity of this cafeteria, and frankly, for the basic tenets of human decency. You, a senior, who has had ample opportunity to learn the rules of civilized conduct, should know better. But it appears some lessons require... perpetual reinforcement, even unto the brink of graduation."

She took a small, deliberate step closer. "Given your repeated inability to comprehend simple social cues, your blatant disrespect, and your decision to target not one, but two young women in a single lunch period after being explicitly told to desist, I am left with very few options. You have, shall we say, exhausted the standard curriculum of our after-school enrichment programs."

Josh looked like he was about to be physically ill. The other students were rapt, a silent, horrified audience.

"Therefore, Mr. Riley," Mrs. DeMarco announced, her voice now ringing with a terrible finality, "your usual afternoon appointment by the dumpsters will, of course, be honored for the remainder of your tenure at Northwood High. However, this particular series of egregious transgressions warrants an additional, more... memorable... consequence. I will be having a word with Principal Thompson this afternoon regarding your conduct. I believe a formal administrative referral for harassment is very much in order. Perhaps a conversation with him, and subsequently with your parents, about the privileges of attending this institution – privileges you seem determined to abuse – will finally impress upon you the importance of respecting others."

The blood drained from Josh Riley's face. The Principal. A referral. His parents. This was it. The nuclear option. This would go on his permanent record. This could affect his graduation, his future, everything.

"No! Please, Mrs. DeMarco!" Josh finally found his voice, a panicked squeak. "Anything but that! I'll do... I'll do double trash duty! Grease traps! For a month! For the rest of the year! I'll write an essay in ancient Sumerian! Just... please don't tell the Principal!" His usual bravado was gone, replaced by raw terror.

Mrs. DeMarco regarded him for a long, silent moment, her expression unreadable. The entire cafeteria held its breath.

"Very well, Mr. Riley," she said at last, her voice softening almost imperceptibly, though the steel remained. "Perhaps there is a flicker of self-preservation in you yet. While I believe your actions today do warrant Principal Thompson's immediate attention, I am... occasionally... amenable to alternative sentencing for a truly repentant, albeit incorrigibly habitual, offender."

She paused. "You will report for trash duty this afternoon at 2:15 PM, as usual. You will perform this duty every school day until graduation. Additionally, for the next two academic weeks, you will also be responsible for the meticulous cleaning of all cafeteria trays and the degreasing of the deep fryer vents. And," she added, the faintest hint of a smile playing on her lips, "you will write me a twenty-page, double-spaced essay on the topic: 'The Importance of Consent and Respectful Interpersonal Communication in a Diverse School Environment, with Specific Reference to the Detrimental Impact of Unwanted Advances and the Abysmal Failure to Heed Clear Social Rejection.' It will be due, on my desk, one month from today. Any deviation, any further complaints, and my conversation with Principal Thompson will be immediate and most unfavorable for you."

Josh Riley looked like he might weep with a perverse kind of relief. It was an astronomical punishment, a sentence that would define the rest of his senior year, ensuring he probably would permanently stink of dumpster. But it wasn't a referral. He had, by the skin of his teeth and the grace of Mrs. DeMarco's unique sense of justice, avoided administrative Armageddon.

"Yes, Mrs. DeMarco," he choked out, utterly defeated. "Thank you, Mrs. DeMarco."

"Don't thank me, Mr. Riley," she said coolly. "Thank your lucky stars, and perhaps the faint hope I hold that even you, after four years, might finally learn something. Now, finish your lunch. Quietly."

With a final, sweeping gaze that took in the entire, now utterly cowed cafeteria, Mrs. DeMarco turned and resumed her patrol.

Alex, Chloé, Luna, and Leo exchanged looks of pure, unadulterated awe.

This time, Josh Riley hadn't just crossed Mrs. DeMarco. He'd almost crossed a line even she wouldn't tolerate without escalating to the highest levels. He truly had, as always, a special space waiting for him at the dumpsters, now with added responsibilities.
