# Part 6: A Day of Two Cultures (December 23, 2003)

The morning of December 23rd dawned bright and chilly on Lisa Court. In the Masons' house, the "Cousins' Suite" was stirring. Mathieu and Isabelle emerged, looking a little rumpled after a night spent sharing a bed for the first time in their lives. Across the lawn, Alex, Chloé, and Megan were already with the Millers, the seven of them enjoying a quiet breakfast.

The plan for the day was a perfect fusion of their two families' traditions: a trip to the grocery store to gather supplies for a massive Christmas Eve Réveillon feast, followed by an afternoon of holiday baking.

The entire group—the Millers, the Masons, the Leclercs, and Megan—descended on the local supermarket in a convoy of two minivans. For Mathieu and Isabelle, the sheer scale of the suburban Virginia supermarket was an attraction in itself.

" *Regarde la taille de ça!* " Mathieu whispered to Isabelle, his eyes wide as they stared down an aisle that seemed to stretch for a mile, dedicated entirely to breakfast cereal. (Look at the size of this place!)

The shopping trip was a loud, happy, bilingual chaos. The cousins, Mathieu and Isabelle, were on a mission of their own, fascinated by the variety of American snack foods. They were in the cracker aisle when Mathieu suddenly stopped, pulling a can off the shelf. "*C'est quoi, ça?*" he asked, turning the can over in his hands. (What is this?)

It was a bright blue can with a picture of a cracker covered in a perfect swirl of orange cheese: "Rocket Cheese."

Megan, who was pushing the cart alongside Alex and Chloé, burst out laughing. "Oh, that's a classic American treasure," she said. "It's cheese in a can."

Isabelle's eyes went wide. "*Du fromage... en canette? Comme de la crème fouettée?*" (Cheese... in a can? Like whipped cream?)

"Exactly!" Chloé confirmed. "You can't get it in Canada at all. Mom says it's because it's so processed it can't legally be called 'cheese' there. It doesn't meet the food standards."

The cousins stared at the can, then at each other. The idea of a cheese that wasn't legally cheese was the most bizarre and fascinating thing they had ever seen. "We have to try it," Isabelle declared.

And so, a can of Rocket Cheese was added to the cart.

Back at the Masons' house, the kitchen became the warm, fragrant heart of their combined holiday. Mémère Madeleine oversaw the *tourtière*, while Sophie and Geneviève worked on other dishes.

In the midst of it all, at the dining room table, a quieter, sweeter job was underway. Alex, Chloé, and Megan were tasked with decorating Christmas cookies. It was a peaceful island away from the kitchen chaos. Alex, with his steady hands, was perfect for the detailed work of piping frosting. As the happy, boisterous energy of the full house reached a crescendo, Alex felt a flicker of sensory overload. Megan, sitting beside him, noticed his shoulders tense. Without a word, she reached over and gave his arm a firm, grounding squeeze, a quiet, two-second hug that was their own special language. He visibly relaxed and gave her a small, grateful smile. Chloé, sitting on his other side, saw the exchange and felt a profound sense of peace. By now, she knew Alex's two primary sources of comfort: her own hugs, and the equally safe, grounding hugs from his cousin.

They worked in a comfortable, happy silence, a perfect trio of friends at the heart of their chaotic, wonderful, cross-border family.
