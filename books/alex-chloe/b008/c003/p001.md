# Part 1: The Video Call Interruption

The late afternoon sun of Fall 2003 cast long shadows across Lisa Court as Vera’s Holt Automotive sedan pulled up in front of the Miller house. This was it – her first visit to Alex’s home.

Emily Miller opened the door with a warm, welcoming smile. "Vera, dear, come in! Alex has been looking forward to you coming over." James Miller looked up from his armchair, offering a polite nod.

"Thank you so much for having me, Mrs. Miller," Vera said, her voice exuding charm as she presented Emily with a small potted chrysanthemum.

Alex watched as Vera followed his mother towards the kitchen, already making herself agreeable. He knew his scheduled video call with Chloé was coming up soon. A few minutes later, as Vera and Emily chatted, Alex navigated his wheelchair to the desk where his computer was set up. He opened the video call application, his fingers moving with familiar speed.

The living room was soon filled with the cheerful, familiar sound of Chloé Mason’s voice. Their conversation was light and easy, a cherished ritual that bridged the miles.

"...so then Professor Abernathy actually tried to explain quantum entanglement using sock puppets!" Chloé was saying from her dorm room, laughing. "It was… memorable!"

Alex grinned, typing quickly on his AAC: HE DID NOT! SOCK PUPPETS? THAT IS AMAZING.

Vera, who had been ostensibly helping Emily, appeared in the living room doorway. "Alex," her voice cut in sharply. "Are you still on the phone with *her*? We were supposed to watch that movie I brought over."

Alex startled, his smile faltering. JUST FINISHING UP, VERA, he typed.

Chloé, on screen, picked up on the sudden tension. "Hey Alex, no worries if you need to go! We can talk later, okay?"

"No, it's fine," Vera said, her voice dripping with false sweetness as she positioned herself in the webcam's view. "I just think it's a little rude, Alex, to ignore your *actual* girlfriend for some long-distance high school buddy."

Alex felt a flush of embarrassment. Instinctively, hoping to create a small space of comfort, he switched his AAC to its Québécois French voice: CHLOÉ, MA BELLE AMIE, VERA EST UN PEU… TENDUE AUJOURD'HUI. (Chloé, my dear friend, Vera is a little… tense today.)

Chloé, understanding perfectly, immediately responded in kind. "*Pas de problème, Alex, mon cher. Prends ton temps.*" (No problem, Alex, my dear. Take your time.)

Vera, hearing the sudden switch to a language she didn’t understand, looked furious. "What the hell? What are you two even saying? Alex, speak English! This is rude!"

At this point, Emily and Sophie Mason, who had been working together in the kitchen, intervened. Emily walked into the living room, a warm, unruffled smile on her face. "*Chère Chloé, bonjour! Comment vas-tu à Lynnwood?*" she said, her own learned Québécois surprisingly warm and natural. (Dear Chloé, hello! How are you in Lynnwood?)

Sophie followed, wiping her hands on an apron. "*On est en train de préparer une surprise pour le souper, ma belle! Une vraie poutine!*" (We're making a surprise for dinner, my sweet! Real poutine!)

Vera stared, completely bewildered. They all spoke French?

Chloé, on screen, let out a gasp of pure, ecstatic delight. "*Pas possible! Maman, vous avez utilisé les bons fromages en grains? Ceux qu'on a rapportés de Stanstead?*" (No way! Mom, did you use the good cheese curds? The ones we brought back from Stanstead?)

"*Bien sûr!*" Sophie confirmed proudly. (Of course!)

James Miller then stepped forward, also addressing Chloé in comfortable Québécois French. "Chloé, *ma fille*, it's good to see your face. Alex, son, please, don't let Vera interrupt your call. You continue chatting with Chloé. Take all the time you need. We don't care if it's in Québécois French or American English – though, frankly, Sophie and Thomas have taught us so well, we rather enjoy hearing the French!" He winked.

Vera stared at the Millers, then at Alex, her face a mask of pure indignation and suspicion. This "Chloé" wasn't just some distant friend; she was deeply enmeshed with Alex's entire family, to the point of a shared private language and secret, special food. The feeling of being an outsider, of being actively excluded, was palpable.

"What is going on?" Vera demanded in American English, her voice shrill. "Since when does everyone in this house speak French except me?"

James Miller turned to Vera, his expression now quite stern. "Vera," he said, "Alex is allowed to speak with his best friend Chloé, who is like family to us, in whatever language they are both comfortable with. It is not 'rude'; it is their connection, and we encourage it."

Emily then addressed Vera, her polite smile not quite reaching her eyes. "Vera, why don't you come with James and me into the other room? You can tell us about that movie you brought. Alex and Chloé will join us when they're done." Her tone was pleasant, but it was a clear, non-negotiable directive.

Vera looked from James's unyielding expression to Emily's calm but firm one. She saw no allies here. They weren't falling for her victim act. With a huff and a venomous glare at Chloé's smiling image on the screen, Vera stalked out of the room, James and Emily following.

Alex let out a shaky breath, the tension slowly ebbing. His parents… they had seen. They had stepped in. They had defended him. He turned back to the computer screen, where Chloé was waiting, her expression a mixture of concern, relief, and wry amusement.

MERCI D'AVOIR ATTENDU! he typed, his relief palpable. MES PARENTS SONT LES MEILLEURS! VERA EST… TRÈS DIFFICILE AUJOURD'HUI. (THANKS FOR WAITING! MY PARENTS ARE THE BEST! VERA IS… VERY DIFFICULT TODAY.)

Chloé’s laughter, warm and genuine, filled the speakers again. "*Je vois ça, Alex! Tes parents sont magnifiques! Alors, la poutine...*" (I see that, Alex! Your parents are magnificent! So, about that poutine...)

Their conversation resumed, Alex feeling a profound sense of gratitude. Vera had made a scene, but in doing so, she had only revealed her own unpleasant nature further and solidified the Millers' understanding that Chloé was, indeed, a far more positive and connected presence in their son's life. The lines had been drawn, and Alex's loving parents had made it unequivocally clear on which side they stood.
