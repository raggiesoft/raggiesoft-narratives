# Part 4: The Flight Home

The trip to Seattle-Tacoma International Airport was a stark contrast to the last time Alex had been in a transport hub. This time, he was surrounded by a loving, protective phalanx of family. The check-in process was a chaotic symphony of echoing announcements and rolling suitcases. Emily, seeing the immediate sensory overload begin to register on Alex's pale, still-gaunt face, quickly produced a pair of foam earplugs.

Boarding the Gateway Airlines flight was the most difficult part of the journey. The transfer from his own wheelchair to the narrow aisle chair was a slow, delicate process. He was still profoundly weak, and James and Thomas had to bear most of his weight, their movements a testament to their gentle strength.

They had pre-boarded, and the family made their way to their seats. Alex took the window seat—his first time on a plane, he deserved the view. For the long first leg to Chicago, Megan slid into the middle seat beside him, with Chloé taking the aisle. The four parents settled in behind them.

As the plane took off, Alex's hand reflexively gripped the armrest. Megan, without a word, wrapped an arm around his shoulders in a firm, steadying, and deeply grounding hug. Throughout the long flight, she was his anchor. Her role was not just emotional support; she was a vigilant caregiver, ensuring the pressure on his still-healing sores was minimized and that he was comfortable.

The family had come prepared. When the flight attendants came by with drinks and snacks, Chloé politely declined. Instead, she reached into a specially packed cooler at her feet and pulled out a small, pre-made container of the simple, bland chicken and rice that was still the core of Alex's recovery diet. It was a quiet, necessary act of care, a stark contrast to the bags of salty peanuts and sugary sodas the other passengers were enjoying.

The connection at Chicago's Midway Airport was a welcome relief. James had deliberately booked their travel through the smaller, more manageable airport. They moved through it as a single, efficient unit, creating a protective bubble around Alex, whose stamina was already beginning to wane.

For the final, shorter flight to Norfolk, the seating arrangement deliberately changed. This time, it was Chloé who slid into the middle seat beside him. The moment the plane was in the air, she took his hand. Her hug was different from Megan's—if Megan's was a fierce, protective shield, Chloé's was a quiet, profound homecoming.

Alex leaned his head against her shoulder, a deep, bone-weary contentment washing over him. The trauma of the past month was a distant, fading storm. Here, suspended thousands of feet in the air, surrounded by his entire family and buffered by the two most important, most trusted, and most beloved women in the entire world, he was not just safe. He was home.
