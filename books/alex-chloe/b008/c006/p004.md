# Part 4: The Drive to Lynnwood – Safety and Silence (Wednesday, November 22, 2003)

The click of the car door shutting as Chloé Mason slid into the driver's seat of her Horizon Avalon felt like a definitive seal, closing off the nightmare of Carol's house in Kent and the suffocating presence of Vera. Alex Miller, eighteen years old and utterly traumatized, was in the passenger seat beside her, his duffel bag with his precious basic AAC device charger and whatever clothes he had. His device sitting safely in his lap. Chloé didn't waste a moment. With a final, quick glance in her rearview mirror to ensure Vera wasn't making some last-ditch effort to follow, she pulled away from the curb, leaving Kent and its oppressive memories behind.

Chloé skillfully navigated the residential streets, her car's dashboard GPS unit promptly chiming in with directions. Chloé had set its language preference to "Français," and its synthesized voice, clear and a bit formal, delivered instructions in Metropolitan French.

"*Continuez tout droit sur Elm Street pendant un kilomètre,*" the GPS intoned. (Continue straight on Elm Street for one kilometer.)

As they approached end of SR-167, and the on-ramp for the next freeway, the GPS announced clearly, "*Prenez la bretelle d'accès à droite pour I-405 Nord en direction d’Bellevue.*" (Take the ramp on the right for I-405 North toward Bellevue.)

Hearing those words – "I-405 Nord en direction d'Bellevue" – even in the formal French of the GPS, caused a huge, almost involuntary sigh of relief to escape Alex. Bellevue. Lynnwood was north of Bellevue, on the way. They were truly heading north, away from Kent, towards Chloé’s home, towards safety. He *knew* he was out of Kent now. That simple directional confirmation was a more powerful reassurance than he could have imagined.

Traffic was indeed picking up, the early signs of the pre-Thanksgiving rush already evident. Cars and trucks merged and flowed. But inside Chloé's small sedan, a profound quiet settled, punctuated only by the GPS and their own soft words, spoken in the language that had become their refuge. Alex felt that Québécois French was *their* "safe language" now, a space Vera couldn't penetrate.

He glanced over at Chloé as she expertly merged onto I-405 North. He reached for his AAC, its voice output also set to the Québécois French Chloé had helped him program years ago.

"*Chloé,*" his device voiced, the Québécois lilt familiar and comforting, "*quand le GPS a dit 'direction Bellevue... j'ai su. J'ai su que nous étions vraiment sortis de là. Merci.*" (Chloé, when the GPS said 'toward you Bellevue... I knew. I knew we were really out of there. Thank you.)

Chloé glanced at him, a small, tired but deeply reassuring smile touching her lips. "*Oui, Alex, mon trésor. On est sortis. Tu es en sécurité maintenant. On rentre à la maison.*" (Yes, Alex, my treasure. We're out. You are safe now. We're going home.)

A comfortable silence held for a few miles, Chloé focusing on the increasingly dense traffic, Alex simply absorbing the feeling of safety. Then Chloé spoke again, her voice soft but filled with a gentle determination, still in their shared Québécois French.

"*Alex, écoute-moi. Quand on arrivera à mon appartement, voici ce qu'on va faire, d'accord?*" (Alex, listen to me. When we get to my apartment, here's what we're going to do, okay?)

Alex turned his head slightly, looking at her, waiting.

"Premièrement," Chloé continued, her tone practical but infused with care, "on va appeler tes parents. Tout de suite. Il faut qu'ils sachent que tu es en sécurité avec moi, et ce qui s'est réellement passé. Vera leur a sûrement raconté des mensonges." (Firstly, we're going to call your parents. Right away. They need to know that you are safe with me, and what really happened. Vera surely told them lies.)

Alex nodded slowly, the thought of his parents, of their inevitable worry and anger at Vera, a fresh wave of emotion.

"Deuxièmement," Chloé went on, her gaze briefly flicking to him, noting his pallor and the way he was still subtly trembling, "tu as désespérément besoin d'un bain. Un vrai bon bain chaud pour te laver de toute cette… cette saleté de Vera. Et il faut vérifier ta peau, s'assurer qu'il n'y a pas de plaies." (Secondly, you desperately need a bath. A real good hot bath to wash off all this… this filth from Vera. And we need to check your skin, make sure there are no sores.)

He flinched almost imperceptibly at the mention of his skin, the memory of the developing pressure sores a fresh sting of shame and fear. He trusted Chloé implicitly to care for him, as she always had during their high school years when he needed help, but the thought of his current neglected state being seen by her was still mortifying.

Chloé, sensing his discomfort, reached over and briefly squeezed his arm. "*Ne t'inquiète pas, Alex. C'est moi. Je vais prendre soin de toi. Comme toujours.*" (Don't worry, Alex. It's me. I will take care of you. Like always.)

Her simple reassurance, spoken in the language of their deepest trust, eased some of his apprehension.

"Et troisièmement," Chloé concluded, her voice softening further, "il faut que tu manges quelque chose de bon, quelque chose de chaud. Tu as l'air affamé, mon pauvre ami. Je ne sais pas quand tu as mangé pour la dernière fois un vrai repas." (And thirdly, you need to eat something good, something hot. You look starved, my poor friend. I don't know when you last ate a real meal.)

The thought of simple, warm food, prepared by Chloé, felt like a distant dream. He *was* starving. Vera had barely bothered with proper meals on the bus or at Aunt Carol's, often just handing him a bag of chips or a stale sandwich, if anything at all.

He typed on his AAC: "Oui, Chloé. Tes plans sont bons. J'ai… j'ai très faim. Et le bain… oui, s'il te plaît. Merci d'être venue pour moi. Merci de me ramener à la maison." (Yes, Chloé. Your plans are good. I… I am very hungry. And the bath… yes, please. Thank you for coming for me. Thank you for bringing me home.)

The GPS chimed in again: "*Restez sur I-405 Nord pendant encore vingt kilomètres.*" (Stay on I-405 North for another twenty kilometers.)

Chloé smiled faintly. "*Vingt kilomètres, Alex. Bientôt, tu seras vraiment chez toi. Dans notre petit sanctuaire à Lynnwood.*" (Twenty kilometers, Alex. Soon, you will truly be home. In our little sanctuary in Lynnwood.)

He closed his eyes, leaning his head against the cool glass of the passenger window, the vibrations of the Horizon Avalon a soothing rhythm. The pain in his hip was still there, the exhaustion was a heavy blanket, but for the first time in what felt like an eternity, Alex felt a tiny, fragile seed of peace begin to take root in the ravaged landscape of his heart. Chloé was driving, her voice a melody of care in their safe language, her GPS confirming their northward path away from the darkness, in the only language he felt safe with at the moment. And he was beside her. That was all that mattered.

The metallic, synthesized voice of the GPS cut through the quiet hum of the car, a proclamation of finality. "Dans deux virgule cinq kilomètres, prenez la sortie à droite pour l'Interstate 5 Sud en direction de Lynnwood et Seattle." (In 2.5 kilometers, take the exit on the right for Interstate 5 South toward Lynnwood and Seattle.)

Alex’s eyes, which had been closed, fluttered open. He looked past Chloé to the large green sign looming ahead, its white letters spelling out the words the GPS had just spoken. *I-5 South. Lynnwood.* These were the signposts of his real life, the one before Vera, the one centered around his family and Chloé. Seeing them now felt like seeing a lighthouse after being lost at sea. A single tear, hot and silent, escaped the corner of his eye and traced a clean path through the grime on his cheek. He wasn't just safe *from* Kent; he was safe *in* Lynnwood. The distinction was monumental. He was home.

Chloé glanced over, her eyes catching the glint of his tear in the afternoon light. Her expression softened. "On y est presque, Alex. Presque à la maison," she murmured, her Québécois a soft counterpoint to the GPS's formal French. (We're almost there, Alex. Almost home.)

She expertly maneuvered the Avalon into the exit lane. The car climbed the long, sweeping ramp that arced over the steady stream of I-405 traffic below. As they merged seamlessly onto I-5 South, the change was immediate. The flow of cars felt different, more familiar. They were on the final leg.

No sooner had their tires settled onto the new freeway than the GPS chimed again, its instruction clipped and efficient. "Prenez la sortie 181A à droite, 196th Street Southwest." (Take exit 181A on the right, 196th Street Southwest.)

"Et voilà," Chloé said, a note of triumph in her voice as she flicked on her right turn signal. "C'est notre sortie." (And there we go. That's our exit.)

The roar of the interstate fell away as they descended the off-ramp, replaced by the familiar stop-and-go of suburban traffic. They passed the Evergreen Commons Mall, its parking lots already beginning to fill with early holiday shoppers.

A few more turns through quiet residential streets lined with -trees that had lost most of their autumn leaves, and Chloé pulled into the parking lot of a clean, modest apartment complex. She navigated to a reserved spot near a covered walkway and brought the car to a gentle stop. She turned the key.

The engine cut out. The GPS screen went dark. The vibrations ceased.

In the sudden, profound silence, the only sound was Alex’s own shaky breath. He looked from the unassuming three-story apartment building to Chloé, whose face was turned toward him, etched with concern and a fierce, unwavering loyalty.

"On est arrivés, Alex," she said softly, her voice thick with emotion. She unbuckled her seatbelt. "Bienvenue chez nous." (We've arrived, Alex. Welcome to our home.)

*Chez nous.* Our home. Not *my* home, but *our* home. The words wrapped around him like the warmest, safest blanket he had ever known. He slowly lifted a hand to his AAC, his fingers fumbling slightly from exhaustion and emotion. He typed a single word.

"Maison," the device spoke, the simple Québécois word filling the car, full of relief, gratitude, and the promise of a new beginning. (Home.)

The drive from Kent to Lynnwood, though filled with the snarl of increasing pre-Thanksgiving traffic on I-405 North, felt like a journey from a shadowed abyss into fragile light for eighteen-year-old Alex Miller. Beside him, Chloé Mason, also eighteen, drove her reliable Horizon Avalon with a focused calm, her presence a steady, reassuring anchor. The car’s GPS unit, set to "Français," occasionally offered directions in formal Metropolitan French, a sound that, while different from Chloé’s familiar Québécois, was still blessedly *not* the American English Vera had used to chip away at Alex’s spirit. Their own conversation in the car had been sparse, conducted entirely in the soft, comforting cadence of Québécois French – the language Alex now clung to as a shield, a safe space Vera couldn't penetrate.

As they finally pulled into the parking lot of Chloé’s small, off-campus one-bedroom apartment complex in Lynnwood – a common choice for students like her who commuted via Transit Authority of the Puget Sound’s express bus routes from Snohomish County to Northwest Pacific University in Seattle – Alex let out a breath he felt like he’d been holding since Vera had first mentioned the disastrous trip. He was safe. He was with Chloé.
