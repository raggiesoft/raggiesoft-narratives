# Part 3: Confrontation and Choice (Wednesday, November 22, 2003)

The drive from Lynnwood to the quiet residential street in Kent felt like an eternity to Chloé Mason. Each mile her Horizon Avalon covered was filled with a gnawing anxiety for Alex Miller, her best friend since they were fourteen. His desperate, fragmented call from Vera's "Aunt Carol's" house – the plea for help in that urgent Québécois French, the fear in his synthesized voice before the line abruptly cut – had been a summons she couldn't ignore.

Chloé pulled up to the small, slightly run-down house, her heart pounding. She took a deep breath, her Québécois mother's more colorful fighting words perhaps echoing in her mind for courage, and marched up the walkway, ringing the doorbell with firm, repeated presses.

The door opened to reveal an older woman with kind, worried eyes. The woman's gaze flickered from Chloé's face to the coppery red hair, and a look of profound, dawning recognition washed over her. She had seen this face before, in a photograph, radiating a joy that was a world away from the fear she had seen in Alex's eyes. This was the girl from the picture. This was help.

"You must be Chloé," Aunt Carol said, her voice full of a quiet, urgent relief, pronouncing her name correctly—*klo-AY*—just as she had heard it from Alex's device. "Thank God you're here. He's in the guest room."

She opened the door wider, a silent, powerful invitation. Before Chloé could even step inside, she spoke, her voice clear and loud enough for Alex to hear, a beacon of safety sent into the house. "I'm here for Alex Miller. He called me. I'm here to take him home."

Just then, Vera appeared in the hallway, her face a mask of fury. "Who the hell are you?" she demanded.

But it was too late. Alex, hearing Chloé's beloved, familiar voice, summoned the very last of his reserves. He wheeled himself with a surge of desperate strength from the living room, the single act of self-propulsion taking everything he had left. It would be the last time he would move his own chair until Chloé could nurse him back to health in Lynnwood. His face, pale and gaunt, was illuminated with a desperate, incandescent hope.

CHLOÉ! MON AMIE! TU ES VENUE! his device cried out. (CHLOÉ! MY FRIEND! YOU CAME!)

Chloé’s eyes were locked on Alex, her heart breaking at the sight of him. She stepped inside, her focus entirely on him, her voice a soothing balm of their shared, safe language. "*Oui, Alex, mon trésor. Je suis là.*" (Yes, Alex, my treasure. I am here.)

Carol stood by the open door, her presence a quiet, unshakeable bulwark. She looked at her furious, manipulative niece, and then at the two best friends, and she knew, with absolute certainty, that she had made the right choice. She was ready to help Chloé get Alex out, no matter what Vera said or did.

"Leave?" Vera shrieked, her initial confusion overcome by possessive rage. "He's not going anywhere! He's my boyfriend! My husband!" She lunged for Alex's wheelchair handle. Chloé moved like lightning, stepping smoothly between Vera and Alex. "*Non, tu ne touches pas à lui!*" (No, you don't touch him!) she snapped instinctively in Québécois French, before reverting to American English for Vera and Aunt Carol's benefit. "Don't touch him, Vera.” To Alex, in soothing Québécois French again, she murmured, "*Je te protège, Alex. Reste calme.*" (I'm protecting you, Alex. Stay calm.).

"He's sick, he doesn't know what he's saying!" Vera insisted, glaring at Chloé. "Aunt Carol, stop her! She’s trying to kidnap my husband!” Carol looked from Vera’s frantic, lying face to Alex’s terrified one, then to Chloé’s calm but resolute stance. Her own face was now pale, but her eyes held a new, firm light. "Vera," she said, her voice surprisingly strong, "he is not your husband, and he is clearly not sick in the way you mean. He is frightened, and this young woman is his friend. He wishes to leave with her."

Chloé, sensing an unexpected ally, knelt beside Alex's chair, speaking to him in soft, reassuring Québécois French: "*Alex, mon ami, tu veux venir avec moi? Dis-le clairement pour elles, si tu peux, en anglais. Mais je sais déjà ta réponse.*" (Alex, my friend, do you want to come with me? Say it clearly for them, if you can, in English. But I already know your answer.). He nodded vigorously, fresh tears of relief streaming down his face. He switched his AAC back to American English, his synthesized voice firm despite his trembling: "YES! YES, CHLOÉ! I WANT TO GO HOME WITH YOU! NOW!"

"Then let's go," Chloé said, her voice soft for Alex. She looked up at Vera, her eyes like steel. "He’s made his choice. I'm taking Alex right NOW! Get his bag. And his medications, if you even bothered to pack them." The last part was pure, biting sarcasm. Vera just sputtered, her face a mask of fury and thwarted control. But before she could unleash another torrent, Carol spoke. "I'll get his bag, dear," she said to Chloé, her voice quiet but firm. She turned to Vera. "You will stay here, Vera. You will not make this any harder for him.” Ignoring Vera’s sputtering protests, Carol went to the guest room and returned a moment later with Alex's single, meager duffel bag. Her lips thinned as she noted its sparse contents. "He doesn't have much," she said to Chloé, handing over the bag, her eyes conveying a deep apology. The small amount of simple food – some crackers and a piece of fruit – she had managed to get into Alex while Vera was distracted earlier had clearly not been nearly enough, and he still looked desperately thin.

Vera tried to block the doorway. "I'm calling the police! You're kidnapping him! He needs me! He's my husband!” "Go ahead, Vera," Chloé said, her voice dangerously calm in American English. "Call them. Explain to them why an adult man, who is clearly distressed, visibly unwell, desperately hungry, and explicitly asking to leave your abusive presence, isn't allowed to. Explain the lack of his essential medications. Explain the pressure sores I can already see forming on his hip from here because you haven't been caring for him properly.” She paused. "Or perhaps you'd prefer I call them and explain it?" Vera paled visibly. Her bluster finally, completely, deflated. She knew she had no leg to stand on. Her control was broken. Carol opened the front door wider. "Go on, children," she urged gently.

With a final, contemptuous look at Vera, Chloé gently took the handles of Alex's wheelchair. "*On y va, Alex,*" she murmured in Québécois French. (Let's go, Alex.).

It was in this moment, the fresh air filling his lungs, Chloé’s steadfast presence a tangible shield beside him, that a sudden, sharp, and fiercely defiant thought sliced through the fog of his misery and gratitude. The summer. Their trip to Seattle and Lynnwood just a few months ago. The king-sized bed in their hotel room, the quiet conversation in their shared Québécois French, their tentative, trusting exploration of different kinds of kisses, including that first, curious tongue kiss. And then, that special evening, their equally gentle, consensual first experience with sex. He was so incredibly grateful for that summer, for Chloé’s unwavering friendship that had allowed for such safe, respectful first experiences. Because it now suddenly, with stark and empowering clarity, meant one undeniable thing: Vera Kowalski, despite everything she had done to him, everything she had taken, *wasn’t* his first. Not for his first real kiss with a tongue, and certainly not for his first sexual experience. For both, his first partner had been Chloé – the one person he trusted more than anyone in the world. A small, almost imperceptible smile touched Alex’s lips, a flicker of triumph amidst the pain. Vera had violated him, yes. She had hurt him, terrified him, stripped him of his dignity with her coercive actions. But she hadn’t taken *those* firsts. Those uniquely precious, consensual explorations were his and Chloé’s, rooted in years of platonic love and absolute trust. And there was nothing Vera could do about it, whether she liked it or not. That specific piece of his history, of his agency, remained untainted by her touch, a small, fiercely guarded sanctuary within him, a truth Vera could never rewrite.

Chloé pushed his chair out the door, Alex too weak and exhausted to propel himself now. As they moved towards her Horizon Avalon, Carol hurried after them, pressing a small wad of cash into Chloé’s hand. "Dear," she whispered quickly, "Lynnwood, that's quite a ways across the region, isn't it? Please, take this. It’s not much, but for gas for your car, and get him something warm to eat as soon as you can. He looks starved.” Her eyes were filled with remorse. "I am so sorry for what my niece has put him through, and for not seeing it sooner. Thank you for coming for him.” Chloé, touched by the genuine apology and the unexpected help, managed a grateful nod. "Thank you, ma’am. We'll be okay.” She helped Alex into the passenger seat, stowed his chair, and quickly got behind the wheel. The Horizon Avalon pulled away from the curb, leaving Vera screaming impotently on the porch and a shaken, guilt-ridden Aunt Carol watching them go.
