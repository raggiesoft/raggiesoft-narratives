# Part 19: Starship Sentinels

After the long, emotionally vital video call with Chloé, a comfortable, peaceful quiet settled over Megan’s apartment. Alex felt more centered than he had in months, the connection with his best friend a powerful balm.

Megan, sensing the need to keep the positive momentum going, grinned at him. "Alright, cuz," she said, gesturing to the small television in her living room. "I think it's time to save the galaxy. You up for a game?"

Hooked up to the TV was a small, cube-shaped Kyoto Entertainment Sphere, the latest gaming console. Megan pulled out two controllers. "I've got this new game, *Starship Sentinels*," she explained. "It's a two-player co-op. We're a team. No competition tonight, just us against a ridiculous alien empire."

The idea was perfect. Vera hated video games, dismissing them as childish and a waste of time. Playing one now felt like another small, wonderful act of rebellion.

They settled onto the sofa, and for the next hour, they were no longer just cousins in a dorm room; they were the co-pilots of the starship *Vindicator*, battling fleets of insect-like alien fighters. It was a simple, colorful, and gloriously fun game.

Alex, with his incredible focus and quick reflexes, was a natural pilot, expertly weaving their ship through asteroid fields and taking down enemy cruisers with precise laser fire. Megan was the gunner, her laughter filling the room as she unleashed volleys of plasma torpedoes. They worked as a seamless team, calling out warnings and celebrating small victories.

It was pure, uncomplicated fun. There was no pressure, no judgment, no one monitoring his performance or sighing with boredom. It was just the simple joy of sharing an adventure, of working together to overcome a silly, pixelated challenge.

When they finally defeated the final boss, a massive alien queen in a shower of explosive, triumphant pixels, they both cheered. Alex looked over at Megan, a wide, genuine, and completely carefree grin on his face. He hadn't just won a video game. He had won back a small, essential piece of his own happiness.
