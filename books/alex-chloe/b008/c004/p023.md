# Part 23: The Aftermath

The drive back to Lisa Court was quiet and thoughtful. The weight of Megan's offer, the lifeline she had extended on behalf of the entire family, settled over Alex. He wasn't just processing the plan; he was grappling with the terrifying reality that his life was something that required an "escape plan."

When they arrived, all four parents were waiting for them in the Millers' living room, their expressions a mixture of love and anxious concern. They had been briefed by Megan via a quick phone call.

James Miller was the first to speak, his voice full of a quiet, powerful gravity. "Alex," he began, "Megan told us about your talk. We want you to know, son, that the offer is real. It is a hundred percent serious."

"All of us," Sophie Mason added, her hand resting on Thomas's arm, her presence a solid, maternal anchor. "This is not just from Megan. This is from all of us. If you say the word, we are all ready to make it happen. You can live with her this winter. You can be safe."

The unified front of his four parents, their love a tangible, protective fortress around him, was almost overwhelming. He knew they were offering him a way out. He knew it was the right thing to do. But the thought of the confrontation, of the emotional battle it would take to break free from Vera, felt like a mountain he was too exhausted to climb. He simply nodded, a silent acknowledgment of their incredible love.

It was time for Megan to leave. She pulled him into one last, long, grounding hug at the front door. "Just think about it, cuz," she whispered into his hair. "The escape hatch is open. You just have to be brave enough to jump."

And then she was gone.

Alex watched her car disappear around the corner of Lisa Court. The moment it was out of sight, the profound sense of safety and comfort she had so carefully built around him for the past forty-eight hours evaporated like mist.

The house felt cavernous. The familiar, loving presence of his parents was a comfort, but it couldn't fill the specific, acute void that Megan's departure had left. He went to his room and sat in the quiet, the echo of her offer hanging in the air.

Later that night, as he lay in his bed, the emptiness was a physical presence. He was alone. For two nights, he had felt the simple, profound, and uncomplicated safety of a shared bed, of a warm, loving presence beside him. But tonight, there was just the cold, empty space. He didn't want to be alone. The loneliness crept back in, a cold, familiar ache, and with it, a deep, weary sense of dread for the Monday morning that was now just a few hours away.
