# Part 4: Welcome to Hell, Welcome to Kent

Aunt Carol was waiting at the Kent Train Station. She was a woman who looked a little overwhelmed but managed a genuinely delighted smile when she saw Vera. "Vera! My goodness, child! The last time I saw you, you were two years old!” She enveloped Vera in a hug. Then she turned to Alex, her smile becoming a little more hesitant as she took in his pale, drawn face, his wheelchair, and how thin he had become. "And this must be your... friend?” Internally, Aunt Carol noted the massive age difference; Vera was a young woman, but this young man looked almost like a boy beside her, so thin and frail. "I wasn't expecting... well, Vera, you certainly are full of surprises!” "This is Alex, Aunt Carol!" Vera trilled. "My wonderful Alex!"

Getting Alex and his wheelchair into Aunt Carol’s small Holt Astra sedan was a struggle; it barely fit, but she managed with some awkward shoving from Vera. The short drive to Aunt Carol's house was made in relative silence, Aunt Carol occasionally glancing at Alex in the rearview mirror with a worried frown. She set them up in the guest room.

Alex felt a wave of despair wash over him. He desperately needed his medication. He needed proper care for his sores. He needed food. He needed Chloé. The promise of seeing her was all that had kept him going. *"Anything in exchange to see Chloé again,"* he'd told himself. But now, trapped in Kent, he knew Vera had deliberately steered him away from Lynnwood, which he knew was so close. That brief, agonizing glimpse of the "LYNNWOOD TC" bus sign in Seattle, and Vera's subsequent panicked redirection, had confirmed his worst fears: Vera had no intention of letting him reach Chloé.

Aunt Carol, though clearly flustered by the unexpected arrivals and the obvious needs of her niece’s companion, tried to be gracious. She grumbled internally about two unexpected mouths to feed for Thanksgiving right before the holiday, especially one who looked so starved, but didn't outwardly complain.

The bus ride from Norfolk, Virginia, to Kent, Washington, had been grueling. Vera Kowalski arrived at her Aunt Carol’s small, neat house exhausted, irritable, and with a story already carefully constructed in her mind. Alex Miller, pale and clearly unwell after the cross-country journey without proper medication or care, was a silent, miserable presence beside her in his wheelchair.

Aunt Carol, a kind woman who hadn't seen her niece in several years, greeted them with a mixture of surprise and concern. She helped Vera get Alex settled into the small downstairs guest room, noting his frail appearance and the way he seemed to shrink from Vera’s touch.

Later, as Vera sat at Carol’s kitchen table, nursing a cup of tea, Carol tried to make polite conversation, her unease growing. "So, Vera," Aunt Carol began gently, "it's been a while. How did you and... Alex, is it?... how did you two meet?"

Vera sighed dramatically, launching into her prepared narrative. "Oh, Aunt Carol, it was at college, back in Virginia Beach. He was in one of my classes, and he just seemed so sweet, you know? Really intelligent, and so, so interested in getting to know me. He was always asking questions, wanting to spend time together." She painted a picture of a devoted, almost worshipful young man, conveniently omitting Alex's disabilities, his youth relative to hers, or the true nature of their interactions which were largely initiated and controlled by her.

"He's a nice boy," Vera continued, stirring her tea, "but he's got this... this friend. A girl, Chloé. She's always around." Vera's voice took on a slightly harder edge. "They have this weird way of talking to each other sometimes, this... gibberish language he speaks with her on the phone. I don’t understand a word of it. And honestly, Aunt Carol, she seems to be taking up so much of our time together. It’s like she’s always inserting herself, always needs his attention for something."

Aunt Carol listened, a small frown line appearing between her brows. Vera's description didn't quite align with the exhausted, almost haunted look she'd seen in Alex's eyes. Vera’s complaints about this "Chloé" and the "gibberish" also struck Carol as odd; if Alex had a close friend he communicated with in another language (Vera didn't seem to know, or care, that it was French), that was his business, not something to be resented. It was an unusual thing for Vera to focus on with such irritation, especially when Alex himself looked so unwell.

"And his parents?" Carol ventured, trying to steer the conversation. "Do they know you're out here with him?"

Vera waved a hand vaguely. "Oh, they're busy. They trust Alex to make his own decisions. And he chose to come with me, Aunt Carol. He really did."

Carol wasn't entirely convinced. There was something in Vera’s tone, a self-serving undercurrent, that didn't sit right. And the young man in the guest room looked less like a willing romantic partner on an adventure and more like someone who was sick and unhappy. For now, Carol held her peace, offering her niece another cup of tea, a growing sense of unease about this unexpected visit settling in her heart. After showing them the small guest room, she bid them goodnight, perhaps suggesting they rest after their long journey.

As soon as Aunt Carol left the room, Vera turned to Alex, the exhaustion of the trip evident in her pinched face, but her eyes held a familiar, demanding gleam. "Well, we're here, sweetie. Alone at last".

Even in his miserable, pain-racked, and starved state, Vera insisted on having sex with him. The old bed in the guest room creaked loudly under their combined weight, each sound a fresh wave of misery for Alex, a sickening soundtrack to his degradation that he feared might be audible throughout the small house.

The moment it was over, a profound, terrifying weakness washed over Alex. The sudden, intense physical activity, forced upon a body that was already in a state of catastrophic systemic failure, had pushed him to the absolute brink. His heart hammered against his ribs in a wild, erratic rhythm, struggling to keep up. A wave of dizziness made the room spin, and the edges of his vision began to go dark and fuzzy. He was gasping for breath, the simple act of pulling air into his starved lungs a monumental effort. He felt a cold, clammy sweat break out all over his body. He was, in that moment, closer to death than he had ever been. He was lucky to be alive.

It was just another layer of misery on top of the physical agony, the gnawing hunger, and the crushing disappointment that Chloé – who he knew was relatively nearby – was being deliberately kept from him by Vera's cruel charade.

Later, as Vera slept beside him, Alex lay awake, trembling, his heart still racing. He was scared. He was in a lot of pain. He hadn’t slept properly in days, and tonight would be no different. He was terrified of Vera, of what she might do next, of being trapped here in this unfamiliar house, with Lynnwood and Chloé feeling both tantalizingly close and impossibly distant. He clutched at the one small hope he had left. He promised himself that tomorrow, somehow, he would find a way to charge his phone or his AAC, and he would call Chloé. He had to. He wanted out of this relationship, out of this house, out of this nightmare. Lynnwood wasn't far; he knew it. He had to believe Chloé could get him out.
