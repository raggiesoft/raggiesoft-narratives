# Part 3: Across the Country on a Pathfinder

The old Pathfinder bus groaned out of the Norfolk, Virginia, terminal, the promise of seeing Chloé the only bright spot in Alex’s rapidly dimming world. Vera Kowalski, beside him, maintained a bright, almost manic cheerfulness, a stark contrast to Alex’s growing physical misery. The first missed dose of his Cerebral Palsy medication that Saturday morning was already making its presence known – a tightening in his limbs, a creeping ache that spread through his back and hips from the unrelenting pressure of the bus seat. Vera had packed no substantial meals for their anticipated four-day journey, only a meager collection of sugary snacks and greasy chips.

The journey became a blur of fleeting towns and impersonal bus stations. Hampton passed, and then came their first chaotic bus change in Richmond. The brief, jarring stop was a harsh dose of reality. The adventure already felt grimy and exhausting. As Vera left him parked by a dirty window to go get them sodas, a wave of profound regret, cold and sharp, washed over Alex.

He thought of the weekend with Megan, of the clean, quiet safety of her dorm room. He thought of her offer, the "Escape Hatch," waiting for him in December. This is a mistake, the thought screamed in his mind. *I should have just waited. I should have told her yes. I could have been safe with her in just a few weeks.* But it was too late. He had traded that certain, loving sanctuary for Vera’s impulsive, chaotic promise. Alex, struggling with increased spasticity, found the transfer to the next bus more difficult, his Quantum Communicator XT cumbersome. Vera, while outwardly solicitous if anyone was watching, was impatient with the delays his needs caused, her "helpful" hands often rough.

Through Charlottesville and Staunton, the pattern repeated. As the bus ground its way through the mountains towards Roanoke, the pain from his missed medications escalating, Alex’s mind, desperate for any thought other than his immediate suffering, replayed the first few weeks of college. He remembered Sarah Jenkins in the library, her kind, perceptive words validating his bond with Chloé. He remembered how Vera had so skillfully twisted that kindness into something suspicious, causing him to pull away. In that moment, with the full weight of his disastrous decision crashing down on him, he realized: *She saw it. Sarah saw it from the very first day. She tried to warn me, and I didn't listen.* The thought was a fresh wave of agony. He wanted nothing more than to somehow call Sarah, to tell her she was right, to ask for her help. But it was too late. He was trapped on a bus, heading west with his abuser, his hope dwindling.

By the time they reached Bristol, Tennessee, Alex was in significant pain. Another bus change in Knoxville, then the long stretch to Nashville. As the bus pulled into the terminal under the harsh fluorescent lights, a wave of profound regret washed over Alex. The city’s vibrant energy felt like a cruel joke. He looked at the bus schedule, saw the route stretching endlessly west, and felt a desperate, impossible longing to turn around. *This is a mistake,* the thought screamed in his mind. *I should go home. I need to go home.* He was terrified, but the fear of Vera's reaction, and the deeper fear of being truly alone, kept him silent.

The second day bled into the third. Through St. Louis, Missouri, and Kansas City. Alex’s world narrowed to pain, exhaustion, and the flickering image of Chloé’s face in his mind. The brief halt in Colby, Kansas, at a grimy truck stop in the middle of the vast, empty plains, was the breaking point, but this time for Vera. As she stood outside in the biting wind, watching Alex struggle, a flicker of her own regret surfaced. *This was a stupid idea,* she thought, a flash of genuine frustration breaking through her manipulative facade. *This is too hard.* The thought of turning back was tempting, but her pride and her determination to see her "surprise" through for her aunt pushed it down. She shoved the feeling aside and hurried Alex back onto the bus.

The fourth day dawned as they crossed into Colorado. Denver meant another bus change, Alex now visibly trembling, his skin clammy, his face gaunt from malnutrition. The high altitude seemed to exacerbate his breathing difficulties. Vera, her own patience frayed, was less careful in her handling of him, her frustration evident.

By the time they reached Salt Lake City, Utah, Alex was on death's doorstep. The cumulative effect of the neglect had pushed his body into a state of critical medical distress. At a brief rest stop, a kind-faced woman, seeing Alex’s gaunt, pale face and the way he was trembling, approached Vera. "Excuse me, ma'am," the woman said, her voice full of genuine concern. "Is your friend alright? He looks very unwell."

Vera immediately positioned herself between the woman and Alex, her smile a tight, aggressive mask. "He's just tired from the long trip," she snapped, her voice sharp and dismissive. "HE'S FINE. Thank you for your concern." The other passengers who overheard the exchange just shook their heads at Vera's rudeness but, seeing her hostile defensiveness, backed off, leaving Alex trapped in his silent medical crisis. He was lucky to be alive when he left that station.

By the time they reached a rest stop in Boise, Idaho, Alex was nearly delirious with pain, exhaustion, and acute hunger. The thought of Chloé was a distant, wavering light. Through Pasco, Yakima, and Ellensburg in Washington, Vera’s facade of cheerfulness had entirely crumbled. She’d snap if he took too long to respond or if his movements were slow. He barely used his AAC anymore, the effort too great, his hope too battered. The single, fragile promise of seeing Chloé was the only thing keeping him going now, the one thread he clung to in the darkness.

Finally, on Tuesday evening, four excruciating days after leaving Norfolk, their bus lumbered into the main Pathfinder terminal in Seattle, right at the height of evening rush hour. The city was a chaotic symphony of horns, hurried footsteps, and glaring headlights. Alex was a wreck – his body screaming from unrelieved pressure and lack of medication, his spirit crushed by Vera's neglect, and he was starving.

Vera, however, seemed to perk up, a strange, almost triumphant glint in her eye as she navigated them off the bus and through the bustling terminal. Alex, disoriented and weak, just wanted this nightmare to end. He longed for Chloé, for safety, for a proper meal.

As they emerged from the terminal building onto the busy city street, the cacophony of evening rush hour hit them full force. Horns blared, commuters hurried along the sidewalks, and a lineup of Transit Authority of the Puget Sound buses idled at designated stops along the curb. Alex’s eyes, despite his exhaustion, scanned the destination signs on the waiting coaches. Amidst the various routes, one sign blazed like a beacon of desperate hope: LYNNWOOD TC. Lynnwood. Chloé’s city. It was right there, a tangible link to the only person he trusted, the only person he wanted to see. He desperately, with every fiber of his being, wanted to get on that bus. He imagined telling the driver, "Lynnwood, please," and being whisked away to safety, to Chloé.

But reality, in the form of Vera, quickly crushed that fragile hope. Vera had his bus fare; he had nothing. And his Quantum Communicator XT, his only means of independent communication, had run out of battery power days ago. His simple cell phone, which he carried for emergencies, had suffered the same fate even earlier in the journey. Both were now useless bricks, casualties of Vera’s complete disregard for his needs during their grueling cross-country ordeal. How could he possibly call Chloé, even if he found a payphone and somehow managed to use it? How could he let her know he was here, in Seattle, so close, yet so impossibly far, about to be dragged onto a bus heading in the wrong direction?

As Vera was scanning the other route signs along the street, her gaze also fell upon the bus signed LYNNWOOD TC. For a split second, the name just registered as another suburb. Then, it hit her with a small, unwelcome jolt. *Lynnwood.* Why did that sound familiar? A vague memory surfaced from that disastrous video call Alex had with his family – the mousy little friend his parents had defended, the one he clearly preferred. *Chloé, that was her name!* The one he spoke that irritating gibberish to, the same gibberish his family seemed to understand and even encourage. *Crap,* Vera thought, a flicker of genuine annoyance and perhaps a tiny seed of unease planting itself. *That IS nearby, isn't it? Aunt Carol said Kent was just south of Seattle, and Lynnwood… isn’t that north?*

A quick, sharp glance at Alex told her he hadn't missed the Lynnwood bus sign either. She could see the desperate longing in his eyes, the almost imperceptible shift in his posture as if he might try to propel his wheelchair towards it. Panic, swift and decisive, spurred Vera into action. The last thing she needed was Alex getting any ideas about contacting that Chloé girl. Her "surprise" for Aunt Carol depended on Alex being compliant and, more importantly, *with her*.

"Aunt Carol said something about Kent... Ah, there it is!" Vera exclaimed with forced brightness, deliberately turning Alex's wheelchair away from the Lynnwood bus and pointing towards a coach further down the block signed \#150 KENT/SOUTHCENTER. "Come on, Alex, *this* is us. Just one more little ride, sweetie, and then our big surprise!" She pushed his chair forward quickly, hoping he hadn't fully registered the Lynnwood sign or, if he had, that his current state of exhaustion and dependence would prevent him from making a scene.

Alex felt a wave of despair wash over him, even deeper now that he suspected Vera had also seen the Lynnwood sign and was actively preventing him from even considering it. He was so close. Lynnwood was *right there*. But he was powerless. With a heavy heart and a sense of crushing reluctance, he allowed Vera to maneuver his wheelchair along the sidewalk towards the Kent-bound bus, each push a physical manifestation of her control, taking him further away from his only hope.

As Vera guided his wheelchair onto the lift and into the crowded, stuffy interior of the KENT/SOUTHCENTER bus, a silent cry of terror ripped through Alex. *No! Not Kent! Lynnwood! I need to go to Lynnwood! I need Chloé!* The words screamed in his mind, a desperate, unheard plea. He so desperately wanted to be on that other bus, the one signed for Lynnwood, the one that could take him to Chloé, to safety, to sanity. He was crying on the inside, an agony of longing and fear. But the cry remained trapped, his face a mask of exhausted resignation that Vera, in her self-absorbed excitement and now a new, subtle layer of guarded urgency, did not pick up on. The doors of the bus hissed shut, sealing his fate for now, and with a lurch, the bus pulled away from the curb, carrying him further into Vera's nightmarish "adventure." The distant dream of seeing Chloé flickered and dimmed, almost extinguished by the grim reality of Vera’s control.
