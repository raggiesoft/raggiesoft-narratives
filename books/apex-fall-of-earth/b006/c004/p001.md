# Part 1: The Rule of Three – Tuesday, March 10, 2048 – 07:30

The Aegis deflector shield could effortlessly absorb the kinetic impact of a hyper-dense anti-matter warhead. It could silence the deafening roar of a corporate orbital bombardment, rendering the apocalypse entirely mute.

It was, however, completely useless against the synchronized, ear-splitting shrieks of three two-year-olds.

Down in the residential kitchen, the morning sunlight was bright and peaceful. The environment inside the room was anything but.

Wyatt Colton, former S.H.O.P. Marine and architect of the most heavily fortified bunker in the known galaxy, was currently being held completely hostage in his own home. He was standing in front of the massive kitchen island, a bowl of warm oatmeal in his heavy left hand, a spoon in his right, staring at his children in sheer, exhausted terror.

"Okay," Wyatt said slowly, raising his hands in a gesture of absolute surrender. "Okay. I understand. The green bowl was a mistake."

Sitting across from him in the custom-built, sprawling wooden bench he had milled to replace standard highchairs, the Vanguard was united in their absolute outrage.

The Terrible Twos had arrived on Planet Eden, and because the triplets operated on a level of profound, unspoken neurological synchronization, their developmental milestones didn't just overlap—they compounded. They didn't have individual tantrums. They executed coordinated, tactical meltdowns based on a set of incredibly rigid, entirely unspoken rules that Wyatt and Sarah had to decipher daily through trial and error.

Rule Number One: Absolute symmetry.

Wyatt had placed three bowls of oatmeal on the long, shared wooden tray. He had given Ellie a blue bowl, Leo a blue bowl, and Nora a green bowl, simply because the third blue bowl was in the dishwasher.

It was a catastrophic logistical failure.

In the center of the bench, Leo’s body had instantly gone rigid. Because of his severe spastic diplegia, the two-year-old couldn't sit up independently. He relied entirely on the physical pressure of his sisters pressing against his sides to remain upright. The moment the green bowl was set down, the break in the visual pattern terrified him, sending his developing nervous system into a panicked, silent lockup.

Sensing her brother's immediate distress, Nora had burst into tears, her empathetic heart shattering. She shoved the offending green bowl away with her tiny, uncoordinated hands, burying her face into Leo’s shoulder to physically anchor him.

And Ellie, the vanguard, went straight on the offensive.

Seeing her brother rigid and her sister crying, Ellie’s dark eyes locked onto Wyatt. She didn't cry; she screamed with furious, territorial indignation. She grabbed her spoon, reared her tiny arm back, and launched it directly at Wyatt’s chest. It bounced harmlessly off his massive pectoral muscle, leaving a dollop of oatmeal on his gray t-shirt.

"Message received," Wyatt sighed, setting his own bowl down.

Sarah rolled her brushed-aluminum wheelchair into the kitchen, a steaming mug of coffee in her hand. She took one look at the oatmeal on her husband's shirt, the sobbing Nora, the rigid Leo, and the absolute, red-faced fury of Ellie.

"Let me guess," Sarah smiled, an exhausted, knowing amusement in her dark eyes. "You broke a rule."

"I gave Nora the green bowl," Wyatt admitted, his broad shoulders slumping as he walked over to the dishwasher to frantically retrieve the dirty blue bowl. "Aria, run a rapid sanitization cycle. Heat only. Three minutes."

"Sanitization cycle initiated, Administrator," the AI chimed helpfully over the sound of the wailing toddlers.

"You know they can't handle asymmetrical inventory, Wyatt," Sarah laughed softly, wheeling herself over to the massive bench.

She didn't try to separate them. If she pulled Nora away to calm her down, Leo would lose his physical support and fall over, and Ellie would likely try to bite someone. Instead, Sarah simply reached across the tray, laying her warm hands over Nora and Ellie's backs, creating a secondary layer of physical pressure that encompassed all three of them.

"It's okay, babies. Daddy's fixing it," Sarah soothed.

As if a switch had been flipped, the addition of their mother's physical touch immediately began to de-escalate the crisis. Nora's sobs turned into wet hiccups. Feeling Nora calm down, Leo's rigid muscles slowly began to unlock, his tight, panicked breathing evening out.

Seeing that her brother and sister were safe, Ellie stopped screaming. She lowered her tiny, oatmeal-covered fists, though she kept a highly suspicious, narrowed glare fixed on Wyatt as he pulled the steaming blue bowl out of the sanitizer.

Wyatt scooped the oatmeal from the green bowl into the blue one, walked back over, and set it down perfectly in front of Nora.

Three blue bowls. Perfect, unbroken symmetry.

Nora sniffled, picking up her spoon. She took a bite, then immediately reached over and rested her left hand firmly against Leo's arm. Only when she was anchored did Ellie pick up her own spoon, her right hand finding Leo's other arm.

Trapped safely in the middle, Leo let out a soft, vibrating grunt of approval. He couldn't hold the spoon himself, but as Wyatt sat down on the stool across from them and began carefully feeding him, the boy was completely content.

The crisis was averted.

Wyatt let out a massive, ragged sigh, leaning his elbows on the kitchen island. He looked at his beautiful, terrifying, incredibly rigid two-year-olds.

"They are operating a tighter protocol than a S.H.O.P. Marine barracks," Wyatt muttered, wiping a splash of oatmeal off his cheek. "If I deviate from the routine by a single millimeter, the entire command structure collapses."

"They're a unit, Wyatt," Sarah smiled, taking a sip of her coffee. "They don't understand that they are three separate people yet. To them, the green bowl wasn't just the wrong color. It meant a piece of them was missing. You just have to learn the blueprint."

Wyatt looked at the three of them, eating their breakfast in perfect, unbroken physical contact. He loved them with a ferocity that defied physics, but keeping up with the Vanguard's specific, silent expectations was going to require more tactical planning than securing the entire planet.
