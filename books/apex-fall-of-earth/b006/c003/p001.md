# Part 1: The Flaw in the Wood – Tuesday, January 30, 2046 – 14:00

Sub-Level 1 was finally empty.

Exactly eight weeks after the harrowing, chaotic delivery, the triplets officially hit their corrected gestational due date. Their lungs were fully developed, their thermal regulation was completely stabilized, and the automated neonatal incubators were permanently powered down.

Wyatt Colton stepped off the pneumatic elevator onto the residential level, his broad chest swelling with a profound, victorious pride that dwarfed any military campaign he had ever survived.

Strapped securely to his massive chest was a specialized, molecularly printed ergonomic carrier holding Ellie and Nora, their tiny heads resting safely against his collarbones. In his heavy, calloused arms, he carried Leo, wrapped snugly in a thermal blanket.

Beside him, Sarah rolled smoothly out of the elevator in her brushed-aluminum wheelchair. Her lap was piled high with the remaining medical supplies and the specialized bottles of fortified milk. She looked up at Wyatt, her dark eyes shining with happy, exhausted tears.

"We made it," Sarah whispered, looking down the warm, mahogany-paneled corridor. "We are actually taking them home."

"They're officially cleared for the surface, Mrs. Colton," Wyatt grinned, his deep voice a soft, vibrating rumble that instantly soothed the three sleeping infants against his chest.

They moved down the hall, the heavy timber doors of the nursery parting silently on proximity sensors.

The room was flooded with brilliant, natural afternoon sunlight from the massive hydro-glass windows. The soft sage-green walls and the pristine, polished stone floors offered a beautiful, welcoming contrast to the sterile, flashing LEDs of the subterranean medical bay.

And waiting perfectly along the northern wall were the three heavy-timber cribs.

Wyatt walked over to the beds he had spent hundreds of hours meticulously hand-milling. He looked at the flawless, countersunk hardware, the perfectly sealed alien pine, and the exact, uncompromising sixty-millimeter spacing of the vertical slats. They were the ultimate expression of his protective engineering—three distinct, flawless sanctuaries.

"Alright, squad," Wyatt murmured. "Time to deploy to your bunks."

Sarah wheeled her chair up to the middle crib, watching with absolute adoration as Wyatt began the transfer.

He leaned over the first crib on the left. Moving with practiced, delicate precision, he unclipped Ellie from the chest carrier and lowered her onto the firm, breathable memory-foam mattress. He tucked a light blanket around her waist.

He moved to the middle crib, repeating the exact same flawless transfer for Nora.

Finally, he stepped to the third crib on the right, gently laying Leo down in the center of the mattress, ensuring the boy’s underdeveloped muscles were perfectly supported.

Wyatt took a step back, slipping his hands into his canvas pockets. He stood shoulder-to-shoulder with Sarah’s wheelchair, looking at the three beautiful, separate cribs holding his three perfectly healthy children.

It was the exact picture he had held in his mind since the day they ratified the planetary constitution.

It lasted exactly four seconds.

In the first crib, Ellie’s eyes snapped open. The absence of Wyatt's physical body heat and the sudden, vast expanse of the mattress around her seemed to trigger an immediate, furious biological alarm. She didn't just fuss; she drew in a massive breath and let out a sustained, piercing shriek of absolute, unfiltered rage, her tiny fists violently punching the air.

Before Wyatt could even flinch, the middle crib erupted. Nora’s eyes squeezed tightly shut as she let out a high-pitched, desperate wail. She violently rolled her head from side to side, her tiny legs kicking frantically against the memory-foam as if she were trying to escape a physical threat.

And in the third crib, the reaction was the most terrifying of all.

Leo didn't cry. The second his back touched the center of his massive, isolated mattress, his entire body went completely rigid. His eyes flew open, wide and completely unseeing, his pupils dilated in pure, paralyzing terror. His tiny chest hitched, seizing up so tightly he couldn't even draw breath to scream.

"Whoa, hey!" Wyatt barked, his S.H.O.P. Marine instincts violently short-circuiting as the peaceful nursery instantly transformed into a chaotic disaster zone.

He lunged forward, his heavy hands instantly dropping onto the edge of the cribs.

"What's wrong? What happened?" Sarah gasped, gripping the armrests of her wheelchair as she leaned forward, her maternal panic spiking. "Are they cold?"

"The ambient temperature is twenty-one point six degrees Celsius, it's perfect," Wyatt said rapidly, his eyes darting between the three screaming, panicking infants. His mechanic's brain immediately began running frantic, logical diagnostics on the environment.

He reached into Ellie's crib, pressing his hand against the mattress. "The memory-foam is firm. There are no pinch points. The humidity is completely stabilized."

He moved to Leo's crib, quickly scooping his rigid, breathless son up and pulling him tightly against his chest. The second Wyatt's solid, physical weight enveloped him, Leo finally gasped, pulling in a ragged, shuddering breath as his tense muscles slowly began to unlock.

"It's the acoustic volume of the room," Wyatt concluded, his dark eyes scanning the high, vaulted ceiling of the nursery while Ellie and Nora continued to wail in their respective beds. "Down in the medical bay, the incubators had a low, constant harmonic vibration from the dual-compressors, and the plastic walls baffled the sound. Up here, it's too quiet. The room is too big. It's triggering their startle reflexes."

"Then we need white noise," Sarah said desperately, her heart aching as she listened to Nora's reedy, distressed cries. She wheeled herself closer to the middle crib, reaching through the sixty-millimeter wooden slats to gently stroke Nora's cheek, trying to soothe her. "Aria! Play ambient rainfall through the localized acoustic array. Heavy volume."

"Playing ambient rainfall, Sarah," Aria complied instantly.

The room filled with the deep, rich sound of a summer thunderstorm, perfectly mimicking the acoustic density of the incubator motors.

Wyatt gently lowered Leo back down into his crib, hoping the acoustic masking would settle his son.

The moment Wyatt's hands left him, Leo went completely rigid again, his eyes locking in silent, absolute terror. In the neighboring cribs, the heavy rainfall didn't even cause Ellie or Nora to pause. Ellie screamed louder, her tiny face turning dark red, while Nora thrashed against the mattress.

"It's not the sound," Wyatt grunted, his frustration and protective anxiety boiling over. He stared at the three beautiful, meticulously crafted pieces of furniture. He had measured everything to the millimeter. He had followed every single 2045-era pediatric safety guideline.

The blueprints were perfect, but his children were falling apart.

"Pick them up, Wyatt," Sarah pleaded, unable to bear the sound of their distress for another second. "Just pick them up."

Wyatt didn't hesitate. He grabbed Leo, tucking him securely against his left shoulder, then reached into the middle crib and scooped Nora up against his right. He stepped over to Ellie's crib, leaning down so Sarah could reach in from her wheelchair to pull her screaming, furious daughter against her own chest.

Within thirty seconds of being held, the wailing finally subsided into exhausted, ragged hiccups.

Wyatt stood in the center of the sunlit room, heavily burdened by the sudden, glaring failure of his engineering. He looked at the three empty, perfectly constructed timber cribs.

"We're missing something, Sarah," Wyatt murmured, his jaw tight as he gently bounced Nora and Leo against his shoulders. "I built these exactly to spec. They should be completely comfortable in there."

"They're just overstimulated," Sarah rationalized gently, resting her cheek against Ellie's dark hair. "It's their first day out of the basement, Wyatt. The transition is just too big right now. We'll try again tonight."

Wyatt nodded slowly, staring at the sixty-millimeter slats. He was determined to figure out the mechanical flaw in his design, entirely blind to the fact that the only flaw was the sixty centimeters of empty air separating the three mattresses.
