# Part 8: The Weight of the World – Monday, November 27, 2045 – 03:15

The automated medical wing on Sub-Level 1 was no longer just a clinic; it had officially become their entire world.

At thirty weeks, the physical reality of carrying three rapidly growing human beings had completely overtaken Sarah’s body. Her center of gravity was so fundamentally altered, and the strain on her lower spine and pelvis was so severe, that Wyatt had formally confined her to the primary diagnostic bio-bed. She wasn't allowed to navigate the estate, even in the wheelchair.

She was on strict, uncompromising bedrest, and where Sarah went, Wyatt stayed.

He had hauled a heavy, molecularly printed mattress down from the residential wing and dropped it directly onto the polished anti-microbial floor plates right next to her bio-bed. He hadn't slept in the master suite in two weeks.

In the dim, localized amber lighting of the quiet medical bay, Wyatt was instantly pulled from a light sleep by the sound of a soft, frustrated sigh.

He sat up immediately, kicking off his blanket.

Sarah was awake. She was lying on her side, her massive, incredibly firm abdomen supported by a wedge of memory-foam pillows. Her face was pale with exhaustion, her dark eyes lined with the deep fatigue of a body working in absolute overdrive.

"I'm sorry," Sarah whispered, her voice raspy as she looked down at him. "I tried to wait, but Leo is using my bladder as a trampoline again."

"Don't ever apologize for waking me," Wyatt rumbled, his voice thick with sleep but completely devoid of hesitation. He was on his feet in a fraction of a second, wearing nothing but a pair of loose athletic shorts.

He stepped over to the sleek, brushed-aluminum wheelchair parked at the foot of the bed and rolled it seamlessly to her side, engaging the locking brakes with a sharp click.

Wyatt reached over, hitting the control panel on the bio-bed. The hydraulic actuators whirred silently, elevating the head of the mattress to a forty-five-degree angle to help her sit up.

"Alright, sweetheart. Let's take it slow," Wyatt murmured, leaning over her.

He didn't let her strain her core muscles at all. Wyatt slid one massive, calloused arm firmly behind her shoulders and the other under the bend of her knees. With a smooth, deeply practiced motion, he pivoted her body, letting her legs drop gently over the edge of the mattress.

Sarah let out a breathless gasp, her hands gripping his broad shoulders as the sheer, crushing weight of the triplets shifted downward.

"I've got you. I've got all of it," Wyatt promised, standing flush against her so she wouldn't feel like she was falling.

He waited a few seconds for her blood pressure to stabilize, then hooked his arms securely under her arms, clasping his hands behind her back. With absolute, effortless strength, he lifted her from the bio-bed and lowered her flawlessly into the memory-foam seat of the wheelchair.

He adjusted the footrests, gently placing her bare feet onto the textured plates, and unlocked the brakes.

Wyatt pushed the chair out of the main exam room and down the short, brightly lit corridor to the medical wing's automated washroom. He parked the chair perfectly parallel to the toilet, locking the brakes once more.

"Up we go," Wyatt said softly.

He executed the second transfer with the exact same mechanical precision, lifting her from the chair and supporting her entirely until she was safely seated. He didn't hover awkwardly; he simply turned around, giving her privacy while keeping his broad back to her, standing perfectly still like a sentinel just a few centimeters away in case she lost her balance.

"I feel like a stranded whale," Sarah mumbled behind him, a profound, tired vulnerability cracking her usually fierce pioneer spirit. "I can't even stand up on my own, Wyatt."

"You are building three human lives from raw elements," Wyatt countered immediately, his deep voice echoing slightly against the sterile tiles. He didn't tolerate a single ounce of self-deprecation from her. "You are doing the hardest, most vital work in the history of this planet. My only job is to be the scaffolding. You use my strength whenever you need it. That's what I'm here for."

A few moments later, she tapped his lower back.

Wyatt turned around, effortlessly lifting her back into the aluminum wheelchair. He grabbed a warm, damp cloth from the automated sanitizer and gently wiped her hands, then rolled her back out to the main medical bay.

He transferred her back into the bio-bed, carefully arranging the massive wedge pillows to support her spine and her stomach. He pulled the heavy thermal duvet up to her shoulders, tucking her in securely.

Wyatt stepped over to the hydration station, filling a glass with ice-cold, perfectly filtered water, and held it to her lips so she could drink without having to lift her head.

"Better?" Wyatt asked softly, setting the glass down.

"Much better," Sarah sighed, her eyes already fluttering shut as the crushing exhaustion pulled her back under. She reached her hand out from under the duvet, her fingers finding his. "Thank you, Wyatt."

"Always," Wyatt whispered.

He didn't go back to his mattress on the floor. Wyatt pulled up the rolling metal stool and sat directly beside the bio-bed. He held her hand in his, his thumb gently stroking her knuckles.

He looked at the digital monitors hovering above the bed. The three tiny, rapid heartbeats were perfectly synchronized, pulsing steadily in the quiet room. He looked at Sarah’s exhausted, beautiful face.

The calm before the storm was heavy, demanding, and grueling. But as Wyatt sat guard in the dim amber light, listening to the hum of the Aegis grid keeping the entire planet locked down around them, he knew he was exactly where he was supposed to be.

They were ready.
