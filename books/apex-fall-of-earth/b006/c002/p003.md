# Part 3: The Hypoxic Clock – Tuesday, December 5, 2045 – 02:52

The sharp, furious cries of his firstborn daughter echoed safely from the heated bassinet behind him, but Wyatt Colton didn't have a single second to celebrate.

He was standing at the foot of the bio-bed, fresh sterile gloves snapped over his wrists, his dark eyes locked onto Sarah. Her chest was heaving beneath the oxygen mask Aria had deployed, her body violently trembling as the sheer adrenaline of the first delivery began to crash.

"One down, Sarah," Wyatt coached, his voice projecting a calm, unbreakable authority that completely masked the racing of his own heart. "You're doing incredible. Just breathe."

But before Sarah could even catch her breath, the biological dynamics of the bio-bed drastically shifted.

With the sudden, massive expulsion of the first baby and her amniotic fluid, the incredibly over-extended uterine wall instantly clamped down to compensate for the empty space. It didn't contract in a rhythmic, productive wave; it seized like a vice.

Sarah let out a sudden, high-pitched scream, her back arching rigidly against the memory-foam supports. Her hands flew to her stomach, her fingers digging agonizingly into her own skin.

"Wyatt, it's tearing!" Sarah shrieked, her eyes rolling back in pure, unadulterated agony. "It's wrong! Something is wrong!"

Above them, the clinical white lighting of the medical bay instantly snapped to a flashing, localized crimson.

"Warning," Aria’s synthesized voice blared, stripping away all bedside manner. The secondary holographic monitor violently flashed yellow, then red. "Subject B is experiencing acute umbilical compression due to rapid uterine deceleration. Fetal heart rate is plummeting. Ninety beats per minute. Seventy-five beats per minute."

Wyatt’s blood turned to absolute ice.

The umbilical cord—the only lifeline supplying oxygen to the baby's brain—was being crushed flat against the pelvic bone by the shifting pressure. Every second that passed was a second the developing neurological tissue was being starved of oxygen.

"Aria, execute Macro Alpha!" Wyatt roared, his S.H.O.P. Marine training entirely taking over the room.

"Macro Alpha engaged," Aria confirmed. "Maternal ambient oxygen flow increased by forty percent. Soft-tissue extraction armatures deployed to standby."

Wyatt heard the hiss of the concentrated oxygen flooding Sarah’s mask, but he knew the ambient air didn't matter if the cord was pinched shut. He didn't have minutes to wait for a natural descent. He had seconds before permanent, catastrophic neurological damage occurred.

He stepped directly into the center of the bio-bed platform.

"Sarah, look at me!" Wyatt commanded, his voice cracking like a whip over her panicked screams. Her dark, terrified eyes locked onto his. "Her cord is compressed. She is losing oxygen. I need her out right now!"

Sarah didn't hesitate. The maternal instinct overrode the blinding pain. She gripped the lateral handles of the bio-bed, took a massive, desperate pull of the pure oxygen from her mask, and bore down with a violent, terrifying force that threatened to completely shatter her own pelvis.

Wyatt guided the descent, his massive hands moving with desperate, calculated precision. The seconds dragged like hours. The monitor above them screamed a continuous, high-pitched warning as the fetal heart rate dipped dangerously into the sixties.

"Come on, come on," Wyatt gritted through his teeth, the muscles in his forearms bulging against the strain.

With one final, agonizing push from Sarah, the second baby breached the perimeter.

Wyatt caught her instantly, but the triumph in his chest completely evaporated.

Unlike her older sister, the second baby was absolutely silent. Her tiny muscles were completely flaccid, lacking the vigorous tension of a healthy newborn. Her skin wasn't a healthy, flushed pink; it was a terrifying, dusky blue.

"Subject B is hypoxic. Independent respiration is absent," Aria reported, the digital voice perfectly calm in the center of the nightmare.

"No you don't," Wyatt growled, his hands moving with blinding, lethal speed. "You are a Colton. You do not quit."

He didn't wait for the automated armatures. He slapped the sterile clamps onto the crushed umbilical cord, sliced through the center, and sprinted the two meters to Bassinet 2.

He laid her limp, blue body onto the heated scale.

"Macro Beta, override!" Wyatt barked. "Initiate positive-pressure ventilation immediately!"

"Executing emergency ventilation," Aria complied.

A specialized, molecularly printed pediatric mask dropped from the ceiling array, sealing perfectly over the infant's tiny nose and mouth. The machine delivered a series of rapid, perfectly calibrated bursts of pure oxygen directly into her underdeveloped lungs, forcing them to inflate.

Wyatt stood over the bassinet, his large hands hovering helplessly over her tiny chest, his own lungs refusing to take a breath. He watched the biometric scanner.

Five seconds. Ten seconds.

Suddenly, her tiny chest hitched independently.

A tiny, weak, raspy cough slipped through the edges of the oxygen mask. The dusky blue color of her skin rapidly began to recede, flushed out by a sudden, aggressive wave of oxygenated blood. Her tiny fists, previously hanging limp, suddenly curled inward, and she let out a thin, vibrating cry.

"Subject B oxygen saturation is climbing. Eighty-five percent. Ninety-two percent," Aria reported, the flashing crimson lights of the bay finally returning to a steady, clinical white. "Heart rate stabilizing at one hundred and thirty. Independent respiration established."

Wyatt’s knees physically buckled for a fraction of a centimeter. He gripped the edge of the bassinet, hanging his head as a ragged, wet gasp of absolute relief tore its way out of his throat.

The delay in oxygen had been incredibly brief, stopped by his rapid extraction and Aria's immediate intervention. Wyatt had no way of knowing that those terrifying seconds of hypoxia had already permanently etched a mild neurological deficit into her motor cortex—a slight limp she would carry for the rest of her life.

All he knew was that the blue was gone, and she was crying. Nora was alive.

He looked at her tiny, breathing chest, then turned his dark, fierce eyes back to his exhausted wife on the bio-bed.

Two down. One to go.
