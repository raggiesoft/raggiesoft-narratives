# Part 5: The Sentinel Treaty – Monday, April 8, 2052 – 14:00 (Standard Galactic Time)

The sprawling, sunlit living room of the mountain estate was a picture of absolute, unshakeable tranquility.

Outside the camouflaged hydro-glass windows, the alien pine forests of Planet Eden were in full, vibrant spring bloom. The muddy scars of the valley floods were long gone, replaced by a thick carpet of emerald ferns and brilliant wildflowers.

Inside, Sarah Colton was resting comfortably on the plush leather sectional. At thirty-four weeks pregnant with identical twins, her belly was a massive, perfectly round sphere. Unlike the terrifying, physically devastating ordeal of carrying the triplets, this pregnancy had been a flawless, textbook experience. Her color was radiant, her energy levels were steady, and her body was handling the biological payload with incredible resilience.

Wyatt Colton walked into the living room, wiping a streak of machine grease from his forearm with a canvas rag. He had spent the entire morning down on Sub-Level 3, completely immersed in his favorite pastime: over-engineering their survival.

"The new capacitor banks are online," Wyatt announced, a deeply satisfied, mechanic's grin spreading across his face as he dropped onto the sofa beside her. He leaned over, pressing a warm kiss to her cheek before resting his heavy hand on her stomach. "Aria and I just finished layering a secondary frequency-modulation matrix into the dampening field."

Sarah smiled, leaning her head against his shoulder. "Upgrading the locks again?"

"Just staying ahead of the curve," Wyatt rumbled softly. "The Earth Remnant's technology is going to advance now that they aren't hiding from Apex. I want to make absolutely sure that no matter what kind of deep-space scanners the galaxy invents in the next fifty years, Eden always registers as a dead, frozen rock."

It had been three months since the Earth Remnant Tribunal officially dissolved the Apex Megacorporation and sentenced High Director Kael to the dark. True to Commander Vance's word, the surviving global governments had expressed their profound, enduring gratitude to the Coltons by granting them the one thing they desired most: absolute isolation.

The Global Defense Coalition had formally designated the Helios System as a Level-1 Protected Biological Preserve.

They had essentially codified the Sentinel Directive into galactic law. Much like the ancient, uncontacted tribes of North Sentinel Island on Earth, the Colton family was legally recognized as a sovereign, isolated entity. The galaxy was strictly forbidden from entering their airspace.

"They're keeping their distance," Sarah murmured, her hand resting over Wyatt's. "The subspace channels have been perfectly quiet."

"They know the rules of engagement," Wyatt agreed, his veteran instincts perfectly at peace with the arrangement. "If a peaceful refugee ship limps into our orbit and knocks on the door, we'll answer the hailing frequency and send them supplies. But if a corporate scavenger or a rogue warlord crosses that line with weapons hot... the upgraded Aegis batteries will turn them into cosmic dust before they even breach the thermosphere."

They were the ultimate, benevolent ghosts of the outer arms. Safe, heavily armed, and completely content to let the universe rebuild itself without them.

Before Wyatt could elaborate on the new plasma-yield metrics he had programmed, the heavy, flat-footed thud of a canvas boot echoed against the polished stone floor.

Wyatt and Sarah both looked up.

At six and a half years old, Ellie Colton marched into the living room. She was wearing her signature miniature tactical vest, her dark eyes sharp and entirely focused on the mission at hand. Trailing faithfully behind her was the rest of the unit.

Nora limped softly into the room, leaning heavily on the side of the Chariot Mark II as she pushed it forward. Inside the wagon, Leo sat strapped into his five-point harness, humming a low, steady note of contentment. Bringing up the rear was two-and-a-half-year-old Jack, walking with perfect, energetic coordination, carrying a small stack of molecularly printed picture books.

Ellie stopped exactly three feet from the leather sofa. She planted her boots shoulder-width apart and crossed her arms.

She looked at Wyatt, and then dropped her intense gaze directly to Sarah's massive stomach.

"Commanding Officer requires a briefing," Ellie stated, her articulation crisp and uncompromising. She pointed a tiny, dictatorial finger at Sarah's bump. "Status report on the new recruits."

Wyatt bit the inside of his cheek, a massive, booming laugh threatening to burst out of his chest. His daughter wasn't asking for a bedtime story. She was conducting a formal logistical audit of her mother's uterus.

Sarah, entirely used to her eldest daughter's rigid, tactical worldview, didn't patronize her. She offered a serious, professional nod.

"Understood, Commander," Sarah replied, keeping a perfectly straight face. "Aria, please project the latest biometric telemetry for the nursery detail."

"Right away, Sarah," Aria’s synthesized voice chimed warmly from the ceiling array.

The heavy glass coffee table in front of the sofa flared to life. A glowing blue, high-definition holographic projection of the twins materialized in the air. The two tiny, perfectly formed silhouettes were curled together, their synchronized heartbeats pulsing in real-time.

Ellie uncrossed her arms and stepped right up to the edge of the glass table.

She leaned forward, her dark eyes intensely scrutinizing the holographic data. She checked the structural integrity of the twins. She checked their proximity to one another.

"Recruit Molly," Ellie identified, pointing to the baby on the left. She moved her finger to the right. "Recruit Claire."

"That's right, El," Wyatt murmured gently, leaning forward. "They are doing incredibly well. They are strong, they are healthy, and their heart rates are perfect."

Ellie didn't take his word for it. She turned to her unit to establish a consensus.

Jack waddled up to the table, his bright, highly empathetic eyes wide with wonder. He reached out, trying to gently poke the blue hologram of his new sisters. "Babies sleeping!" Jack beamed, looking up at Ellie. "Happy babies!"

Nora reached over the wooden rail of the Chariot, gently guiding Leo's shaking hand down to his ruggedized AAC tablet.

Leo slammed his palm against the bright green square.

"Good," the tablet announced loudly into the living room.

Ellie processed the data. The visual telemetry was optimal. The medical readouts were solid. The diplomatic liaison (Jack) reported high morale, and the core of the unit (Leo) had given his official endorsement.

The commander stepped back from the table, crossing her arms over her tactical vest once again. She looked Sarah directly in the eyes and gave a single, sharp nod of absolute approval.

"Assets are secure," Ellie declared, officially signing off on the medical briefing. She pointed a thumb over her shoulder toward the hallway. "Unit is returning to the playroom. Dismissed."

"Thank you, Commander," Wyatt chuckled, throwing a crisp, highly respectful salute to his six-year-old daughter.

Ellie returned the salute flawlessly. She spun on her heel, marching back toward the hallway. Nora pushed the Chariot after her, and Jack trotted happily alongside them, the perfectly synchronized, fiercely loyal machinery of the Colton family operating at peak efficiency.

As the Vanguard disappeared down the hall, Wyatt wrapped his arms securely around Sarah, pulling her and the twins back against his chest.

"I think Molly and Claire are going to be in very good hands," Wyatt smiled, resting his chin on top of Sarah's head as they watched the holographic heartbeats pulse above the table.

"The best hands in the galaxy," Sarah agreed softly, closing her eyes and letting the absolute, impenetrable peace of Planet Eden wash over her.
