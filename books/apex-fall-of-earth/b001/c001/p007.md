# Part 7: The Trough – Thursday, October 20, 2044 – ~04:40

Wyatt zipped up the front of his stiff, abrasive black utility uniform, the heavy collar scratching against his neck. The nametape pinned over his right breast pocket read COLTON.

He stepped out of the washroom corridor and merged with the sluggish stream of exhausted S.H.O.P. Marine contractors shuffling down the neon-lit hall. The noise hit him before he even entered the room—a deafening, echoing roar of overlapping conversations, scraping metal benches, and the low, constant hum of the facility's massive ventilation scrubbers.

The Mess Hall was a cavernous concrete cavern packed shoulder-to-shoulder with hundreds of contractors. Above the sea of gray and black uniforms, a massive digital billboard looped a blindingly bright Apex recruitment ad: BE A MARINE™ - A CLEAR PATH TO ADVANCEMENT!

Wyatt ignored it, stepping into line at the wall dispensers. When it was his turn, he pressed his thumb against the biometric scanner. The machine whirred, clunked heavily, and unceremoniously dispensed a dense, gray brick of synthetic nutrient paste onto a stamped metal tray. It smelled faintly of yeast and artificial chicken flavoring. Calories were calories. He grabbed a plastic spork and turned around.

Through a thick wall of reinforced plexiglass on the far side of the hall, he could see Sector 2's dining area: the Scientist and Technician Mess.

It wasn’t a paradise, but the psychological divide was intentionally jarring. The lighting over there was a warmer, softer amber instead of the harsh, migraine-inducing fluorescent white beating down on the S.H.O.P. Marine contractors. The Apex Analytics and research staff, wearing clean lab coats, were sitting in actual chairs with backrests instead of bolted metal benches. Their dispensers spit out food that was dyed and textured to vaguely resemble scrambled eggs and hash browns, and they had access to low-grade, machine-brewed coffee.

Wyatt watched a junior technician take a sip from a ceramic mug, looking through the glass at the sea of S.H.O.P. Marine grunts with a subtle expression of pity and superiority.

Wyatt chewed a mouthful of his gray paste, his face completely stoic. He knew exactly how the corporate game was played. The lab coats thought they'd made it, but they were just as trapped as he was. The real dining halls—the ones with natural sunlight, imported steak, and coffee grown in actual dirt—were fifty floors up, locked behind biometric Executive Vaults where the Apex executives sat. The corporation just gave the lower-tier scientists enough amenities to make them feel important so they wouldn't unionize with the muscle.

Finding no open seats, Wyatt leaned against a concrete support pillar near the exit to eat alone. He finished his block in four massive bites, tossed the metal tray into the magnetic reclamation bin, and headed for Sector 1: Security Processing to clock in. He had a perimeter to walk.
