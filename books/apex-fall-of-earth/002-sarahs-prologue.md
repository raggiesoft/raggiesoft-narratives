# Sarah’s Prologue

## Chapter 1: Sarah – The Promise

### Part 1: First Flight – Mid-October 2044 (Days prior to arrival)

The Tri-Sector Interstellar Freight & Personnel terminal was the cleanest room Sarah Hayes had ever stood in.

She stood near the edge of the massive, pristine observation window, her small hands tightly gripping the strap of her canvas duffel bag. At eighteen years old, she had never been off-world. Her home planet was a fading, terrestrial agricultural colony where the soil had given out a decade ago, leaving behind rusted silos and empty towns. When her parents passed away, there was no extended family to take her in, and the local economy offered nothing but dead ends.

She had just wanted a bed to sleep in. She had wanted a future.

Sarah looked down at her clothes, smoothing out a wrinkle in the crisp, breathable fabric. She wasn't wearing hand-me-downs anymore. She was wearing the official uniform of the Helios Geoscience Institute: slate-blue with soft gold trim. Neatly pinned to her chest, right below a stylized planetary-arc logo, was a nametape that read *HAYES*.

She was a Field Safety Technician. It sounded important. It sounded safe. The Helios recruiter had promised an academic environment, collaborative research, and a company culture that valued its people. For a terrified teenager with absolutely no safety net, it felt like she had just won the lottery.

A soft, melodic chime echoed through the quiet terminal, pulling her attention back to the boarding gate.

"Now boarding all passengers for Earth transit," a calm, professional voice announced over the intercom. "Please have your identification ready."

Sarah took a deep, steadying breath and joined the orderly queue. There was no shouting, no pushing, and no sense of panic. The other passengers in line were a mix of field researchers, technicians, and scientists, all speaking in hushed, polite tones.

Through the glass, she could see her TSIFP transport. It was a beautiful ship, its gleaming white hull accented with sweeping lines of gold and blue. It didn't look like an industrial freighter; it looked like a vessel of discovery.

When she reached the front of the line, a uniformed flight attendant offered her a warm, reassuring smile.

"Good morning," the attendant said gently. "Badge, please?"

Sarah fumbled with the lanyard around her neck, holding up her newly issued Department of Defense identification card. It had a gray stripe across the top and the words *C0-BASIC* printed in black block letters. She didn't really understand how the military clearance system worked, but it didn't matter. She was part of the team.

The attendant tapped the card against a sleek, modern scanner. It chimed a pleasant green.

"Welcome aboard, Miss Hayes. Your seat is located in section four. Have a wonderful flight."

"Thank you," Sarah breathed out, a massive wave of relief finally washing over her.

She stepped through the boarding bridge and into the cabin. The interior was softly lit, the air smelling faintly of lavender and sanitized climate control. She found her assigned seat—plush, wide, and perfectly comfortable—and stowed her duffel bag carefully in the overhead bin.

As she sat down and clicked her safety harness into place, she looked out the small, perfectly clear window at the fading agricultural sprawl of her home world. There was nothing left down there for her. No family, no attachments, and no reason to look back.

The sub-light engines hummed to life, a gentle, barely noticeable vibration that vibrated softly through the padded seat.

Sarah rested her hands in her lap, her posture finally relaxing as the ship lifted off the tarmac, carrying her up through the atmosphere and into the quiet dark of space. She closed her eyes, a small, genuine smile touching her lips. She was safe. She was finally going to be part of something good.

### Part 2: The Voyage – Mid-October 2044 (Interstellar transit)

The Tri-Sector Interstellar Freight & Personnel transport slipped through the void with barely a whisper.

Unlike the shuddering, industrial cargo haulers that serviced the outer agricultural colonies, the TSIFP cabin was a sanctuary of quiet efficiency. Soft, warm amber lighting illuminated the spotless aisles, reflecting off the clean slate-blue and silver accents of the bulkheads. There was no rattling, no suffocating heat, and no shouted orders. The air was perfectly climate-controlled and smelled faintly of fresh linen.

Sarah unclipped her safety harness, sinking deeper into the plush cushioning of her assigned seat. She let out a long, slow breath, finally allowing the knots of anxiety in her shoulders to fully untangle.

For the first few hours of the flight, she just watched the other passengers. They were a mix of older academics, geologists reviewing data pads, and other technicians in crisp Helios uniforms. It was a culture of quiet, collaborative professionalism. A few rows up, two researchers were softly debating seismic pressure differentials, their tones respectful and engaged. Sarah smiled to herself, tracing her thumb over the stylized planetary-arc logo on her chest. She was actually going to be a part of this.

"Excuse me, Miss Hayes?"

Sarah looked up. A flight attendant in a neat, tailored uniform was standing in the aisle, offering a warm, professional smile from behind a sleek service cart.

"We're beginning our dinner service," the attendant said gently. "Would you prefer the herb-roasted chicken or the vegetarian pasta? It's all included with your transit package."

"The pasta, please," Sarah said, her eyes widening slightly. She hadn't expected real food.

The attendant handed her a steaming tray and a cloth napkin wrapped around polished stainless-steel cutlery. Sarah peeled back the foil lid. It wasn't a synthetic nutrient brick or reconstituted gray paste. It was actual penne pasta in a rich tomato sauce, accompanied by a crisp, fresh side salad and a warm roll. It felt like a luxury she couldn't possibly deserve, but the Helios brochure had promised that employee welfare was their highest priority.

As she ate, she tapped the interactive glass screen on the seatback in front of her. The entertainment system was loaded with documentaries, academic lectures, and historical media archives.

Bypassing the standard orientation modules, Sarah navigated deep into the archive folders until she found exactly what she was looking for to calm her nerves. It was a digitized playlist of eccentric, late-20th-century science education programs—the kind with fast-talking hosts in lab coats, zany sound effects, and actors in giant animal suits enthusiastically explaining how the physical universe worked.

She slipped her noise-canceling headphones over her ears, completely captivated. There was something incredibly comforting about watching people passionately build functional science demonstrations out of everyday items. It was bright, optimistic, and deeply curious—the exact same academic spirit she believed she was flying toward.

She finished her meal, leaving the empty tray to be quietly whisked away by the attentive crew, and spent the rest of the interstellar transit wrapped in a blanket, watching the archived broadcasts and imagining her new life.

Hours later, the cabin lights gently brightened, simulating a soft morning sunrise.

"Good morning, Helios team," the captain’s voice chimed over the intercom, smooth and reassuring. "We have officially entered the Sol System and are beginning our final approach to Earth. Please secure your belongings and return your seats to their upright positions. Welcome home."

Sarah pulled off her headphones and leaned toward the thick, clear plexiglass window.

Below her, suspended in the vast blackness of space, was the brilliant, swirling blue-and-white marble of Earth. Her breath caught in her throat. It was beautiful. It looked like a world of endless possibilities, completely untouched by the rust and decay of her home planet.

She pressed her hand against the cool glass, her heart fluttering with genuine, unadulterated excitement. She was a Field Safety Technician for the Helios Geoscience Institute, and her new life was about to begin.

### Part 3: The Transfer – Thursday, October 20, 2044 – 08:00 (Local Spaceport Time)

The TSIFP transport glided into the docking cradle at the Las Vegas Interplanetary Spaceport with a gentle, barely perceptible shudder. The docking mechanisms locked into place with a muffled, hydraulic sigh, and the soft amber cabin lights shifted to a bright, welcoming white.

"Ladies and gentlemen, welcome to Earth," the captain announced, his voice warm over the intercom. "Local time is 0800 hours. Please disembark safely and proceed to your atmospheric transfers."

Sarah unbuckled her safety harness and stood up in the aisle. The moment she was fully upright, a sudden, heavy pressure pulled at her limbs. Earth's natural gravity. It was denser, more unyielding than the artificial gravity on the ship or the failing terraform generators of her home colony. It made her canvas duffel bag feel ten pounds heavier, but instead of feeling oppressive, the weight felt incredibly solid. It was grounding. Real.

She walked down the boarding bridge and stepped into a bright, airy terminal. Large, pristine windows let in the brilliant Nevada sunlight, reflecting off polished floors. Everything was orderly and serene. Digital monitors overhead displayed the familiar Helios intersecting-arc logo in soft gold and slate-blue, guiding the arriving researchers and technicians toward their connecting flights.

Smiling personnel in crisp, clean uniforms stood at the intersections, answering questions and directing traffic. It felt less like a military spaceport and more like a high-end academic campus.

Sarah followed the signs for "Atmospheric Transfers," walking with a light, eager step until she reached the Department of Defense security checkpoint. It was a mandatory federal bottleneck—a row of thick glass booths and heavy turnstiles manned by uniformed military officers.

There was no shouting, no shoving. The line of Helios personnel moved with quiet efficiency.

When it was Sarah's turn, she stepped up to the yellow line and proudly held up her new identification card. The DoD officer didn't ask her any questions or scrutinize her uniform. He simply took the badge and swiped the gray C0-BASIC stripe through a standard federal reader. The machine chirped a pleasant, immediate green.

The officer handed the badge back with a polite nod and waved her through the turnstile.

On the other side of the glass, the climate-controlled concourse opened up to a series of private, contractor-only gates. Parked on the sun-drenched tarmac just outside the terminal was a sleek atmospheric shuttle. It bore the elegant livery of Desert Star Aerospace—a pristine white hull accented with soft teal and silver sweeping lines.

A Desert Star flight attendant stood at the base of the boarding ramp, checking manifests on a digital tablet with a welcoming smile.

"Good morning," the attendant said cheerfully as Sarah approached. "Desert Star service to the Nevada Research Facility. May I see your seat assignment?"

Sarah showed her datapad.

"Perfect. Row four, window seat. Welcome to Earth, Miss Hayes. Please watch your step."

Sarah climbed the ramp and stepped into the shuttle. The interior was spotless, cool, and incredibly quiet. The seats were heavily padded and smelled of fresh upholstery. She found her window seat, stowed her duffel bag, and settled in, buckling her harness. The safety briefing was delivered in a calm, soothing voice, emphasizing the comfort and security of the passengers.

A few minutes later, the atmospheric thrusters engaged with a smooth, even hum. The shuttle lifted off the tarmac effortlessly, banking away from the glittering sprawl of Las Vegas and out toward the endless, sun-baked expanse of the Nevada desert.

Sarah leaned her head against the cool, vibration-free glass, looking down at the rugged, red-brown mountains and sweeping valleys. To her, it didn't look like a harsh, unforgiving wasteland. It looked like a pristine frontier of science and discovery. She folded her hands in her lap, her heart full of bright, optimistic anticipation as the Desert Star shuttle carried her deeper into the desert.

### Part 4: The Handoff – Thursday, October 20, 2044 – 04:00 (NTTR Local Time)

The Desert Star atmospheric shuttle touched down with a gentle, controlled hiss of hydraulics, settling onto the tarmac with barely a bump. The ambient cabin music faded out, replaced by the soft chime of the seatbelt indicator turning off.

"Welcome to the Nevada Research Facility," the flight attendant announced. But as Sarah unbuckled her harness and stood up, she noticed a sudden, tight strain in the attendant’s typically serene voice. The woman wasn't smiling anymore. She was staring out the small window set into the main cabin door, her posture rigidly tense.

The heavy door hissed open, and the blinding, suffocating heat of the Nevada desert flooded the pristine cabin.

Sarah grabbed her canvas duffel bag and stepped into the aisle, following the line of other Helios technicians and researchers toward the exit. She walked down the boarding ramp, expecting to see the welcoming, slate-blue and white banners of a corporate campus. She expected scientists with data-pads and golf carts waiting to take them to their dormitories.

Instead, she stepped into a nightmare.

The landing pad was entirely enclosed by towering, blast-scorched concrete walls. There were no Helios logos. There were no smiling academics. Waiting at the bottom of the ramp was a squad of men in heavy, industrial-black tactical gear, their uniforms marked with abrasive hazard-yellow chevrons and blocky, angular lettering: *APEX DEFENSE SOLUTIONS*.

They wore vivid red armbands, and the weapons slung across their chests were not for scientific research.

"Move! Off the ramp, now!" one of the Apex supervisors bellowed through a handheld megaphone, his voice echoing violently off the concrete walls. "Helios transfers, form a single file line! Keep your mouths shut and do not deviate from the perimeter markers!"

Sarah froze, her boots hitting the scorching tarmac. The crushing gravity of Earth suddenly felt less like a grounding force and more like a trap.

All around her, the illusion of the prestigious Helios Geoscience Institute shattered in real-time. The other passengers—older geologists, young safety technicians, and field researchers—huddled together like frightened sheep. In their clean, breathable slate-blue and soft gold uniforms, they stood out like glowing targets against the brutalist, gray-and-black militarized staging ground.

They weren't an academic research team arriving for an orientation. They looked like prisoners of war.

"What is this?" an older man in a white Helios lab jacket stammered, stepping out of line to address one of the guards. "I was told my department head would be here to—"

"Get back in line!" the supervisor roared, stepping forward and racking the slide of his weapon with a terrifying, metallic clack. "Your department head doesn't own you anymore! Apex owns your contract! Now pick up your bag and get in the transport, or you will be processed for insubordination!"

The man paled, shrinking back into the line.

Sarah’s heart hammered furiously against her ribs, a cold, paralyzing knot of terror forming in her stomach. She looked down at the stylized planetary-arc logo on her chest, realizing with horrifying clarity that the ethical, safe company she had signed her life away to was dead. Apex hadn't just acquired Helios; they had swallowed it whole, and they hadn't bothered to preserve an ounce of its humanity.

"Load up! Move!"

She was herded toward a fleet of massive, windowless ground transports painted matte gray. She climbed into the dark, stifling cabin, taking a seat on a hard metal bench alongside a dozen other terrified Helios recruits. The heavy rear doors slammed shut, plunging them into a claustrophobic gloom illuminated only by a single red overhead light.

The engine roared, and the transport lurched forward.

Nobody spoke. The silence in the cabin was suffocating, thick with the shared, unspoken realization that they had been sold into a corporate meat grinder. Sarah hugged her canvas duffel bag to her chest, feeling the angle of the floor tilt sharply downward as the transport drove deep into the earth. The air grew steadily colder, the desert heat replaced by the damp chill of subterranean concrete.

When the transport finally ground to a halt and the rear doors swung open, Sarah stepped out into a cavernous, echoing bunker.

The intake facility was a massive, subterranean loading dock illuminated by harsh, buzzing fluorescent tubes. The air tasted of ozone, cheap floor wax, and industrial dust. It was completely overwhelming. Worse, the room was already filled with hundreds of other contractors—men and women dressed in the abrasive, dust-stained gray and black uniforms of Apex Defense Solutions.

Walking through the center of the room in her bright slate-blue Field Safety Technician uniform, Sarah felt entirely exposed. She was a walking, breathing relic of a conquered company. She felt the eyes of the hardened Apex veterans tracking her, their stares devoid of any warmth or academic camaraderie.

She kept her head down, her hands shaking as she was funneled into a long line leading toward a row of fortified turnstiles.

When she reached the front, there was no one to welcome her to the team. There was only a battered, uniform Department of Defense badge reader bolted to the cinderblock wall.

Sarah fumbled with her lanyard, her fingers trembling as she pressed her badge against the glass. The machine read the gray *C0-BASIC* stripe. It gave a dull, indifferent chirp, and a small LED flipped to green.

The heavy metal teeth of the turnstile clunked open.

Sarah pushed through the cold steel gate, stepping into the long, windowless concrete corridor that led deeper into the facility. The polite, comfortable world of the Desert Star shuttle felt like it belonged to another universe. She tightened her grip on her bag, entirely alone, terrified, and utterly out of her depth in the dark.

### Part 5: The Glitch – Thursday, October 20, 2044 – ~04:15 (Shift starts in exactly forty-five minutes)

The heavy steel gate of the security checkpoint slammed shut, sealing off the deafening roar of the intake bunker.

Instead of being directed down the main corridor toward the dormitories with the massive, gray-clad contractor herd, the Apex guards diverted the small group of Helios transfers into a secondary holding room. The room was a harsh, windowless concrete box illuminated by a single, aggressively bright fluorescent panel. There were no chairs. A dozen Helios personnel—geologists, researchers, and technicians—stood shoulder-to-shoulder, their crisp slate-blue and soft gold uniforms looking entirely absurd against the brutalist gray walls.

Sarah hugged her canvas duffel bag to her chest, trying to make herself as small as possible.

A heavy metal door at the front of the room hissed open. An Apex Defense Solutions administrative official stepped in, flanked by a heavily armored supervisor wearing a vivid red armband. The official wasn't wearing tactical gear, but his cheap, ill-fitting gray suit and dead-eyed expression were just as intimidating. He carried a stack of battered, heavy-duty datapads.

"Listen up," the official droned, his voice completely devoid of inflection. "I don't care what the recruitment brochures told you before you boarded your transit. The Helios Geoscience Institute is now a wholly owned subsidiary of Apex Defense Solutions. Your previous corporate culture, your safety committees, and your academic autonomy do not exist here at the Nevada Test and Training Range."

A heavy, terrified silence fell over the room.

"Your titles as Field Safety Technicians or Deep-Mantle Researchers are officially obsolete," the official continued, pacing slowly in front of the group. "As of this moment, you are reclassified as Subcontracted Hazard Operations Personnel. You are S.H.O.P. Marines. You will follow the Apex chain of command, you will meet your operational quotas, and you will not deviate from your assigned perimeters."

Sarah felt a cold knot form in her stomach. Everything the recruiter had promised her had evaporated before she even saw the sun.

"Your current uniforms are out of compliance and are a violation of Apex trademark policy," he stated flatly, beginning to walk down the line and shoving a battered, scratched Apex datapad into the hands of each terrified transfer. "You will be issued standard industrial-gray S.H.O.P. Marine apparel at the end of your first shift. Until then, you are visually marked. Keep your heads down so the veterans don't mistake your bright colors for insubordination."

Sarah looked down at the heavy, cracked screen of the datapad. Blocky green text illuminated the display:

- **NAME:** HAYES, S.

- **CLEARANCE:** C0-BASIC

- **STATUS:** ACQUISITION TRANSFER

- **ASSIGNMENT:** S.H.O.P. BARRACKS 4

- **BUNK:** 42-C

- **ROTATION:** SHIFT 2

Sarah frowned, her heart fluttering with a sudden, panicked confusion. Before they had landed, the Desert Star flight attendant had quietly mentioned that all incoming Helios transfers were slated for Shift 3, the overnight rotation.

"Excuse me," Sarah said, her voice trembling slightly as she stepped a fraction of an inch forward.

The red-banded supervisor instantly rested his hand on the grip of his weapon, but the gray-suited official just let out an annoyed sigh. "What?"

"My datapad," Sarah stammered, holding it up. "It says I'm assigned to Shift 2. I thought the Helios transfers were on Shift 3?"

The official didn't even bother looking at her screen. He just tapped his own master tablet. "System glitch. The HR algorithm assigns the slots. If your pad says Shift 2, you are on Shift 2. Apex will fix the paperwork later. Just get moving."

He turned back to address the entire room. "The facility is operating at maximum capacity. You are assigned to a three-person hot-bunking rotation. Shift 2 starts in exactly forty-five minutes. You have just enough time to drop your gear at your assigned bunks in Barracks 4 and use the washrooms before you are expected to clock in at the Sector 1 Security Checkpoint. Do not be late. Welcome to Apex. Dismissed."

The heavy doors hissed open again, spilling the suffocating noise of the subterranean facility back into the room.

Sarah slung her duffel bag over her shoulder, her slate-blue uniform feeling like a target painted directly on her back. She had forty-five minutes to navigate the chaotic maze of Barracks 4, find a bunk she was supposed to share with two other strangers, and somehow clean the cold sweat of terror off her skin before facing her first shift.

She stepped out into the neon-lit corridor, entirely alone and utterly out of her depth, unaware that the massive, scarred veteran assigned to Shift 2 was already walking toward those exact same washrooms.

### Part 6: The Washroom – Thursday, October 20, 2044 – ~04:30

Sarah dropped her canvas duffel bag on the floor next to Bunk 42-C, clutching her hygiene kit and a thin towel to her chest. She stepped back out into the narrow, 64-inch aisle of the barracks, her heart hammering against her ribs.

The air grew heavy and humid as she approached the archway to the communal washroom. Harsh, sickly yellow-green light spilled out onto the concrete floor, accompanied by the echoing sound of failing plumbing.

She stepped inside and froze.

The room was a massive, tiled concrete box with a long, rusted industrial drain running right down the center. There were no stalls. No partitions. No cheap plastic curtains.

A dozen veterans from the earlier shift were standing under the weak, sputtering showerheads. Men and women were mixed completely together, methodically scrubbing the pale desert dust and industrial grime off their skin. They were dead-eyed and entirely apathetic. Modesty was a luxury Apex clearly didn't provide.

A wave of profound nausea washed over her. Back home, her bedroom door hadn't even had a lock, but the thought of being walked in on had always been a nightmare. Retreating instinctively, Sarah hunched over and backed into the darkest corner of the room. She pressed her back against the damp, dingy tiles, crossing her arms tightly over her chest, trying desperately to protect whatever little dignity she had left.

Her eyes were wide with a mixture of shock, vulnerability, and profound regret. The recruiter's lies about collaborative environments and safety protocols vanished, replaced by the crushing realization that she was nothing more than inventory.

As she stood there shivering, another contractor walked in. He was massive, standing well over six feet tall, his face and arms marked by thick, weathered scars. Sarah tensed, pressing herself harder against the cold tiles, bracing for the leering gaze or the crude comment she expected from the hardened mercenaries that populated the facility.

But the man didn't even blink.

He kept his eyes respectfully fixed on the cracked tile floor, moving toward an empty, dripping pipe. He didn't stare. He didn't offer a pitying glance or try to reassure her—somehow knowing that acknowledging her panic would just make it worse. He simply stepped under his own rusted showerhead and deliberately turned his broad back to the room, entirely shielding her from his line of sight.

It was a small, silent gesture, but in the hostile, subterranean bunker, it felt like a lifeline.

Taking a shaky breath, Sarah forced herself to move. She quickly stepped under the nearest sputtering showerhead, twisting the squeaking valve. Freezing, metallic-smelling water hit her shoulders, washing away the sweat of the transport shuttle.

Ten minutes later, she was dressed back in her clean slate-blue Helios uniform, the bright fabric feeling less like a mark of prestige and more like a target. She stepped out of the humid washroom, clutching her damp towel, and followed the neon signs pointing toward the Mess Hall.

### Part 7: The Divide – Thursday, October 20, 2044 – ~04:40

Sarah stepped out into the neon-lit corridor, clutching her damp towel, and was immediately swept up in a massive tide of Apex contractors heading toward the Sector 1 Mess Hall.

The noise of the room hit her like a physical blow. It was a deafening, echoing roar of overlapping conversations and scraping metal benches, underscored by the heavy, vibrating hum of the facility's air scrubbers.

The Mess Hall was a cavernous concrete bunker, packed shoulder-to-shoulder. Above the sea of intimidating black and hazard-yellow armor, a massive digital billboard glared down at them, looping an aggressive corporate advertisement for the S.H.O.P. program. Walking through the center of it in her bright slate-blue Helios uniform, Sarah felt entirely exposed.

She kept her head down, filtering into line at the wall dispensers. When she reached the front, she tapped her new *C0-BASIC* badge against the reader. The machine ground loudly, clunking as it dispensed a dense, gray brick of synthetic nutrient paste onto a stamped metal tray. The smell of yeast and artificial flavoring hit her nose, and her stomach immediately turned over.

She grabbed a plastic spork and turned around, desperately searching for an empty spot to sit.

As she scanned the room, her eyes caught on a thick wall of reinforced plexiglass on the far side of the hall. Through the transparent barrier, the lighting shifted to a warmer, softer amber. Inside, men and women in crisp lab coats were sitting in actual chairs with backrests, drinking from ceramic coffee mugs and eating food that actually resembled a meal.

Sarah stared through the glass, a profound, crushing weight settling on her chest.

That was where she was supposed to be. That was the life the Helios recruiter had sold her—a safe, academic environment filled with bright minds and collaborative research. Instead, she was standing on the wrong side of the glass, holding a tray of gray paste, surrounded by hardened mercenaries who looked at her like she was a walking target.

Unable to look at the scientists any longer, Sarah squeezed her way to the far edge of the room and found a few inches of space at the end of a bolted metal bench.

She sat down, staring at her tray, poking the gray nutrient brick with her plastic spork. She looked slightly green. Between the claustrophobia of the bunker, the horrifying reality of the hot-bunking rotation, and the smell of the synthetic food, the weight of her eighteen-month contract was visibly crushing her.

She forced herself to swallow two bites, knowing she would pass out if she didn't get the calories. When she couldn't stomach another mouthful, she stood up, tossed the metal tray into the magnetic reclamation bin, and headed for Sector 1: Security Processing.

It was time to face whatever came next.

### Part 8: The Checkpoint – Thursday, October 20, 2044 – ~04:50

Sarah tossed her empty food tray into the reclamation bin and headed for Sector 1: Security Processing.

The Checkpoint was a severe, fortified bottleneck. A thick wall of bulletproof glass separated the S.H.O.P. Marines from the Apex Supervisors—heavily armored men in ballistic vests holding combat shotguns. She stepped up to the yellow line, her heart hammering against her ribs.

"Name, serial, age, and retinal scan," the Supervisor barked through the two-way speaker, not even looking up from his terminal.

"Um... Hayes, Sarah M.," she stammered, leaning into the scanner. "Contractor ID: 912-Echo. Age... age eighteen.".

The scanner beeped green. A heavy metal transfer drawer slid open under the glass. Inside was a battered Level 1 PDA and a heavy, Mark-IV industrial flashlight. No sidearm. She grabbed the gear, clutching the heavy flashlight to her chest like a shield.

"Sector 4 Perimeter Patrol," the Supervisor droned. "Take a seat on the bench past the turnstile and wait for your partner.".

She finished at the glass, pushed through the heavy metal turnstile, and nearly tripped over her own steel-toed boots. She looked up, realizing her assigned partner was already waiting for her.

It was him. The massive, scarred veteran from the showers—the one guy who didn't stare at her.

Sarah froze, her posture instantly turning defensive. He stood up to his full 6'1" height, a hardened twenty-three-year-old wrapped in the abrasive black and hazard-yellow armor of Apex Defense Solutions. In a facility where the veterans were usually predators, he looked terrifying. If he wanted to make her first shift a nightmare, there was absolutely nothing she could do to stop him.

But he didn't step toward her. He kept his distance, leaving a wide, respectful gap between them. He didn't try to force a smile—that would just look unnatural and creepy. Instead, he offered a simple, grounding nod. Professional and calm.

"You're Hayes, right?" he asked, keeping his voice low and even to cut through the harsh buzzing of the overhead lights. "I'm Colton. Supervisor says we're partnered up for Sector 4.".

Sarah blinked, processing the total lack of hostility in his voice. She looked at the nametape on his chest, then back up to his scarred face. The defensive tension in her shoulders dropped just a fraction of an inch. He wasn't treating her like acquired inventory; he was treating her like a partner.

"Y-yes," she stammered, clearing her throat to find a steadier voice. "Sarah Hayes. It's... it's my first day.".

"I know," he said softly. He gestured down the long, dimly lit corridor with his flashlight. "Stay close, keep your head down, and we'll get through the shift. I've got your back.".

She looked at him, really looking this time, and realized he meant it. For the first time since she stepped off the Desert Star transport shuttle, a tiny sliver of relief washed over her face.

"Okay," she breathed, her grip on the flashlight relaxing just enough to let the blood return to her knuckles. "Right behind you, Colton.".
