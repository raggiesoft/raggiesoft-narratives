# Part 3: The Golden Cage – Thursday, June 18, 2071 – 10:00 (Local Eden Time)

Sub-Level 6 of the mountain estate was a marvel of pristine, automated medical technology, but to Sloane, it was starting to feel incredibly isolating.

She was sitting upright in the primary diagnostic bio-bed, a plush, molecularly printed blanket pulled over her lap. The dark, blooming contusion across her cheekbone was already fading into a dull yellow, and the agonizing ache in her side was completely muted by the steady, painless drip of Aria's molecular regeneration agents.

Sitting on a rolling metal stool beside her bed was Jack.

"The scan shows your internal tissue is regenerating beautifully, Sloane," Jack smiled, his melodic voice incredibly warm as he checked the diagnostic monitor above her. When he said her name, he carefully honored the unique pronunciation she had given him—*Slow-anne*—the syllables rolling off his tongue with a gentle, comforting respect.

Sloane offered a soft, deeply appreciative smile, reaching out to rest her hand gently over his where it sat on the edge of the mattress.

"I can't thank you enough, Jack," Sloane murmured, her voice carrying a fragile, mesmerizing vulnerability. "I've never seen medical tech like this before. You didn't just save my life; you gave it back to me."

Jack’s empathetic heart swelled. He gently squeezed her hand. "I'm just glad I found you in time. We take care of people here."

He hesitated for a moment, his dark eyes filled with gentle concern. "Sloane... my older sister, Ellie, is the Commander of the estate. She’s incredibly protective of the perimeter. She wants me to ask you for more details about the scavenger colony, or the registration of your ship, so we can vet your story and formally clear you to leave the medical bay."

Sloane shrank back slightly against the pillows. Her breathing shallowed, and her eyes welled with sudden, bright tears. She looked away, her hand trembling under his.

"It's... it's all just a blur, Jack," Sloane whispered, her voice cracking with genuine distress. "I just remember the alarms, and the screaming. The pirates took everything. If I try to think about the registration numbers or the coordinates, my chest just gets so tight. Please... I just need a little more time to process it."

Jack instantly felt a wave of guilt for pushing her. His natural instinct as a peacemaker completely overrode Ellie's tactical demands. He shifted his stool closer, entirely disarmed by her tears.

"Hey, it's okay," Jack soothed quickly, his thumb gently stroking the back of her hand. "You don't have to talk about it until you're ready. Take all the time you need. I'll run interference with Ellie."

Sloane looked back at him, offering a fragile, watery smile. "Thank you, Jack. You're the only person who makes me feel safe right now. Are you... are you going to stay with me this afternoon?"

Jack’s smile faltered slightly, a flash of apologetic regret crossing his face.

"I'm sorry, Sloane, but I can't," Jack admitted gently. He pulled his hand back, resting it on his knee. "My siblings and I planned an overnight camping trip in the eastern valley. It’s a family tradition. We're going to be gone until tomorrow morning."

Sloane’s chest tightened. A sudden, sharp pang of loneliness flared in her gut. She had been locked in this sterile white room for two days, and Jack was her only tether to sanity.

"Camping?" Sloane asked, her brow furrowing slightly. "Like... out in the woods?"

"Yeah," Jack chuckled, his eyes lighting up with profound affection as he talked about his family. "My oldest brother, Leo, is profoundly autistic and has cerebral palsy. He loves the outdoors, but the unpredictability of the forest can completely dysregulate him. So, my dad builds us a localized, climate-controlled pavilion right by the lake. We lay down a molecularly printed interlocking floor grid so Leo's powerchair doesn't sink into the mud, and we use a white-noise generator to perfectly mimic the muffled hum of the mountain's geothermal reactors so he has his auditory baseline."

Sloane listened, her eyes slightly wide.

"Nora and Ellie unroll a massive, modular memory-foam pad right onto the floor of the pavilion so they can sleep pressed against him and provide deep pressure therapy under the stars," Jack continued, entirely proud of the lengths his family went to. "Molly, Claire, Willow, and I handle the campfire and the food. It's a massive logistical operation, but it's brilliant."

Sloane maintained a perfectly sweet, entirely supportive smile, though a heavy, sinking feeling settled in her stomach. *Willow.* The teenage girl with the dark braid who trailed after Jack like a shadow. He was leaving her in a medical bay to go build a custom wilderness fortress for his siblings and sit by a fire with another girl.

"That sounds wonderful," Sloane said softly, her voice carrying a genuine, wistful sadness. "They are incredibly lucky to have you looking out for them, Jack. I've never known a family that devoted to each other."

"I'm the lucky one," Jack smiled, completely missing the quiet ache of jealousy behind her eyes. He stood up from the stool, adjusting his canvas jacket. "My sister Nora is going to be running your medical diagnostics from the remote terminal while we're gone, and Aria is monitoring you constantly. If you need anything, just ask the ceiling."

"I will," Sloane replied, reaching out to gently touch his arm one last time before he stepped away. She looked up at him, her dark eyes wide and incredibly earnest. "Have a wonderful time, Jack. I'll miss you."

"I'll be back tomorrow morning," Jack promised, his heart fluttering slightly at the depth of her gaze. He turned and walked out of the medical bay.

The heavy brushed-steel doors hissed shut behind him, sealing with a heavy, magnetic *thud*.

Sloane sat alone in the brilliant, clinical white light of the bio-bed. She pulled the plush blanket tighter around her chest, letting out a long, frustrated sigh.

She hated being stuck in this room. She thought the Commander's strict quarantine rules were paranoid and entirely unfair, especially when she was feeling so much better. She wanted to be out there in the woods. She wanted to sit by the fire with Jack.

But as she looked at the brushed-steel doors, she swallowed her frustration. Jack was the kindest, most wonderful man she had ever met, and he was worth waiting for. She would endure the medical bay, she would play by Ellie's rules, and she would count the seconds until he came back to her.
