# Part 5: The Stabilizers – Friday, October 25, 2052 – 14:15

The fleet of specialized transports was a resounding success, but Wyatt Colton’s engineering summit wasn't quite finished.

While Ellie and Nora expertly maneuvered Leo’s new Daily Driver around the perimeter of the living room, Wyatt reached back into the cargo compartment of the hovering maintenance drone. He pulled out a long, narrow canvas sleeve.

"Hold up, squad," Wyatt called out, stepping into the center of the plush rug. "I have one more piece of gear to issue."

Ellie immediately halted the wheelchair. Nora, leaning her weight against the rear push-bar, looked up with wide, curious eyes.

Wyatt dropped to one knee right in front of his middle daughter. He unzipped the canvas sleeve and pulled out a pair of custom-fabricated forearm crutches.

They were masterpieces of ergonomic design, molecularly printed from the same lightweight, matte-silver titanium as the new shuttles. The forearm cuffs were lined with breathable, dark memory-foam, and the handgrips were molded precisely to the shape of Nora’s small palms. The bases featured wide, articulated, shock-absorbing rubber treads perfectly engineered to grip polished stone or loose forest dirt.

"Nora," Wyatt said gently, holding them out to her. "I know the wagon helps you balance. But you're getting older, and you shouldn't have to drag that left leg when you want to walk across the room by yourself. These will take the weight off."

Nora let go of Leo’s push-bar. She reached out, her dark eyes shining with a mix of awe and slight hesitation, and slipped her arms into the cuffs. Her hands naturally found the grips.

"Try them out," Wyatt encouraged, stepping back to give her space.

Nora shifted her weight. Instead of leaning heavily to her right side and dragging her weaker left foot, she pressed down on the titanium grips. The crutches flawlessly absorbed the burden of gravity. She took a step. Then another.

For the first time in her nearly seven years of life, Nora walked across the living room with a perfectly even, level gait. The severe limp was entirely mitigated by the stabilizers.

A massive, breathless smile broke across Nora’s face. She turned around, easily pivoting on the shock-absorbing tips, and looked at her father.

"They're perfect," Nora beamed, her breathy voice filled with an immense, profound gratitude. "Thank you, Dad."

Wyatt’s heart swelled. He noticed the vocabulary shift instantly. Sometime over the last few months, the childish, toddler-esque Dada and Momma had naturally, quietly vanished from the Vanguard's lexicon. They were growing up.

"You're welcome, sweetheart," Wyatt smiled, his chest aching with pride.

Ellie observed her sister's new mobility. She inspected the titanium shafts and the rubber treads. Satisfied that the gear enhanced the unit's overall combat readiness, the commander gave a firm nod.

"Good gear, Dad," Ellie approved.

Nora walked a quick lap around the massive reclaimed-wood coffee table, completely reveling in the effortless, painless movement. But as she completed the circuit, she didn't wander off to explore the rest of the estate.

She walked right back to the rear of Leo's titanium wheelchair.

With a practiced, completely natural motion, Nora clicked the two forearm crutches into a pair of custom magnetic holsters Wyatt had thoughtfully integrated into the sides of the chair's frame. She immediately grabbed the padded push-bar, leaning her cheek against the back of Leo's memory-foam seat.

Leo, who had been humming anxiously while Nora was walking away, instantly relaxed as her physical presence anchored him once again. He slammed his palm against his AAC tablet.

"Happy," the automated voice declared.

Wyatt let out a low, deeply affectionate chuckle, looking over at Sarah on the sofa. Nora appreciated the crutches for solo missions, but the core of the Vanguard remained entirely unbroken. She would always choose to push her brother. To be disconnected from Leo was simply a violation of her empathetic nature.

"She likes the gear, but she loves her post," Sarah smiled warmly, resting her hands over her pregnant belly.

Sarah looked away from the Vanguard and down at the padded floor mat resting near the sofa.

Nearly-three-year-old Jack was sitting cross-legged, entirely unfazed by the engineering summit happening a few feet away. He had a digital stylus in his hand and a ruggedized datapad resting on his lap. Molly and Claire, the five-month-old twins, were propped up on their bellies, their identical dark eyes completely locked on their older brother.

"Look, Mom," Jack said, his voice carrying the crisp, shockingly advanced articulation of a child twice his age. He held up the datapad.

Sarah leaned forward. Written on the screen, in neat, perfectly legible block letters, was the word: EDEN.

"Jack, did you write that?" Sarah gasped, her eyes going wide.

"Aria showed me the letters in the library," Jack explained casually, setting the tablet down and pointing at the word for his infant sisters. "E-D-E-N. That's where we live."

Wyatt walked over, his veteran instincts completely floored by the sheer cognitive speed of his youngest son. Jack wasn't just walking and talking perfectly; he was actively reading and writing before his third birthday. His neurotypical brain was absorbing the university archives like a sponge.

Molly let out a loud, bubbly squeal, slapping her tiny hands against the padded mat as if applauding him.

"Ma... ma..." Claire babbled beside her, a string of wet, unformed consonants bubbling from her lips as she tried desperately to mimic Jack's clear speech. "Ba... ba!"

"That's right, Claire. That's an E," Jack encouraged patiently, completely slipping into the role of the estate's youngest professor.

Wyatt sat down on the edge of the leather sofa next to Sarah, wrapping his heavy arm around her shoulders. He looked at the pristine, sunlit living room.

Nora, Ellie, and Leo were communicating seamlessly, a unified front of neurodivergent brilliance. Jack was teaching his infant sisters how to spell the name of their planet, his diplomatic leadership already firmly established. And inside Sarah, Evan and Tyler were safely growing, waiting to join the ranks.

"They aren't babies anymore, Sarah," Wyatt murmured, listening to the chorus of his children echoing through the mountain fortress. "They're really growing up."

"They're going to be ready, Wyatt," Sarah whispered back, resting her head against his shoulder. "When the time comes to wake the vault... they're going to be ready to lead it."
