# Part 2: The Rhythm Section – Thursday, July 18, 2052 – 14:00

The sprawling residential living room was filled with a sound that hadn't echoed across a planet in nearly a decade.

Wyatt Colton had spent the last week in the Fabrication Foundry, putting his mechanic's brain to work on something entirely luxurious. He had molecularly printed a massive, high-fidelity analog stereo system, complete with glowing vacuum tubes and towering acoustic-foam speakers.

Currently, the speakers were perfectly tuned, broadcasting the lush, atmospheric soundscape of The Stardust Engine. It was their 1995 underground masterpiece, Escape Velocity (Ad Astra). The track was a staggering 15:33 progressive-rock epic—an autobiographical suite where Cassidy O’Connell had written every single note and sung every lyric, her high, ethereal vocals floating like a ghost over the hypnotic, sweeping synthesizers.

Outside the camouflaged hydro-glass windows, Planet Eden was completely at peace. The Global Defense Coalition had honored their word, legally codifying the planet's absolute isolation. The Aegis shield had been upgraded yet again, its dampeners reinforced to mask their thermal footprint from any new deep-space scanning technology that the Earth Remnant might invent. They were a ghost in the void, and they were perfectly content to stay that way.

Sarah was curled up on the oversized leather sofa, a warm cup of herbal tea in her hands. At roughly ten weeks pregnant, her new baby bump was a small, distinct curve beneath her tunic. Unlike the brutal, violently exhausting gauntlet of carrying the triplets, this pregnancy was flowing with a smooth, incredible ease. There was no severe nausea, no crushing fatigue—just a warm, steady biological progression.

"Commander requires a status report," a crisp, demanding voice announced.

Sarah looked down. Six-and-a-half-year-old Ellie was standing perfectly at attention at the edge of the sofa, her dark eyes expectant.

"The recruits are operating at peak efficiency, Commander," Sarah smiled, gesturing toward the center of the plush rug.

Lying side-by-side on a thick, padded mat were two-month-old Molly and Claire. They were wide awake, their tiny legs kicking happily. But they weren't looking at their mother or their father. Their identical, dark eyes were locked entirely onto the two-and-a-half-year-old boy sitting cross-legged right in front of them.

Jack didn't have Ellie's rigid tactical discipline, and he didn't need it. He possessed an advanced, naturally gravitational empathy. He was currently holding a brightly colored, molecularly printed picture book, turning the pages and explaining the illustrations with a vocabulary that was staggeringly advanced for his age.

"And this one is a dog," Jack explained clearly, his voice a soft, melodic rumble. He gently tapped the page. "Daddy says dogs used to live on Earth. They say woof."

Molly let out a happy, burbling coo, reaching her tiny hand toward him. Claire instantly mirrored her sister, kicking her feet and letting out a soft sigh. They were utterly entranced. Whenever Jack spoke, the twins naturally oriented their entire world around him, completely soothed by his diplomatic, calming presence.

Ellie watched the interaction closely. She evaluated Jack’s handling of the assets. Satisfied that the perimeter was secure and the recruits were being properly educated, she gave a firm, sharp nod.

"Good recruit," Ellie approved, turning on her heel to check on the rest of her unit.

The music from the massive speakers suddenly shifted. Part II: Ignition began, and the dark, shimmering synth pads were cut by a rapid, driving analog sequencer and a pounding acoustic drum beat.

The sudden acoustic acceleration was a massive sensory input. Across the room, Leo went completely rigid in his Chariot, his scissored legs locking tight. He let out a sharp, distressed hum, entirely overwhelmed by the driving rhythm section.

Wyatt immediately pushed himself off the stone counter, ready to step in, but he didn't have to.

Jack looked up, but he stayed perfectly seated, knowing exactly what the boundaries were. Leo had recently drawn a very strict, unspoken line in the sand: when he was dysregulated, he only accepted deep pressure therapy from his sisters.

Ellie and Nora were already moving. Nora limped quickly to the right side of the wagon, throwing her arms around Leo's chest and burying her face in his shoulder. Ellie took the left flank, pressing her solid, unyielding back against his side to box him in. Within ten seconds, Leo’s rigid panic melted into a soft, vibrating purr.

Wyatt let out a long, heavy exhale, a massive smile spreading across his scarred face. He walked over to the sofa, sitting down heavily next to Sarah. He wrapped his arm around her shoulders, resting his calloused hand gently over her small bump.

"They're a machine," Wyatt murmured, absolutely awestruck by his children. "Ellie and Nora lock Leo down, and Jack naturally takes over the twins. We're just the maintenance crew at this point."

"We built a good team," Sarah agreed, leaning her head against his chest as the soaring, emotional electric guitar solo of the song washed over the room. "Speaking of the team... we need to finalize the roster."

Wyatt looked down at her stomach. The localized ultrasound in the medical bay had confirmed the impossible genetic lottery they had won. Identical twin boys.

"We need names," Wyatt nodded, his veteran instincts pivoting entirely to fatherhood. He listened to the massive, clock-like precision of the drums playing through the speakers, supported by the driving, atmospheric layers of the bass guitar.

"I was reading the archival liner notes on this album," Sarah noted, gesturing toward the massive stereo system. "The Stardust Engine was a family band. The rhythm section—the bass player and the drummer—they were identical twins. Evan and Tyler Wright. The band called them the 'texture' and the 'power'."

Wyatt’s eyes lit up. He thought about the two boys currently growing inside his wife. With his own physical genetics, they were going to be massive. They were going to be the brawn, the heavy armor, and the physical enforcers of the Colton family.

"Evan and Tyler," Wyatt tested the names, a slow, deeply satisfied grin breaking across his face. "The rhythm section. The muscle. I love it. It fits perfectly."

"I wonder if they made it," Sarah murmured softly, looking out the massive hydro-glass window at the blue sky. "The band. When the mantle fractured and the evacuation fleets launched... I wonder if they got off Earth."

"If they did, they'd be old by now," Wyatt calculated, his mind running the chronological math. "If this album came out in 1995, and the twins were twenty back in '87... they'd be eighty-five years old today. Ryan would be eighty-eight. Cassidy would be eighty-five, and Holly would be eighty-three."

"Eighty-five," Sarah breathed, a bittersweet warmth in her voice. "I hope they made it to the Earth Remnant flotillas. I hope they're sitting on a transport ship somewhere out in the Perseus Arm, knowing their music is still playing on a brand new world."

"If they survived the 90s music industry and the end of the world, I'm sure they're doing just fine," Wyatt chuckled, pulling Sarah closer.

He looked around the sprawling living room. Leo was safely anchored by his fiercely protective sisters. Jack was diligently explaining the concept of a "tree" to Molly and Claire. And Evan and Tyler were safely growing, waiting to join the ranks.

The galaxy was vast, old, and scarred. But inside the impenetrable walls of The Sanctuary, the music was loud, the perimeter was perfectly secure, and the future was incredibly bright.
