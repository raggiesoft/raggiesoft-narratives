# Part 3: The Gestation Protocol – Friday, October 28, 2058 – 18:00 (Local Eden Time)

The nine-month gestation cycle of the Genesis Ten was not a period of waiting; it was a period of absolute, highly coordinated planetary construction.

By the third month of the cycle, the spring thaw had flooded the valley floor, but the durasteel foundations of the Genesis village held perfectly. Up on the elevated plateau, the skeleton of the new town was rapidly taking shape.

Wyatt and his eight-year-old Site Foreman, Ellie, stood on the newly laid timber decking of the primary communal hall. The air was filled with the sharp, clean scent of milled Eden pine.

"Drones three and four, adjust the pitch by two degrees," Ellie commanded, her dark eyes narrowing critically as a pair of heavy-lifters lowered a massive roof truss into place. She tapped her titanium multi-tool against her thigh. "Alignment must be flawless."

"You heard the Commander," Wyatt grinned, adjusting the telemetry on his datapad.

A few yards away, five-year-old Tyler and Evan were functioning as the ultimate logistical support team. The identical twins were hauling heavy, molecularly printed crates of durasteel rivets across the decking. Their dense, muscular frames absorbed the immense weight effortlessly.

"We are maintaining a steady pace, Commander," Tyler reported, his deep voice carrying over the hum of the drones as he set his crate down with controlled precision.

"Kinetic output is perfectly regulated," Evan added, mirroring his brother's stance.

"Strewth, you blokes are making short work of that timber!" Aria’s bright, energetic voice echoed from Wyatt's comm-link. For three months, the AI had exclusively utilized her Sydney, New South Wales phonetic matrix, ensuring the ten embryos developing in the vault were constantly bathed in the rolling, sunlit cadence. "Just calling down to give you an update on the little rippers. We’re at week fourteen. Neural pathways are firing beautifully. They're already starting to hear my voice through the amniotic fluid. Hard yakka pays off, mates."

"Keep talking to them, Aria," Wyatt smiled, wiping sweat from his brow. "We'll have their houses ready."

By the sixth month, the focus shifted from the external architecture of the village to the internal logistics of the mountain.

Inside the climate-controlled hydroponics bay, Nora was leaning heavily against a reinforced steel planter box, having holstered her titanium crutches in the magnetic clips Wyatt had installed along the walls. Using the microscopic scalpels from her birthday grafting kit, she was meticulously splicing a resilient strain of Earth ivy into the broad-leafed Eden ferns.

Sarah worked beside her, harvesting the first crop of easily digestible, nutrient-dense vegetables they would soon need to puree for the infants.

"The cross-pollination is holding, Mom," Nora smiled, her empathetic eyes tracking the successful graft. She didn't want to expend the vocal energy required to push past her slight speech impediment, so she tapped her forearm AAC. "The nursery will have beautiful greenery. It will help clean the air and provide a calming sensory environment for the babies."

"It's brilliant work, Nora," Sarah praised, kissing her daughter's temple.

Down the hall in the library, the academic curriculum was being fiercely debated.

Eight-year-old Jack sat at the holographic glass table, organizing a massive digital queue of early-childhood developmental media. Molly and Claire, the brilliant six-year-old twins, were actively reviewing the files.

"The phonetic structure of this Earth reading program is adequate," Claire analyzed, swiping through a virtual textbook. "However, Aria will need to adapt the audio to match the Oceania regional dialect to prevent linguistic confusion."

"Agreed," Molly nodded sharply. "Consistency is key for early language acquisition. We must ensure their auditory environment remains perfectly aligned with Sydney parameters."

"I'll make sure Aria runs the translation algorithm," Jack promised, his diplomatic mind already anticipating the needs of his future students. "They're going to feel right at home."

By the eighth month, the Vanguard initiated their final tactical review.

The twelve-year-old triplets took the elevator down to the frigid staging bay on Sub-Level 6. The ten artificial wombs were no longer just glowing cylinders of amber fluid; the physical forms of five boys and five girls were entirely visible, floating peacefully as the automated life-support matrices pulsed like mechanical heartbeats.

Leo sat in his titanium Daily Driver, the heavy, pneumatic bladders of his compression vest inflated to provide the exact, crushing pressure his changing teenage nervous system required. Nora stood flush against his right wheel, Ellie locked onto his left shoulder.

Leo’s dark eyes scanned the biometric data cascading across the primary console. The growth curves were mathematically perfect. He reached down to his lap tray.

"The biological mass is optimal. The geometric symmetry of their development is flawless," Leo’s deep, synthesized baritone echoed in the cold room. "They are fully formed."

"They are beautiful," Nora’s AAC chimed warmly, her empathetic heart swelling as she looked at the ten sleeping lives.

Ellie didn't look at the data; she looked at the room itself, visualizing the exact pathways they would use to transport the infants upstairs.

"Extraction routes are clear," Ellie finalized. "Incubators in the nursery are powered and holding at thirty-seven degrees. The perimeter is ready."

And then, the final day arrived.

It was 18:00 on the evening before the decanting. The entire Colton family was gathered in the living room. The localized geothermal fireplace cast a warm, flickering glow across the stone floor. No one was reading. No one was wrestling. The anticipation in the air was so thick it was almost physical.

Wyatt was sitting on the sofa with Sarah, his arm wrapped tightly around her. Jack, Tyler, Evan, Molly, and Claire were sitting in a semi-circle on the plush rug. The Vanguard was stationed by the hydro-glass windows, staring out at the darkening valley where the completed timber roofs of Genesis caught the last rays of the setting sun.

The acoustic array chimed softly.

"Righto, Colton squad. Listen up," Aria’s voice filled the room, the bright Sydney accent carrying a note of profound, quiet reverence. "We are T-minus twelve hours. The artificial wombs have officially locked their atmospheric seals. I am initiating the final, gradual fluid drain sequence to simulate the birth transition."

Sarah let out a shaky breath, leaning her head against Wyatt’s shoulder.

"The biometric markers are perfectly stable," Aria continued softly. "Get a good night's sleep, mates. Because tomorrow morning... we're going to have ten new voices in the mountain."

Jack looked up at his parents, his bright eyes wide with absolute wonder. Tyler and Evan shared a solemn, heavy nod. The Vanguard remained perfectly still, a unified, unbreakable shield standing watch over the valley.

The nine-month wait was over. The Genesis Ten were arriving at dawn.
