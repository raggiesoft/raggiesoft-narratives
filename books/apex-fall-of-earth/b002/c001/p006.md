# Part 6: The Washroom – Thursday, October 20, 2044 – ~04:30

Sarah dropped her canvas duffel bag on the floor next to Bunk 42-C, clutching her hygiene kit and a thin towel to her chest. She stepped back out into the narrow, 64-inch aisle of the barracks, her heart hammering against her ribs.

The air grew heavy and humid as she approached the archway to the communal washroom. Harsh, sickly yellow-green light spilled out onto the concrete floor, accompanied by the echoing sound of failing plumbing.

She stepped inside and froze.

The room was a massive, tiled concrete box with a long, rusted industrial drain running right down the center. There were no stalls. No partitions. No cheap plastic curtains.

A dozen veterans from the earlier shift were standing under the weak, sputtering showerheads. Men and women were mixed completely together, methodically scrubbing the pale desert dust and industrial grime off their skin. They were dead-eyed and entirely apathetic. Modesty was a luxury Apex clearly didn't provide.

A wave of profound nausea washed over her. Back home, her bedroom door hadn't even had a lock, but the thought of being walked in on had always been a nightmare. Retreating instinctively, Sarah hunched over and backed into the darkest corner of the room. She pressed her back against the damp, dingy tiles, crossing her arms tightly over her chest, trying desperately to protect whatever little dignity she had left.

Her eyes were wide with a mixture of shock, vulnerability, and profound regret. The recruiter's lies about collaborative environments and safety protocols vanished, replaced by the crushing realization that she was nothing more than inventory.

As she stood there shivering, another contractor walked in. He was massive, standing well over six feet tall, his face and arms marked by thick, weathered scars. Sarah tensed, pressing herself harder against the cold tiles, bracing for the leering gaze or the crude comment she expected from the hardened mercenaries that populated the facility.

But the man didn't even blink.

He kept his eyes respectfully fixed on the cracked tile floor, moving toward an empty, dripping pipe. He didn't stare. He didn't offer a pitying glance or try to reassure her—somehow knowing that acknowledging her panic would just make it worse. He simply stepped under his own rusted showerhead and deliberately turned his broad back to the room, entirely shielding her from his line of sight.

It was a small, silent gesture, but in the hostile, subterranean bunker, it felt like a lifeline.

Taking a shaky breath, Sarah forced herself to move. She quickly stepped under the nearest sputtering showerhead, twisting the squeaking valve. Freezing, metallic-smelling water hit her shoulders, washing away the sweat of the transport shuttle.

Ten minutes later, she was dressed back in her clean slate-blue Helios uniform, the bright fabric feeling less like a mark of prestige and more like a target. She stepped out of the humid washroom, clutching her damp towel, and followed the neon signs pointing toward the Mess Hall.
