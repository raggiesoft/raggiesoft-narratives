# Part 3: The Transfer – Thursday, October 20, 2044 – 08:00 (Local Spaceport Time)

The TSIFP transport glided into the docking cradle at the Las Vegas Interplanetary Spaceport with a gentle, barely perceptible shudder. The docking mechanisms locked into place with a muffled, hydraulic sigh, and the soft amber cabin lights shifted to a bright, welcoming white.

"Ladies and gentlemen, welcome to Earth," the captain announced, his voice warm over the intercom. "Local time is 0800 hours. Please disembark safely and proceed to your atmospheric transfers."

Sarah unbuckled her safety harness and stood up in the aisle. The moment she was fully upright, a sudden, heavy pressure pulled at her limbs. Earth's natural gravity. It was denser, more unyielding than the artificial gravity on the ship or the failing terraform generators of her home colony. It made her canvas duffel bag feel ten pounds heavier, but instead of feeling oppressive, the weight felt incredibly solid. It was grounding. Real.

She walked down the boarding bridge and stepped into a bright, airy terminal. Large, pristine windows let in the brilliant Nevada sunlight, reflecting off polished floors. Everything was orderly and serene. Digital monitors overhead displayed the familiar Helios intersecting-arc logo in soft gold and slate-blue, guiding the arriving researchers and technicians toward their connecting flights.

Smiling personnel in crisp, clean uniforms stood at the intersections, answering questions and directing traffic. It felt less like a military spaceport and more like a high-end academic campus.

Sarah followed the signs for "Atmospheric Transfers," walking with a light, eager step until she reached the Department of Defense security checkpoint. It was a mandatory federal bottleneck—a row of thick glass booths and heavy turnstiles manned by uniformed military officers.

There was no shouting, no shoving. The line of Helios personnel moved with quiet efficiency.

When it was Sarah's turn, she stepped up to the yellow line and proudly held up her new identification card. The DoD officer didn't ask her any questions or scrutinize her uniform. He simply took the badge and swiped the gray C0-BASIC stripe through a standard federal reader. The machine chirped a pleasant, immediate green.

The officer handed the badge back with a polite nod and waved her through the turnstile.

On the other side of the glass, the climate-controlled concourse opened up to a series of private, contractor-only gates. Parked on the sun-drenched tarmac just outside the terminal was a sleek atmospheric shuttle. It bore the elegant livery of Desert Star Aerospace—a pristine white hull accented with soft teal and silver sweeping lines.

A Desert Star flight attendant stood at the base of the boarding ramp, checking manifests on a digital tablet with a welcoming smile.

"Good morning," the attendant said cheerfully as Sarah approached. "Desert Star service to the Nevada Research Facility. May I see your seat assignment?"

Sarah showed her datapad.

"Perfect. Row four, window seat. Welcome to Earth, Miss Hayes. Please watch your step."

Sarah climbed the ramp and stepped into the shuttle. The interior was spotless, cool, and incredibly quiet. The seats were heavily padded and smelled of fresh upholstery. She found her window seat, stowed her duffel bag, and settled in, buckling her harness. The safety briefing was delivered in a calm, soothing voice, emphasizing the comfort and security of the passengers.

A few minutes later, the atmospheric thrusters engaged with a smooth, even hum. The shuttle lifted off the tarmac effortlessly, banking away from the glittering sprawl of Las Vegas and out toward the endless, sun-baked expanse of the Nevada desert.

Sarah leaned her head against the cool, vibration-free glass, looking down at the rugged, red-brown mountains and sweeping valleys. To her, it didn't look like a harsh, unforgiving wasteland. It looked like a pristine frontier of science and discovery. She folded her hands in her lap, her heart full of bright, optimistic anticipation as the Desert Star shuttle carried her deeper into the desert.
