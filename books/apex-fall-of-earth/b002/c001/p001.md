# Part 1: First Flight – Mid-October 2044 (Days prior to arrival)

The Tri-Sector Interstellar Freight & Personnel terminal was the cleanest room Sarah Hayes had ever stood in.

She stood near the edge of the massive, pristine observation window, her small hands tightly gripping the strap of her canvas duffel bag. At eighteen years old, she had never been off-world. Her home planet was a fading, terrestrial agricultural colony where the soil had given out a decade ago, leaving behind rusted silos and empty towns. When her parents passed away, there was no extended family to take her in, and the local economy offered nothing but dead ends.

She had just wanted a bed to sleep in. She had wanted a future.

Sarah looked down at her clothes, smoothing out a wrinkle in the crisp, breathable fabric. She wasn't wearing hand-me-downs anymore. She was wearing the official uniform of the Helios Geoscience Institute: slate-blue with soft gold trim. Neatly pinned to her chest, right below a stylized planetary-arc logo, was a nametape that read *HAYES*.

She was a Field Safety Technician. It sounded important. It sounded safe. The Helios recruiter had promised an academic environment, collaborative research, and a company culture that valued its people. For a terrified teenager with absolutely no safety net, it felt like she had just won the lottery.

A soft, melodic chime echoed through the quiet terminal, pulling her attention back to the boarding gate.

"Now boarding all passengers for Earth transit," a calm, professional voice announced over the intercom. "Please have your identification ready."

Sarah took a deep, steadying breath and joined the orderly queue. There was no shouting, no pushing, and no sense of panic. The other passengers in line were a mix of field researchers, technicians, and scientists, all speaking in hushed, polite tones.

Through the glass, she could see her TSIFP transport. It was a beautiful ship, its gleaming white hull accented with sweeping lines of gold and blue. It didn't look like an industrial freighter; it looked like a vessel of discovery.

When she reached the front of the line, a uniformed flight attendant offered her a warm, reassuring smile.

"Good morning," the attendant said gently. "Badge, please?"

Sarah fumbled with the lanyard around her neck, holding up her newly issued Department of Defense identification card. It had a gray stripe across the top and the words *C0-BASIC* printed in black block letters. She didn't really understand how the military clearance system worked, but it didn't matter. She was part of the team.

The attendant tapped the card against a sleek, modern scanner. It chimed a pleasant green.

"Welcome aboard, Miss Hayes. Your seat is located in section four. Have a wonderful flight."

"Thank you," Sarah breathed out, a massive wave of relief finally washing over her.

She stepped through the boarding bridge and into the cabin. The interior was softly lit, the air smelling faintly of lavender and sanitized climate control. She found her assigned seat—plush, wide, and perfectly comfortable—and stowed her duffel bag carefully in the overhead bin.

As she sat down and clicked her safety harness into place, she looked out the small, perfectly clear window at the fading agricultural sprawl of her home world. There was nothing left down there for her. No family, no attachments, and no reason to look back.

The sub-light engines hummed to life, a gentle, barely noticeable vibration that vibrated softly through the padded seat.

Sarah rested her hands in her lap, her posture finally relaxing as the ship lifted off the tarmac, carrying her up through the atmosphere and into the quiet dark of space. She closed her eyes, a small, genuine smile touching her lips. She was safe. She was finally going to be part of something good.
