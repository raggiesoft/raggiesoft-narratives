# Book 4: The First Generation

## Chapter 1: The Architect

### Part 1: The Tolerances of Eden – Monday, September 4, 2045 – 10:00

Sub-Level 2 smelled sharply of vaporized ozone and fresh, fragrant alien pine sap.

Wyatt Colton stood at the center of the Fabrication Foundry, covered in a fine layer of sawdust, the heavy safety goggles pushed up onto his forehead. He was wearing his faded canvas work pants and nothing else, his heavily muscled torso slick with sweat from three straight hours of manual labor.

He ran a thick, calloused hand over the top rail of the heavy-timber crib sitting on the workbench. It was flawlessly smooth.

He didn’t trust the automated molecular printers for this. The printers were perfect for titanium dreadnought plating and memory-foam mattresses, but for the beds his children were going to sleep in, Wyatt’s mechanic brain demanded tactile control. He had felled the trees in the valley himself, hauled the timber into the mountain, and milled every single board by hand.

He picked up a set of digital calipers from the metal tray, his jaw locking into a mask of absolute, unyielding focus. He wedged the calipers between two of the vertical wooden slats.

"Sixty millimeters," Wyatt muttered to himself, reading the glowing digital display. He moved down the line, checking the gap between the next two slats. "Sixty millimeters."

He tested every single gap. If a slat was off by a fraction of a millimeter, he pulled it out and sanded it down again. Aria’s pediatric safety parameters were permanently burned into his mind, and on Planet Eden, there was a zero-percent margin for error.

The heavy, brushed-steel doors of the foundry hissed open.

Wyatt instantly set the calipers down and turned around, his protective instincts flaring before his conscious mind even registered the sound.

Sarah rolled smoothly into the industrial workspace in her custom brushed-aluminum wheelchair. The frictionless bearings made her approach completely silent.

Wyatt’s breath hitched in his chest, exactly the way it did every single time he looked at her lately.

At nearly twenty weeks along, the triplet pregnancy had fundamentally altered her physical reality. The loose, molecularly printed cotton tunic she wore draped perfectly over the massive, undeniable swell of her abdomen. Because her body was carrying three distinct life-support systems, she looked like she was in the final days of a standard singleton pregnancy.

But her color was excellent. The terrifying, violently dehydrating bouts of hyperemesis gravidarum from the summer had finally faded, replaced by a radiant, deeply exhausted calm.

"I thought I heard the saws stop," Sarah said, bringing the wheelchair to a smooth halt near the edge of the workbench and engaging the brakes. She rested her hands naturally over the top of her bump. "You've been down here since breakfast, Wyatt."

"I hit a rhythm," Wyatt replied, grabbing a clean rag to wipe the sawdust from his hands and chest before he closed the distance between them.

He dropped to one knee right beside her chair, putting himself exactly at eye level with her. He reached out, his massive hands gently enveloping hers where they rested on her stomach. Beneath the soft fabric, he could feel the incredible, taut firmness of her skin.

"How are the structural foundations holding up upstairs?" Wyatt asked, his dark eyes scanning her face for any hidden signs of pain or fatigue. "Any dizziness? Contractions?"

"No dizziness. The foundations are just heavy," Sarah smiled, letting out a soft, tired exhale. "Ellie and Nora are having a kickboxing tournament against my ribs, and Leo is currently using my bladder as a pillow. But we're good. I just missed you."

Wyatt felt a sharp, sudden kick against the palm of his hand. His eyes widened slightly, a profound, overwhelming wave of awe washing over him. It didn't matter how many times he felt it; the physical proof of his children moving beneath his hands short-circuited his S.H.O.P. Marine conditioning every single time.

He leaned forward, pressing a tender kiss to her forehead.

"I'm almost done with the first one," Wyatt promised, standing back up and gesturing toward the massive wooden structure on the table. "I wanted you to inspect the prototype."

Sarah leaned forward in her chair, her dark eyes tracking the beautiful, heavy-timber craftsmanship. It was sturdy, completely devoid of sharp edges, and looked like it had been grown directly from the mountain rather than manufactured.

"Wyatt, it's beautiful," Sarah breathed, genuinely moved by the sheer amount of care poured into the wood. "It's so solid."

"It has to be," Wyatt stated, his tone dropping into a serious, pragmatic rumble. "I countersunk all the hardware and capped it with flush timber plugs. There is no exposed steel anywhere on the frame. The memory-foam mattress Aria printed fits with a zero-millimeter tolerance against the rails. Nothing gets trapped. Nothing gets pinched."

He looked from the crib to Sarah's expanding stomach. The timeline was closing in fast.

"I'll have the other two finished and assembled side-by-side in the nursery by the end of the week," Wyatt said, running a hand through his dark hair. He looked around the massive, echoing foundry. "Then I need to start engineering the highchairs, the automated diaper-reclamation unit, and baby-proofing the hydroponics bays. The entire valley is a hazard zone, Sarah."

Sarah let out a bright, ringing laugh, reaching out to grab his hand and pull him back to her side. She could see his mechanic's brain spiraling into a logistical panic over the safety of the entire planet.

"Wyatt, stop," Sarah smiled, her thumb stroking his knuckles. "They aren't going to be sprinting into the river the day they're born. They aren't going to need highchairs for six months. We have time."

Wyatt looked down at her, his broad shoulders dropping a fraction of a centimeter as her calm logic cut through his anxiety. He let out a low, gravelly chuckle.

"I just want the perimeter secure," Wyatt murmured, looking back at the empty, waiting crib. "We're bringing three lives into an empty world, Sarah. I just want to make sure the world is ready for them."

"You already made it ready, Wyatt," Sarah promised, her voice fiercely certain. "You claimed the mountain. You built the walls. Now, you just have to finish the cribs."

### Part 2: The Silent Scout – Tuesday, September 5, 2045 – 14:00

Down on Sub-Level 3, the buried Apex dreadnought functioned as the ultimate, heavily armored nervous system for the entire mountain estate.

Wyatt sat in the center of the Combat Information Center (CIC), the primary tactical holotable illuminating his scarred face in a wash of cool blue light. He had spent the last two months aggressively hardening the planet's defense grid. The absurd visit from the tax collectors had been a wake-up call. The galaxy still knew these coordinates existed, and Wyatt refused to leave his pregnant wife and their three unborn children exposed.

He was in the middle of routing auxiliary power to the localized repulsor-shields when the CIC’s ambient lighting instantly snapped from blue to a harsh, flashing crimson.

A high-pitched, oscillating klaxon cut through the quiet hum of the servers.

"Alert, Administrator," Aria’s synthesized voice echoed sharply, completely devoid of her usual warmth. "Proximity sensors have detected an unauthorized orbital breach. A high-velocity, unmanned telemetry probe has just dropped out of sub-light transit. It is currently establishing a geosynchronous orbit directly above our hemisphere."

Wyatt’s S.H.O.P. Marine instincts flared instantly. He slammed his heavy hand onto the console, bringing up the optical telemetry.

Hovering on the screen was a sleek, matte-black satellite bristling with high-gain sensor arrays and deep-core scanning lasers. It had no identifying insignia, no national flags, and no municipal registry codes.

"Is it from the water-world?" Wyatt demanded, his jaw locking tight. "Did the tax collectors send a drone?"

"Negative, Wyatt," Aria reported, her data streams scrolling rapidly across the glass. "The metallurgical composition and propulsion signature do not match municipal government tech. This is a Tier-1 corporate mass-surveyor. It is currently executing a deep-core geological sweep. It is mapping the valley’s raw timber density, the freshwater aquifers, and the subterranean mineral deposits."

Wyatt’s blood went ice cold.

It wasn't a bureaucratic ledger algorithm. It was a corporate scout. Some massive, nameless conglomerate was dragging the outer sectors looking for fresh planets to strip-mine, harvest, and burn to ash, exactly like they had done to Earth.

"Not this planet," Wyatt growled, his deep voice vibrating with absolute, lethal authority. "Open all standard hailing frequencies. Wideband."

"Frequencies open and broadcasting, Wyatt."

Wyatt leaned over the console, staring at the glowing red icon of the probe.

"Unidentified surveyor," Wyatt barked into the comms, his voice ringing with the iron-clad authority of a planetary sovereign. "You are violating the restricted airspace of the independent world of Eden. Cease your geological scans immediately and withdraw from orbit, or you will be fired upon. Acknowledge."

He waited. The only sound in the CIC was the hum of the dreadnought’s reactors.

"The probe is receiving our transmission, Wyatt," Aria confirmed. "But it is ignoring the handshake protocol. It has increased its scanning resolution. It is currently attempting to ping the mountain's power grid."

They were looking for the source of the heat signature. They were looking for his home.

Wyatt didn't hesitate. He wasn't a diplomat, and he wasn't going to negotiate with a greedy algorithm designed to strip a planet bare. Up on the residential level, Sarah was resting in their bed, carrying the entire future of their bloodline.

"Warning phase is over," Wyatt stated, his dark eyes narrowing into a deadly glare. "Aria, cycle the dreadnought's primary dorsal plasma batteries. Route targeting control to my console."

"Plasma batteries online and charged to maximum yield. Targeting lock acquired," Aria confirmed instantly.

Wyatt looked at the tactical map. The probe was hovering exactly 400 kilometers above the surface, completely unaware that the "empty" rock it was scanning possessed a buried military warship.

"Get off my lawn," Wyatt whispered.

He punched the heavy, glowing red execution rune on the glass table.

Miles above the mountain, the camouflaged peak of The Sanctuary parted slightly. A massive, concentrated beam of superheated blue plasma erupted from the dreadnought’s concealed cannons. It tore through the atmosphere in a fraction of a second, perfectly striking the exact coordinates of the surveyor.

On the holotable, the red icon of the probe didn't even flash. It simply ceased to exist, vaporized into a cloud of harmless, molten slag that immediately began burning up in the upper thermosphere.

"Target neutralized," Aria reported calmly, the CIC lighting shifting smoothly from crimson back to a calm, secure blue. "Orbital airspace is clear."

Wyatt let out a slow, heavy exhale, leaning back in the command chair.

He had successfully swatted the fly, but the reality of the situation settled heavily onto his broad shoulders. If a corporate probe had found them, eventually, a corporate harvesting fleet might follow.

He looked at the planetary grid hovering above the table. He couldn't just rely on shooting them down; he needed to make sure they couldn't see the planet in the first place.

"Aria," Wyatt commanded, his mechanic's brain rapidly shifting from defense to stealth. "I need to completely mask our thermal and electromagnetic footprint. I want to pull the dreadnought's cloaking tech and expand it. We are going to build a planetary-scale dampening field."

"That will require a massive reallocation of the Fab-Foundry's resources and significant subterranean engineering, Wyatt," Aria calculated.

"I don't care," Wyatt replied, pushing himself out of the chair. "I'm not letting a megacorporation anywhere near my family. Lock down the grid, Aria. I have to go check on Sarah, and then I'm getting back to work."

The automated medical wing on Sub-Level 1 was a sanctuary of brilliant white LED light and perfectly filtered, sterile air.

Wyatt Colton sat on a rolling metal stool beside the primary diagnostic bio-bed, his massive shoulders hunched forward as his dark eyes locked onto the high-definition ultrasound monitor. "Twenty weeks," Wyatt breathed, his voice a low, gravelly whisper of pure reverence. "They're getting huge."

"Each subject currently measures approximately one hundred and sixty millimeters from crown to rump. Their skeletal structures are hardening, and their cardiac rhythms are perfectly synchronized and incredibly strong."

On the screen, the three distinct, glowing nodes had transformed into undeniably human shapes.

Wyatt watched in absolute awe as the transducer caught the profile of the firstborn—Ellie. She was incredibly active, her tiny arms pushing against the boundaries of her amniotic sac. A few centimeters over, Nora was resting quietly, her hands curled near her face. And tucked safely near the bottom of the screen, Wyatt saw the unmistakable profile of his son, Leo, executing a slow, sleepy roll.

"They're perfect," Sarah smiled, tears of profound relief welling in her dark eyes. After the terrifying bouts of hyperemesis gravidarum early in the summer, seeing them thriving and completely healthy was the ultimate reward. "They're really okay."

"They're more than okay," Wyatt agreed fiercely. He reached out, his thick fingers gently tracing the glowing outline of his son on the monitor's glass. "They're Colton tough."

But as he looked at their tiny, vulnerable forms, the protective instincts that had been redlining in his brain since the orbital probe incident two weeks ago spiked hard. They were perfectly safe inside the bio-bed, but he was making absolutely sure they stayed safe once they were born.

Sarah wiped the gel from her stomach with a sterile cloth and pulled her tunic back down. She looked over at Wyatt, immediately reading the heavy, calculating shift in his dark eyes.

"You're thinking about the foundry again," Sarah noted, swinging her legs over the edge of the bed and leaning against his broad shoulder. "How is the dampener coming?"

Wyatt let out a long, heavy exhale, wrapping his arm securely around her waist.

"It's massive, Sarah," Wyatt admitted, his mechanic's brain shifting gears to the staggering logistical reality of what he was building beneath their feet. He wasn't just building a cloaking device; he was wrapping the entire planet in an impenetrable, electromagnetic vault.

"The planetary dampener will completely scrub Eden's thermal, biological, and electrical signatures from deep-space scans," Wyatt explained, his voice taking on the uncompromising tone of a fortress commander. "To the rest of the galaxy, this coordinates will look like a dead, frozen rock. But on top of that, I'm layering a localized kinetic deflector shield."

"A shield gate," Sarah clarified, her analytical mind perfectly grasping the architecture.

"Exactly," Wyatt nodded. "If another corporate probe, or a mercenary cutter, or a tax collector physically blunders into our atmosphere, they are going to hit a wall of solid hard-light energy. The only way through is a single, heavily fortified shield gate positioned directly above this estate. And the only way that gate opens is if they identify themselves and I personally authorize the handshake."

Sarah looked at him, completely understanding the sheer exhaustion lining his face. He had been working twenty-hour days in the foundry to get the system operational. It was a staggering drain on their immediate resources, effectively pausing any luxury construction in the estate.

"It's a lot of production," Sarah said softly, resting her hand against his bearded cheek. "But I completely agree with you. With the three of them coming so soon... better safe than sorry. We lock the front door."

"We lock it tight," Wyatt confirmed, leaning into her touch. "But I didn't hardcode it to just this mountain. I designed the matrix to be modular."

Sarah raised an eyebrow. "Modular? For what?"

Wyatt looked down at the floor, thinking of the Genesis Vault buried miles beneath them in the freezing dark.

"For them," Wyatt said, his voice softening. "Right now, it's just us and the triplets. One house, one gate. But in twelve years, when we wake up Cohort Alpha... and in thirty years, when the population starts expanding across the valley... we're going to need more than one door."

He looked back up at her, a fierce, visionary pride in his eyes.

"When the time comes, we can expand the grid," Wyatt promised. "We can open new shield gates over the agricultural sectors, or the new cities our kids are going to build. The system will grow exactly as fast as our population does. The Colton family holds the master keys, but the shield protects everyone."

Sarah smiled, a deep, resonant warmth filling her chest. He wasn't just acting like a paranoid soldier; he was thinking like a sovereign ruler, planning a safe, secure future for fifty thousand sleeping souls.

"You're a good architect, Wyatt," Sarah whispered, kissing him gently.

"I'm just protecting my investments," Wyatt smirked, resting his hand back on her twenty-week bump. "Now, let's get you back upstairs. You need calories, and I need to go finish building the biggest lock in the galaxy."

### Part 5: The Dead Rock – Thursday, October 12, 2045 – 02:15
