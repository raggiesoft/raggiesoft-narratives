# Part 4: The Grind – Thursday, October 20, 2044 – ~08:00

Wyatt stepped back out of the maintenance closet and into the deafening hum of Sector 4. The heavy, lead-lined door clicked shut behind them, and the internal tracker on his standard-issue PDA violently pinged back to life, the warning yellow fading into a compliant, steady green. They were back on the grid.

For the next three hours, the patrol was agonizingly boring. This was the true reality of Apex Defense Solutions. Between the brief moments of sheer corporate terror, it was just endless, repetitive walking along rusted catwalks, checking the same pressure valves, and swiping his ID at the same security checkpoints. The adrenaline wore off, leaving behind a dull, heavy ache in his legs and lower back.

As they rounded the corner of a massive filtration tank, Sarah let out a heavy, exhausted sigh.

"I was working the front desk at a recreation center back home before I signed this contract," she muttered over the drone of the scrubbers, kicking at a loose bolt on the metal grating. "The hourly rate was garbage, but at least the worst thing I had to deal with was a jammed turnstile or a missing locker key. They promised us a massive hazard premium for this deployment. Seeing those supervisors panicking... whatever they're paying us, it isn't nearly enough to deal with this."

Wyatt stopped walking. He turned around and looked at her. He wasn't angry, but his expression was intensely serious.

"Sarah," he said quietly.

She stopped, her flashlight beam dropping to the floor. She looked up at him.

"I'm going to give you the best rule for surviving a place like this," he told her, keeping his voice steady and gentle so she knew it wasn't a reprimand. "Never complain about the pay out loud. Every single contractor on this base is underpaid, in debt, and trapped. We all know we're getting screwed. Reminding everyone of that doesn't fix the paycheck; it just makes the shift feel twice as long and paints a target on your back. The red-banded supervisors will write you up for insubordination, and the veterans will just hate you for whining."

She blinked in the dim light. The frustration immediately drained out of her posture, replaced by a quick, sharp understanding. She realized he wasn't scolding her to toe the corporate line; he was protecting her from making a rookie mistake in front of the wrong people.

"Right," she said softly, squaring her shoulders. "Keep it quiet. I understand."

Wyatt held her gaze for a second longer, making sure it really sank in, then gave her a firm, approving nod. "You're doing good. Let's keep moving."

He turned back around, his flashlight cutting through the smog, and continued the patrol. Behind him, Sarah's footsteps fell right into rhythm with his. She didn't say another word about the credits, the contract, or the boredom.

She was coachable, and she genuinely wanted to pull her weight. She wanted to earn his trust just as much as he had earned hers. Wyatt had been chained to plenty of lousy, unpredictable partners in previous hellholes—bitter veterans who would just as soon sell you out for an extra ration or leave you behind at the first sign of trouble. But as he listened to her steady footsteps matching his pace, he realized that despite her young age and complete lack of experience, he could actually trust her. Having a partner who listened and respected him made this particular black site feel just a little bit more survivable.

He was glad she was the one who had walked through that turnstile.
