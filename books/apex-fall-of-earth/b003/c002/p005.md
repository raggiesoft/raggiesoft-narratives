# Part 5: The Anchor – Friday, October 21, 2044 – ~03:00

Wyatt followed Sarah into the dense, rusted labyrinth of the outer filtration banks. The air here was noticeably cooler, choked with a thick layer of pale Nevada dust that the automated scrubbers had long since stopped cleaning. The heavy lead shielding in the walls created a dead zone, and the ambient roar of the deep-core drills faded to a dull, distant throb.

As they rounded a massive, cylindrical water-reclamation tank, Sarah suddenly froze, her hand shooting up in a sharp fist. Wyatt stopped instantly, his heavy boots making zero sound on the grating.

Up ahead, a heavy maintenance hatch was jammed ajar, spilling a wedge of sterile white light across the dusty floor.

Wyatt pressed his back against the curved metal of the tank, sliding up right behind Sarah. He looked over her shoulder through the narrow gap. Two high-level Apex scientists in pristine white lab coats were standing in the corridor, completely ignoring the "RESTRICTED" signs on the bulkheads.

"I am telling you, the thermal limits are breached!" the first scientist hissed, running a trembling hand through his hair. "The nitrogen flood didn't stabilize the cavern. It just delayed the expansion. Whatever the deep-mantle array woke up down there, it's pushing right through the bedrock layer."

"Keep your voice down," the second scientist snapped, checking his datapad frantically. "Command locked the executive elevators. If you cause a panic among the S.H.O.P. contractors, the Supervisors have standing orders to shoot deserters. We just need to keep the containment field up until the Board sends an evac shuttle."

"They aren't sending a shuttle!" the first one fired back, his voice cracking. "We're a write-off!"

Wyatt looked down at Sarah. In the dim spill of his flashlight, her face was perfectly calm. She had already figured out everything they were panicking about. She gave him a subtle shake of her head. *Not our problem. Keep moving.* Wyatt nodded. If their PDAs reconnected to the grid and flagged them for loitering near an unauthorized lab, the guards would be on them in minutes. They both slipped away in total silence, leaving the doomed scientists to their argument, and pushed deeper into the dead zone of the filtration sector.

Ten minutes later, they found it: a small, dead-end alcove wedged between two massive copper coils. The cameras here had been ripped out years ago, and the lead-lined walls ensured their internal trackers showed up as a standard "signal glitch" on the Supervisor's monitors. It was completely off the grid.

Wyatt powered down his heavy Mark-IV flashlight, leaving only the dim, ambient emergency lighting to see by.

"Take a minute," he told her softly.

Sarah slid down the cold metal bulkhead, pulling her knees to her chest with a heavy, exhausted sigh. Wyatt sat down opposite her, resting his forearms on his knees. The silence between them was heavy, but it wasn't tense.

She rested her chin on her knees, looking across the narrow space at him. The corporate armor she wore out on the catwalks slowly melted away.

"You know why I signed that contract?" she asked, her voice barely above a whisper in the quiet alcove. "The Helios recruiter told me the acquisition guaranteed room and board. That was the whole pitch."

Wyatt listened, giving her his full attention.

"I'm an only child," Sarah continued, her eyes dropping to the scuffed toes of her boots. "My parents died a few years ago. I didn't have any extended family to lean on, no safety net. No boyfriend, no husband to help pay the rent. Just me. When the money completely ran out, I didn't have anywhere else to go. I literally just wanted a bed to sleep in. And Apex gave me thirty inches of a broken mattress on a concrete floor."

Hearing her say she had no boyfriend, no husband—that she was completely unattached—hit Wyatt with a sudden, sharp jolt of adrenaline that had nothing to do with the failing facility around them. He perked up internally, a profound sense of relief washing over him. But he kept his expression completely steady. She was opening up a deeply vulnerable part of her life to him, and the absolute last thing he was going to do was break that trust by making it weird.

Instead, he leaned back against the copper coil, letting out a low, empathetic breath.

"The recruiters prey on isolation," he told her gently. "They look for people who don't have anyone waiting for them. It makes us easier to disappear."

She looked up at him, searching his face.

"I don't have a family to go back to either, Sarah," Wyatt admitted, his voice a quiet, grounding rumble. "No wife back home, no girlfriend waiting for my contract to end. I’ve just bounced from one deep-sea rig and remote hellhole to the next, taking hazard pay because there was nothing else keeping me anywhere. I came here for the exact same reason you did. It was just another job."

In the dim light, he saw a subtle, undeniable shift in Sarah's posture. When he mentioned that he was single, her shoulders dropped the last fraction of an inch of tension. Her eyes widened slightly, and a sudden, warm light chased away the exhaustion in her expression. She had been wondering, hoping, and now she knew.

They looked at each other across the three feet of grated metal floor. The failing deep-core drills, the panicking scientists, the heavily armed guards—it all temporarily ceased to exist. There was a sudden, electric clarity between the two of them.

"Well," Sarah said softly, a small, incredibly genuine smile touching her lips. "I guess they were wrong about us being isolated."

"Yeah," Wyatt replied, a matching smirk breaking through his stoic exterior. "I guess they were."

She reached into her pocket, pulling out her battered Apex datapad. She looked at the screen, tapping the corner of the plastic casing.

"About the shift rotation," she said, her voice taking on a new, quiet certainty. "The HR error. If we tell the administration, they’re going to fix my schedule. They'll put me back on Shift 3."

"Which means you'd be patrolling with someone else," Wyatt finished for her, "and sleeping in the bunk while I'm out here."

"I'm not doing that," she said firmly, locking eyes with him. "We don't say a word to management. We stay on the same shift, and we share the bunk."

"Deal," Wyatt agreed instantly.

Sarah slipped the datapad back into her pocket. The thought of the crushing, claustrophobic barracks didn't fill her with dread anymore. In fact, for the first time since she arrived at the Nevada facility, she was actually looking forward to the twelve-hour klaxon. That cramped, thirty-inch Twin XL mattress wasn't a corporate punishment anymore. It was the only place she got to curl up against him, safe behind his shoulders, completely shielded from the nightmare outside.

"Come on," Wyatt said, pushing himself up off the metal grating and offering her his hand. "We still have a perimeter to walk."

Sarah took his hand, letting him pull her up. This time, she didn't let go quite as fast, her fingers lingering against his taped palm for a second longer than necessary.

They both clipped their flashlights back onto their belts and stepped out of the dead zone, back into the harsh reality of Sector 4. But as they resumed the patrol, walking shoulder-to-shoulder down the rusted catwalks, the heavy, oppressive atmosphere of the facility didn't feel quite so crushing.

They were in absolute sync. The tension of the unknown was gone, replaced by a quiet, steady comfort. Apex Defense Solutions might be cracking at the foundations, but as Wyatt listened to her footsteps falling perfectly in rhythm with his, they both knew exactly what they had to hold onto.
