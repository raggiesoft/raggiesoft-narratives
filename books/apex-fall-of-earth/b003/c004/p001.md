# Part 1: Structural Failure – Sunday, October 23, 2044 – 05:00 (PDA vibrates at 0500 hours)

The alarm on Wyatt's Level 1 PDA didn't wake him. He was already awake.

He was lying in the dark of the barricaded break room, staring up at the rusted ceiling panels. He was zipped into the heavy deployment sleeping bag, Sarah’s back pressed firmly against his chest, his arm wrapped tightly around her waist. She was still asleep, her breathing slow and even, but the room around them felt fundamentally wrong.

The low, ambient vibration of the facility had completely changed. It was no longer the steady, rhythmic pulse of the deep-core drills. The concrete floor beneath the vinyl sofa was shuddering in erratic, violent spasms. The heavy blast door rattled against the steel pipe wedged under the handle. The Apex staging facility felt like a submarine whose hull was actively groaning under crushing, impossible pressure.

Wyatt tightened his arm around Sarah, burying his face in her hair. He knew the drills weren't spinning anymore. Apex Defense Solutions didn't hit a pocket of gas; they woke something up, and the structural integrity of Sector 4 was rapidly failing.

At 0500 hours, his PDA vibrated against the floorboards.

Sarah stirred immediately. The heavy tremors vibrating through the sofa pulled her out of sleep faster than usual. She turned over within the cramped confines of the sleeping bag, her eyes finding his in the dim, strobing light of the failing emergency bulbs.

She didn't ask if everything was okay. She could feel the floor shaking.

"It's getting worse," she whispered, her hands resting flat against his chest.

"I know," Wyatt replied quietly, brushing a stray lock of hair behind her ear. "But the plan doesn't change. We keep our heads down, we get our biometrics scanned at breakfast, and we find our window to break the contract."

She nodded, leaning up to kiss him. It wasn't a lingering, lazy morning kiss. It was urgent, filled with a sharp, terrifying awareness that the clock was running out on this facility. Wyatt kissed her back, pouring every ounce of his protective resolve into her.

"Let's get out of here," she murmured against his lips.

They both slid out of the sleeping bag. The air in the room was stiflingly hot today, the failing environmental scrubbers no longer able to cycle the heat bleeding up from the lower levels.

Wyatt packed the sleeping bag into his canvas duffel. Sarah walked into the executive washroom to twist the shower valve, hoping for a quick blast of cold water to wake up. The pipes screamed violently, but nothing came out. Not a single drop. The main water lines had been severed somewhere deep in the Nevada bedrock.

She walked back out, meeting his eyes. Apex wasn't just failing; it was dying.

They both pulled on their stiff, black S.H.O.P. Marine armor. They zipped up the heavy collars, adjusted the nametapes—*COLTON* and the faded *HAYES*—on their chests, and clipped their heavy Mark-IV flashlights to their belts. They shared one last, long look in the quiet sanctuary that had kept them human for the last three days.

Wyatt stepped up to the heavy blast door and kicked the steel pipe loose.

When he heaved the door open, the stench of Sector 4 hit them like a physical wall. It smelled of melted slag, burning ozone, and that unmistakable, coppery scent of rotting blood. The ambient noise was a deafening, chaotic chorus of whining machinery and blaring hazard klaxons in the distance.

Wyatt took her hand, lacing his fingers firmly with hers. He didn't care about the cameras today. The facility was in a full-blown meltdown.

They navigated the maze of maintenance corridors, heading for the main elevators. The blind spots were completely choked with exhausted, broken bodies. S.H.O.P. Marines, deep-core miners, and high-level scientists in filthy white coats were huddled together in the shadows, staring blankly at the shuddering walls.

They reached the primary lift. It groaned agonizingly as it pulled them up to Sector 1.

When the metal doors ground open, they stepped out into a staging area that looked like a warzone prep camp. Wyatt and Sarah merged into the massive, surging crowd of black-and-yellow uniforms heading toward the Mess Hall. The panic in the air was thick enough to cut with a knife. Nobody was talking. Everyone was just trying to get to the food dispensers before the power grid completely failed.

Wyatt guided Sarah into the cavernous room, his broad shoulders acting as a wedge through the terrified crowd. The biometric wall dispensers were flickering wildly.

He pressed his thumb to the glass. It ground, stuttered, and finally registered a dull green *Clunk.* Sarah scanned her wrist right after him. The machines dispensed two misshapen, lukewarm blocks of gray nutrient paste.

They didn't even bother looking for a table. The Mess Hall was standing-room only, completely packed with thousands of contractors. The armed Supervisors weren't just standing by the blast doors anymore; they had formed a physical, heavily armored barricade across the primary exits, their combat shotguns raised to the low ready.

Sarah stepped flush against his side, her shoulder pressing into his chest. She looked up at him, her eyes wide. Management wasn't just terrified anymore. They were locking the facility down.

Before she could even unwrap her foil brick, the power grid surged violently, and the blinding *BE A MARINE™* recruitment billboards abruptly cut to black.
