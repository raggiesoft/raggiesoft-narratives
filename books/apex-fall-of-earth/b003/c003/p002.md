# Part 2: The Veterans – Saturday, October 22, 2044 – ~05:15

Wyatt and Sarah carried their dented metal trays away from the wall dispensers, scanning the packed Mess Hall for an empty spot. The deafening hum of the room was different today—more chaotic, underscored by the nervous energy of the two hundred new S.H.O.P. contractors who had arrived last night.

Wyatt spotted a narrow gap at the end of a bolted metal table. He steered Sarah toward it, using his broad shoulders to clear a path through the crushing crowd. They both sat down, dropping their foil-wrapped nutrient bricks onto the scratched metal.

Because Sarah was no longer wearing the bright slate-blue of a Helios FST, no one batted an eye at them sitting together. No one cared that her scavenged black armor was clearly a size too small, the stiff fabric pulling tight across her shoulders. For the first time in the Mess Hall, they weren't a glaring corporate violation. They could actually have a normal conversation without Wyatt having to play the part of the callous veteran.

"I miss the Helios uniform," Sarah murmured quietly, unwrapping her foil brick. She tugged uncomfortably at the tight collar of the heavy utility shirt. "The fabric breathed better, and it didn't weigh ten pounds."

Wyatt let out a low, genuine chuckle, keeping his voice just above the din of the room. "It makes you invisible to HR. Learn to love it."

Sitting directly across from them were two of the new recruits. Wyatt spotted them instantly. Their abrasive black and hazard-yellow Apex uniforms were completely pristine, lacking the permanent layer of pale Nevada dust and industrial grime that settled into the fabric after a single shift. But it was their eyes that really gave them away. They were wide, bloodshot, and darting frantically around the room. They were staring at the heavily armored Supervisors standing by the blast doors. They were staring at the flickering lights. They looked exactly like Sarah did on Day 1, only worse, because the facility’s baseline terror level had skyrocketed since then.

One of them—a skinny kid who looked barely nineteen, his nametape reading MILLER—was picking at his gray nutrient paste with a trembling plastic spork. He hadn't eaten a single bite.

"Did you see the guards at the turnstiles?" Miller whispered frantically to the girl sitting next to him, his voice cracking. "They have their hands on the triggers. And the barracks... there weren't even enough beds. People were sleeping in the aisles. This isn't an acquisition. It's a prison. We signed up for a prison."

The girl next to him was staring at her tray, her face pale green. "The recruiter said it was a standard security detail. He said the housing was guaranteed."

"He lied!" Miller hissed, panic visibly rising in his chest. "We have eighteen months of this. We're not going to make it. If the guards don't shoot us, whatever is shaking the floorboards is going to crush us. We need to go to the Sector Commander. We need to tell HR—"

"If you go to the Sector Commander," Wyatt interrupted, his voice a low, commanding rumble that cut effortlessly through the noise of the table. "He will write you up for insubordination, dock your first month's pay, and put you on latrine duty in Sector 2. And if you complain to HR, they'll just reassign you to the deepest maintenance shaft they can find."

Miller froze, his panicked eyes snapping up to Wyatt.

He took in Wyatt's 6'1" frame, the heavy scarring on his hands, and the absolute, stoic calm radiating from his posture. Then he looked at Sarah sitting right beside him. She wasn't shaking. She was eating her synthetic paste with methodical precision, completely unfazed by the armored guards or the groaning concrete. To these terrified recruits, Wyatt and Sarah looked like hardened, unbreakable veterans who had survived this hellhole for months.

They didn't need to know they had only stepped off the transport shuttle forty-eight hours ago.

"You want to survive your eighteen months?" Wyatt asked, leaning slightly forward over the scratched metal table.

Miller swallowed hard, nodding frantically.

"Then you keep your head down, and you toe the corporate line," Wyatt told him, his tone firm but entirely devoid of malice. "You do not ask questions about the drills. You do not stare at the Supervisors. When the klaxon rings, you walk your perimeter, and when it rings again, you find a piece of floor and you sleep. You become a ghost."

Sarah finished chewing a bite of her gray paste, her expression perfectly calm. She seamlessly picked up exactly where Wyatt left off.

"Apex wants you exhausted and terrified," Sarah said to the girl, her voice steady and grounded. It was a stunning transformation from the vulnerable rookie she was on the first day. "If you panic, you make mistakes, and mistakes down here get you killed. Eat your paste, even if it tastes like ash. You need the calories. Stick together, watch each other's backs, and don't expect management to save you."

The two recruits just stared at them both, completely hanging on every word. The hysterical edge of their panic slowly began to drain away, replaced by the grim, pragmatic reality they had just been handed. Wyatt had given them the exact same lifeline he gave Sarah on Day 1—a set of rules to survive the corporate meat grinder.

"Okay," Miller breathed out, his shaking hands finally steadying enough to grip his spork properly. "Okay. Keep our heads down. We can do that."

"Good," Wyatt said, taking a massive bite of his own ration block.

Under the table, completely out of sight of the recruits and the roving cameras, Sarah shifted her knee until it was pressed firmly against his. It was a quiet, private spark of affection. She knew exactly what he was doing. He was protecting these kids the same way he had protected her, and she loved him even more for it.

"Finish up," Wyatt told the table, his eyes sweeping the cavernous hall as the power grid flickered ominously overhead. "Shift rotation starts in ten."

Wyatt and Sarah finished their synthetic breakfast in comfortable, unified silence, letting the new recruits pull themselves together. As they stood up to toss their trays into the reclamation bin, they exchanged a brief, secret look. They were playing the part of the hardened company veterans perfectly, keeping the Apex illusion alive for the new blood.

But as they turned their backs on the Mess Hall and headed toward the Security Checkpoint, they both knew the truth. They weren't surviving this place by toeing the line. They were surviving it by breaking every rule the company had, and as long as they had that barricaded break room waiting for them, they could play this game forever.
