# Part 6: Clear Parameters – Saturday, October 22, 2044 – ~08:00 (About twenty minutes into the patrol)

Wyatt stepped out of the sanctuary, the heavy, rusted blast door screeching slightly before clunking shut behind them. The oppressive, sulfur-choked heat of Sector 4 hit him immediately, replacing the quiet intimacy of the break room with the deafening, bone-rattling roar of the deep-core drills.

He and Sarah seamlessly fell back into step. The professional gap of six inches returned, and their heavy Mark-IV flashlights cut through the smog. They were technically back on the grid, playing the part of two compliant S.H.O.P. Marines walking the line.

But as they navigated the winding, grated catwalks of sub-level 4-B, the oppressive atmosphere didn't touch them. They both had a secret burning in their chests, a shared future that made the corporate nightmare around them feel entirely irrelevant.

About twenty minutes into the patrol, Wyatt's veteran instincts caught a subtle flicker of movement.

It was in a recessed maintenance alcove, tucked behind a massive, dormant coolant manifold where the automated cameras couldn't reach. Wyatt immediately raised a closed fist. Sarah stopped instantly behind him, her hand dropping to the heavy flashlight at her belt.

Wyatt stepped quietly toward the shadow, his boots making zero sound on the metal grating. He expected to find a panicked scientist tampering with a pressure valve, or a piece of the bleeding bedrock that had somehow breached the containment level.

Instead, the ambient glow of the failing corridor lights caught a flash of pristine black and hazard-yellow uniform fabric.

It was Miller. And the new girl.

He had her pressed gently against the rusted bulkhead, and they were kissing desperately. It wasn't just a brief, nervous exchange; it was a deep, frantic cling for survival. They were completely lost in each other, trying to forcefully drown out the terror of the facility with the only genuine human connection they had left.

Wyatt lowered his flashlight so the beam hit the deck plates near their boots, not wanting to blind them.

Miller flinched violently at the sudden spill of light. He pulled back, his eyes wide. But Wyatt watched closely as the kid processed the interruption. His eyes didn't just widen with standard fear; they darted rapidly, completely avoiding eye contact. His posture went rigidly stiff, his hands balling into tight fists at his sides as a massive wave of system overload hit him. He was running through the Apex rulebook in his head, entirely paralyzed by the absolute, catastrophic breach of protocol he had just been caught in.

Wyatt recognized it almost immediately. He'd worked with guys on the deep-sea rigs who processed the world the exact same way. Miller was autistic. He relied on clear rules and literal parameters to navigate the chaos, and right now, his internal system was crashing because he knew he broke a massive rule and expected the literal corporate punishment.

Wyatt looked over his shoulder at Sarah. She saw it too, her expression softening with profound empathy. She gave him a subtle nod. Let them have this.

Wyatt knew a vague, silent nod of approval wasn't going to work here. Ambiguity was just going to leave Miller agonizing over the implications for the rest of his eighteen-month contract. He needed explicit, literal boundaries.

Wyatt stepped forward, keeping his movements slow and predictable. He reached down and clicked his heavy flashlight completely off to lower the sensory intensity of the moment.

"Miller," Wyatt said, his voice a low, steady, and entirely calm rumble over the machinery.

He tensed, bracing for the reprimand. The new girl shrank behind his shoulder.

"Listen to me carefully," Wyatt told him, making sure his words were unambiguous. "My partner and I did not see you two in this alcove. As far as we are concerned, the sector is empty. You are not going to be reported."

Miller blinked, the sheer panic in his posture halting as his brain latched onto the clear statement of fact. But Wyatt wasn't finished.

"However, we are establishing a new protocol right now," Wyatt continued, holding his ground. "If you or your partner happen to notice Sarah and me walking off the primary grid, or if you realize our bunk assignments are empty, you did not see that, either. You do not report us. We keep your secret, and you keep ours. Is that rule understood? Do we have a deal?"

The absolute clarity of the transaction worked perfectly. The paralyzing anxiety vanished from Miller's face, replaced by the profound relief of a new, logical parameter he could easily follow.

"Understood," Miller said quickly, his voice firming up. "Yes. It's a deal. We didn't see you."

The new girl nodded fervently from behind him. "We won't say a word to anyone. We promise."

"Good," Wyatt said, giving them a single, definitive nod.

He turned away, clicking his flashlight back on and pointing the beam down the empty, industrial corridor.

"Sector looks clear," Wyatt said, resuming his patrol.

"Completely clear," Sarah agreed effortlessly, falling perfectly into step beside him.

As they walked away, leaving the two young lovers to their stolen moment in the dark, Sarah looked up at him. The way Wyatt had just handled that—recognizing exactly what Miller needed to hear to feel safe, and doing it without an ounce of condescension—made her heart physically ache with how much she loved him.

She stepped closer, entirely ignoring the six-inch professional gap. Without missing a beat, she reached down, her fingers finding his in the dim, strobing light of the catwalk. She laced them together tightly.

"You're a good man, Wyatt Colton," she murmured, her thumb tracing the scarred knuckles of his taped hand.

"Just making sure our blind spots are covered," Wyatt replied, a soft, genuine smile breaking through his stoic mask as he squeezed her hand.

They continued down the catwalk, the oppressive weight of the Apex facility completely neutralized by the warmth of her fingers interlocked with his. The corporation thought it owned everyone down here, but the human spirit was quietly organizing in the dark.
