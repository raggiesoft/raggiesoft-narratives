# Part 3: The Echo – Saturday, October 22, 2044 – ~05:30

Wyatt led the way out of the Mess Hall, tossing his empty tray into the reclamation bin. Sarah was right at his side, her steps perfectly synchronized with his. A few paces behind them, Miller and the new girl were following closely in their wake, instinctively using his broad, 6'1" frame to part the sea of aggressive, gray-clad contractors heading toward the Security Checkpoint.

The processing line was a miserable bottleneck. Through the bulletproof glass, the Supervisor looked even more haggard than yesterday, his combat shotgun resting uneasily across his lap.

Wyatt stepped up to the yellow line. "Colton, Wyatt J. Contractor ID: 884-Bravo. Age twenty-three."

He leaned into the retinal scanner, grabbed his heavy Mark-IV flashlight and PDA from the transfer drawer, and pushed through the turnstile. Sarah followed immediately after, her voice steady and practiced as she recited her serial number and age. Her scavenged black armor drew zero suspicion from the exhausted guard.

They both waited on the steel bench on the other side of the glass, clipping their gear to their belts.

A moment later, Miller and the new girl pushed through the heavy metal gate. They were clutching their flashlights to their chests like shields, staring down the long, descending corridor that led into the blistering heat of Sector 4. The ambient roar of the deep-core drills was already echoing up the shaft, shaking the grated floor beneath their boots.

Miller looked terrified. But as a particularly violent tremor rattled the catwalk, the new girl stumbled slightly, letting out a sharp gasp.

Wyatt watched Miller’s posture completely change. The panic in his eyes was forcefully shoved down. He reached out, grabbing her elbow to steady her. He wasn't much older than she was, and he was entirely out of his depth, but Wyatt could see the desperate, protective instinct kicking in. He wanted to be solid for her.

"I've got you," Miller whispered over the mechanical drone, keeping his hand on her arm. "Just stay behind me."

The girl looked up at him, her wide, frightened eyes locking onto his. The sheer relief washing over her face was unmistakable. She nodded, stepping closer until their shoulders brushed.

Wyatt leaned back slightly against the cold concrete wall, his arms crossed over his chest. He looked at Sarah. She was watching the two recruits with a quiet, knowing expression. She caught his eye, and a faint, private smirk touched the corner of her mouth.

It was an absolute mirror image.

Wyatt was watching the exact same dynamic that had played out between the two of them forty-eight hours ago. Apex Defense Solutions stripped away every piece of your humanity, and in response, people desperately clung to the first genuine connection they found just to prove they were still alive. He could already see the sparks of a trauma-bonded romance taking root. By the end of their shift, those two would be terrified of being separated, just like he and Sarah had been.

"Think they'll figure out the bunk situation?" Sarah murmured, leaning in close so only he could hear her over the industrial noise.

"If they're smart, they'll realize the HR algorithm deleted them too, and they'll find a quiet corner," Wyatt replied, his voice a low, steady rumble.

He looked back at the two kids. He could warn them about the missing beds. He could tell them that management wasn't actually watching the tracking grid, and that the patrols were a complete farce.

But Wyatt quickly dismissed the idea. He and Sarah were sitting on the most dangerous, valuable secret in the entire Nevada facility. They had an off-the-grid sanctuary with a locking blast door, working plumbing, and a sofa where they could sleep holding each other. If he started handing out survival tips to every terrified recruit, or if he drew attention to the fact that the surveillance grid was broken, management might actually wake up and run a diagnostic.

His first, second, and only priority was Sarah. He was not going to risk their hidden paradise for two strangers.

Wyatt uncrossed his arms, pushing off the concrete wall. He unclipped his flashlight, turning the heavy beam down the dark, descending corridor.

"Alright, rookies, listen up," Wyatt called out, adopting the gruff, stoic tone of a hardened Apex veteran. Miller and the girl immediately snapped to attention. "My partner and I are taking the primary perimeter route on sublevel 4-A. You two are assigned to the secondary filtration catwalks. Keep your flashlights up, don't loiter in the blind spots, and don't touch the coolant valves."

"Yes, sir," Miller said, nodding quickly, clearly grateful for the clear instructions. The girl beside him echoed the nod, staying firmly tucked behind his shoulder.

"Keep your heads down," Sarah added, offering them a perfectly calibrated look of professional encouragement. "You'll be fine."

Wyatt turned his back on them, leading Sarah down the steep metal stairs into the sweltering, smog-choked depths of Sector 4. They listened to the recruits' hesitant footsteps echoing down a branching corridor as they headed off to start their own terrifying shift.

As soon as they were out of sight, the rigid veteran posture melted off Wyatt's shoulders. The heavy, oppressive atmosphere of the facility felt like a cheap theatrical backdrop. They were on the clock, technically walking a perimeter, but the reality was entirely different.

Sarah stepped up flush against his side, entirely ignoring the six-inch professional gap. Without missing a beat, she reached down, her fingers finding his in the dim, strobing light of the catwalk. She laced them together tightly.

"Think they'll make it?" she asked softly, her thumb tracing the scarred knuckles of his taped hand.

"They have each other," Wyatt said, looking down at her, the memory of last night in the barricaded break room sending a jolt of warmth through his chest. "That's a pretty good start."

"It really is," she agreed, a bright, uninhibited smile crossing her face.

Wyatt squeezed her hand, setting a slow, relaxed pace down the empty industrial corridor. He had ten hours left to kill before they could disappear back into their sanctuary, but walking the line with her fingers locked in his, the shift didn't feel like a punishment anymore.
