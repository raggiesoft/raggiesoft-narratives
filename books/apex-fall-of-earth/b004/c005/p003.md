# Part 3: The Billionaire’s Secrets – Tuesday, October 25, 2044 – ~14:30

The horrific broadcast about Yellowstone and the ash cloud hung heavily in their minds, a grim reminder of the world they were leaving behind. But Outpost Delta 9—no, *The Nomad*, as Sarah had quietly started calling it in her head—was currently millions of miles away from the ash, the corporate assassins, and the dying earth.

They needed to know exactly what kind of machine they were living inside.

Wyatt and Sarah left the Operations center, walking side-by-side down the central corridor. They were both still entirely barefoot. It wasn't a conscious decision they had discussed; it was an instinctual one. Shedding their boots and walking skin-to-metal on the grated floor plates and the plush carpets of the upper decks was a physical declaration. They weren't visitors anymore. They weren't Apex contractors. This massive, purring freighter was their home.

"The blueprints I found in the computer core showed the aft section terminating right behind the reactor room," Wyatt said, his bare heels striking the metal deck as they climbed down the heavy ladder past the Boiler Room. "It said everything behind the fission containment was solid lead and titanium shielding."

"You think the blueprints were lying?" Sarah asked, her toes gripping the cold rungs as she followed him down into the sub-levels they had yet to explore.

"I think a paranoid Apex executive wouldn't buy a massive freighter just to use five percent of the hull," Wyatt replied, his eyes gleaming with the thrill of the puzzle.

They reached the absolute lowest deck, a narrow, dimly lit maintenance corridor that dead-ended at a massive, featureless bulkhead. There was no wheel. There was no handle. Just a smooth expanse of heavily riveted iron and a small, archaic mechanical keypad covered in decades of dust.

Wyatt didn't even try to guess the code. He pulled his heavy utility knife, pried the faceplate off the keypad, and shorted the heavy copper ignition wires directly against the steel frame.

With a deep, pneumatic *HISSS*, the massive "solid armor" bulkhead split down the middle and slowly groaned open.

The air that rushed out wasn't stale. It was perfectly climate-controlled, carrying the faint, electric scent of ozone and warm silicon. Wyatt reached inside and slammed a heavy breaker switch on the wall.

Row after row of pristine, brilliant white LED daylight strips flickered to life, illuminating a cavernous, two-story hangar that stretched the entire length of the ship's aft section.

Sarah actually gasped, her hand instinctively finding Wyatt's.

It was a technological marvel hidden inside a rusted shell. Directly in the center of the room sat a massive, polished ring of exotic dark metal, suspended inside a heavy magnetic containment field. It wasn't connected to the crude nuclear thrusters.

"My god," Wyatt breathed, his mechanic's brain trying to process the sheer physics of what he was looking at. He walked slowly toward the ring, his bare feet entirely silent on the polished polymer floor. "Sarah... that's a localized spacetime folding engine. An **FTL Drive**. He didn't just want to hide. He wanted the ability to jump to entirely different star systems in the blink of an eye."

Sarah looked from the massive drive ring to the heavy, illuminated consoles lining the walls. "Wyatt, look at this."

She was standing in front of a tactical suite. The screens were dark, but the analog labels etched into the brushed aluminum were perfectly clear. **POINT-DEFENSE KINETIC RAILGUNS**. And right next to them: **ELECTROMAGNETIC DEFLECTOR SHIELDING**.

Wyatt walked over, staring at the shield diagnostics. He let out a breathless, disbelieving laugh. "We ran from that dropship," he muttered, shaking his head. "Sarah, this deflector grid draws straight from the FTL containment field. If we had thrown this switch, that Apex dropship would have shattered its hull trying to breach our airspace. We’re not in a lifeboat. We’re in a dreadnought."

But the paranoid executive's unlimited checkbook didn't stop at military-grade hardware.

They walked past the drive core and opened a set of heavy, soundproofed doors at the very back of the hangar. The executive hadn't just built a bunker; he had built a sanctuary to preserve his own sanity.

The first room was a massive, climate-controlled server architecture. Aisle after aisle of towering black racks hummed with power. Wyatt checked the primary terminal. It wasn't connected to any external grid; it was a completely isolated network of highly stable virtual machines.

"It's an archive," Wyatt realized, scrolling through the directories. "He backed up everything. Blueprints, literature, offline encyclopedias, decades of entertainment... he brought the entire sum of human knowledge with him."

Sarah had wandered into the adjoining room, and Wyatt found her standing perfectly still, staring at the center of the space.

It was a breathtaking, state-of-the-art music studio. The walls were heavily lined with acoustic foam. In the center sat a massive, fully analog synthesizer board, complete with dozens of patch cables, drum machines, and a high-fidelity recording suite. Guitars hung securely on the walls, completely untouched by the apocalypse outside. It was a playground of pure creation, preserved perfectly in the vacuum of space.

Sarah looked at Wyatt, her eyes shining in the soft light of the studio. "He really thought of everything, didn't he?"

"He was preparing to be the last man in the universe," Wyatt said softly, stepping into the room. He ran his hand lightly over the keys of the synthesizer. "He built a world where he would never be bored, never be vulnerable, and never have to rely on anyone else."

He turned back to Sarah, his dark eyes locking onto hers. The bare feet, the soft civilian clothes, the premium hot chocolate sitting upstairs in the Ops center... it was all coming together.

"But he isn't here," Wyatt said, stepping close and wrapping his arms around her waist. "We are."

Sarah smiled, a deep, radiant warmth filling her chest as she rested her hands on his chest. "We definitely aren't Delta 9 anymore. It doesn't fit."

"No," Wyatt agreed, kissing her forehead softly. "This ship moves. We go where we want, when we want. From now on, she's *The Nomad*."

Sarah leaned her head against his shoulder, listening to the quiet, powerful hum of the FTL drive in the next room. Earth was burning, and Apex was hunting them. But standing barefoot in the heart of their new, heavily armed dreadnought, the universe suddenly felt like it belonged to them.
