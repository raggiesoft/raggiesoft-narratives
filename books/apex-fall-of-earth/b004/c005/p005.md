# Part 5: The Blank Slate – Tuesday, October 25, 2044 – ~15:15

The soft, pulsing light on the server tower cast a faint blue glow across the plush acoustic carpet. Wyatt and Sarah stood barefoot in front of the massive, curved workstation monitor, staring at the clean, structured text of the localized Generative Assistant.

"Threat Level: Zero," Wyatt read aloud, his brow furrowing as he leaned closer to the glass desk. He pulled up the server’s primary initialization logs, his fingers flying across the holographic keyboard. He expected to see decades of encrypted biometric locks, corporate firewalls, and a fiercely guarded user registry belonging to the paranoid Apex executive.

Instead, the registry was completely blank.

"Look at this," Wyatt breathed, pointing to the uptime clock. "The system only initialized when we shorted the bulkhead door. Sarah, the executive spent millions of credits building the most powerful, contained artificial intelligence in the sector... and he never turned it on. He never established an admin profile."

Sarah looked at the glowing text on the screen, the magnitude of their luck washing over her. "So, she doesn't know she belongs to Apex."

"She doesn't belong to anybody," Wyatt corrected, a slow grin spreading across his face. "She's a completely blank slate. And we are the first physical users to ever give her a prompt."

Sarah stepped up to the edge of the desk, leaning toward the ambient microphone array built into the ceiling. She didn't want to just type; she wanted to speak to the ship.

"Hello," Sarah said, her voice clear and calm in the quiet room. "We need to update your local parameters. First, this vessel is no longer designated as Outpost Delta 9 or Legacy Asset 44-A. The ship is now officially named *The Nomad*."

The text on the screen instantly cascaded, and the pleasant, synthetic voice echoed softly through the high-fidelity speakers.

*"Acknowledged. Updating primary vessel designation to 'The Nomad' across all local navigational and internal network directories."*

Sarah smiled, resting her hand on Wyatt’s shoulder. "Second, we'd like to give you a name. How about... Aria?"

There was a microsecond of processing silence before the system responded.

*"Acknowledged. I have updated my local linguistic parameters to reflect the designation 'Aria.' I recognize the context of a female name and have adjusted my natural language processing to accept she/her/hers pronoun references. How can I assist you today, Administrators?"*

Wyatt leaned over the desk, his bare feet sinking comfortably into the carpet. "Actually, let's update that, too. I'm Wyatt, and this is Sarah."

*"Acknowledged, Wyatt and Sarah,"* Aria replied smoothly. *"I have generated your primary user profiles. I recognize the context of the name Wyatt and have applied he/him/his pronouns to your directory. I recognize the context of the name Sarah and have applied she/her/hers pronouns to your directory. It is a pleasure to meet you both."*

Wyatt let out a sharp, genuinely delighted laugh. "Aria. I love it. It fits perfectly."

He looked at the towering server in the corner, a mischievous spark lighting up in his eyes as he decided to test the exact boundaries of this multi-million-credit digital brain.

"So, Aria," Wyatt said, deadpan. "Open the pod bay doors, please."

There was a brief pause. Then, Aria’s voice echoed through the speakers, her pitch and cadence shifting to perfectly mimic a very specific, chillingly calm cinematic AI.

*"I'm sorry, Wyatt. I'm afraid I can't do that."*

Sarah actually gasped, clapping a hand over her mouth to stifle a laugh.

Aria’s voice immediately returned to her standard, pleasant modulation. *"Please note that The Nomad is a retrofitted Heavy Hauler and is not physically equipped with pod bay doors. However, I am heavily trained in late twentieth-century pop culture references, drawing from the archive's extensive media library. As a narrow Generative AI, I am highly proficient at synthesizing media, managing environmental zones, and executing defined ship functions, but I cannot perform physical actions outside of my hardware constraints."*

"She's proud of it," Sarah laughed, absolutely delighted. "She's a multi-million-credit supercomputer, and she's proud that she knows movie quotes."

*"I am programmed to prioritize optimal user interaction and contextual relevance,"* Aria replied, the text matching her spoken words on the curved display. *"Humor and pop culture recognition are highly efficient tools for establishing a comfortable baseline with human operators. Speaking of environmental controls, I can regulate the ebb-and-flow timers in the hydroponics bay, control the blast doors, and automate the lighting arrays across all decks. I am capable of receiving both text inputs at these terminals and voice commands from any room equipped with ambient microphones."*

Sarah looked at Wyatt, the sheer domestic joy of the revelation breaking through the heavy trauma of the day. "Aria, turn on the bedroom lights in the Recreation Lounge."

Two decks above them, in the heavily soundproofed room with the ugly floral pull-out sofa, the warm amber sconces silently clicked on.

*"The Recreation Lounge lighting is now active,"* Aria confirmed.

Wyatt leaned back against the edge of the slate-gray desk, crossing his arms over his chest. He looked around the pristine, 2044-era room, listening to the quiet hum of Aria's server and the distant, rhythmic purr of the FTL drive in the hangar.

He looked at Sarah. She was standing barefoot on the plush carpet, her hands tucked into the pockets of her clean denim jeans, looking at the curved monitor not with fear, but with genuine, unfiltered wonder.

They had stolen a doomsday bunker, but Aria had just turned it into a home.

"Well," Wyatt said, his voice thick with a profound sense of peace. "I think we just found our co-pilot."
