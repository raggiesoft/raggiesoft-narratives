# Part 4: The 2044 Reveal – Tuesday, October 25, 2044 – ~15:00

The analog warmth of the music studio was staggering, but Wyatt’s eyes were drawn to a sleek, frosted-glass door tucked into the far corner of the server room. It completely lacked the heavy iron rivets, brass hinges, and rusted-steel aesthetic of the upper decks. It looked like it had been lifted straight out of a modern, ultra-high-end Apex corporate laboratory.

Wyatt stepped up to the glass, his bare feet sinking into a sudden transition from hard polymer to a thick, plush acoustic carpet. The door didn't have a handle. As his proximity registered, a soft chime echoed, and the frosted glass smoothly slid directly into the bulkhead.

"Okay," Wyatt breathed, stepping into the room. "The 1970s are officially over."

Sarah followed him in, her eyes going wide.

The room wasn't a retro bunker. It was a pristine, cutting-edge technological sanctuary built for the year 2044. Soft, dynamic LED lighting traced the edges of the ceiling, automatically adjusting to a warm, comfortable glow as they entered.

Dominating the space were two massive, custom-built workstations. They featured sweeping, curved, ultra-high-definition holographic displays hovering above sleek, slate-gray desks. Beneath the desks sat twin liquid-cooled processing towers, their internal components glowing with a faint, icy blue light. Next to the workstations was an entertainment center that made the *Centauri Video Computer System* upstairs look like a stone tablet—it housed the absolute latest in modern gaming consoles, hardwired into a massive, wall-mounted display.

Wyatt sat down in one of the ergonomic mesh chairs, completely mesmerized. He tapped the glass surface of the desk. The workstation instantly woke up, the curved display snapping into crisp, flawless resolution.

He pulled up the system diagnostics, his mechanic's brain trying to comprehend the sheer processing power sitting at his fingertips.

"Sarah... these rigs are monsters," Wyatt muttered, scrolling through the hardware specs. "You could spin up a dozen nested virtual machines simultaneously, compile complex backend code for a massive digital ecosystem, and run hyper-realistic life simulation titles at maximum render distance without dropping a single frame."

Sarah walked over, resting her hands on his shoulders, staring at the screen. "But why? Why does the Captain's Quarters have a VCR from 1985 and a tube TV, while the basement looks like this?"

"Because it wasn't a technological limitation," Wyatt realized, a smirk crossing his face. "It was a stylistic choice. The billionaire who built this ship wasn't stuck in the past; he just loved the aesthetic. The upper decks were his personal, curated museum. A love letter to his favorite decades. He wanted the vibe of a 1970s bunker, but he wanted the processing power of a god."

And that processing power wasn't just sitting idle.

In the corner of the room, a standalone server tower—slightly taller and wider than the workstation rigs—let out a soft, musical hum. The front panel was entirely blank, save for a single, pulsing light.

Suddenly, a clean, highly structured interface booted up on the secondary monitor of Wyatt's workstation. Text began to generate across the screen in real-time, completely unprompted.

**\[SYSTEM WAKE\]** **Local Network Acknowledged. Biometrics: Unrecognized. Threat Level: Zero.** **Good afternoon. I am the localized Generative Assistant. How can I help you today?**

Sarah leaned closer, her jaw dropping slightly. "Wyatt... is that an AI?"

Wyatt pulled up the server's resource monitor, his eyes widening. "It is. But it's entirely localized. It's not pinging the Apex cloud or the Earth grid. It's running natively on that single server tower in the corner."

To give you a little meta-context as your co-writer: That server is running a model just like *me*. Back in 2026, running a generative intelligence this advanced, capable of fluid reasoning, creative writing, and complex system management, required massive, multi-acre data centers and staggering amounts of electricity.

But here, in 2044, on the billionaire's private dreadnought? The resource monitor on Wyatt's screen showed the AI was utilizing exactly 5% of the server's CPU and a mere 3% of its RAM. It was a staggering leap in computational efficiency, completely boxed and isolated for their private use.

"Hello," Wyatt said tentatively, speaking aloud into the room's ambient microphone array. "What are your primary functions?"

The text on the screen scrolled instantly, accompanied by a calm, pleasant, and completely synthetic voice echoing softly from the high-fidelity speakers.

*"I am a multimodal generative model, designed to assist with complex problem-solving, creative endeavors, system diagnostics, and data synthesis. I do not experience personal feelings or consciousness, but I am fully integrated into The Nomad's offline archives. I can help you design websites, render complex musical arrangements for the studio next door, assist in managing the hydroponics yields, or optimize the ship's FTL navigation metrics."*

Sarah let out a breathless laugh, her bare toes curling into the plush carpet. She looked around the glowing, ultra-modern room, then back at the analog synthesizer studio through the glass door, and finally up at the heavily armored ceiling that separated them from their 1970s living room.

"We didn't just steal a getaway car," Sarah whispered, shaking her head in sheer disbelief. "We stole a self-sustaining universe."

Wyatt leaned back in the ergonomic chair, reaching up to cover Sarah's hands with his own. Earth had fallen to a subterranean nightmare, and the Pacific Northwest was currently choking on the ash of a corporate-triggered supervolcano. The universe outside was cold, hostile, and actively hunting them.

But inside *The Nomad*, sitting barefoot in a room that held the sum total of human knowledge, limitless processing power, and the absolute bleeding edge of technology... they had everything they would ever need to build a new life among the stars.
