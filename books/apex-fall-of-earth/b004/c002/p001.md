# Part 1: The Hydroponics Bay – Monday, October 24, 2044 – ~10:30

The heavy, airtight bulkhead door in the lower ring didn't just slide open; it groaned, the rusted hydraulic seals fighting Wyatt’s grip on the manual release wheel. With a final, metallic *shriek*, the iron door gave way, and the dry, recycled ozone of the corridor was instantly blasted away by a wall of heat.

It smelled like rain.

Sarah stepped through the threshold, freezing in her tracks. After weeks of breathing the sterile, metallic air of the NTTR and the dead, static chill of the station’s upper decks, the air in the Hydroponics Bay was staggeringly thick. It was intensely humid, carrying the rich, heavy scent of wet earth and growing life.

Instead of the dim, amber emergency lights, the ceiling was packed with caged, ultra-violet fluorescent tubes. They cast the entire massive, curved room in a vibrant, synthetic pink-and-purple glow. It sounded like the inside of a massive, mechanical lung—the constant, rhythmic rush of water, the heavy *clack* of mechanical timers, and the steady drip of condensation falling onto the grated steel floor.

"Oh, wow," Sarah breathed, her eyes wide as she took in the sprawling rows of deep, galvanized steel trays. Thick, leafy greens and heavily clustered dwarf-tomatoes practically exploded over the edges of the metal troughs, thriving aggressively under the UV glare.

Tucked away in the far corner, a massive, bulbous flytrap hybrid snapped a jaw-like leaf at a stray drop of condensation, but Sarah barely registered it. She walked slowly down the central aisle, completely mesmerized.

She reached out, gently cupping a vibrant red tomato in her hand. A genuine, relaxed smile spread across her face. Then, she looked down at the base of the plant, her brow furrowing.

"Wyatt, there's no dirt," she said, sounding almost betrayed.

Wyatt stepped into the room, letting the heavy bulkhead door slide shut behind him. The lock engaged with a reassuring *clack*. He walked over to where she was standing and looked down into the steel tray. Instead of dark, rich soil, the thick, white roots of the plants were buried in a bed of porous, round clay pebbles.

"It's an ebb-and-flow hydroponic system," Wyatt noted, tracing a thick, industrial PVC pipe down to a massive cast-iron water pump on the floor. "The timer kicks on, floods the trays with nutrient water, and then gravity drains it back into the reservoir. It keeps the roots from rotting in the zero-G transit, and it’s completely self-contained."

Sarah sighed, pulling her hand away from the plant. She looked over at the central control station. It was a terrifying array of heavy glass carboys filled with raw, neon-colored liquid nutrients, surrounded by analog pH meters with trembling needles and heavy brass mixing valves.

"I used to garden," Sarah murmured, her voice carrying a soft, nostalgic weight. She crossed her arms over her slate-blue uniform shirt. "When I was a little girl, maybe ten or eleven. I had this little patch in the backyard. I used to spend hours out there, just digging my hands into the actual dirt. You could feel the life in it. You knew exactly what the plants needed just by touching the soil."

She gestured toward the massive, intimidating nutrient-mixing station.

"This isn't a garden, Wyatt. This is a high-pressure chemistry lab. I don't know how to balance a localized pH level or calibrate an analog nutrient drip. If I mix those carboys wrong, I'll kill our entire food supply in a week."

Wyatt watched her. He saw the hesitation, but he also saw the desperate spark in her eyes. She wanted this. In a universe entirely out of their control, she wanted something she could nurture.

He walked past her, stepping up to the terrifying array of pipes and glass carboys. He tapped a heavy iron wrench against the side of the main water reservoir.

"The plumbing is my problem," Wyatt said, his voice a low, steady rumble of absolute confidence. "If a pump seizes, or a timer gear strips, I’ll fix it. But look at the trays, Sarah."

She turned, looking back at the sprawling, vibrant canopy of green leaves under the purple lights.

"It doesn't matter if the roots are in dirt or clay pebbles," Wyatt told her, stepping back to stand right in front of her. He reached out, gently resting his hands on her shoulders. "They still need to be pruned. The fruit still needs to be harvested. They still need someone to actually care if they live or die. The Apex executives who lived here definitely didn't. You can figure out the water mix. It’s just trial and error."

He offered her that warm, grounded smile that had kept her sane through the darkest days of the NTTR.

"Give it a try, Sarah. It’s gardening."

Sarah looked up at him, the harsh pink light reflecting in her eyes. The tension slowly bled out of her shoulders. She looked back at the rows of plants, the thick humidity soaking into her skin, warming her to the bone.

"I could stay in here for hours," Sarah admitted quietly. Then, her grip tightened instinctively on his forearm. The lingering ghost of the Nevada facility flared in her chest. The thought of him walking out that heavy iron door and leaving her alone, even in this sanctuary, made her heart skip a terrifying beat. "As long as..."

"As long as I'm right here," Wyatt finished smoothly. He didn't hesitate. He didn't make her explain the trauma or the fear. It was an unbreakable rule.

Wyatt walked over to a stack of rusted, military-grade supply crates in the corner. He dragged one over to the edge of the central aisle, sitting heavily on the reinforced plastic lid. He leaned back against the warm metal bulkhead, crossing his arms and stretching his legs out.

"Take your time," Wyatt smiled, completely content. "I've got nowhere else to be."

Sarah let out a breath she didn't realize she was holding. The knot of anxiety vanished, replaced by a profound, radiant warmth. She reached down, unbuttoning her slate-blue uniform shirt and tying the sleeves securely around her waist.

Wearing just her black undershirt, she walked up to the analog nutrient station, picked up a heavy, glass measuring beaker, and got to work.
