# Part 5: Scorched Earth – Monday, October 24, 2044 – ~12:15 (The military broadcast states scorched earth was authorized at 1300 hours on Earth)

The heavy, metallic silence in the Hydroponics Bay was completely suffocating. The rush of the newly repaired water pump, which had sounded like a triumph just minutes ago, now sounded like a ticking clock.

Sarah’s legs finally gave out. She slid down the warm, rusted iron bulkhead, pulling her knees tight against her chest as she hit the grated floor. Wyatt sank down right next to her. The heavy canvas of his pants scraped against the steel as he shifted closer, immediately wrapping his arm securely around her shoulders.

Sarah leaned into him, burying her face against the warm, grease-smudged cotton of his black undershirt. Wyatt held her tightly, resting his chin against the top of her head. He could feel her trembling, a fine, high-frequency shake that mirrored the adrenaline spiking in his own blood.

They had just escaped the NTTR. They had fought so hard just to survive a corporate assassination attempt. And now, the very planet they were born on was being erased from the cosmos in an afternoon.

"It's all moving so fast," Sarah whispered, her voice muffled against his chest. "How do you lose the entire coastline in an hour? How big is it?"

Wyatt tightened his grip on her, his eyes staring blankly at the galvanized steel trays across the aisle. "It's not just one organism anymore," he theorized grimly. "It has to be a network. A hive. It hit the ocean and used the water to cool its digging friction, accelerating the collapse..."

Before he could finish the thought, the PA speaker above the door crackled back to life.

It wasn't the harsh, grating dual-tone of the Emergency Broadcast System this time. There was no synthesized voice. It was a raw, unencrypted military frequency—a desperate, open-channel loop bleeding through the station's hardwired passive receiver.

The voice that echoed through the humid bay was human. It was a male voice, gruff, heavily distorted by atmospheric interference, and stripped of all protocol. He sounded exhausted, terrified, and entirely defeated.

*"...to all remaining NORAD and Northern Command assets. This is General Miller, STRATCOM Base Omaha. If you are receiving this on an open channel, the chain of command has been broken. Washington is dark."*

Wyatt went completely rigid. Sarah lifted her head, her breath catching as she stared up at the rusted black speaker.

*"The anomaly has breached the Nevada-Utah border and is multiplying at an incalculable rate. Conventional munitions have zero effect. It absorbs the biomass of our casualties. As of thirteen-hundred hours, the President authorized the Scorched Earth protocol along the Continental Divide."*

The general took a ragged breath. The sound of heavy, concussive thuds echoed in the background of his transmission—artillery, or maybe the earth itself cracking open.

*"Operation Glass Wall is in effect. We are detonating maximum-yield subterranean nuclear devices along the entire length of the Rocky Mountains, from the Canadian border down to New Mexico. Strategic bombers are currently flooding the western valleys with thermobaric payloads. We are attempting to create a one-hundred-mile-wide trench of radioactive slag and glass. We have to burn the earth to the mantle to starve it."*

Sarah’s hand shot up to cover her mouth, a horrified sob trapped in her throat.

*"Millions of civilians were unable to clear the evacuation zones in time. To the families of those trapped on the western side of the Rockies... God forgive us. We are dropping the fire now. End transmission."*

The channel snapped shut. The heavy, hissing static of deep space rushed back in to fill the void.

Wyatt stared at the speaker, the blood draining completely from his face. The United States military wasn't fighting an enemy. They were amputating half the country. They were turning thousands of miles of the Rocky Mountains into a radioactive crater just to build a wall of fire.

He looked down at Sarah. She was crying now, silent, terrified tears cutting through the engine grease on her cheeks. She was grieving for a world that was tearing itself apart, for millions of people who woke up this morning thinking it was just a normal Monday.

Wyatt pulled her fiercely into his lap, wrapping both of his arms completely around her. He buried his face in her hair, closing his eyes against the harsh purple glare of the UV lights.

He was terrified. The sheer scale of the violence happening back on Earth was beyond human comprehension. But underneath the terror, buried beneath the staggering survivor's guilt, was an intense, overwhelming wave of relief.

They were off the board. They were millions of miles away in a rusted, forgotten tin can, floating in the silent dark of space. The anomaly couldn't reach them. The nuclear fire couldn't touch them. As long as the life-support scrubbers hummed and the water pumps rushed, they were safe.

"We're here," Wyatt whispered fiercely, rocking her gently against his chest on the grated floor. "Sarah, listen to me. We're here. We're on Delta 9."

Sarah gripped his undershirt, her knuckles white, anchoring herself entirely to the steady, rhythmic beating of his heart. She squeezed her eyes shut, listening to the rushing water of the hydroponics bay, letting the reality of their lifeboat wash over her.

They were frightened, deeply scarred, and completely alone in the universe. But as they held onto each other in the warm, humid glow of the garden, they had never been more grateful for the silence of deep space.
