# Part 2: Shedding the Armor – Tuesday, October 25, 2044 – Morning (e.g. ~08:00) (The deep-space morning cycled over the outpost)

The heavy, mechanical reality of the Nevada Test and Training Range was designed to break you. One of the most insidious ways Apex Defense Solutions achieved this wasn't through grueling physical labor or the constant threat of violence, but through the systematic destruction of basic human dignity.

They issued every contractor exactly one uniform. There were no laundry rotations. There were no spares. You wore it, you sweat in it, you bled in it, and eventually, the sheer physical degradation made you stop caring about yourself entirely. You weren't a person; you were just a tool in a slate-blue wrapper.

Sarah only technically had two uniforms because she had scavenged an abandoned break area in Sector 4 and found a S.H.O.P. Marine jacket that was a full size too small. It was suffocatingly tight across her shoulders, but it meant she could occasionally peel off her primary Field Safety Technician gear just to let it air out.

For the last four days—through the panicked escape, the zero-gravity transit, and the desperate repairs on Delta 9—they had been living inside those same stiff, foul-smelling synthetic fabrics.

But as the deep-space morning cycled over the outpost, that nightmare finally ended.

Wyatt walked into the Recreation Lounge, his arms loaded with a massive, vacuum-sealed plastic bundle he had hauled out of the executive storage lockers in the Captain's Quarters. He dropped the heavy cube onto the foot of the pull-out "Grandma" sofa with a dull thud.

"The previous Director didn't just hoard freeze-dried food," Wyatt said, a massive, genuine grin breaking across his exhausted face. "He hoarded a wardrobe."

Sarah sat up, the heavy wool blanket falling to her waist. She stared at the plastic bundle. Wyatt pulled his heavy utility knife from his belt and sliced the vacuum seal.

With a soft *hiss* of equalizing pressure, the plastic burst open. The smell of clean, manufactured cotton wafted into the room, entirely devoid of ozone, machine grease, or fear.

It was a treasure trove of civilian normalcy. There were stacks of soft, perfectly broken-in denim jeans, heavy cotton t-shirts, and thick, pristine white socks. Beneath the denim were brand-new, sealed packages of underwear—men's boxer-briefs, women's cotton panties, and sports bras.

Sarah’s hands trembled slightly as she reached out, her fingers brushing against the soft heather-gray cotton of a t-shirt. It felt like an absolute, impossible luxury. A heavy, emotional knot formed in her throat.

"Let's get rid of them," Wyatt said quietly, his voice losing its usual gruff edge. He pulled a heavy canvas laundry bag from the bottom of the pile and snapped it open.

They didn't retreat to separate rooms. The trauma of Sector 4 had burned away any need for distance between them; right now, the only thing that mattered was shedding the corporate skin.

Sarah stood up, her fingers gripping the hem of that suffocatingly tight S.H.O.P. Marine jacket. She pulled it over her head and threw it onto the carpet with profound disgust. She unbuttoned the stiff, sweat-stained slate-blue FST shirt beneath it, peeling the synthetic fabric off her skin. She stripped down entirely, shivering slightly in the cool, recycled air of the lounge, and immediately reached for the fresh clothes.

The feeling of sliding into clean, soft cotton underwear and a sports bra that actually fit sent a wave of pure, physical relief crashing through her nervous system. She pulled on a pair of the denim jeans, the heavy fabric feeling wonderfully grounding, and slipped the heather-gray t-shirt over her head.

Beside her, Wyatt was fighting his way out of his heavy, grease-stained canvas mechanic's pants and the black undershirt he had practically lived in for a week. He kicked the heavy boots aside, pulling on a pair of clean boxer-briefs, fresh denim jeans, and a soft, fitted black t-shirt. He sat down on the edge of the mattress, letting out a long, shuddering groan of relief as he pulled a pair of thick, clean white socks onto his feet.

"Oh my god," Sarah whispered, her hands running down the soft denim of her jeans. She looked at Wyatt. Without the heavy, militarized Apex uniforms, they didn't look like runaways anymore. They looked human.

Wyatt stood up. He grabbed the S.H.O.P. Marine jacket, the FST uniform, his grease-stained pants, and the black undershirt, shoving them all aggressively into the canvas laundry bag. He pulled the thick drawstring violently tight, sealing the smell of the NTTR inside.

He tossed the bag into the dark corner of the closet and slammed the louvered door shut. It was over.

"Come on," Sarah said, a bright, radiant smile breaking across her face. She grabbed his hand, her clean fingers interlacing with his. "I raided the pantry yesterday. It's time for a real breakfast."

The narrow, brushed-steel galley kitchen was instantly filled with the greatest smell Wyatt had ever experienced.

Because of the "One-at-a-Time" rule in the cramped space, Wyatt sat at the small, square dining table securely bolted to the grated floor, sipping a mug of aggressively strong diner coffee from the heavy percolator. He watched Sarah work through the narrow doorway, looking entirely at peace in her gray t-shirt and jeans.

Whoever the previous Apex Director was, he had lived like absolute royalty while ignoring the rest of the universe.

Sarah had raided the heavy walk-in mechanical freezer and the vacuum-sealed dry stores. Now, a heavy iron skillet was sizzling on the induction stove. She cracked rehydrated eggs into the pan, tossing in thick, diced chunks of premium cured ham and tearing up slices of real, sharp Swiss cheese.

"I went down to the bay before you woke up," Sarah called over her shoulder, tossing a handful of sliced, deep-space hydroponic mushrooms into the skillet. They hit the hot butter with a loud, mouth-watering hiss. "The UV yields are incredible. The mushrooms grew almost overnight."

She expertly folded the omelets over, sliding them onto two heavy ceramic plates alongside thick slices of toasted wheat bread. She carried them out to the dining nook, setting one down in front of Wyatt before returning for two tall glasses of reconstituted orange juice.

Wyatt took a bite. The fresh, earthy snap of the mushrooms paired with the rich, melted Swiss cheese and the salty ham was staggering. It wasn't just sustenance; it was an experience.

"Sarah," Wyatt groaned, closing his eyes and leaning back against the heavy vinyl chair. "This is incredible."

Sarah sat across from him, tucking her legs up underneath her, absolutely beaming. "I told you. We aren't just surviving anymore. We're living."

Wyatt swallowed, picking up his mug of coffee. He looked out the thick, circular viewport next to the table at the slow, steady rotation of the starfield outside, his expression shifting into something quiet and deeply relieved.

"I checked the passive receiver board when I was pulling the clothes from the lockers," Wyatt said, keeping his voice calm, not wanting to ruin the warmth of the morning.

Sarah paused, her fork hovering over her plate. The memory of the Scorched Earth protocol still haunted the edges of her mind. "Did the Glass Wall hold?"

Wyatt nodded, his dark eyes meeting hers. "It held. The military intercepts confirmed it. The anomaly couldn't dig through the radioactive slag. The entire northern expansion completely stalled out."

Sarah let out a shaky breath, the tension leaving her shoulders. "Where did it stop?"

"Just south of Salem, Oregon," Wyatt answered, the relief evident in his heavy baritone. "The Pacific Northwest survived, Sarah. The military frequencies were listing the green zones. Salem, Portland, Olympia, Tacoma, Seattle... all the way up through Bellingham, Blaine, and up into Vancouver, British Columbia. The anomaly had to stop growing to heal itself from the burns. They actually saved the northern coast."

Sarah looked down at her plate, a profound, heavy gratitude washing over her. Millions of people had woken up this morning because that wall of fire had held. The monster in the earth wasn't invincible. It could be stopped. It could be contained.

She looked across the small, faux-wood table at Wyatt. He was wearing clean civilian clothes, drinking coffee, and the universe outside their little metal bubble was finally fighting back.

"Eat your omelet," Sarah said softly, reaching across the small table to rest her hand gently over his. "You have a hydroponics pump to check on later."
