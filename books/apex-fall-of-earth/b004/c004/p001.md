# Part 1: An Attempt at Normalcy – Monday, October 24, 2044 – Evening (e.g. ~20:00)

The amber sconces in the Recreation Lounge cast a warm, low-level glow over the faux-walnut paneling. After the deafening, grease-stained chaos of the engine room and the sheer psychological terror of the afternoon's broadcasts, the heavy soundproofing of the room felt like a physical embrace.

For the first time all day, the station was entirely quiet.

Wyatt stood in front of the massive, unapologetically ugly floral sofa. He grabbed the heavy, burnt-orange seat cushions and tossed them onto the carpeted floor.

"Alright," Wyatt muttered, his voice quiet in the insulated room. "Let's see if this thing actually works."

Sarah stood beside him, her slate-blue Helios uniform shirt still tied around her waist. She grabbed the heavy metal handle of the hidden bed frame. Together, they pulled backward. With a terrifyingly loud, metallic *shriek* of neglected springs, the heavy steel frame unfolded, locking into place and revealing a surprisingly thick queen-sized mattress.

It was a stark contrast to the plush luxury of the Captain's Quarters, but right now, neither of them wanted to be in a massive room staring out a viewport at the cold void of space. They wanted a bunker. They wanted a den.

Sarah walked over to the narrow closet built into the bulkhead next to the sofa. She pulled open the louvered door and pulled out two heavy, woven wool blankets and a stack of dense, foam pillows wrapped in sterile Apex plastic.

"They smell a little like ozone and old dust," Sarah noted, tossing the pillows onto the mattress and shaking out the heavy blankets.

"Better than the engine room," Wyatt replied, pulling off his boots and leaving them by the heavy wooden television cabinet.

They crawled onto the mattress, the steel springs groaning softly under their weight. Sarah immediately shifted toward the center, pulling the thick wool blankets up over them. Wyatt slid in right behind her, his arm wrapping securely around her waist, pulling her back flush against his chest.

It was the "Anchor Rule" in its purest form. They were tangled together, entirely grounded by the physical weight and warmth of the other person. Sarah let out a long, shuddering sigh, her hands resting over Wyatt’s arm, feeling the steady, rhythmic thump of his heart against her back.

The silence of the room was heavy, but for the first time in hours, it wasn't suffocating. It was hopeful.

Before coming to the lounge, Wyatt had stood at the massive comms board in Operations, his hand hovering over the dials. He hadn't turned the receiver off—it was hardwired to stay open—but he had tuned the frequency just enough to catch the fragmented, dying echoes of military chatter bouncing off the atmosphere.

Operation Glass Wall had been executed.

The United States military had successfully detonated the subterranean nuclear payload along the Continental Divide. They had unleashed hell on their own soil, turning thousands of miles of the Rocky Mountains into a trench of radioactive glass and boiling slag.

And, incredibly, the chatter suggested it was working.

The seismic shockwaves from the anomaly's terrifying eastward sprint had slammed into the molten, radioactive barrier and stopped. The biological mass couldn't dig through liquid glass. It couldn't consume radioactive fire. The scorched-earth trench had effectively severed the continent in half, but it had starved the immediate expansion.

The anomaly was trapped on the burning western seaboard. The tide, at least for tonight, had turned.

"Do you think it's over?" Sarah whispered into the quiet dimness of the room, her voice barely carrying over the hum of the station's ventilation.

"The digging stopped," Wyatt answered softly, his chin resting against her shoulder. "The military frequencies were reporting zero seismic movement past the Rockies. The wall held, Sarah. They actually managed to trap it."

"They burned half the world to do it," she murmured, the grief still heavy in her chest.

"They saved the other half," Wyatt corrected gently, his thumb brushing a slow, comforting rhythm against her hip. "We escaped the NTTR, Sarah. Humanity is going to escape this. They just bought themselves time to figure out how to kill it."

Sarah closed her eyes, letting his steady, quiet confidence wash over her. They were from two entirely different planets, lightyears away from the agricultural fields of Wyatt's youth or the dirt gardens of hers. But lying here on a rusted, screaming metal bed frame covered in ugly floral fabric, none of that mattered.

They were alive. The lifeboat was holding. And down on Earth, humanity was fighting back.

Sarah shifted closer, burying her face into his chest, letting the exhaustion of the longest day of their lives finally pull her under. Wyatt held her tight, his eyes watching the dark screen of the 1970s console TV, standing guard in the quiet dark.
