# Part 2: The Spacetime Fold – Wednesday, October 26, 2044 – 09:15 HRS

Wyatt and Sarah walked barefoot back up the heavy iron ladders of the central hub, leaving the pristine, 2044-era technology of the lower decks behind. As they stepped through the heavy double doors into the Operations center, the familiar, archaic atmosphere of the 1970s command room greeted them.

The heavy amber CRT monitor was waiting, and the grated bulkhead speakers were still emitting the low, relentless hiss of the open military channel. The hardwired receiver had been a constant, terrifying tether to the dying Earth, a reminder that they were still in the same solar system as the apocalypse.

Wyatt walked up to the massive analog board. He took the sleek, brushed-aluminum data-crystal Aria had encoded and slid it into a retrofitted universal port he had wired into the main console.

The analog systems ground heavily for a second as they translated the staggering processing power of the lower-deck AI into mechanical commands.

*CLACK-CLACK-CLACK.* A series of heavy brass toggles on the navigation board automatically snapped upward. The main heading indicator—the liquid-suspended compass sphere—spun wildly before locking onto the exact coordinates for System 88-Tango.

"Telemetry locked," Wyatt said, his voice dropping into a low, focused register. He looked over his shoulder.

Sarah was standing right behind him, her hands tucked into the pockets of her denim jeans. She wasn't looking at the terrifyingly complex array of dials; she was looking out the massive, heavily scratched viewport at the slow drift of the stars.

"Ready?" he asked.

Sarah took a deep breath, her bare toes gripping the grated floor plates. She stepped up beside him, resting her hand securely against his waist.

"Take us home, Wyatt."

Wyatt reached out, his taped hands gripping the two heavy, mechanical throttle levers built into the center of the console. He didn't just push them forward; he gripped the heavy FTL engagement clutch spanning the two bars and squeezed.

Deep in the absolute lowest deck of the ship, the localized spacetime folding engine woke up.

It didn't roar like the crude nuclear thrusters. It emitted a sound so deep it was almost sub-audible—a heavy, vibrating bass tone that Wyatt felt in the marrow of his bones. The floor plates hummed with a smooth, terrifyingly immense surge of power.

Outside the massive viewport, the stationary starfield didn't streak into white lines. Instead, the universe simply began to warp.

The light from the distant stars bent and refracted, curling around the edges of the thick glass like a massive, invisible magnifying glass was being pulled over the ship. The spacetime fold was wrapping around the hull, encapsulating *The Nomad* in an impenetrable bubble of physics.

Wyatt shoved the throttles all the way to the firewall.

There was no violent G-force pinning them to the console. There was no rattling hull. With a sudden, silent flash of warped light, the Sol System simply ceased to exist outside their window.

The viewport was suddenly filled with a swirling, kaleidoscopic tunnel of deep blues, shifting purples, and brilliant streaks of exotic light as the ship punched a hole straight through the fabric of reality.

And then, something incredible happened.

The low, relentless hiss of deep-space static bleeding from the hardwired receiver abruptly died.

The absolute, profound silence that rushed into the Operations center was staggering. The electromagnetic fold of the FTL drive had completely severed the analog radio waves. The Emergency Broadcast System, the military chatter, and the frantic corporate telemetry of Apex Defense Solutions physically could not reach them anymore.

The tether to Earth was cut.

Wyatt let go of the throttles, the heavy iron levers locked firmly in the red zone. He looked up at the dead speakers, a massive, leaden weight finally lifting off his broad shoulders.

"The comms," Sarah whispered, her voice sounding incredibly loud in the perfectly quiet room. She looked up at the board, her eyes wide with a profound, overwhelming relief. "They're gone."

"They're gone," Wyatt confirmed, turning away from the console to face her. "Nothing gets through the fold. We are entirely off the board."

Sarah let out a breathless, joyful laugh. The corporate meat grinder, the biological nightmare, the scorched earth—all of it was trapped in normal space. In here, inside the fold, it was just the two of them.

She threw her arms around his neck, burying her face in his shoulder. Wyatt caught her, his arms wrapping tightly around her waist, lifting her bare feet an inch off the deck plates. He spun her around once in the quiet, glowing light of the command center, his own laugh rumbling deep in his chest.

They were traveling faster than light, completely untrackable, and headed toward a pristine, untouched world.

For the very first time since they had signed their lives away, the universe actually belonged to them.
