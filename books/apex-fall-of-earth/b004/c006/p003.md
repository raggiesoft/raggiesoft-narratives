# Part 3: The Interstellar Road Trip – Wednesday, October 26, 2044 – 09:30

The Operations center was perfectly silent, save for the low, powerful thrum of the FTL drive vibrating through the floor plates. Outside the massive viewport, the universe was a swirling, hypnotic tunnel of refracted blue and purple light.

Wyatt and Sarah stood barefoot at the console, entirely mesmerized by the sheer scale of what they were doing.

"I still can't believe the comms are completely dead," Sarah murmured, looking up at the silent bulkhead speakers. "It feels like someone finally turned the volume off on the apocalypse."

"Nothing gets through the fold," Wyatt agreed, wrapping his arm comfortably around her waist. "Speaking of which, we should probably figure out exactly how long we're going to be in this bubble."

They left Operations, their bare feet silent on the grated floor plates, and descended the ladders to the 2044-era tech room on the lower deck. The frosted glass door slid open smoothly, welcoming them into the pristine, climate-controlled sanctuary.

Wyatt stepped up to the curved holographic display, tapping the slate-gray desk to wake the system.

"Aria," Wyatt said to the ambient microphones. "We have successfully engaged the FTL drive. We are currently in transit to System 88-Tango."

The server tower in the corner hummed a pleasant, musical note.

*"Congratulations, Wyatt and Sarah,"* Aria’s warm, synthesized voice echoed from the high-fidelity speakers. *"Telemetry confirms the localized spacetime fold is stable. The deflector grid is successfully repelling all micro-debris within the slipstream. You are entirely off the grid."*

"What's our ETA to Exoplanet 4?" Sarah asked, leaning against the edge of the desk.

*"Calculating spatial drift and the raw output of the primary drive core,"* Aria replied, the screen cascading with complex navigational mathematics for a fraction of a second before resolving. *"Estimated time to arrival is approximately one hundred and forty-four hours. Six Earth-standard days."*

Wyatt and Sarah looked at each other.

Six days. A hundred and forty-four hours with no shift klaxons, no automated corporate trackers, and no heavily armed Supervisors watching their every move. Just the two of them, locked inside a heavily armored, self-sustaining luxury bunker flying through another dimension.

"Six days," Sarah breathed out, a slow, radiant smile spreading across her face. She looked up at the ceiling array. "Aria, do we need to manually monitor the engine output or adjust the navigational headings while in transit?"

*"Negative, Sarah,"* Aria confirmed, the text matching her spoken words on the display. *"The FTL containment field is fully automated and self-regulating. The physical drive required manual ignition, but I am fully capable of monitoring the slipstream telemetry. You are not required in Operations until it is time to manually disengage the drive upon arrival. You are officially off the clock."*

Wyatt let out a low, genuine laugh, shaking his head. He looked at Sarah, the heavy weight of the last four days completely evaporating from his broad shoulders.

"Did you hear that?" Wyatt asked, a mischievous spark lighting up his dark eyes. "We're off the clock."

"I heard," Sarah smiled, her own eyes shining with bright, uninhibited joy. "What do you want to do with our first day off?"

"I think," Wyatt said, stepping forward and pulling her flush against his chest, "we have an appointment in the Captain's Quarters. There's a VCR up there flashing 07:45 that demands our attention."

Ten minutes later, the heavy pneumatic door to the Captain's Quarters slid shut, sealing them inside the spacious, wood-paneled room.

Wyatt had made a quick detour to the galley, carrying two tall glasses of reconstituted orange juice and a small metal bowl filled with the freshly harvested, brilliant red strawberries from Sarah's garden.

He set the snacks down on the retro-futuristic nightstand and climbed into the massive, queen-sized bed. Sarah was already there, wearing her soft heather-gray t-shirt and denim jeans, propped up against a mountain of thick, sterile-plastic-free pillows.

Wyatt picked up the chunky, matte-black IR remote from the console.

He pressed the heavy mechanical power button. The massive 27-inch CRT television flared to life with a sharp crackle of static, bathing the room in a cool, flickering glow. He aimed the remote at the silver VCR and hit *PLAY*.

The machine ground heavily, the mechanical spools catching the magnetic tape.

On the screen, the static cleared, replaced by the warm, slightly degraded analog tracking of a vintage 1980s studio logo. The heavy, ticking sound of dozens of clocks filled the room, followed immediately by the iconic, eccentric chaos of a wild-haired professor's laboratory.

Sarah let out a delighted, utterly relaxed sigh. She shifted across the mattress, curling up flush against Wyatt’s side, resting her head comfortably on his shoulder.

Wyatt wrapped his arm around her, popping one of the incredibly sweet, fresh strawberries into his mouth. He looked at the screen as the skateboard-riding teenager plugged his guitar into a massive amplifier, and then looked down at the woman he loved, safe and warm in his arms.

They ended up completely losing track of time, letting the VCR run through the entire beloved time-travel trilogy back-to-back.

Their absolute favorite was the second installment. As the movie depicted the "distant future" of the year 2015, Wyatt and Sarah couldn't stop laughing.

"Flying cars," Wyatt chuckled, shaking his head as the silver gull-wing time machine touched down in a retro-futuristic skyway. "The writers were so incredibly optimistic."

"It's 2044," Sarah giggled, resting her hand flat on his chest. "We just spent the last four days running for our lives from a megacorporation that still makes its grunts ride on heavy diesel ground transports. We have faster-than-light travel, but still no flying cars."

By the time they reached the third movie, they were both comfortably exhausted, happily immersed in the dusty, steam-powered aesthetic of the 1885 Old West frontier town.

But during a bustling town festival scene on the screen, Wyatt suddenly frowned, squinting at the flickering CRT monitor.

"Wait," Wyatt said, pointing the IR remote at the screen to pause the tape. The image froze with a heavy line of static tracking across the middle. He pointed at the town's frontier band playing on stage. "Look at those three guys spinning the acoustic guitars. Is that...?"

Sarah sat up slightly, squinting at the screen. She looked at the three musicians with their massive, iconic chest-length beards, cheap western dusters, and cheap sunglasses.

"No way," Sarah laughed, genuinely amazed. "Is that really that bearded Texas rock trio? Playing a country-acoustic version of their own synth-rock anthem in an 1880s clock tower dedication?"

"The director just snuck them right in there," Wyatt grinned, hitting *PLAY* again to let the upbeat, twangy music resume.

Outside the hull, the universe was a terrifying, violent place. But inside *The Nomad*, hurtling through the spacetime fold at impossible speeds while laughing over a sixty-year-old movie cameo, everything was absolutely perfect.
