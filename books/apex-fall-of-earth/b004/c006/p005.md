# Part 5: The Blank Slate – Thursday, October 27 to Tuesday, November 1, 2044 (FTL Transit)

For the next six days, *The Nomad* became a universe unto itself.

The profound, unbroken isolation of the spacetime fold provided something neither Wyatt nor Sarah had experienced since the day they signed their corporate contracts: time to breathe. There were no shift klaxons. There were no quotas.

But the trauma of the Nevada Test and Training Range didn't just vanish overnight. It manifested in a quiet, undeniable physical tether between them. Wherever Wyatt went, Sarah went. If Wyatt was deep in the engine room checking a pressure valve, Sarah was sitting on a rusted crate five feet away with her notebook. If Sarah was in the Hydroponics Bay pruning the tomato vines, Wyatt was sitting in the aisle, tinkering with a piece of salvaged tech. They never let each other out of their line of sight. The fear of forced separation was still too fresh, too deeply ingrained by the horrors of the subterranean bunker.

On the second day of transit, they tackled the ship's maintenance.

Wyatt had fully expected to spend his week fighting failing gaskets and rusted pneumatics, but as he pulled the heavy access panels off the primary life-support junctions in the lower ring, he found a mechanic's paradise.

"The rusted exterior is just camouflage," Wyatt said, his voice echoing slightly in the narrow access shaft. He wiped a perfectly clean, freshly greased gear with his thumb. "This paranoid billionaire spared absolutely no expense. He kept every primary system in pristine condition. There's not a stripped bolt or a frayed wire anywhere in the primary grid."

Sarah handed him a heavy iron wrench from the red toolbox, her bare feet silent on the grated floor plates. "So she's not going to fall apart on us?"

"Not a chance," Wyatt grinned, tightening a perfectly machined locking nut. "She’s built like a tank. The only damage is a few cosmetic dents and scratches on the outer hull."

Sarah leaned against the bulkhead, watching him work. She reached up, absentmindedly tracing the faded *HAYES* nametape on the black utility shirt she had finally discarded, opting today for one of the soft cotton t-shirts from the executive stash.

"Wyatt," Sarah said thoughtfully. "When the NTTR collapsed... all the corporate servers went down with it, right?"

"Everything in Sector 1 is buried under millions of tons of bedrock," Wyatt confirmed, sliding out from under the manifold. "Why?"

"I was just thinking about the HR algorithm," she murmured. "Our intake files, our biometric logs, our contracts... they're gone. We don't have datapads anymore. We don't have physical ID cards or passports. The copies on Earth are ash, and the originals are lightyears away on our home planets."

Wyatt set the wrench down, the profound reality of her words settling over him.

"We have absolutely no documentation proving who we are," Sarah whispered, a slow, wondrous smile breaking across her face.

"We're blank slates," Wyatt agreed, stepping closer and wrapping his arms around her waist. He pulled her flush against him in the narrow corridor. "Apex doesn't own our names anymore. Nobody does. We get to decide exactly who we are from now on."

By the fourth day of the jump, the quiet domesticity had entirely healed their exhausted nerves. The heavy, dead-eyed S.H.O.P. Marine masks had been completely discarded.

That evening, they found themselves in the 2044-era tech deck, standing inside the heavily soundproofed analog music studio.

The acoustic foam lining the walls made the room feel incredibly intimate. Wyatt walked over to the rack of perfectly preserved instruments and lifted a heavy, solid-body electric guitar from its stand. He plugged the thick copper cable into a massive, vintage amplifier stack in the corner.

He flipped the power toggle. The amp hummed with a low, potent analog hiss.

Across the room, Sarah was sitting on a padded stool in front of a sprawling, physical drum machine. She was tapping the heavy rubber pads, her eyes lighting up as each one produced a crisp, synthesized snare or a deep, booming bass kick.

Wyatt adjusted the volume dial on his guitar and struck a heavy, distorted power chord.

The sound blasted through the soundproofed room, a rich, aggressive wall of noise that vibrated right through the soles of their bare feet.

Sarah actually jumped, laughing out loud at the sheer volume of it.

"Too loud?" Wyatt yelled over the ringing feedback, a massive grin on his face.

"No!" Sarah shouted back, her eyes shining with pure, unadulterated joy. "Play it louder!"

For the last month, they had lived in absolute terror of making too much noise. They had whispered in maintenance closets and walked on eggshells to avoid drawing the wrath of a S.H.O.P. Foreman—the miserable, low-level managers who treated the grunts just as horribly as the executives did. They had lived in fear of waking up the subterranean monsters beneath the bedrock.

But here, wrapped in the spacetime fold, they were untouchable. No S.H.O.P. Foreman was going to bang on the bulkhead door and write them up. No anomaly was going to hear them through the vacuum of space.

Sarah laid down a clumsy, chaotic, but incredibly enthusiastic beat on the drum machine. Wyatt followed her lead, shredding a messy, heavily distorted rock riff that he hadn't played since he was a teenager. They weren't musical geniuses; they were just two kids who had survived the end of the world, reveling in the absolute freedom of making as much noise as humanly possible.

On the fifth day, they had their first official date night.

Sarah had spent the afternoon in the Hydroponics Bay, carefully harvesting a basket of fresh, deep-space greens, crisp dwarf-carrots, and a handful of earthy mushrooms.

Wyatt had taken on the galley. He raided the billionaire's vacuum-sealed executive freezer and pulled out two thick, premium cuts of actual beef—an absolute impossibility for anyone below the Apex Board of Directors.

He pan-seared the steaks in the heavy iron skillet, filling the narrow brushed-steel kitchen with a rich, savory aroma that completely banished the lingering memory of gray nutrient paste.

They ate at the small, square dining table securely bolted to the grated floor. They were both barefoot, dressed in soft civilian clothes, sharing a bottle of reconstituted red wine Wyatt had found tucked behind the dry goods.

They clinked their heavy ceramic mugs together, toasting to the empty universe outside their window. They laughed, they ate, and they treated the massive dreadnought like it was a luxury apartment. The ghosts of Nevada were finally, truly behind them.
