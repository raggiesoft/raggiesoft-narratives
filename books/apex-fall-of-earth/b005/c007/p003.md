# Part 3: Tax Evasion and Timber – Sunday, May 12, 2045 – 18:45

They left the sterile, brilliantly lit medical wing in a complete daze of absolute euphoria.

The pneumatic elevator carried them back up to the main living quarters, but they bypassed the kitchen and the library entirely, heading straight for the master suite. If they were going to head down to the Fabrication Foundry to start planning the future, the Eden Rule had to be suspended. Heavy machinery required clothes.

They walked into the massive dressing room. Wyatt pulled a fresh black t-shirt over his head and grabbed his heavy canvas work pants.

Beside him, Sarah picked up the soft, perfectly broken-in denim jeans she had claimed from the billionaire's stash. She pulled them up her legs, but as she reached for the metal button at the waist, she stopped.

She let out a sudden, breathless laugh.

Wyatt turned around, pausing as he buckled his belt. "What's funny?"

"Wyatt, look," Sarah giggled, holding the two sides of the denim waistband apart. There was a solid, undeniable two-inch gap. The sudden, massive hormonal bloating from the triplets made it physically impossible to button them without aggressively digging into her swollen abdomen.

She looked up at him, a look of profound, beautiful realization washing over her face.

"I can't wear these anymore," Sarah said, dropping the denim and letting it fall to the floor. She rested her hands over her stomach, her eyes shining with a deeply maternal pride. "Wyatt... I need maternity clothes."

Wyatt’s heart practically melted. The absolute reality of their situation hit him all over again. He walked over, wrapping his arms around her and kissing her deeply.

"A massive milestone, Mrs. Colton," Wyatt murmured against her lips. He looked up toward the ceiling. "Aria, can the molecular printers in the foundry handle textiles? We're going to need a completely new wardrobe for Sarah."

*"Affirmative, Wyatt,"* Aria chimed warmly. *"The secondary printers can weave high-tensile cotton, elastane, and supportive polymers. I can synthesize a complete maternity wardrobe tailored to accommodate a rapid multiple-gestation expansion. I will add it to the production queue."*

For now, Sarah simply pulled on a pair of the billionaire's loose, oversized sweatpants with an elastic waistband and one of Wyatt's spare, baggy t-shirts. It swallowed her small frame, but it was incredibly comfortable.

As Wyatt sat on the edge of the dressing room bench to lace up his heavy leather work boots, a completely different, highly pragmatic thought suddenly crossed his mind.

"You know," Wyatt chuckled, shaking his head as he pulled the laces tight. "When Aria corrected the math downstairs... she reminded us that in Galactic Standard Time, it's actually mid-November right now. Not May."

"Right," Sarah nodded, pulling on her own hiking boots. "The planet's calendar versus the universe's calendar. Why?"

"Because," Wyatt smirked, looking up at her with a mischievous glint in his dark eyes, "my home planet—the water-world—operates on a universal tax code. It doesn't matter if you're working on the core rigs or contracted off-world; as long as you're a breathing citizen, they tax your existence. And their fiscal year ends in December."

Sarah raised an eyebrow, a smile playing on her lips. "Are you telling me you owe back-taxes?"

"I definitely owe for 2044," Wyatt laughed, standing up and crossing his arms. "But the best part is, their automated galactic revenue system always sends the physical bill to your last known corporate address."

Sarah started to giggle, the image perfectly forming in her mind. "The Nevada Test and Training Range."

"Exactly," Wyatt grinned broadly, walking over to pull his wife against his side. "Earth is currently a biologically inert, shattered husk covered in toxic ash and radioactive glass. Good luck to the repo men trying to deliver a tax bill to a molten tungsten crater in Sector 4. Because I am certainly not giving them my forwarding address."

"Tax evasion," Sarah laughed out loud, the sound echoing brightly in the closet. "You stole a dreadnought, you kidnapped an Apex contractor, and now you're dodging the planetary IRS. You're a menace, Mr. Colton."

"I'm a pioneer," Wyatt corrected smoothly, kissing her temple. "Now, let's go to the machine shop. I have a nursery to build."

They walked hand-in-hand to the elevator and took it down to Sub-Level 2.

The Fabrication Foundry was just as they had left it the day before, humming with intense, localized power. In the secondary hangar, the sleek, aerodynamic shell of *The Nomad's* new runabout was already halfway printed, the blue lasers dancing across the titanium frame.

But Wyatt didn't go to the heavy machining bays. He walked straight to the primary architectural terminal overlooking the foundry floor and brought up the holographic blueprint of the mountain estate.

"Okay, Aria," Wyatt said, his massive hands resting on the cold glass of the desk. "Highlight the residential wing. We have eight empty guest suites."

The glowing blue wireframe of the hallway expanded.

"I want the largest suite closest to the master bedroom," Wyatt instructed. The system immediately illuminated a massive, sprawling room located directly adjacent to their own quarters. "Strip out the billionaire's furniture. Clear the floor plan."

The holographic beds, dressers, and seating areas vanished, leaving a perfectly blank, glowing canvas.

"We have three babies coming," Wyatt muttered, his mechanic's brain running a hundred miles a minute as he looked at the square footage. "We could technically put them each in their own room. We have the space."

"No," Sarah said immediately, stepping up beside him.

She looked at the glowing footprint of the room, her maternal instincts flaring with absolute certainty. She reached out and placed her hand over his on the glass.

"One room, Wyatt. I want a single nursery. They shared the same space from the very beginning. They're going to grow up together, and I want them to sleep together. I want them to always know they have each other."

Wyatt looked at her, his expression softening instantly. He completely understood. The fierce, unbreakable bond they shared as a couple was exactly what they wanted to foster in their children.

"One nursery," Wyatt agreed without a second thought. "Aria, partition the northern wall for three identical cribs. I want them constructed from the heavy, raw timber of the alien pines in the valley. We'll mill it down here so it's perfectly smooth, but I want it to look natural. Like it belongs to Eden."

*"Logging material requests and initiating schematic drafts for three heavy-timber cribs,"* Aria confirmed, three beautifully rendered, sturdy wooden cribs popping into the holographic layout side-by-side.

Wyatt and Sarah stood shoulder-to-shoulder in the industrial hum of the foundry, staring at the three glowing blue cribs hovering in the air.

Just a few weeks ago, their biggest concern was surviving a twelve-hour shift without getting written up by a shotgun-toting S.H.O.P. Foreman. Now, they were officially committing tax evasion on a multi-billion-credit estate, designing a wooden nursery for three little miracles they hadn't even met yet.

"Three of them," Wyatt whispered, a massive, disbelieving smile on his face as he stared at the hologram.

"We are going to be so incredibly tired," Sarah laughed, leaning her head happily against his shoulder.

"We are," Wyatt agreed, wrapping his arm around her. "But it's going to be the best kind of tired."
