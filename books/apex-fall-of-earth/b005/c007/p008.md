# Part 8: The Toll of Three – Sunday, June 18, 2045 – 10:00

The silver, teardrop-shaped atmospheric shuttle glided silently over the sprawling alien pine canopy, banking smoothly as it descended toward the river valley.

It had been over a month since the apocalyptic super-cell storm had drowned the basin. The violent, muddy lake had long since drained away, and the intense summer sun of Planet Eden had spent the last several weeks baking the waterlogged earth dry.

Wyatt eased the digital yoke backward, bringing the shuttle into a gentle hover. The repulsorlifts flattened the tall, newly sprouted river grass as the landing gear made contact with the solid, sun-baked silt.

"We're down," Wyatt announced, powering off the main drive.

Sarah unbuckled her four-point harness, her face lighting up with a bright, nostalgic smile. She was wearing her molecularly printed maternity leggings and a loose, comfortable tunic that perfectly accommodated the distinct, incredibly rapid expansion of her pregnant belly.

They popped the gull-wing doors and stepped out into the warm, 25°C morning air.

The valley had healed beautifully. The river was back within its banks, running crystal-clear over the polished stones, and the towering pines smelled sharply of fresh sap and blooming summer foliage.

They walked hand-in-hand down the gentle slope, heading toward the exact spot where they had staked out their very first 96-square-meter garden grid.

The neon-orange string and the wooden stakes had been completely washed away by the floodwaters. But as they reached the center of the clearing, Sarah stopped, pointing down at the ground.

"Look," Sarah murmured, a bittersweet warmth in her voice.

The earth had been scoured, but the deep trenches Wyatt had dug for the potatoes and the turned soil from Sarah's hoe had left faint, geometric scars baked permanently into the hardened silt. The ghost of their first harvest was still there.

"We made our mark," Wyatt smiled, wrapping his arm around her waist and pulling her back against his chest. "Even the flood couldn't completely erase it."

Sarah leaned into his solid warmth, taking a deep breath of the valley air. She intended to savor the smell of the damp earth and the pine needles.

Instead, her highly sensitized olfactory receptors completely short-circuited.

The scent of the river silt—a smell she had found incredibly grounding just a month ago—suddenly hit the back of her throat with a thick, suffocating wave of pure biological revulsion. The sharp, sweet tang of the pine sap amplified it a hundredfold.

Sarah stiffened, her hands flying to her mouth.

"Sarah?" Wyatt asked, instantly feeling the rigid tension spike through her body.

Before she could even answer, a violent, uncontrollable wave of nausea slammed into her. She ripped herself out of Wyatt's arms, stumbling forward two steps before her knees hit the baked silt.

She dropped heavily onto all fours, her chest heaving as she violently emptied her stomach onto the ground.

"Sarah!" Wyatt dropped to the dirt right beside her, his massive hands instantly hovering over her back, pulling her hair away from her face.

It wasn't just a mild bout of morning sickness. It hit her like a localized earthquake. Her body convulsed as she retched again and again, her stomach muscles clenching painfully as she expelled the entire contents of her breakfast.

The biological math of her condition was ruthlessly collecting its toll. Building a single human life-support system is taxing; building three distinct placentas simultaneously requires an astronomical, exponential surge of human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) and progesterone. Her endocrine system was completely flooded, operating at three times the standard biological capacity, and her body simply couldn't handle the chemical equilibrium.

Sarah let out a ragged, gasping sob, trying to catch her breath, but another vicious wave of nausea seized her core. She retched again, dry-heaving violently until her ribs ached and the edges of her vision began to spark with dark, fuzzy static.

"I've got you, I've got you," Wyatt gritted out, his voice a low, intensely focused anchor. He kept one hand firmly on her back, supporting her trembling frame.

Sarah slumped forward, her arms shaking so badly they threatened to give out entirely. The world around her was spinning, the roar of the river fading into a high-pitched ringing in her ears. She felt lightheaded, entirely drained of color and energy, hovering dangerously on the precipice of passing out.

"Wyatt," Sarah whimpered weakly, her eyes fluttering shut as her head drooped toward the dirt. "I'm dizzy... I can't..."

"You're okay. We're leaving," Wyatt commanded.

He didn't wait for her to try and stand. He slid his thick arms under her knees and behind her back, effortlessly scooping her up from the dirt. He cradled her tightly against his chest, completely ignoring the sick on her clothes, and sprinted back up the slope toward the silver runabout.

Sarah let her head loll against his shoulder, her breathing shallow and erratic. She felt entirely broken, her body completely hijacked by the three tiny lives demanding every ounce of her energy.

Wyatt reached the shuttle, gently depositing her into the co-pilot's seat and hastily securing the harness across her chest.

He vaulted into the pilot's seat, slamming his hand down on the ignition sensors. The repulsorlifts whined violently as he pushed the throttles to maximum, tearing the shuttle off the ground and launching it in a steep, aggressive arc back toward the mountain.

"Aria!" Wyatt roared into the comms, banking the ship so hard the inertial dampeners struggled to keep up. "Prep the medical wing! Bio-bed one! Sarah is severely dehydrated and unresponsive!"

"Acknowledged, Wyatt. Medical suite initialized. Intravenous hydration and antiemetic protocols are prepped and standing by," Aria responded instantly, sensing the raw panic in his voice.

Wyatt kept one hand on the yoke, reaching out with the other to firmly grip Sarah's trembling, ice-cold hand.

"Stay with me, Sarah," Wyatt urged, his eyes locked on the rapidly approaching, camouflaged rock face of The Sanctuary. "We're almost home."
