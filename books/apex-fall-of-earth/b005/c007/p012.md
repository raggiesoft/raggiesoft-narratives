# Part 12: The Foundation – Sunday, June 18, 2045 – 14:00

The frictionless glide of the brushed-aluminum wheelchair was completely silent as Wyatt pushed Sarah through the wide, open doorways of the estate's library.

Her bizarre, highly specific craving for sausage and strawberry jam had successfully stayed down, and the heavy wave of nausea was fully replaced by a calm, focused energy. Wyatt parked her wheelchair smoothly at the head of the massive holographic glass table, engaging the brakes before pulling up a heavy leather chair right beside her.

They were officially off the clock from physical labor, but the architectural planning had just begun.

"Alright, Aria," Wyatt said, leaning his heavy forearms on the cool glass. "Bring up the blueprints for the nursery in the eastern residential wing. I need to finalize the schematics for the Fabrication Foundry."

The center of the table rippled with a soft blue light, projecting a flawless, three-dimensional wireframe of the massive bedroom adjacent to the master suite.

Three heavy-timber cribs materialized along the northern wall, sitting side-by-side in perfect alignment.

"They shared the exact same space from the second they were conceived," Wyatt murmured, looking at the three glowing beds. He reached over, resting his hand gently on Sarah’s thigh. "They're going to share a room. But Aria, I need the absolute baseline for 2045-era pediatric safety. I know how to build an engine, but I don't know the structural tolerances for a newborn."

"Accessing standard pediatric safety parameters, Wyatt," Aria responded, her synthesized voice echoing warmly in the mahogany-paneled room. "For the cribs, the timber slats must be spaced no more than two and three-eighths inches apart to prevent entrapment. The mattresses must be constructed from firm, breathable, high-density memory-foam to eliminate suffocation risks, and the alien pine must be sealed with a completely non-toxic, biologically inert polymer."

Wyatt nodded, his mechanic's brain instantly memorizing the tolerances. "Update the CAD files in the foundry. No sharp corners, flush hardware only. I'll cut the alien pine myself tomorrow."

"They're going to be beautiful, Wyatt," Sarah smiled, perfectly content to let him handle the heavy machinery. She turned her attention to the other half of the glowing table. "But while you build the furniture, I need to build their minds. Aria, pull up the educational archives. We need a curriculum."

The blue wireframe of the nursery shifted to the right, making room for a massive, cascading web of academic subjects—mathematics, localized botany, physics, literature, and history.

"We have three kids coming right now, but eventually, we're going to have fifty thousand," Sarah said, her dark eyes scanning the staggering amount of data. "We need a structured K-through-12 educational track. Aria, I need you to organize these modules into a scalable curriculum."

"I can easily structure the data into progressive learning modules, Sarah," Aria chimed. The web instantly reorganized itself into a neat, color-coded timeline. "However, I must remind you of my safety guardrails. While I can serve as an interactive encyclopedia and grade standardized assessments, I cannot act as a primary educator or an ethical guide. The interpretation of the data, the emotional support of the students, and the actual teaching must be conducted by human agents."

"I know," Sarah said softly, an overwhelming wave of maternal purpose washing over her. She looked at Wyatt. "No corporate indoctrination algorithms. No machines telling our kids how to think or what their labor is worth. We are the teachers."

"Professor Colton," Wyatt grinned, deeply admiring the fierce, pioneer spark in her eyes. "I like the sound of that. I'll handle the physical engineering and agriculture; you handle the sciences and the humanities."

"Deal," Sarah agreed, reaching out to scroll through the organized curriculum. "But Wyatt... if we're finalizing how they'll sleep, and we're finalizing how they'll learn, we can't keep putting off how they'll live."

She tapped the glass, and the educational web faded, replaced by the golden-hued, holographic text they had drafted just a few weeks prior: the constitution for their post-scarcity agrarian democracy.

Wyatt looked at the glowing document. The sheer magnitude of what it represented hung heavily in the quiet library.

This wasn't just a thought experiment anymore. With Sarah’s expanding belly serving as the ultimate biological clock, the theoretical future was rapidly becoming their immediate reality. Ellie, Nora, and Leo were going to be born into whatever system they chose today.

"No CEOs," Wyatt said, his voice dropping into a low, uncompromising rumble. He read the preamble hovering in the air. "No red-banded supervisors, no fiat currency, and no one starving because they missed a quota."

"A society built on mutual contribution," Sarah read aloud, her hand resting over her womb. "Every citizen has an unalienable right to the estate's resources—food, water, shelter, and medical care—in exchange for proportional labor to maintain the colony. Leaders are elected directly by the populace to manage distribution, not to hoard wealth."

She looked over at Wyatt. They had spent their entire lives as expendable cogs in the Apex Defense Solutions meat-grinder. Now, sitting barefoot in a multi-billion-credit mountain sanctuary, they had the absolute power to ensure their children never knew that kind of fear.

"Are we doing this?" Sarah asked, her heart beating a steady, powerful rhythm against her ribs.

Wyatt didn't hesitate. He placed his massive, calloused hand flat against the holographic glass, right over the signature line of the digital document.

"We're doing it," Wyatt confirmed, his dark eyes locking onto hers with absolute devotion. "Aria, register my biometric signature. I officially ratify the agrarian constitution for Planet Eden."

"Biometric accepted, Wyatt. Logged and secured," Aria chimed.

Sarah smiled, a fierce, protective warmth flooding her chest. She placed her own hand on the glass right beside his.

"Aria, register my biometric signature," Sarah commanded clearly. "The constitution is ratified. Let it be the absolute law of the Sanctuary."

"Biometric accepted, Sarah. The foundational government of Planet Eden is now officially established in the central archives," Aria announced, a faint, triumphant trill in her synthesized voice as the golden text solidified and vanished into the secure servers.

Wyatt leaned over, wrapping his arm around Sarah’s shoulders and pulling her into a deep, lingering kiss.

They were a mechanic and a farm girl who had stolen a spaceship to escape a dying world. But as they sat in the quiet, mahogany library, they were officially the founding mother and father of an entirely new, deeply human civilization.
