# Part 7: The Canopy – Tuesday, May 7, 2045 – 19:00

The digital scrub had bought them anonymity on the galactic grid, but as Wyatt looked out the viewport of the tech room at the fading golden light of the evening, his mechanic’s pragmatism refused to let him relax.

"Aria’s right. The Ghost Protocol hides us from the long-range scanners," Wyatt said, crossing his arms as he stared out at the exposed, flat expanse of the silicate plateau. "But if an Apex scout ship actually enters this atmosphere and does a visual sweep, the digital handshake won't matter. They’re looking for a specific, heavily armored cylinder with a massive radar dish. If they see *Delta 9* sitting in the middle of an open rock, they’ll just shoot first and read the transponder data later."

Sarah followed his gaze. The dreadnought was massive, its oxidized hazard-yellow armor sticking out like a sore thumb against the natural, vibrant greens and browns of the valley.

"So we change what the ship looks like," Sarah said, her analytical mind immediately shifting to problem-solving. "And we put it somewhere they can't see it."

Wyatt looked at her, a slow, appreciative smile breaking through his tension. "You want to hide a multi-ton heavy hauler in the woods."

"I want to bury it," Sarah cor7rected, her eyes locking onto the dense, towering tree line that bordered the eastern edge of the plateau. The alien pine-like trees were incredibly massive, their trunks easily three meters thick, with sprawling canopies that cast deep, impenetrable shadows. "If we back the ship into that tree line, the canopy will cover the hull from aerial sweeps."

"And we change the silhouette," Wyatt added, the mechanical logistics already clicking into place. He looked up at the ceiling. "Aria, the massive parabolic radar dish on the upper spine. Is it strictly necessary for our localized sensors?"

*"Negative, Wyatt,"* Aria responded smoothly. *"The parabolic dish is a relic of the ship's original design as an Apex listening post, intended for deep-space subspace intercepts. For localized atmospheric sweeps and orbital tracking, I can seamlessly transition to the modern phased-array sensors integrated flush against the outer hull plating."*

"Retract the dish," Wyatt ordered. "Fold it flat against the hull. We don't need it acting as a giant, metallic beacon."

*"Executing physical retraction,"* Aria confirmed.

A deep, grinding vibration echoed through the ceiling as the massive, iconic radar dish—the defining visual characteristic of Legacy Asset 44-A—slowly folded backward, locking flush into a recessed housing on the roof.

"And the solar arrays," Sarah pointed out. "Keep them fully deployed. Apex thinks this ship is a rusted tin can running on a localized fission reactor. If a scout gets a glimpse of us through the trees, they'll see a flat, winged solar-freighter, not a blocky listening post."

Wyatt laughed, completely amazed by her tactical foresight. "They don't even know the solar panels exist. The billionaire hid them under the armor plating."

He paused, a deeper realization hitting him as he looked at the glowing server tower in the corner of the room.

"They don't know about the panels. They don't know about the 1985 VCR or the 2044 workstations," Wyatt said, his voice dropping into a low, sharp register. "And they definitely don't know about Aria."

Sarah looked at the server. She knew exactly how the megacorporation operated on Earth and Acheron.

"Apex hates AI," Sarah murmured, the terrifying reality of the dictatorship fueling her confidence. "The High Directors think generative intelligence is too expensive to maintain and too unpredictable to control. That's why they rely on the S.H.O.P. Marines. That's why they use forced labor and reeducation camps. Flesh and blood is cheaper than silicon in their empire."

"Exactly," Wyatt nodded, a fierce, predatory gleam in his eyes. "Apex is looking for two terrified, minimum-wage grunts flying completely blind in a 1970s analog relic. They have absolutely no idea they're hunting a cloaked dreadnought co-piloted by a state-of-the-art supercomputer."

*"I find their reliance on organic subjugation highly inefficient and morally objectionable,"* Aria chimed in, her synthesized voice carrying a distinctly protective edge. *"I will ensure our digital footprint remains entirely obfuscated."*

"Let's get her moved," Wyatt said, taking Sarah's hand.

They sprinted barefoot back up the ladders to the Operations center. Wyatt slid into the captain's chair, his hands gripping the heavy throttles.

"Aria, feed the rear-proximity sensors to the main CRT."

The amber screen flickered, displaying a wireframe view of the massive forest behind them.

Wyatt engaged the repulsorlifts. The deep, guttural purr of the engines vibrated through the deck plates as *The Nomad* slowly lifted five meters off the bedrock.

"Backing her in," Wyatt said, pulling the heavy steering yoke backward.

The massive ship glided in reverse. The sound of thick, heavy branches scraping and snapping against the ablative armor echoed through the hull as Wyatt forced the dreadnought deep into the thickest part of the tree line. The massive, deployed solar wings acted like a canopy of their own, sliding seamlessly beneath the towering alien pines.

He pushed the ship backward until the dense foliage completely swallowed the forward viewport, plunging the Operations center into deep, natural shadows.

Wyatt cut the throttles. The landing struts groaned as they settled into the soft, rich earth of the forest floor.

He killed the main drive, leaving only the silent, localized life-support running off the solar batteries.

They walked back down to the cargo bay and lowered the heavy ramp. It didn't clatter against hard bedrock this time; it sank with a soft *thud* into a bed of thick, fragrant pine needles and dark moss.

Wyatt and Sarah stepped down the ramp, ducking under a massive, leafy branch that had draped itself over the airlock.

They walked out into the clearing they had measured earlier and turned around to look at the ship.

It was virtually invisible. The dark, oxidized armor blended perfectly into the deep shadows of the towering trunks. The massive solar wings looked like a bizarre extension of the forest canopy, filtering the fading sunlight. From the sky, it would look like nothing more than a dense grove of ancient trees.

Their digital footprint was scrubbed. Their physical silhouette was completely altered. Their ship was buried in the woods.

Sarah stepped back until she was standing inside the neon-orange string grid that marked their future living room. She looked at the hidden dreadnought, and then out toward the rushing river as the sun finally dipped below the horizon, painting the sky in brilliant strokes of violet and gold.

Wyatt wrapped his arms around her from behind, resting his chin on her shoulder.

"Let the Apex trackers sweep the quadrant," Wyatt whispered, his voice a steady, unbreakable anchor in the twilight. "They're looking for ghosts. And we don't live there anymore."
