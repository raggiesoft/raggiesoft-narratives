# Part 1: The Maiden Voyage – Saturday, May 11, 2045 – 13:45

Wyatt sat in the pilot’s seat of the sleek, silver runabout, his hands resting lightly on the incredibly streamlined digital control yoke. The leather was soft and pristine, the cabin smelling like fresh polymer and clean air.

"Before we take this out," Sarah said, pausing with her hand hovering over the co-pilot's harness release. "If we're going to really explore an entire planet, we shouldn't go empty-handed. We have a cargo hold in the back. We should pack for a camping trip."

Wyatt looked over at her, a wide, adventurous grin spreading across his face.

"I love the way you think, Mrs. Colton," Wyatt agreed, instantly unbuckling his own harness.

They hopped out of the shuttle and spent the next twenty minutes raiding the estate. Wyatt grabbed the heavy, insulated deployment sleeping bag, two Mark-IV flashlights, and a medical kit from the armory. Sarah hit the master kitchen, loading a canvas duffel bag with vacuum-sealed sandwiches, cold cuts, fresh strawberries from the hydroponics bay, and several canteens of purified glacial water.

They stowed the gear securely in the runabout's aft cargo compartment and climbed back into the cockpit, snapping their four-point harnesses into place.

"Alright, Aria," Wyatt said, his thumbs resting over the ignition sensors. "We're loaded up. Give us a door."

"Initiating secondary hangar bay doors," Aria chimed through the shuttle's pristine internal comms. "Pre-flight diagnostics are green. Battery reserves are at one hundred percent."

Directly in front of the teardrop-shaped shuttle, a massive section of the cavern wall silently began to split apart, revealing the misty, rain-washed sky.

Wyatt engaged the repulsorlifts. Unlike the bone-rattling, guttural roar of The Nomad's massive nuclear drives, the runabout woke up with a smooth, virtually silent electronic hum.

He eased the yoke forward. The shuttle glided effortlessly off the hangar floor, completely unburdened by the sluggish, heavy-iron drag of the dreadnought. They slipped through the camouflaged rock face and out into the open air of Planet Eden.

The massive, tinted hydro-glass canopy offered a breathtaking, panoramic view of the world. The bruised gray clouds of the super-cell storm were breaking apart, allowing brilliant rays of the golden sun, Helios, to hit the landscape.

"Take us down to the valley," Sarah requested, her hands resting on her knees. "I want to see our First Landing Site."

Wyatt banked the yoke, sending the agile shuttle into a smooth, sweeping dive toward the river valley below.

As they dropped altitude, the sheer scale of the ecological anomaly came into focus. The winding, crystal-clear river they had splashed in just days prior was entirely gone. The valley was a massive, churning lake of muddy brown water stretching from canyon wall to canyon wall. The towering alien pines that had once offered them shade were now submerged halfway up their massive trunks.

"Telemetry confirms the water levels are steadily dropping," Aria reported as Wyatt brought the shuttle into a hovering glide just a few meters above the muddy surface. "The floodwaters crested at exactly twenty-one meters at the height of the storm. Yesterday's readings indicated nineteen meters. The current depth is fifteen meters and slowly draining back toward the primary riverbed."

"Fifteen meters of standing water," Wyatt muttered, shaking his head. He looked down through the glass canopy. The water was thick with displaced topsoil and floating debris from the upper mountains. "Even if we wanted to set her down, the alluvial mud would swallow the landing gear whole. The ground is going to be incredibly soft for weeks."

"It's okay," Sarah said softly, looking out at the drowned clearing where they had planted their very first garden. The neon-orange string and the tilled soil were completely washed away, but she didn't feel a sense of loss. The mountain estate had given them everything they needed. "The valley needs time to heal. We have an entire planet to see."

"An entire planet," Wyatt agreed, his eyes lighting up.

He pulled back on the yoke and pushed the throttles forward.

The runabout surged upward with incredible, silent speed, leaving the flooded valley behind. They climbed higher and higher, breaking through the last remnants of the storm clouds and soaring into the clear, brilliant blue sky.

With a gravity of 1.02 Earth-standard, the physics of the world felt perfectly natural, a comforting rounding error that kept their bones strong and the atmosphere perfectly pressurized. But as they flew westward, cresting the massive mountain range that housed The Sanctuary, the true majesty of Eden unveiled itself.

Through the massive glass canopy, they watched the geography unfold.

They flew over sprawling, emerald-green plains that stretched for hundreds of miles, completely devoid of concrete highways, power lines, or corporate strip mines. They saw massive, thundering waterfalls cutting through deep red-rock canyons, and vast, mirror-like freshwater lakes nestled between snow-capped peaks.

It was an Earth-sized world, entirely untouched and completely pristine.

Sarah pressed her hands against the glass, her breath catching in her throat. "Wyatt, it goes on forever."

"And it's all ours," Wyatt smiled, banking the ship gently to follow the winding path of a massive canyon. "We can go anywhere we want. We can camp anywhere we want."

The sun was slowly beginning its descent toward the western horizon, painting the sky in vibrant shades of violet and gold. They had the deployment sleeping bag and a cargo hold full of food.

"So, Mrs. Colton," Wyatt asked, glancing over at her as the runabout glided smoothly over the pristine landscape. "Where are we setting up camp tonight?"
