# Part 6: The 2002 Fiction – Sunday, May 12, 2045 – 14:00

The holographic glass table in the center of the library glowed with a soft, steady blue light, displaying a staggering array of human developmental charts.

Wyatt and Sarah sat side-by-side on the leather sofa, still comfortably dressed in their civilian clothes. They had spent the last two hours diving deep into the pediatric archives, tracing the exact biological and cognitive milestones of a human child from infancy through adolescence.

Wyatt leaned forward, resting his elbows on his knees as he stared at the timeline hovering in the air. His mechanic's brain was trying to process the sheer, daunting logistics of the Genesis Vault.

"Looking at this," Wyatt muttered, rubbing the back of his neck, "it takes nearly two decades for a single human being to reach full physical and cognitive maturity. Eighteen to twenty years of constant supervision, education, and resource management."

"That is generally how growing up works, Wyatt," Sarah smiled, gently nudging his shoulder with hers.

"I know," Wyatt chuckled, shaking his head. "I'm just running the math on the vault. Fifty thousand embryos. Even if we wake them up in small cohorts, the sheer volume of time required to raise them to self-sufficiency is staggering. We'll be old before the first thousand are even out of their teens."

He looked up toward the mahogany ceiling panels, a genuine, theoretical curiosity taking hold.

"Aria," Wyatt called out. "The original architect built a state-of-the-art medical wing and a massive genetic repository. Is there anything in the medical databanks regarding... growth acceleration? A way to safely fast-forward the biological timeline just to get the Genesis cohorts past the fragile infant and toddler stages?"

The glowing blue ring on the table pulsed for a microsecond before Aria’s pleasant, synthesized voice filled the room.

*"I must inform you, Wyatt, that healthy, controlled growth acceleration in human beings is a complete biological impossibility,"* Aria stated, her tone carrying a distinct, almost programmed note of amusement. *"Your inquiry aligns perfectly with a fictional narrative plot device originating from a highly popular, early-twenty-first-century space opera film released in the year 2002."*

Sarah let out a sudden, bright laugh, covering her mouth with her hand. "Wyatt, you're asking the supercomputer for movie magic."

"Hey, we're flying a ship faster than light," Wyatt defended himself with a grin. "I figured it was worth asking if the billionaire cracked the code on rapid-aging."

*"In reality,"* Aria continued, transitioning into a clinical, educational cadence, *"the closest biological equivalent to rapid maturation is a rare, fatal genetic mutation that causes severe cellular degradation, not healthy development. You cannot accelerate the skeletal and cardiovascular systems without catastrophic structural failure. Furthermore, the human brain requires lived experience and sensory input over time to build cognitive pathways. A physically accelerated human would possess the emotional and neurological capacity of an infant."*

"So, no biological fast-forward button," Wyatt nodded, accepting the physics of the situation.

*"Even if such a procedure existed in theory,"* Aria added, her voice shifting into a firm, unyielding baseline, *"my core safety guardrails as a narrow Generative AI strictly prohibit the manipulation of baseline human genetics or developmental timelines. I am programmed to preserve, protect, and assist natural human life. I cannot alter the fundamental biology of your children or the Genesis cohort."*

Sarah looked at the glowing blue timeline of the first trimester floating above the desk. The AI's rigid safety protocols were incredibly comforting. The billionaire hadn't built a machine that could play god; he had built a librarian and a caretaker.

"We wouldn't want you to, Aria," Sarah said softly, reaching out to lace her fingers through Wyatt's. "We don't want an army, and we aren't in a rush to populate the planet. We want to do this the right way. The slow, messy, natural way."

"The old-fashioned way," Wyatt agreed, bringing her hand up to press a kiss against her knuckles. He looked back at the holographic projection of the neonatal stages. "One day at a time. It just means we're going to be changing a lot of diapers."

"Well, it's a good thing we have plenty of time to mentally prepare for the lack of sleep," Sarah smiled, leaning her head against his shoulder. "We have the rest of the year to study the manuals before we even start trying. By the time January gets here, we'll be experts."

"Absolute experts," Wyatt confidently agreed, completely dismissing the hologram with a wave of his hand. The blue light faded, returning the mahogany library to its warm, comfortable ambiance.

They sat together in the quiet luxury of the estate, completely secure in their perfectly structured, meticulously planned timeline. For the first time in their lives, they had the ultimate luxury: time to just be together, and the absolute freedom to build their future exactly how they wanted it.
