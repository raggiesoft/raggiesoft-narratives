# Part 3: The Husk – Saturday, May 11, 2045 – 09:15

The sprawling master kitchen was clean, the plates were loaded into the automated sanitization basin, and the comforting aroma of French toast was already fading into the ambient, filtered air of the estate.

Wyatt wiped down the black-marble island, entirely satisfied with their morning. He looked over at Sarah, who was finishing the last drops of her reconstituted orange juice.

"Since we're already dressed for it," Wyatt suggested, tossing the damp cloth into a nearby pneumatic chute, "we should head down to Sub-Level 2. I want to see how the foundry is handling *The Nomad's* new hull."

"Let's go," Sarah agreed, sliding off the heavy oak barstool. "I want to see what our new dreadnought looks like."

They left the kitchen, walking side-by-side down the wide stone corridor to the main pneumatic elevator. The brushed-steel doors parted with a soft hiss, and they stepped inside, descending smoothly into the mountain's depths.

When the doors opened onto Sub-Level 2, the warm, quiet luxury of the upper decks vanished. It was replaced by the intense, industrial hum of the Fabrication Foundry and the sharp, metallic tang of vaporized titanium.

Wyatt and Sarah stepped out of the lift, both of them stopping in their tracks as they took in the sheer scale of the automated construction.

The massive, cavernous room was a hive of precise, relentless activity. Dozens of heavy, articulated robotic arms were working in perfect, choreographed synchronization around the massive, cylindrical hull of *The Nomad*. Bright, blinding sparks of plasma welled cascaded to the floor as the drones seamlessly fused the new, custom-printed aerospace plating directly over the rusted, blocky armor of the old listening post.

It didn't look like Outpost Delta 9 anymore. The blunt, ugly nose of the freighter had already been completely encapsulated by sleek, swept-back titanium panels, transforming it from a flying metal brick into a predatory, aerodynamic dreadnought.

"They're moving fast," Wyatt noted, thoroughly impressed as he walked up to the heavy safety glass separating them from the sparks. "Aria's routing the raw tungsten and polymers perfectly. The new hull will be completely sealed by tomorrow."

"It's beautiful," Sarah murmured, resting her hands against the glass. She looked at the massive ship, and then down at the sprawling, empty space on the far side of the hangar floor.

"Wyatt," Sarah said, a pragmatic thought crossing her mind. " *The Nomad* is incredible, but... it's a dreadnought. It's massive, and it runs on a localized fission reactor. If we just want to fly over to the eastern ridge to check the snowpack this winter, or if we want to explore the southern hemisphere for a few hours, firing up a ship that big seems like massive overkill."

Wyatt followed her gaze to the empty hangar floor. His mechanic's brain instantly latched onto the logic.

"You're absolutely right," Wyatt nodded. "It's like taking a tank to the grocery store. We need something smaller. An atmospheric runabout. Something that runs entirely on high-capacity solar batteries and standard repulsorlifts."

He walked over to the secondary command terminal bolted near the safety glass and woke the holographic display.

"Aria," Wyatt called out, his hands flying across the digital keyboard to pull up the foundry's raw material inventory. "I want to commission a secondary build. An atmospheric shuttle. Four seats, light cargo capacity, and completely unarmed. A family car."

*"Acknowledged, Wyatt,"* Aria’s synthesized voice echoed through the industrial roar of the room. *"I have loaded a standard, civilian-grade atmospheric chassis template from the archives. The foundry has sufficient raw titanium and polymerized composites remaining to construct the shuttle without compromising The Nomad's hull integrity. Initializing secondary molecular printers now."*

At the far end of the hangar, a cluster of massive, dormant 3D printers hummed to life, a blue laser grid immediately beginning to map the foundation of their new runabout.

Wyatt smiled, turning away from the terminal. They were building a fleet.

But as the holographic display on the terminal automatically reverted to its standard idle screen, a string of heavily compressed, archived audio files caught Wyatt's eye.

"Aria," Wyatt said, his smile fading slightly as he stared at the screen. "What are these files in the local cache? They're timestamped for yesterday."

*"Those are the final, synthesized data packets intercepted from the Sol System prior to the complete collapse of the human communications grid, Wyatt,"* Aria explained smoothly. *"I compiled them while you were sleeping to provide a definitive conclusion to the events on Earth."*

Sarah walked over, the industrial noise of the foundry suddenly feeling very far away. The memory of the horrific broadcasts they had listened to in the FTL slipstream—the loss of Las Vegas, the evacuation of Los Angeles, and the military's desperate, apocalyptic attempt to burn the Rocky Mountains to the mantle—flooded back.

"Play them," Sarah said softly, bracing herself against the edge of the terminal.

The audio that filtered through the terminal's speakers wasn't human. It was a cold, objective, automated damage assessment from a dying orbital satellite.

*"Planetary stability compromised. The subterranean anomaly has achieved critical mass. Operation Glass Wall successfully halted westward expansion, but the detonation of the Yellowstone Caldera introduced weaponized biological spores to the upper stratosphere."*

Wyatt gripped the edge of the console. Apex Defense Solutions had dropped a kinetic weapon from orbit to cover their tracks, fracturing the mantle and handing the biological nightmare a direct, molten highway right underneath the military's radioactive blockade.

*"The North American continent is no longer geographically cohesive,"* the automated voice droned on. *"Global atmospheric toxicity has reached lethal thresholds. The planet's tectonic plates have shattered. Earth is currently classified as a biologically inert husk."*

The audio feed cut out with a sharp hiss of static, plunging the terminal back into silence.

Sarah stared at the blank screen, the sheer, unimaginable scale of the destruction settling heavily in her chest. The planet she had dreamed of visiting as a little girl on the agricultural colonies, the cradle of humanity, was gone. It had been cracked open and eaten alive.

"A husk," Wyatt whispered, his voice a low, gravelly rasp. He looked up at the ceiling array. "Aria... did the evacuation hold? Did the transport ships make it out?"

*"Affirmative, Wyatt,"* Aria confirmed, her tone shifting to a slightly warmer, reassuring frequency. *"The final transmission from the Global Defense Coalition indicated that the mass off-world evacuation was successful. The majority of the surviving civilian population was relocated to the designated staging flotillas beyond Lunar orbit before the atmosphere collapsed. Humanity survived the loss of the homeworld."*

Sarah let out a long, shaky breath, her eyes welling with a complex mixture of profound grief and intense relief. The billions who had fled would scatter into the stars, joining the vast network of existing colonies. They had lost the planet, but they hadn't lost the species.

Wyatt reached out, pulling Sarah firmly against his chest. He held her tight, anchoring her right there in the noise and the heat of the Fabrication Foundry.

"They made it," Sarah murmured against his shoulder, her hands gripping his black t-shirt. "They're safe."

"They're safe," Wyatt agreed, pressing a fierce kiss to the top of her head. He looked past her, out through the heavy safety glass at the brilliant sparks of plasma welding their new dreadnought together.

Earth was gone, left behind as a scorched, broken monument to corporate hubris. But here, millions of miles away, completely off the grid and entirely untouched by the horrors of the galaxy, the Coltons were already building a new one.
