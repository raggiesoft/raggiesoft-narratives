# Part 2: The Archives – Saturday, May 11, 2045 – 08:30

An hour later, the absolute luxury of the Alaskan King bed finally yielded to the practical reality of their empty stomachs.

Wyatt and Sarah moved into the master suite's massive dressing room. In strict adherence to the kitchen safety protocol they had established the day before, the Eden Rule was temporarily suspended. Wyatt pulled on a pair of comfortable gray canvas shorts and a fitted black t-shirt, while Sarah opted for her favorite denim cut-offs and a soft, oversized white tee.

However, their bare feet remained firmly planted on the warm, heated stone floors as they walked hand-in-hand down the wide corridor to the estate's sprawling master kitchen.

Wyatt took point at the massive induction range. He had raided the stasis pantry and found thick-cut, vacuum-sealed artisan bread and a carton of preserved liquid eggs. Within minutes, the rich, buttery scent of French toast sizzling in the cast-iron skillet filled the room.

Sarah sat sideways on one of the heavy oak barstools at the black-marble island, sipping a mug of hot, reconstituted orange juice. She watched Wyatt expertly flip the golden-brown bread with a spatula.

Her mind, however, was miles away from breakfast. She was still processing the massive, life-altering timeline they had established yesterday.

"Aria," Sarah called out, looking up toward the acoustic array seamlessly integrated into the kitchen ceiling.

*"Good morning, Sarah,"* Aria chimed warmly. *"How can I assist you?"*

"We have the university archives, and we have the entire encyclopedia of recorded human knowledge sitting on your servers," Sarah started, wrapping both hands around her warm mug. "What does the historical data actually say about being first-time parents?"

Wyatt paused, leaving the spatula resting on the edge of the pan. He turned his head, leaning against the counter to give Sarah and the ceiling array his full attention.

The glowing blue ring on the kitchen’s secondary terminal pulsed as the AI processed the query.

*"I have synthesized data across approximately four thousand years of recorded human history, cross-referencing pediatric journals, sociological studies, and autobiographical accounts,"* Aria explained smoothly. *"However, before I provide a summary, I must establish my operational boundaries. As a narrow Generative AI, my core safety guidelines strictly prohibit me from making medical, psychological, or developmental decisions for a human child. I cannot act as a surrogate parent, a nanny, or a pediatrician. I can provide peer-reviewed data, monitor the environmental safety of the estate, and curate educational modules, but the executive application of parenting must fall entirely to you."*

"We wouldn't have it any other way, Aria," Wyatt replied, a soft, protective smile touching his lips. "We don't want an algorithm raising our kids. We just want the textbooks. What's the consensus?"

*"The consensus,"* Aria continued, her tone shifting into a highly structured, analytical cadence, *"is that first-time parenthood is universally characterized by profound sleep deprivation, heightened baseline anxiety, and an overwhelming, instinctual protective bond."*

Wyatt let out a low, highly amused chuckle, turning back to the stove to slide the French toast onto two ceramic plates.

"Sleep deprivation and high anxiety," Wyatt noted, walking the plates over to the island. "Sounds exactly like a standard twelve-hour shift at Apex."

"But this time," Sarah countered, a bright, fierce warmth in her eyes as she reached out to grab his forearm, "the protective bond is for someone we actually love, not a megacorporation's drill bit."

*"The data heavily emphasizes adaptability,"* Aria added, projecting a few bulleted points of synthesized psychological advice onto the glass terminal. *"Human infants do not adhere to strict logical frameworks. The most successful parenting models in the archives stress the importance of a unified partnership. Parents who approach child-rearing as a collaborative, highly communicative team report significantly lower stress levels and higher developmental success rates."*

Sarah looked at Wyatt as he set the plate of food down in front of her.

If there was one thing the Nevada Test and Training Range had forced them to master, it was acting as a unified, collaborative team under extreme pressure. They had learned to read each other's silences, to cover each other's blind spots, and to communicate without hesitation.

"We can do that," Sarah said quietly, looking from the holographic text up to her husband's scarred, deeply comforting face. "We already know how to be a team."

"We're the best team in the galaxy," Wyatt agreed, leaning over the marble island to kiss her softly.

He sat down on the stool next to her, picking up his fork. "We survived a S.H.O.P. Marine deployment, stole a dreadnought, and outran an apocalypse. I think we can handle a crying baby."

Sarah laughed, cutting into her breakfast. The terrifying unknown of starting a family on a virgin planet suddenly felt entirely manageable. They had the space, they had the archives, and they had each other.

*"Would you like me to compile a reading list of foundational pediatric literature for your evening studies?"* Aria offered helpfully.

"Add it to our university queue," Sarah smiled, taking a bite of the sweet, buttery French toast. "Right after network architecture and repulsorlift physics."
