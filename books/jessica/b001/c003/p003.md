# Part 3: The Confrontation at Aurora

Inside the fourth-floor loft at Aurora Systems, the stairwell door banged open and Evan Rothwell strode in. He didn’t notice the shift in the room at first—the way conversations clipped short, the way eyes flicked up from monitors and then back down again.

“Alright, people, I’ve been thinking about our talent strategy,” he announced. “We need a name. A *big* name. Someone who’ll put AuroraOS on the map. And I think we all know who that is.”

No one answered. It was then that he started to see them: little black-and-white pins, clipped to hoodies, flannel shirts, and lanyards. NOT FOR SALE. On every chest.

“What’s with the buttons?” he asked, his tone half-amused, half-dismissive. “Some kind of… retro protest thing?”

Silence. He moved deeper into the loft, peering over shoulders at code he didn’t understand but felt compelled to comment on anyway. “You know, if we had Jessica Taylor in here, we could clean this up in half the time. She’s brilliant, but she needs direction. The right leadership. Someone who can see the big picture.”

No one dared to say what they were thinking—that Jessica didn’t need his “direction,” and that she couldn’t even get into the building without being carried. Once Evan had an idea in his head, it was immovable. And now that idea was Jessica Taylor. He tapped his legal pad. “I want outreach started this week. Make it happen.”

The buttons stayed on. The eyes stayed down. And the quiet, stubborn wall between Evan and his developers grew another brick higher.
