# Part 2: Kelly and Ryan’s First Day

The automatic glass doors of Quantum's Building Four slid open with a soft hiss, and Kelly Madsen rolled her eyes at her twin brother, Ryan.

"Okay, stop looking so terrified," she whispered, giving his arm a playful shove. "It's just an internship."

"You got to meet them all before," Ryan whispered back, clutching the strap of his new messenger bag. "You know they're not secretly judging my taste in flannel."

"They're not," she assured him, though her own heart was beating a little faster than usual. "They're the good ones."

A friendly face at the reception desk greeted them, and within minutes, they had their own blue-and-white Quantum employee badges. Paid interns. The words still felt unreal to Kelly after her months at Aurora.

They were met in the lobby not by an HR coordinator, but by one of the senior engineers from the "War Council," a woman with a warm smile who introduced herself as Maria. She led them past a bustling café and walls lined with patent plaques to a sunny corner office on the third floor.

The office was unlike any Kelly had seen. One wall was a massive whiteboard covered in architectural diagrams. Another was a floor-to-ceiling window overlooking a grove of fir trees. The desk was a custom-built, U-shaped surface, high enough to accommodate a power wheelchair, with multiple monitors displaying lines of crisp code.

And at the center of it all was **Jessica Taylor**.

She looked up from her screen as they entered, and the calm, steady presence Kelly remembered from their first meeting filled the room.

"Kelly. It's good to see you again," Jessica said, her voice warm. She turned her gaze to Ryan. "And you must be Ryan. Welcome to Quantum."

Ryan, who had been expecting a formal, intimidating interview, just managed a nod.

"Alright, let's get you two settled," Jessica said, gesturing to two workstations set up next to each other near the window. "Your machines are ready to go. Logins and first-day docs are on the monitors. Maria will walk you through the basics—timesheets, benefits, where the best snacks are—and then you'll come back here and we'll talk about what we're building."

She paused, her eyes meeting Kelly's. The unspoken memory of Aurora—of an unpaid internship, of a CEO who didn't care—hung in the air for just a second.

"Just so we're clear," Jessica continued, her tone direct but kind. "You're part of the team now. That means you're in our code reviews, our design meetings, and our bug bashes. Your voices matter here. We hired you because you're smart, and we expect you to ask questions when you don't understand something and to speak up when you think we can do better. Understood?"

Kelly felt a lump form in her throat. She looked at Ryan, who was staring at Jessica with a look of awe. This wasn't just a job. This was a place to learn, to contribute, to be respected.

"Understood," Kelly said, her voice coming out stronger than she expected.

As Maria led them out to the main developer area, a few engineers looked up from their keyboards. One of them, a former Aurora developer Kelly recognized from the DevNet threads, gave her a thumbs-up.

"Welcome aboard, Kelly!" he called out. "Good to have you here for real."

For the first time in months, Kelly felt like she was exactly where she was supposed to be. The community had taken care of its own.
