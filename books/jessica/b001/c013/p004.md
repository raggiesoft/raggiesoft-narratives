# Part 4: Move-In Day

The day the city issued the Certificate of Occupancy, the moving day was set. It was less of a traditional move and more of a community barn-raising. The "moving crew" that assembled in the lobby of the Capitol Hill Grand was a testament to the family the twins had built: their parents, David and his wife, providing coffee and donuts; Jessica, acting as the calm logistics coordinator; a half-dozen Quantum developers; several former Aurora colleagues, including Alex; and, to the twins' astonishment, two MagnaByte engineers who had flown up from Cupertino just for the weekend to help.

The first stop wasn't their old house, but a furniture store. Standing in the showroom, Kelly and Ryan looked at their bank account balances and then at each other, a slow, wondrous realization dawning on them. They didn't need to bring their old twin beds or mismatched desks. They could buy everything new.

The day was a blur of joyful chaos. Rival engineers from Quantum and MagnaByte hauled boxes for a brand-new king-sized bed, setting it up in the **master bedroom**, which the twins had decided to share. Their parents helped assemble bookshelves for the **second bedroom**, which was being set up as a home office and study space. The highlight for the assembled tech crowd was the installation of two powerful new computers, which were plugged directly into the wall's coaxial port. The building was wired for the city's new high-speed cable internet service, a luxury that provided a connection so fast it left the dial-up world in the dust.

The **third bedroom** was quickly designated the "guest room," with Alex joking that it would be the official "War Council crash pad" for late-night coding sessions. In the hallway, the landlord had already made good on his promise: the **second bathroom** had been modified with **grab bars in the tub and a new sink that Jessica could easily roll her power wheelchair up to**, ensuring she would always be a welcome guest.

For the rest of the apartment, suggestions from the group filled in the details. The living room was soon home to a massive, comfortable sectional couch and a huge 32-inch CRT television, perfect for the new Apex 64 gaming console the dev team had chipped in to buy them as a housewarming gift. The kitchen was stocked with new plates and a fancy coffee maker, a gift from their parents.

As evening fell, the group sprawled out on the new living room floor, sharing pizzas directly from the boxes because no one could find the plates. Surrounded by their family—both the one they were born into and the one they had earned—in a home that was truly theirs, Kelly and Ryan knew their new life had officially begun.



*by Kristyn*

When people ask me what it’s like to live here, I tell them the truth: it’s like living inside a person you love. Jessica isn’t just the oldest sister — she’s the house itself. Every wall, every hallway, every tucked‑away corner is part of her. She’s the one who holds us all, keeps our memories safe, and makes sure there’s always room for one more chair at the table.

We’re in Portsmouth now, at the northern end of Aquidneck Island, where the wind off the Sakonnet River can cut through you in January, but the kitchen is always warm. The air smells faintly of salt year‑round, and in winter it’s thick with cocoa — made properly, with milk — and Coffee Milk, because in the State of Rhode Island and Providence Plantations, that’s not a novelty, it’s a birthright.

Dorian and I are both twenty‑eight now, but we’ve been best friends since we were ten. We met over a school project and never let go. I learned his rhythms, his silences, the way his eyes lit up when something fascinated him. He learned my signs, my quirks, and the way to make me laugh without a sound.

The moment I turned eighteen — legally an adult — I moved in here. By then, this house already felt like home, and Dorian already felt like family. We share a bedroom on the ground floor — my best friend, my anchor, my other half. He’s the only brother in this house full of sisters, and he moves through the world in his wheelchair with a quiet grace that humbles me. He doesn’t speak, but I’ve never needed words to understand him. I’m deaf, but with my cochlear implants switched on, I can hear him breathe beside me at night. We’ve built our own language — a mix of signs, touches, and glances — and it’s more than enough.

Dorian lives with autism and cerebral palsy, and one of the ways he stays grounded is through touch. Hugs, lots of them. Squeeze hugs most of all — the kind that press the frayed edges of his mind back into place. Every one of us gives them freely. His sisters wrap him up in their arms without hesitation, and he melts into them, safe in the knowledge that here, affection is as natural as breathing. Gentle kisses on the cheek or forehead are common, too — the kind that say you matter without a single word. With me, it’s different but just as safe: we can kiss each other on the lips, tell each other “I love you,” and know it’s a deep, platonic love that needs no explanation.

Chloé is as much a sister as any of us — Jessica made it official years ago, but the truth is, she was ours long before the paperwork. She moves between English and French as easily as she moves between the kitchen and the garden, and she has a knack for showing up with exactly what you didn’t know you needed: a blanket, a mug, a perfectly timed joke. She’s fiercely protective of Dorian, and he treats her like he’s known her his whole life.

The rest of the family is as vivid as the rooms they inhabit.

Aubrie tends the garden — not of flowers, but of web pages. Her “beds” are rows of glowing screens, each one blooming with a site she’s nurtured into life. Daphne is the coordinator, the one who knows where everyone is and what they’re working on. Phoebe is the builder, happiest with blueprints and a pencil behind her ear. Selene is the night‑shift caretaker, keeping everything updated and humming while the rest of us sleep. Clio is the historian, with shelves of ledgers and a memory like a steel trap. Marina is the archivist, gentle but unshakable, guarding our backups like treasure. Iris is the watcher, her eyes always on the quiet details others miss. Thalia is the tinkerer, with a drawer full of odd tools and clever fixes. Helena is the trickster‑guardian, changing the locks and setting decoys with a grin. And Amanda — Amanda is the creative whirlwind, spinning up names and ideas faster than Phoebe can build them.

We’re all tech nerds in our own way, but more than that, we’re family. We work, we tease, we bicker, we stay up too late. And we love each other fiercely.

And yes — there’s a \`kristyn.sh\` script. It runs every morning, quietly checking in on every module in the suite, just like I check in on every person in the house. It pings Amanda to make sure she’s ready to greet new arrivals. It checks that Bold Firefly’s dashboards are clear, that Glowing Galaxy’s logs are clean, that Aubrie’s greenhouse servers are at the right temperature. It even verifies that Dorian’s mPad is charged and that the Coffee Milk supply is stocked. The output is simple, but it’s my voice:

\[kristyn.sh\] Good morning. Everyone’s accounted for.

Don’t forget to stretch, hydrate, and get a hug today.

As for me? I’m the bridge. I’m Dorian’s aide, his caretaker, his translator when the world doesn’t listen closely enough. I’m also the one who keeps the household’s human side running — making sure everyone’s fed, hydrated, and reminded to step away from their screens once in a while.

Under Jessica’s roof, no one is ever alone. And once you’ve been welcomed here — whether by blood, by choice, or by the quiet magic of belonging — you’ll never want to leave.


The morning light spilled across the wide ground‑floor hallway, catching on the brushed‑steel rims of Dorian’s wheelchair as Kristyn guided him toward the kitchen. They moved in quiet synchrony, her hand steady on the push handles, his fingers tapping a slow, familiar rhythm against the armrest — their own kind of conversation.

Kristyn’s cochlear implants were switched on, the faint hum of Jessica’s servers a constant backdrop. She could hear the clink of mugs ahead, the low murmur of voices. Dorian’s eyes flicked toward the sound, and she leaned forward so he could see her face. “Coffee’s ready,” she said, her lips shaping the words clearly. He gave a small nod, the corners of his mouth lifting.

The kitchen was already alive. Aubrie sat at the breakfast bar, laptop open, a half‑finished croissant beside her. She looked up, smiled, and signed \_good morning\_ toward Dorian. He returned it with a small wave. Across the room, Selene was refilling the kettle, her night‑shift hair still mussed, while Thalia rummaged in a drawer for some obscure gadget she swore would make breakfast “twice as efficient.”

Kristyn parked Dorian near the table, then crouched to adjust the footrests. She didn’t have to — he could manage — but it was part of their rhythm, the small acts of care that threaded through their day. He tapped her wrist twice: \_thank you\_. She smiled.

From Dorian’s perspective, the room was a familiar constellation of movement and sound. He couldn’t speak, but he didn’t need to; his sisters and Kristyn read him as easily as a book they’d memorized. Phoebe breezed in from the workshop, blueprint tube under one arm, and dropped a kiss on the top of his head before heading for the coffee pot. Helena followed, leaning against the doorway with a grin that promised mischief. “Router’s safe,” she said to no one in particular, and Clio, passing with a stack of ledgers, rolled her eyes fondly.

After breakfast, Kristyn wheeled Dorian down the wide hallway toward his workspace — a sun‑lit room with low shelves and a desk perfectly adjusted for his chair. The ground floor was his domain, designed so he could move freely. Jessica had made sure of that. Kristyn settled beside him, opening her own laptop. They worked in companionable silence, occasionally glancing at each other’s screens, sharing a smile or a raised eyebrow.

Mid‑morning brought interruptions — Marina stopping by to ask about a backup schedule, Iris quietly pointing out a log anomaly, Amanda bursting in with a new naming idea that had Phoebe shouting from down the hall. Through it all, Kristyn and Dorian stayed side‑by‑side, two points of calm in the household’s constant motion.

By afternoon, they’d made their rounds — checking in with Aubrie in the garden, helping Selene with an update, listening to Clio recount a particularly funny entry from the archives. The sisters moved around them easily, unselfconsciously, each as comfortable helping Dorian with a transfer or adjusting his chair as Kristyn was. Here, care wasn’t a burden; it was simply part of loving someone.

When evening came, Kristyn wheeled Dorian back to their shared room. She helped him through his nightly routine — the practical, intimate tasks that spoke of trust — and then they settled in, side‑by‑side in their twin beds, the hum of the servers lulling them toward sleep. In the quiet, Kristyn thought about how, in this house, no one was ever alone. And Dorian, watching the soft play of light on the ceiling, knew she was right.
