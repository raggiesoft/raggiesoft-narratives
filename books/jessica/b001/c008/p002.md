# Part 2: The Board Meeting

That evening, the executive conference room was dim, the air thick with tension. The last of the leadership team—Marcy, Lydia, and Dana—sat on one side of the long table. At its head sat the board chair, Tom Berringer. The only other voice in the room came from a speakerphone, the clipped tones of Graham Cho, the lead venture capital partner. Evan Rothwell, who was not a voting member of the board, sat opposite them, his protests having no say in the board's operations.

“Evan, this isn’t about spin anymore,” Tom Berringer began. “This is about survival—and we’ve decided Aurora Systems isn’t going to survive.”

From the speakerphone, Graham Cho’s voice was cold and final. “We’re not here to talk about a turnaround. We’re here to talk about an orderly shutdown.”

Lydia Park, the General Counsel, interjected. "Tom, before we formalize, we need to address the intern, Kelly Madsen. Her original agreement was for a one-month unpaid internship, to be followed by a paid position. She has been here nearly three months and has never been compensated."

Berringer nodded grimly. "Thank you, Lydia. That will be included." He cleared his throat and addressed the room. "Therefore, I move that the board take the following actions, effective immediately:

- First, we authorize one final paycheck for all remaining employees.

- Second, we will pay the intern, Kelly Madsen, in full for her entire three months of service, including the first month that was meant to be unpaid, as the board feels a moral and legal obligation to her.

- Third, we begin an orderly wind-down of all company assets, with a full cessation of operations for Aurora Systems by end of day, Friday, September 19, 1997."

A brief silence followed, broken only by the crackle of the speakerphone.

"I second the motion," Graham Cho's voice stated flatly.

"The motion has been made and seconded," Berringer announced. "All in favor?"

A chorus of "Ayes" came from Marcy, Lydia, Graham, and Dana.

"All opposed?"

The silence was absolute.

"The motion carries unanimously," Berringer declared.

It was only then, after the decision was irrevocably made, that Evan found his voice. “You can’t just kill the company!” he protested, his voice cracking.

Berringer looked at him, his expression devoid of sympathy, and hit the table with his hand. “It’s ours, Evan.”

One by one, the board members rose and filed out of the room, leaving Evan alone at the far end of the table. In the dim, airless boardroom, Aurora Systems had just taken its last breath.
