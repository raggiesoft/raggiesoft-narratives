The Grand Hyatt ballroom felt cold and impersonal. Kelly Madsen found a seat in the back row, trying to make herself invisible. She didn't know why she had come, only that Evan Rothwell’s terse demand on her pager had triggered a conditioned, fearful obedience. She knew the developers had walked out, but what did that mean? Was this Evan's desperate, last-ditch effort to save the company?

The room was packed with reporters from every major tech outlet. Evan strode to the podium, a look of defiant confidence on his face that made Kelly’s stomach clench. He began to speak.

“Thank you for coming,” he started. “There have been some misconceptions about a recent internal, informal brainstorming document…”

Kelly almost sighed aloud. She had heard that exact phrase a dozen times in the office, usually right before Evan announced a terrible idea. She watched as the journalists scribbled notes, their faces unreadable. He talked about "strategic restructuring" and "AuroraOS milestones" as if the entire development team wasn't currently across the lake at Quantum.

Then the questions started. A reporter from *TechWire* asked how he could ship a product with no engineers. A woman from *Code & Culture* asked if he was aware the board had already announced a shutdown—a fact Kelly hadn't known until that very second, a piece of information that hit her like a physical blow. *It's actually over*, she thought, the finality of it sinking in.

With each sharp question, Evan deflected. When a reporter from *DevWire* asked if he would apologize to Jessica Taylor, and he replied with a flat "no comment," Kelly saw a visible shift in the room.

It started with a reporter from *ByteLine* packing up her camera. Then two more stood and walked out. It was a slow-motion evacuation. She watched as the journalists' expressions turned from professional interest to boredom, and then to outright dismissal. They had their story, and it was one of delusion and failure.

Soon, half the room was gone. Then it was just a handful of people left. She recognized the independent developer, the one the others called seattle_dev_guy on DevNet, as he stood, shook his head, and walked out.

Suddenly, Kelly realized she was the only person left in the audience who wasn't being paid to be there. Evan was still talking, his voice echoing in the nearly empty ballroom, oblivious. The power he once held over her, the fear he commanded, had completely vanished. He wasn't a CEO anymore; he was just a man talking to himself in an empty room.

She stood up, clutching her tote bag. Her movement was quiet, but in the cavernous silence, it felt like a declaration. Evan didn't notice. He was already launching into his seventh repetition of "informal brainstorming document." Without a backward glance, Kelly Madsen turned and walked out the door, leaving the ghost of Aurora Systems behind her for good.
