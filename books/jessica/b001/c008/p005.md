Kelly pushed through the ballroom doors and into the bustling hotel lobby, her heart hammering with adrenaline. Ryan, who had been anxiously pacing near a potted plant, rushed to her side.

"Kelly! Are you okay? What happened in there?" he asked, his hands hovering near her shoulders, ready to steady her.

A slow, triumphant smile spread across her face. "I'm fine," she said, her voice stronger than it had been in months. "I'm better than fine. I walked out on him, Ryan. While he was still talking to an empty room."

Ryan’s face flooded with relief, followed by a wave of immense pride. He pulled his sister into a tight hug. "Good," he said into her hair. "I'm so glad you're okay."

When he let go, Kelly’s expression was set with a new kind of determination. "But I'm not done," she said, her eyes flashing. "He doesn't get to just get away with all of it. The community, the people on DevNet... they deserve to know the whole truth." She scanned the lobby. "Is there a business center here?"

He pointed toward a small, quiet alcove with a sign that read "Business Center." Inside were two beige tower PCs with bulky CRT monitors. Kelly sat down at one, Ryan standing protectively behind her as she navigated to the DevNet forums. She quickly found the thread started by seattle_dev_guy.

Her fingers flew across the keyboard, a torrent of every frustration and injustice from the past three months pouring out onto the screen. She typed about the broken promises of a paid role, the unethical tasks, and Evan's terrifying, all-consuming obsession with Jessica Taylor. She didn't hesitate. She signed it with her real name, and with a final, decisive click, she hit "post."

Almost instantly, the replies began to flood in.

Kelly — we’ve got you. Seconded. Send me your resume. You’re one of us now. And you’re Not For Sale.

She watched, mesmerized, as the developer community—the one she had been tasked to spy on—wrapped its arms around her. The invitation to meet at Quantum appeared, a beacon of hope. She typed out her reply, agreeing to the Monday meeting, her hands steady now.

Thank you, she typed, her last message on the thread. I didn’t think anyone would have my back after this.

She logged off, the fear that had shadowed her for months finally gone, replaced by an overwhelming sense of belonging. She and Ryan walked out of the business center and through the grand lobby doors to the curb, where their father was waiting to take them home.
