# Part 2: A Poisoned Well

The conference room door clicked shut behind Kelly, leaving the members of the War Council in a heavy, contemplative silence. Through the glass wall, they could see her at the reception desk, smiling faintly as she confirmed her visitor badge return. A senior engineer had already slipped her a list of peer mentors and internship contacts.

Jessica broke the silence, her voice low and steady. “You all heard it. ‘Win her over’ wasn’t about a role or a project. It was personal. And it was obsessive.”

Alex, the senior developer from Aurora, nodded grimly. “Kelly’s not exaggerating. We lived it. He derailed a Q3 code review for a critical kernel module because he’d ‘had an idea’ about how to get you on board. Everything stopped for his fixation.”

A project manager from Quantum leaned forward, his hands clasped on the table. “If that’s the culture baked into AuroraOS, then it’s not just bad code we’re inheriting. It’s his shadow.”

Another ex-Aurora developer spoke up, his voice filled with a weary frustration. “It’s more than his shadow; it’s his fingerprints all over the architecture. Every shortcut we took because he needed us to ‘clear the decks’ for his recruitment strategy—that’s in the code. Every poorly designed module he insisted on because he thought it would look impressive to outsiders—that’s in there, too. We spent months implementing features he thought would appeal to you, Jessica, instead of building a stable core.”

The former Aurora UI lead said what they were all finally ready to admit: “We can’t keep it. Not even as a fork. The foundation is rotten. It’s poisoned.”

Heads nodded in grim agreement around the table. The code wasn't just associated with a toxic person; it was the direct product of his toxicity. To build on it would be to build on a foundation of obsession and flawed logic.

Jessica looked at the faces of the developers who had walked away from their jobs—the ones from Aurora, and the allies from Quantum, Cupertino, and Texas who stood with them. She took a slow, deep breath, as if releasing the last remnant of a project that should have been great. “Then we let it go,” she said, her voice clear and resolved. “We start fresh. No baggage, no ghosts.”

A Quantineer stood, walked to the large whiteboard, and uncapped a black marker. He drew a firm line through the words "AuroraOS - Fork or Rebrand?". Beside it, he wrote in bold, block letters:

**AuroraOS → DECOMMISSIONED. (Reason: Toxic Inception)** **PROJECT PHOENIX (Working Title) → PROPOSED. (Principles: Accessible, Community-Driven, No Ghosts)**

Kelly’s honesty had not only confirmed their fears; it had given them the moral clarity to make the right decision. Her testimony had closed the book on AuroraOS for good.
