Evan Rothwell strode into the fourth-floor loft, thinking the postal pickup had already happened. He dropped into his chair in the executive suite and woke his monitor to a flood of emails with subject lines like "Have you seen this?" and "Damage control?". He clicked the first link, and the *TechWire Daily* headline hit him like a gut punch.

He didn't stab an intercom button. He bellowed.

"KELLY!"

From her desk by the stairwell, Kelly Madsen flinched and was on her feet in a second. She hurried into his office, her notebook in hand, heart pounding. Evan jabbed a furious finger at the screen, not even looking at her.

"I want this spun," he snapped. "Any way possible. Call it brainstorming, call it aspirational, say it was taken out of context. I don't care. Your job is press sentiment. So fix the sentiment. Make this go away. Now."

Before Kelly, who had no media training whatsoever, could even form a response, the phone on Evan's desk rang. He snatched it up. It was the lead venture capital partner. The one-sided conversation was tense and short. Evan slammed the phone down.

His face was pale. He finally turned to Kelly, his eyes wild. "That was our money," he hissed. "They want this fixed. Fast." He gestured vaguely at the phone and the computer screen. "So fix it."

He stormed out of his office and into the small executive conference room, leaving Kelly standing alone, staring at the headline. She was an 18-year-old unpaid intern. She had no idea how to spin a story, let alone fix a multi-million-dollar investor confidence crisis. She felt the blood drain from her face as the weight of his impossible demand settled on her.

Minutes later, in the conference room, the rest of the leadership team knew the truth.

"We’re done," said Marcy Leland, the PR Director, who had seen Evan dump the crisis on the intern as he stormed past.

"The memo is evidence now," added Lydia Park, the General Counsel. "If the ADA angle gains traction, we’re looking at formal complaints. And he just told an 18-year-old to 'handle' it."

"Recruiting is dead," said Dana Wu, the Marketing Director. "Every candidate will search us and see that headline."

They all knew this was the beginning of the end. The only question was how fast the ship, now being captained by a panicked man yelling orders at an intern, would sink.

He stormed out of his office and into the small executive conference room, leaving Kelly standing alone, staring at the headline. The weight of his impossible demand—"fix it"—crashed down on her. The hum of the fluorescent lights in Evan's sterile office suddenly felt deafening. The crisp lines of the furniture blurred, and the air grew thick and heavy, pressing in on her. Her breath hitched. Overwhelmed and utterly abandoned, Kelly’s legs gave out, and she crumpled onto the floor, pulling her knees to her chest as quiet, wracking sobs shook her small frame. *Oh, how she wished Ryan was here.*

A few minutes later, the senior developer who had intercepted the memo noticed she hadn't returned to her desk. Frowning, he walked quietly toward the executive suite and peered into Evan's office. He saw her immediately, a small, crying heap on the floor.

He didn't startle her. Instead, he softly pushed the office door almost closed, shielding her from the view of the rest of the loft. He grabbed a bottle of water from the small office fridge and knelt down a few feet away from her, placing it gently on the carpet.

"Hey, Kelly," he said, his voice low and calm. "It's okay. You're safe. Just breathe. We're not going to let him put this on you."

He didn't ask what was wrong; he already knew. He just sat there in the quiet, a silent, steady presence, until her sobs began to subside into shuddering breaths.

When the end of the day came, Kelly was emotionally drained, her face pale and her movements slow. The senior developer watched her pack her bag, his expression full of concern. The thought of her navigating the long, crowded bus ride home alone in this fragile state was unthinkable.

"Hey," he said, approaching her desk. "I live out south, not too far from you. Let me give you a ride home tonight. No sense in taking the bus when you're this worn out." He looked at her with genuine understanding. "You need to get home to your brother."

Kelly looked up, her eyes glassy with unshed tears. The offer was a lifeline. She just nodded, unable to find the words to express her gratitude. The quiet car ride, away from the noise of the city and the weight of the office, was a small sanctuary. The developer didn't pry or try to make small talk; he just drove, ensuring she got home safely to the one person in the world who could provide the grounding hugs she desperately needed.
