# Part 9: The Bug Bash

After lunch, David led them to a small, comfortable conference room with a large monitor at one end. A handful of MagnaByte's senior QA engineers were already there, laptops open.

"Jessica had an idea," David announced. "We're doing a focused, one-hour bug bash on a new transitions module for Aurora Presenter. The goal is simple: find as many bugs as you can, log them properly, and bonus points for anything that crashes the system."

For the next hour, the room was a flurry of focused activity. Ryan, with his methodical approach, started testing every feature in order. Kelly, however, thought like an artist and a saboteur, trying things the engineers would never expect: pasting a massive image into a text box and applying three different animations to the same object.

Ten minutes in, Kelly raised her hand. "Got one," she said. "If you apply the 'dissolve' transition and then immediately switch to the 'checkerboard' transition while it's still rendering, it creates a memory leak."

One of the MagnaByte QA leads whistled. "That's a nasty one. Good catch, Kelly."

Near the end of the hour, Ryan was deep into testing the slide-sorter view. "Hey, watch this," he said, turning to Kelly. "If I select five slides, apply the new 'flip' transition to all of them, and then drag-and-drop them into a new position while the preview thumbnails are still generating..."

He didn't get to finish the sentence. The entire MagnaByte test machine suddenly went black. A moment later, the screen flickered back to life with the MagnaOS boot-up chime. He had triggered a hard reboot of the entire system.

The room went silent, then erupted in a mix of groans and impressed laughter.

"Whoa," David said, walking over to their station. "You didn't just crash the app; you crashed the whole OS. That's a kernel panic. That's a whopper of a bug." He looked at the twins, who were staring at the rebooting machine, wide-eyed.

By the end of the hour, the twins had logged **five** bugs between them—including the system-crashing one. They weren't just participating; they were excelling.

As the session wrapped up, David walked over to Jessica, shaking his head in mock disbelief. "Okay, now I'm serious," he said, just loud enough for Kelly and Ryan to hear. "I'm telling you, Jess, if you don't have full-time offers waiting for these two on the day they graduate, I'm personally walking them over to MagnaByte's HR department. They're naturals."
