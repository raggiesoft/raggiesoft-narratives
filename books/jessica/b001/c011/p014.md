Instead of a formal restaurant, the MagnaByte van dropped them off at a large, sun-drenched courtyard nestled in the center of the Infinite Loop campus. Long picnic tables were arranged on a vast lawn, and the air was filled with the smell of fresh coffee, bacon, and grilled pastries. Hundreds of MagnaByte employees were already there, chatting in groups and filling their plates from a lavish breakfast buffet. It was a company-wide, picnic-style breakfast, a regular tradition to mark the end of the quarter.

David greeted them with a wide smile, handing them each a plate. "Welcome to our version of a team meeting," he said. "Less MagnaPodium, more pancakes."

They found a spot at a table with the QA team from the bug bash, and were immediately welcomed into the conversation. For Kelly and Ryan, it was another surreal experience—casually eating breakfast alongside the engineers who designed the operating system they had spent the last year learning about.

Halfway through breakfast, a hush fell over the crowd as a figure walked onto the small, permanent stage at the front of the courtyard. It was Stephen Marchand.

This was the first time the twins had seen him in person. He wasn't the larger-than-life icon from the keynote video; he was just a man in a black mock turtleneck and jeans, but his presence commanded the attention of the entire campus. He spoke for a few minutes, outlining his vision for the next quarter, praising the work of various teams, and reinforcing the importance of the new partnership with Quantum.

From their table, Kelly and Ryan watched, captivated. They weren't close enough to shake his hand, but they were there, part of the company he was leading. He was no longer just a name in a tech magazine; he was a real person, and they were working on a project that was a key part of his vision.

As Marchand finished his speech to a round of applause, David leaned over to the twins. "Pretty cool, huh?"

"Yeah," Ryan said, his eyes still on the stage. "It is."

The breakfast wound down, and soon it was time to leave for the airport. They said their goodbyes to David and the other engineers, who all wished them a safe flight and told them to "keep shipping great code." As the accessible van pulled away from Infinite Loop, Kelly looked back at the campus, the last, extraordinary morning of their trip cementing a feeling of profound belonging.
