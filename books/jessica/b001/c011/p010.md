# Part 10: The Kernel Panic Fix

The bug bash was officially over, but Ryan couldn't let it go. He kept staring at the notes he’d taken just before the system crashed, his brow furrowed in concentration.

"I think I know what's causing it," he said, turning to Kelly. "It's a race condition. The drag-and-drop event is fighting with the thumbnail renderer for the same memory block. We just have to make sure one waits its turn."

He looked over at David, who was tidying up the conference room. "David? Would it be okay if we... took a crack at fixing it?"

David raised an eyebrow, surprised and impressed by the initiative. "That's a low-level OS bug, Ryan. It's not in your team's code; it's a flaw in how MagnaOS is handling the graphics driver under stress. That's a senior-level problem."

"I know," Ryan said, his voice steady. "But I think I see the pathway. We can't fix the driver, but we can write a workaround in the Aurora Presenter code to prevent the conflict from happening in the first place."

David looked at the twins, then at Jessica, who gave a subtle nod of approval. He grinned. "The lab is yours. Just log your hours. And order a pizza on the company card. You've earned it."

For the next three hours, with the MagnaByte campus growing quiet around them, the twins worked. Ryan, with his methodical logic, mapped out the sequence of events, while Kelly, with her intuitive knack for seeing how systems break, identified the precise moment the conflict occurred. Together, they wrote a clever, elegant patch: a small piece of code that would check if the thumbnail renderer was active and, if it was, would pause the drag-and-drop UI event for a few milliseconds—just long enough for the conflict to be avoided.

They compiled the fix, ran the test, and held their breath. Ryan selected the five slides, applied the transition, and dragged them while the previews were rendering. Nothing happened. The app didn't crash. The OS didn't crash. It just worked.

When Jessica returned to check on them, they showed her the fix and the log of their successful tests. She was stunned. They hadn't just found a bug; they had diagnosed and fixed a complex kernel-level conflict on their first day, on a foreign operating system.

She excused herself and walked to a quiet corner of the lab, pulling out her cell phone. She dialed a number at Quantum headquarters in Redmond.

"Mark, it's Jessica," she said, her voice low but urgent. "I'm still at MagnaByte. We need to talk about the Madsen interns." She paused, listening. "No, everything's great. It's better than great. Ryan just fixed a kernel panic bug that your QA team and the entire MagnaByte OS division missed."

She let that sink in. "Listen, I know they still have two years of community college left, but we cannot lose them when this internship is over. David is already half-joking about making them an offer, and I don't think he's entirely kidding. They're a package deal—you get both, to ensure Kelly's support needs are met. I'm telling you, Mark, these two are the real deal. I need you to talk to HR and see what we can do about a long-term plan. Now."
