The shared laughter settled back into the comfortable hum of the Creamery. For a few more minutes, everything was as it should be: the scrape of forks on plates, the sweet aftertaste of Coffee Milk, the easy rhythm of family conversation. The bubble of normalcy felt solid, safe.

Then it popped.

The two women who had been watching from the counter finally stood up and approached the booth, their steps hesitant. One clutched her purse, the other a folded copy of the *Harbor News*.

“Excuse me,” the first woman said, her voice soft. “I’m so sorry to bother you. Are you Jessica Taylor?”

The question hung in the air, instantly changing the temperature at the table. Elise’s posture straightened, her expression shifting from relaxed to watchful. Amanda, however, leaned forward with a look of pure fascination.

Jessica managed a small, tight smile. “I am.”

The woman’s face broke into a relieved grin. “I thought so! My daughter, Sophie—she’s fourteen and she’s learning to code. She follows your work. When she saw the article last night, she nearly came unglued. She thinks what you’re doing with accessible design is… well, she called you a legend.”

Jessica’s throat felt dry. A legend? She was an engineer who spent her days chasing down bugs and refactoring code. The word felt like a costume that didn't fit.

“That’s… very kind of her,” Jessica said, the words feeling inadequate.

The second woman unfolded the newspaper, revealing the tech section with Jessica's picture. “We were wondering—and please say no if it’s too much—but would you be willing to sign this for her? She’d never forgive us if we didn’t ask.”

An autograph. The request was so genuine, so disconnected from the world of compilers and beta freezes, that Jessica was momentarily speechless. She wasn’t a rock star. She wasn’t an actor. She was a developer. Developers didn't sign autographs.

Elise started to open her mouth, ready to politely decline for her, but Amanda gave a tiny, excited shake of her head.

Seeing the hopeful look on the women’s faces, Jessica knew she couldn’t say no. She took the pen they offered. The cheap plastic felt foreign in her hand. For a beat, she didn’t know what to write. She settled on signing her name just above the headline, and below it, she added two simple words:

*Keep building.*

She handed it back. The women beamed, thanking her profusely before heading for the door, clutching the signed paper like a treasure.

The moment they were gone, the silence at the table was thick with unspoken thoughts.

Amanda broke it first, her voice a gleeful whisper. “You’re signing autographs now! Jess, this is officially a thing.”

“Are you alright?” Elise asked, her gaze searching Jessica’s face. “We can leave.”

“No, I’m okay,” Jessica said, though the smile she gave didn't quite reach her eyes. She looked past her family, her gaze landing on her own face in the window, circled in red pen. The girl in the photo looked confident, poised, a part of history. The woman in the booth just wanted to finish her johnnycakes.

Her old manager came by to clear their plates, a proud grin spreading across his face. “Saw that,” he said, nodding toward the door. “Breakfast is on the house today. For our local celebrity.”

The word hung there, another reminder that the quiet, anonymous life she had known was slipping away, one signature at a time.
