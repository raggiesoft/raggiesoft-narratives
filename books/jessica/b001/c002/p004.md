Morning broke clear and bright, the kind of late‑summer day that made the bay look like polished glass. Jessica had been home long enough now for the Expo’s noise to fade into the background, replaced by the steady rhythm of island life.

Today, they were all going into Newport for breakfast — the whole Taylor crew. Which meant, for the first time in years, Jessica and Dorian would be riding the bus together again.

At the corner stop, the green‑and‑white \#60 rumbled into view, brakes sighing as it pulled up. The driver — a man with a weathered face and a **Boston Beacons** cap pulled low — caught sight of them in the mirror, then rose from his seat and walked to the back door.

“Two lifts today,” he said with a grin, swinging the door open.

The familiar clunk and whir of the hydraulic platform filled the air as it lowered to the pavement. Jessica went first, rolling onto the metal plate, the sun warming her tires. The lift rose smoothly to meet the bus floor, and she wheeled into the securement spot near the back.

Then it was Dorian’s turn. Elise stayed close, one hand on the back of his chair as he rolled forward. The driver worked the controls with practiced ease, chatting about the weather as the platform lifted again.

Once both chairs were locked in, the rest of the family filed aboard — Aubrie with her tote bag of notebooks, Amanda already craning to see who else was on the bus, Elise taking the seat closest to Dorian.

The ride was a moving scrapbook: Portsmouth’s quiet streets giving way to Middletown’s mix of shops and clapboard houses, the bakery where Aubrie and Jessica used to split almond macaroons, the park where Amanda once tried to fly a kite in a dead calm, the stretch of road where the bay suddenly opened up in a flash of blue.

By the time they crossed into Newport, the city was waking up in earnest. Sidewalk cafés were setting out chairs, gulls wheeled over the harbor, and the brick facades along Broadway caught the morning light.

Jessica felt a grin tug at her face. She’d missed this — the easy chatter of strangers, the jolt of the bus over a pothole, the way the driver waved to every other bus they passed.

Today wasn’t about the Expo, or the headlines, or the knocks at the door. Today was about breakfast in town, the clink of coffee cups, and the comfort of being just another family on the \#60 — two wheelchairs, four sisters, one easy morning.
