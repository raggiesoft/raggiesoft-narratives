The menus were the same laminated sheets Jessica remembered, the corners softened from years of use. The specials board still leaned slightly to one side, chalk letters announcing *Blueberry Pancakes — \$3.99* and *Johnnycakes with Maple Syrup*.

They slid into a corner booth — Jessica and Dorian on the aisle side, the others crowding in opposite. The tabletop was cool under her hands, the Formica pattern unchanged since the summer she’d worked here, balancing trays and refilling coffee cups for the morning regulars.

Her old manager came by with a pot of coffee and a stack of glasses. “Coffee Milk for the lot of you?” he asked, already reaching for the syrup bottle.

“You know us too well,” Elise said.

The first sip was exactly as Jessica remembered — sweet, malty, and just enough caffeine to take the edge off the morning. Plates began to arrive: steaming stacks of pancakes, eggs over easy, toast with slabs of salted butter. The clink of cutlery and the low hum of conversation filled the air.

Halfway through her johnnycakes, Jessica noticed two women at the counter glancing toward their booth. One of them nodded toward the taped‑up *Harbor News* article outside, then back at her. Jessica gave a polite smile and returned to her plate.

Her manager stopped by again, topping off coffee cups. “You still got the same smile you had when you worked here,” he said. “Only difference is now it’s in the paper. You want me to put you down for a Colossal Cooler?”

Jessica laughed. “Only if you’re ready to comp me the fourth one.”

Amanda grinned. “Wait — you’re not actually going to try the three‑Cooler challenge, are you?”

Jessica shook her head. “Not today. I’ve seen what happens when you push it. Whipped cream headaches for hours.”

From the next booth, an older man in a **Boston Beacons** cap leaned over. “Back in my day, we called that Tuesday.”

The table erupted in easy laughter, the kind that came from years of shared shorthand. Outside, the morning sun caught on the glass, lighting up her photo in the window. Inside, the Creamery smelled of coffee, syrup, and vanilla ice cream, and Jessica felt the rare, solid comfort of belonging — not as a headline, but as herself.
