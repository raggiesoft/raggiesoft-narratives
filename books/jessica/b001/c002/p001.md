# Part 1: Homecoming

The late‑summer light was already slanting low when the car turned off the main road and onto the long gravel drive. Jessica leaned her head against the window, watching the familiar silhouette of the old building rise ahead — brick and stone, broad‑shouldered, with the kind of symmetry that spoke of its past life as something official. A school, once. Now, it was theirs.

The driver eased to a stop in front of the wide steps. The front doors stood open, letting the warm air spill out along with the sound of voices — her siblings, unmistakable even at a distance.

She wheeled her suitcase inside, the scent of the place wrapping around her: wood polish, laundry soap, and the faint trace of coffee that seemed to live in the walls.

Aubrie was the first to spot her, appearing in the hallway with her hair pulled back and a smudge of something — probably from her latest web project — on her cheek. “Jess!” she called, and in a heartbeat they were hugging, the kind of hug that pressed away months of absence.

From somewhere deeper in the house came Amanda’s voice, bright and quick: “She’s here?” Footsteps pounded, and then Amanda was there too, grinning, already talking about a dozen things at once.

Elise emerged more quietly, but her smile was no less warm. She didn’t rush — she never did — but when she reached Jessica, her hand rested on her shoulder in a way that said \*welcome home\* without words.

And then Dorian appeared, rolling down the wide hallway in his chair, his eyes lighting up when he saw her. Jessica crouched to meet him, their foreheads touching for a moment in greeting. He tapped her wrist twice — their shorthand for \*I’m glad you’re here\*.

The rest of the evening unfolded in the easy rhythm of family: voices overlapping in the kitchen, the clatter of plates, the smell of something baking. They ate at the long table, the windows open to the sound of crickets, and for the first time in weeks, Jessica felt the Expo’s noise and lights fade into the background.

Here, she wasn’t the lead developer who’d stood on stage beside Stephen Marchand and William Keswick. She was just Jessica — eldest sister, home at last, surrounded by the people who knew her best.
