# Part 3: Evening Edition

By the time the sun had dropped low over the Sakonnet, the Taylors were gathered around the long dining table. The smell of baked haddock and roasted vegetables mingled with the faint salt drifting in through the open windows.

Halfway through passing the bread basket, the familiar thump of the evening paper landing on the porch broke the rhythm. Aubrie was up in a flash, returning with the *Newport Harbor News* folded under her arm. She set it beside Jessica’s plate with a little flourish.

“Harbor News,” she said. “Straight from Newport.”

The front page was all Aquidneck Island: a feature on the upcoming harvest festival in Middletown, a report on ferry schedule changes, and a photo of the Tiverton High girls’ soccer team celebrating a win.

Flipping to the business section brought déjà vu — the same bold headline the *ProStan* had run that morning: **Keswick Draws Record Crowds at MagnaWorld Expo**. Same grainy podium photo, same focus on market impact and investor buzz.

But in the technology section, Jessica’s name was impossible to miss.

**Newport Native Shares Stage with Industry Giant**\
Beneath the headline, a candid shot from the Expo stage caught her mid‑gesture, eyes bright. The article traced her path from growing up in Newport — with time spent in Middletown and now Portsmouth — to graduating from Rogers High School, heading west to Northwest Pacific University, and joining Quantum straight out of college. It highlighted her role as lead developer and co‑architect of Aurora Presenter, praised her work in accessible design, and noted she was “home for a short visit” after the Expo.

Jessica stared at the photo for a moment, fork suspended.

Amanda leaned over. “They actually got your hometown right.”

Jessica gave a small laugh. Of course they had. On Aquidneck Island, if you were born in a maternity ward — civilian or military — you were born in Newport. It was just how the geography worked, and the *Harbor News* knew its own backyard.

Elise, without looking up from her plate, said, “Doesn’t matter. They still don’t get to come knocking.”

Jessica set the paper down, the clink of cutlery and low hum of conversation wrapping around her again. Outside, the last light faded over the bay. Inside, she was still just Jessica — eldest sister, home for dinner — even if the rest of the island was starting to take notice.
