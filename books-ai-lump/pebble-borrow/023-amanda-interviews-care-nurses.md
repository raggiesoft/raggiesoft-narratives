# Amanda Interviews Care Nurses

## The Verification

### Call 1: The Surgeon

# Call 1: The Surgeon

The first number belonged to Dr. Marcus Vance, Chief of Neuro-Trauma Surgery at Johns Hopkins. Amanda expected to be routed to a voicemail or brushed off by a receptionist, but when she stated she was calling regarding Benjamin Torres, she was patched through to the surgeon's office immediately.

"Miss Wilcox," Dr. Vance’s voice came through the receiver, crisp and hurried, but undeniably warm. "I only have about three minutes before I scrub in, but if this is about Ben Torres, I’ll make the time."

"Dr. Vance, I'm vetting Mr. Torres for a highly specialized, private-duty trauma role," Amanda said, keeping her tone strictly professional. "He would be managing below-the-knee paralysis and severe dysautonomia independently. Clinically, is he capable?"

Dr. Vance actually laughed. "Clinically? Ben is the only nurse on my floor who catches a vitals crash before the telemetry monitors do. He's hyper-vigilant. But if you want to know why you should hire him, it’s not just his charting. It’s his character. Last year, we had a nineteen-year-old kid wake up from a spinal fusion realizing he couldn't feel his legs. The kid was terrified, swinging wildly, completely inconsolable. Ben didn't sedate him. He just stood by the bed, held his hand, and talked him down for three hours straight after his shift had officially ended. Ben is a rock. You'd be incredibly lucky to have him."

### Call 2: The Social Worker

# Call 2: The Social Worker

Amanda carefully noted Dr. Vance's exact words on her legal pad before dialing the second number. It belonged to Elaine Croft, the Lead Clinical Social Worker for the Hopkins SCI (Spinal Cord Injury) unit.

"Oh, Benjamin," Elaine sighed warmly when Amanda explained the nature of the call. "I was devastated when he put in his notice, but he told me he was looking for a quieter environment where he could focus on 1:1 patient care."

"My siblings require strict modesty protocols and have specific neurodivergent boundaries regarding physical touch," Amanda pushed, her voice hardening slightly as she remembered Keller and Hayes. "Does Mr. Torres respect physical boundaries?"

"Absolutely and unequivocally," Elaine replied instantly, her tone turning fiercely protective of the nurse. "In acute rehab, a lot of providers accidentally strip patients of their autonomy because it's 'faster' to just do things for them. Ben never does that. He treats every single patient—especially the young, vulnerable ones—with profound dignity. He asks permission before he touches them, he guards their privacy like a hawk, and he never infantilizes them. He is just a genuinely, deeply good human being, Miss Wilcox."

### Call 3: The Director of Nursing

# Call 3: The Director of Nursing

Amanda’s shoulders had finally dropped away from her ears. The knot of anxiety in her stomach was unraveling. She dialed the final number: Helen Ruiz, the Director of Critical Care Nursing at Johns Hopkins.

Helen didn't mince words. "Miss Wilcox, I oversee four hundred nurses. Benjamin Torres is in the top one percent. Not just in skill, but in absolute, unshakeable integrity."

"He mentioned on his application that he is a lifelong non-smoker and has never consumed alcohol," Amanda asked, needing to verify the final, most critical piece of the puzzle given the liquid Fentanyl locked in her cabinet.

"It's true," Helen confirmed gently. "Ben is a straight arrow. He doesn't drink, he doesn't smoke, he doesn't gossip at the charting station, and he doesn't cut corners. If he tells you a medication was pushed at 0800, you can bet your license it was pushed at exactly 0800. If you are running a private medical wing for your family, there is no one on this earth I would trust more to guard their safety."

Amanda slowly hung up the receiver.

She stared at her legal pad. Three separate, high-level hospital executives hadn't just confirmed Benjamin’s clinical abilities; they had gone out of their way to explicitly praise his soul. He wasn't a predator. He wasn't a fugitive.

He was exactly who he said he was.

Amanda let out a long, shaky breath. She leaned forward, rested her forehead against the cool wood of her desk, and for the first time since she had started this brutal vetting process, she let herself cry a few quiet, exhausted tears of pure relief.

She picked up her phone, found Benjamin’s cell number, and dialed.

"Mr. Torres?" Amanda said, her voice finally warm and completely unguarded. "This is Amanda Wilcox. The position is yours. Can you start tomorrow morning?"


Over the next fourteen days, Amanda’s ground-floor charting station became a revolving door of the East Coast's elite medical talent. Using the unshakeable standard Benjamin Torres had set, Amanda ruthlessly filtered the candidates sent by the executive search firm.

If a nurse hesitated at the \$100-an-hour, 8-hour shift structure, they were cut. If a CNA questioned the strict modesty protocols for Drake and Ashleigh's below-the-knee paralysis, they were shown the door. If a therapist raised a single, judgmental eyebrow at the triplets' neurodivergent need for deep pressure therapy, Amanda had security escort them off the Great Falls estate.

By the end of the second week, the roster was complete.

## The Fortress is Staffed

### The Night Watch (The RNs)

# The Night Watch (The RNs)

Amanda hired two hyper-vigilant Cardiac ICU veterans for the night shifts. They were the silent owls of the operation. They didn't need the warm bedside manner of the day shift; their only job was to sit in the dimly lit charting station, their eyes glued to the synchronized telemetry monitors, ready to push IV fluids the exact second Jessika, Drake, or Ashleigh’s dysautonomic heart rates fluttered in their sleep.

### The Muscle (The CNAs)

# The Muscle (The CNAs)

Amanda hired a rotation of highly experienced Certified Nursing Assistants. Paid the exact same \$100-an-hour flat rate as the RNs, these men and women were built like linebackers but possessed the gentlest hands in the industry. They treated Drake and Ashleigh with unparalleled reverence, acting as the physical anchors during the dangerous, highly coordinated pivot transfers to the titanium standing frames.

### The Rebuilders (The PTA and OT)

# The Rebuilders (The PTA and OT)

The Physical Therapist Assistant was a strict but deeply empathetic former military medic who knew exactly how to push Drake and Ashleigh's upper-body conditioning without triggering orthostatic hypotension.

But the greatest victory was the Occupational Therapist. On her very first day, the OT watched Drake and Ashleigh holding onto each other to stave off a sensory overload panic attack. Instead of pathologizing it, the OT immediately wrote “Proprioceptive Deep Pressure Therapy - Patient Regulated” into their official medical charts, permanently, legally validating Amanda’s "Safe Person" dynamic.

### The Shield (The Administration)

# The Shield (The Administration)

Finally, Victor Thorne’s trust finalized the back-office hires. Operating out of a converted guest house near the estate's security gates, a ruthless former hospital compliance officer and a meticulous bookkeeper took over the business side of the PLLC. They managed the massive W-2 payrolls, maintained the DEA narcotic logs with terrifying precision, and ensured that Amanda never had to look at a spreadsheet.

Amanda Wilcox was no longer just a terrified twenty-three-year-old sister trying to keep her family out of foster care. She was the CEO and Clinical Director of a multi-million-dollar, fully operational private medical fortress.

The perimeter was secure. The staff was vetted. The Fentanyl was locked up.

It was time to heal.



At exactly 6:45 AM, Benjamin Torres swiped his newly minted proximity badge against the security reader outside the dedicated clinical entrance of the Great Falls estate. The heavy, frosted-glass commercial double doors clicked open, and he stepped directly into the pristine, climate-controlled hallway of Wilcox Medical Management, PLLC.

He didn't have to walk through a grand foyer or pass a billionaire's art collection. He stepped straight onto hospital-grade flooring and immediately smelled the faint, sterile scent of sanitizing wipes.

He walked down the straight-shot hallway to the central charting station. Amanda was already behind the heavy oak desk, her dual monitors glowing with the live telemetry feeds of the night shift. Standing beside her were two people Benjamin instantly recognized from his pre-employment research: Dr. Victor Thorne and Professor Erika Thorne.

"Good morning, Mr. Torres," Amanda said, her voice crisp but carrying a genuine undercurrent of relief.

"Good morning, Miss Wilcox," Benjamin replied, setting his leather portfolio down and logging into his designated terminal. He turned to the two billionaires. "Dr. Thorne. Professor Thorne. It’s a privilege."

Victor stepped forward and extended his hand. The grip was firm, grounded, and deeply appreciative.

"The privilege is ours, Benjamin," Victor said quietly. "My niece has spent the last two weeks building a fortress to keep my family safe. You are the first person she trusted enough to let inside the gates. I am paying for your absolute vigilance, but I am also asking you, as an uncle, to look after them. When you are on that floor, Amanda is your CEO, but you have the full weight of the Thorne estate behind you. Whatever you need to execute your clinical duties, you ask, and it will be there in an hour."

"Understood, sir," Benjamin nodded respectfully.

"We'll let you get to the handover," Erika smiled warmly, stepping back. "We just wanted to be here to officially welcome you to the family."


At 7:00 AM sharp, Amanda closed the MAR binders and stood up. "Let's go meet your patients."

Amanda led Benjamin out of the charting station and down the short hall to the siblings' sensory-friendly living quarters. As soon as Benjamin crossed the threshold, his clinical senses immediately adjusted to the environment. The room was kept at a strict, chilly 68 degrees to prevent any ambient heat from triggering the triplets' damaged hypothalamuses. The lighting was soft and indirect, preventing optical overstimulation.

Sitting on the massive, U-shaped sectional sofa were the three survivors of the 140-degree cavern.

Drake and Ashleigh were sitting close together, their backs braced against the plush cushions. Because of their below-the-knee paralysis, their legs were carefully elevated on supportive wedges to help gravity return blood to their hearts. Benjamin immediately noted the thick, medical-grade compression garments gripping their thighs and calves—the artificial "second heart" they now relied on. Jessika was curled up on the adjacent chaise lounge, a portable IV pole resting silently beside her, delivering a slow drip of morning electrolytes.

All three of them had heavy, black rectangular telemetry transmitters clipped to the waistbands of their sweatpants, the thin wires disappearing under their shirts to the electrodes on their chests.

When Amanda and Benjamin walked in, the room went entirely still. The triplets looked at the broad-shouldered trauma nurse, their expressions guarded, waiting to see what kind of professional had walked into their sanctuary.

Benjamin didn't rush forward. He didn't put on a booming, fake-cheerful voice. He stopped a respectful six feet away, keeping his hands loosely clasped in front of him so he wasn't looming over them.

"Good morning," Benjamin said, his voice calm, steady, and perfectly modulated. "My name is Benjamin Torres. I'm the new day-shift RN."

He looked directly at Drake, making eye contact without pity. "Drake, Ashleigh, Jessika. Your sister has briefed me extensively on your autonomic baselines, your transfer protocols, and the mechanical realities of your crush injuries. I've spent the last decade running a Neuro-Trauma floor, and I want to be very clear about how I operate."

He paused, ensuring he had all of their attention.

"I am not here to hover over you, and I am not here to treat you like fragile glass," Benjamin stated clearly, his eyes sweeping respectfully over the three of them. "I am here to manage the telemetry, push the IV fluids, and ensure your blood pressure stays exactly where it needs to be so you can focus on just being eighteen years old. I will never initiate physical contact without explaining exactly what I am doing and asking for your permission first. You maintain your dignity, and I maintain your vitals. Are we on the same page?"

Drake stared at the nurse for a long moment. He was so used to medical professionals looking at his paralyzed legs with sad, patronizing eyes, or treating his sisters like delicate porcelain dolls. Benjamin was looking at them like human beings who simply had complex mechanical needs.

Slowly, Drake’s shoulders dropped. He leaned his head against Ashleigh’s shoulder, a physical anchor to ground himself. Ashleigh instinctively wrapped an arm around her brother's chest, squeezing gently.

Benjamin didn't raise an eyebrow. He didn't ask if Drake was okay. He looked right at the deep-pressure co-regulation happening in front of him, mentally logged it as a successful, patient-initiated vagus nerve intervention, and simply gave a respectful nod.

"We're on the same page, Ben," Drake said quietly, the tension finally leaving his voice.

Jessika smiled from the chaise lounge, her shoulders relaxing. Ashleigh let out a long, quiet breath, resting her cheek against Drake's hair.

Amanda stood by the door, watching her siblings completely drop their defensive walls within sixty seconds of Benjamin speaking. She reached into her scrub pocket, her fingers brushing against her gold Presidential Challenge Coin, a profound wave of peace washing over her.

The fortress was secure.
